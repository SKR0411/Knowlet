Kinetic Theory of Gases (KTG)

[span_0](start_span)

Postulates of Kinetic Theory of Gases[span_0](end_span)

This theory explains the macroscopic properties of gases (like pressure, temperature) by considering the motion of their microscopic components (molecules). The key postulates for an ideal gas are:

  1. Molecular Nature: Gases consist of a large number of tiny particles (molecules) that are in continuous, random, and rapid motion.
  2. Negligible Volume: The actual volume of the gas molecules is negligible compared to the total volume of the container. (Molecules are treated as point masses).
  3. No Intermolecular Forces: There are no forces of attraction or repulsion between the gas molecules.
  4. Elastic Collisions: Collisions between molecules, and with the walls of the container, are perfectly elastic. (Total kinetic energy is conserved).
  5. Pressure: The pressure exerted by a gas is due to the continuous bombardment of its molecules on the walls of the container.
  6. Kinetic Energy and Temperature: The average kinetic energy of the gas molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (in Kelvin).
[span_1](start_span)

Derivation of the Kinetic Gas Equation[span_1](end_span)

This equation relates the macroscopic pressure ($P$) to the microscopic properties of the molecules ($m$, $N$, $\overline{c^2}$).

Step-by-step derivation:

  1. Consider a cubic container of side length $l$ containing $N$ molecules, each of mass $m$.
  2. Consider one molecule moving with velocity $c_1$ and components $u_1, v_1, w_1$ in the x, y, and z directions.
  3. When the molecule collides with the wall perpendicular to the x-axis, its x-velocity becomes $-u_1$.
    Change in momentum = (Final) - (Initial) = $(-mu_1) - (mu_1) = -2mu_1$.
    Momentum transferred to the wall = $+2mu_1$.
  4. The time between two successive collisions on the same wall is $\Delta t = \frac{2l}{u_1}$.
  5. Force exerted by this one molecule on the wall is $\frac{\text{Change in Momentum}}{\text{Time}}$:
    $F_1 = \frac{2mu_1}{2l/u_1} = \frac{mu_1^2}{l}$.
  6. Pressure from this one molecule is $\frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}$:
    $P_1 = \frac{F_1}{l^2} = \frac{mu_1^2/l}{l^2} = \frac{mu_1^2}{l^3} = \frac{mu_1^2}{V}$ (since $l^3 = V$, volume of cube).
  7. The total pressure $P$ from all $N$ molecules in the x-direction is the sum:
    $P_x = \frac{m}{V}(u_1^2 + u_2^2 + ... + u_N^2)$.
  8. We define the mean square velocity in the x-direction as $\overline{u^2} = \frac{u_1^2 + u_2^2 + ... + u_N^2}{N}$.
    So, $P_x = \frac{mN\overline{u^2}}{V}$.
  9. The total mean square velocity $\overline{c^2}$ is given by $\overline{c^2} = \overline{u^2} + \overline{v^2} + \overline{w^2}$.
  10. Because motion is random, the average velocity in all three directions is equal: $\overline{u^2} = \overline{v^2} = \overline{w^2}$.
    Therefore, $\overline{u^2} = \frac{1}{3}\overline{c^2}$.
  11. Substituting this into the pressure equation (and knowing $P_x = P_y = P_z = P$):
    $P = \frac{mN}{V} \left( \frac{1}{3}\overline{c^2} \right)$.
Kinetic Gas Equation: $PV = \frac{1}{3}mN\overline{c^2}$
Where: $P$ = Pressure, $V$ = Volume, $m$ = mass of one molecule, $N$ = total number of molecules, $\overline{c^2}$ = mean square velocity.
($c$ is often used for root-mean-square velocity, so $\overline{c^2}$ is $c^2$ or $v_{rms}^2$).

This can also be written in terms of total Kinetic Energy ($KE = \frac{1}{2}mN\overline{c^2}$):
$PV = \frac{2}{3} \left( \frac{1}{2}mN\overline{c^2} \right) \implies$ $PV = \frac{2}{3}KE_{total}$.

Collision Properties and Viscosity

[span_2](start_span)

Collision Properties[span_2](end_span)

  • Collision Diameter ($\sigma$): The distance of closest approach between the centers of two colliding molecules. It's the effective diameter for collisions.
  • Collision Frequency ($Z_1$): The average number of collisions experienced by a single molecule per unit time (per second).
  • Mean Free Path ($\lambda$): The average distance a molecule travels between two successive collisions.
    Formula: $\lambda = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\pi\sigma^2 n^*}$
    Where $n^*$ is the number density ($N/V$).
[span_3](start_span)

Viscosity of Gases[span_3](end_span)

Definition: The internal resistance to flow in a fluid. For gases, it arises from the transfer of momentum between adjacent layers of gas flowing at different speeds.
[span_4](start_span)

Relation between Mean Free Path and Coefficient of Viscosity ($\eta$)[span_4](end_span)

The coefficient of viscosity ($\eta$) is given by the formula:

Formula: $\eta = \frac{1}{3}\rho\overline{v}\lambda$
Where: $\rho$ = density of the gas ($mn^*$), $\overline{v}$ = average molecular speed, $\lambda$ = mean free path.

By substituting the formula for $\lambda$, we can get a relation for $\eta$ in terms of molecular parameters:
$\eta = \frac{1}{3}(mn^*) \overline{v} \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\pi\sigma^2 n^*} \right) = \frac{m\overline{v}}{3\sqrt{2}\pi\sigma^2}$

[span_5](start_span)

Temperature and Pressure Dependence of Viscosity[span_5](end_span)

  • Temperature Dependence:
    • We know that average speed $\overline{v}$ is proportional to $\sqrt{T}$.
    • From the formula $\eta = \frac{m\overline{v}}{3\sqrt{2}\pi\sigma^2}$, we see that $\eta$ is directly proportional to $\overline{v}$.
    • Conclusion: $\eta \propto \sqrt{T}$. The viscosity of a gas increases with an increase in temperature.
    • Reason: Hotter molecules move faster and transfer more momentum between layers, increasing the internal "friction".
  • Pressure Dependence:
    • The formula $\eta = \frac{1}{3}\rho\overline{v}\lambda$ contains density ($\rho$) and mean free path ($\lambda$).
    • As pressure increases (at constant T), density ($\rho$) increases.
    • As pressure increases, $n^*$ increases, so the mean free path ($\lambda$) decreases.
    • These two effects (increase in $\rho$, decrease in $\lambda$) cancel each other out.
    • Conclusion: The viscosity of a gas is largely independent of pressure (at moderate pressures).

Maxwell Distribution of Velocities

The molecules in a gas do not all travel at the same speed. [span_6](start_span)The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution describes the probability distribution of molecular speeds in a gas at a given temperature.[span_6](end_span)

[span_7](start_span)

Evaluating Molecular Velocities[span_7](end_span)

From the distribution curve, we can evaluate three important types of molecular speeds:

  1. Most Probable Velocity ($c_{mp}$ or $v_{mp}$): The speed possessed by the maximum fraction of molecules. This is the peak of the curve.
    Formula: $c_{mp} = \sqrt{\frac{2kT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2RT}{M}}$
  2. Average Velocity ($c_{avg}$ or $\overline{v}$): The arithmetic mean of the speeds of all molecules.
    Formula: $c_{avg} = \sqrt{\frac{8kT}{\pi m}} = \sqrt{\frac{8RT}{\pi M}}$
  3. Root Mean Square (RMS) Velocity ($c_{rms}$ or $c$): The square root of the mean of the squares of the speeds of all molecules. (This is the one from the KTG derivation).
    Formula: $c_{rms} = \sqrt{\frac{3kT}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{3RT}{M}}$

Key Relationship: $c_{rms} > c_{avg} > c_{mp}$ (Ratio $\approx$ 1.224 : 1.128 : 1)

[span_8](start_span)

Average Kinetic Energy[span_8](end_span)

Using the KTG equation ($PV = \frac{1}{3}mN c_{rms}^2$) and the Ideal Gas Law ($PV = nRT = (N/N_A)RT$), we can find the average kinetic energy.

$\frac{1}{3}mN c_{rms}^2 = \frac{N}{N_A}RT$
$\frac{1}{3}m c_{rms}^2 = \frac{RT}{N_A} = kT$ (where $k = R/N_A$ is the Boltzmann constant)
$\frac{1}{2}m c_{rms}^2 = \frac{3}{2}kT$

Average Kinetic Energy (per molecule): $\overline{KE} = \frac{3}{2}kT$
Crucial Point: The average kinetic energy of a gas depends only on the absolute temperature, not on the mass or nature of the gas.

Law of Equipartition of Energy

The Law: For a system in thermal equilibrium, the total energy is divided equally among all its degrees of freedom. [span_9](start_span)Each quadratic degree of freedom (like $\frac{1}{2}mv_x^2$ or $\frac{1}{2}I\omega_y^2$) has an average energy of $\frac{1}{2}kT$ per molecule.[span_9](end_span)
