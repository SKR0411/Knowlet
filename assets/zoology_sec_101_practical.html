1. [span_34](start_span)Identification of different casts in honeybees[span_34](end_span)

For this "spotting" exercise, you must be able to visually distinguish the three castes of honey bees.

Feature Queen Drone (Male) Worker (Sterile Female)
Overall Size Largest (longest body) Stout and robust (wider than worker) Smallest
Abdomen Long, pointed, and tapered. Extends far beyond the wings. Rounded and blunt at the end. Shorter and pointed.
Eyes Small, separate. Very large, compound eyes that meet at the top of the head. Small, separate.
Hind Legs No special features. No special features. Has Pollen Baskets (Corbicula) - a wide, flat area with hairs for carrying pollen.
Stinger Present (smooth, can sting multiple times, but rarely does). Absent. Present (barbed, stings once and dies).

2. [span_35](start_span)Structure of honey comb[span_35](end_span)

You will identify a piece of natural or framed honeycomb. Look for these features:

  • Material: The comb is made of beeswax, secreted by worker bees.
  • Shape: Composed of hexagonal cells. This shape is the most efficient, using the minimum amount of wax to store the maximum amount of honey.
  • Cell Types:
    • Worker Cells: The most numerous and smallest cells. Used to raise worker bees and to store honey and pollen (bee bread).
    • Drone Cells: Noticeably larger than worker cells. Used to raise drones. Often located on the edges of the comb.
    • Queen Cells: Very large, peanut-shaped cells that hang vertically from the comb. Used only for raising a new queen.
  • Contents: You may see capped honey (covered with a white wax cap), capped brood (covered with a darker, brownish cap), pollen (packed, colorful), or open cells with larvae.

3. [span_36](start_span)Morphological peculiarities of worker bees[span_36](end_span)

This involves identifying the specialized body parts (adaptations) of a worker bee that allow it to perform its jobs.

  • Pollen Basket (Corbicula): A wide, concave, polished area on the outer surface of the hind tibia (leg), surrounded by stiff hairs. Used to transport large pellets of pollen.
  • Honey Stomach (Crop): An internal organ (not visible externally) for transporting nectar back to the hive.
  • Wax Glands: (Also internal/microscopic) Located on the ventral (under) side of the abdomen, secreting wax scales.
  • Barbed Stinger: A modified ovipositor (egg-laying organ) at the tip of the abdomen. The barbs cause it to lodge in the skin of mammals, and the bee dies after stinging.
  • Proboscis: The "tongue" of the bee. A set of modified mouthparts adapted for sucking up liquids like nectar and water.

4. [span_37](start_span)Identification of bee keeping instruments and bee hives[span_37](end_span)

This is a "spotting" exercise to identify the tools of the trade.

  1. Langstroth Hive: The large, rectangular, multi-box hive. Identify its parts: Bottom Board, Brood Chamber, Super, Frames, Inner Cover, Top Cover.
  2. Newton Hive: A smaller, single-box hive used for Apis cerana.
  3. Bee Veil: The mesh hat/helmet used to protect the face.
  4. Smoker: The metal can with bellows used to puff smoke.
  5. Hive Tool: The L-shaped or J-shaped crowbar used for prying frames.
  6. Honey Extractor: The large centrifuge (drum) used to spin honey out of frames.
  7. Uncapping Knife: A special knife (often electric or heated) used to slice the wax caps off honeycombs.
  8. Queen Excluder: A flat grid designed to keep the queen out of the honey supers.

5. [span_38](start_span)Visit to an Apiculture Farm and Preparation of a Field Report/Project Report/PowerPoint presentation[span_38](end_span)

This practical requires you to observe a real-world apiary and document your findings. Your report should be structured, clear, and scientific.

Structure for your Report / Presentation:

  • 1. Introduction:
    • Aim: To study the practices of bee rearing and management at [Name of Farm].
    • Date and Location: When and where was the visit?
    • Brief Overview: A short summary of the farm (e.g., "a 50-colony apiary specializing in litchi honey").
  • 2. Observations:
    • Apiary Site: Describe the location. Was it near water? What was the pasturage? Was there a windbreak?
    • Bee Species: What species was being hived (e.g., Apis mellifera)?
    • Hive Type: What type of hives were used (e.g., Langstroth)?
    • Equipment: What tools did the beekeeper demonstrate (smoker, hive tool, etc.)?
    • Colony Health: Did you observe brood, pollen, and honey? Did the colonies seem strong?
    • Honey Extraction: (If observed) Describe the process. Was it modern or traditional?
  • 3. Discussion (Interview with the Beekeeper):
    • What are the main products (e.g., honey, wax, pollination services)?
    • What are the main challenges (e.g., diseases, pests, low prices, pesticide use by nearby farmers)?
    • What is the economics (e.g., cost of one hive vs. annual profit)?
    • What government support, if any, do they receive?
  • 4. Conclusion:
    • Summarize what you learned.
    • What is your assessment of the farm's success and sustainability?
    • Include photographs (if a report) or slides with bullet points (if a presentation).
