Salient Features (of Land Plants)

Land plants, also known as Embryophytes, are distinguished from their aquatic algal ancestors by a set of key features (salient features) that evolved in response to the challenges of a terrestrial environment.

  • Embryo Formation: This is the defining feature. The zygote (fertilized egg) is retained within the female parent's tissues (the archegonium), where it develops into a multicellular embryo that is protected and nourished. Algae lack this feature; their zygote is often released into the environment.
  • Alternation of Generations: A heteromorphic life cycle with a multicellular haploid (n) gametophyte and a multicellular diploid (2n) sporophyte.
  • Multicellular Gametangia: Sex organs (antheridia and archegonia) are multicellular and have a protective outer layer of sterile cells. This prevents the gametes from drying out.
  • Cuticle: A waxy, waterproof layer covering the plant's aerial parts to reduce water loss.
  • Stomata: (Present in most land plants, except liverworts). Pores for regulating gas exchange and transpiration.
  • Spores with Sporopollenin: The sporophyte produces haploid spores via meiosis. These spores are covered in sporopollenin, one of the most durable organic materials known, which protects them from desiccation and UV radiation.
  • Apical Meristems: Regions of cell division at the tips of roots and shoots, allowing the plant to grow in length, both upwards towards light and downwards into the soil for water and nutrients.

Adaptations to Land Habit

This topic details the specific solutions plants evolved to survive on land. These adaptations can be categorized by the problem they solve.

1. For Preventing Water Loss (Desiccation):

  • Cuticle: A waxy layer on the epidermis. The thicker the cuticle, the more arid the environment the plant can withstand.
  • Stomata: Pores, mainly on the underside of leaves, that can close to conserve water and open to allow $CO_2$ for photosynthesis.
  • Spores/Pollen with Sporopollenin: Protects reproductive cells from drying out.

2. For Transporting Water and Nutrients:

  • Rhizoids/Roots: Simple rhizoids in bryophytes anchor the plant and absorb water by capillarity. True roots in Pteridophytes/Gymnosperms actively absorb water and minerals from deep in the soil.
  • Vascular Tissues (Xylem/Phloem): Evolved in Pteridophytes (Tracheophytes).
    • Xylem: Transports water and minerals from roots to leaves. The lignin in xylem walls also provides structural support.
    • Phloem: Transports sugars (food) from leaves to other parts of the plant.

3. For Structural Support (Against Gravity):

  • Lignin: A complex polymer that strengthens the cell walls of xylem, providing rigidity and allowing plants (like ferns and trees) to grow tall.
  • Collenchyma and Sclerenchyma: Specialized supportive tissues.

4. For Reproduction on Land:

  • Multicellular Gametangia: The sterile jacket of cells protects the sperm and egg from desiccation.
  • Retention of the Embryo: The zygote develops within the protection of the female archegonium.
  • Shift to Sporophyte Dominance: The diploid sporophyte is more resilient to mutations (having two copies of each gene) and better adapted to the harsh land environment.
  • Pollen (in Gymnosperms): A "flying" male gametophyte that carries sperm directly to the ovule, eliminating the need for water for fertilization.
  • Seed (in Gymnosperms): A "baby plant in a box with its lunch." The seed contains the embryo, a food supply (endosperm/gametophyte), and a protective seed coat. This allows the new plant to wait for favorable conditions before germinating.
Exam Tip: When asked about adaptations, structure your answer into categories: 1) Water Conservation, 2) Nutrient/Water Transport, 3) Structural Support, and 4) Reproduction. This shows a clear understanding of the *problems* land plants had to solve.

Amphibian Nature of Bryophyte

Bryophytes (mosses, liverworts, hornworts) are often called the "amphibians of the plant kingdom." This analogy is used because, like amphibians (frogs, salamanders), they are partially adapted to land but are still fundamentally dependent on water to complete their life cycle.

Why are they "Amphibians"?

1. Terrestrial Adaptations (The "Land" part):

  • They have a (thin) cuticle to reduce water loss.
  • Mosses and hornworts have stomata on their sporophytes for gas exchange.
  • They have rhizoids for anchoring to the substrate.
  • Their spores are protected by sporopollenin and dispersed by air.
  • They have multicellular, protected gametangia.

2. Aquatic Dependencies (The "Water" part):

  • Water for Reproduction: This is the most critical dependency. The antheridia release motile, flagellated sperm that must swim through a film of water (rain, dew) to reach the egg in the archegonium. Without this external water, fertilization cannot occur.
  • Water for Hydration: Most bryophytes lack true vascular tissue (xylem/phloem). They are poikilohydric, meaning their internal water content varies with the environment. They absorb water directly through their surfaces (like a sponge) and can dry out quickly. This forces them to live in moist, shady habitats.
  • Lack of True Roots: Rhizoids are not efficient at absorbing water from deep soil. Their water uptake is largely superficial.
In summary: Bryophytes can live on land (unlike algae), but they cannot reproduce or thrive far from a source of moisture. This dual-life, tied to both land and water, is why they are called the amphibians of the plant world.

Evolution of Land Plants

The evolution of land plants is thought to have occurred from a group of green algae (Charophytes) about 470 million years ago. The evolutionary history of archegoniates shows a series of major innovations that allowed for greater success on land.

Key Evolutionary Steps:

  1. Origin of the Embryo (The first land plants - Bryophytes):
    • The first major step was the development of the Cembryo and the archegonium to protect it.
    • This group (Bryophytes) was still non-vascular and gametophyte-dominant.
  2. Origin of Vascular Tissue (The Tracheophytes - Pteridophytes):
    • The next big leap was the evolution of xylem and phloem. This allowed for efficient transport of water, enabling plants to grow tall and away from immediate water sources.
    • This led to the sporophyte becoming the dominant generation, as seen in ferns and other Pteridophytes. These plants could now form forests.
  3. Origin of the Seed (The Spermatophytes - Gymnosperms):
    • The evolution of the seed was the final step in conquering the land, breaking the "amphibian" dependence on water for reproduction.
    • Pollen delivered sperm directly to the ovule, making water for fertilization unnecessary.
    • The ovule matured into a seed, which protected the embryo and provided it with food, allowing it to be dispersed and to survive unfavorable conditions.
    • This group (Gymnosperms) also saw the complete reduction of the gametophyte, making it fully dependent on the dominant sporophyte.
