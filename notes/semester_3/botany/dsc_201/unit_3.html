Distinguishing Features

Bryophytes are the simplest and most primitive group of land plants. They are non-vascular embryophytes.

  • Gametophyte-Dominant Life Cycle: The main plant body is the haploid gametophyte (n), which is independent, photosynthetic, and long-lived.
  • Dependent Sporophyte: The diploid sporophyte (2n) is simple, short-lived, and partially or completely dependent on the gametophyte for nutrition.
  • Non-Vascular: They lack true vascular tissues (xylem and phloem). Water transport occurs via simple parenchyma cells or by capillary action.
  • Lack of True Organs: The plant body is a thallus (undifferentiated) or has simple leaf-like (phylloid) and stem-like (cauloid) structures. They lack true roots, stems, and leaves.
  • Anchorage: Anchored to the substrate by unicellular or multicellular rhizoids.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By fragmentation or specialized structures called gemmae.
    • Sexual: Oogamous type. Sex organs (antheridia and archegonia) are multicellular with a sterile jacket.
  • Water for Fertilization: Motile, biflagellate sperm require water to swim to the archegonium.

Classification

Bryophytes are broadly classified into three main classes (or divisions):

  1. Class Hepaticae (Liverworts):
    • Gametophyte is thalloid (flat, dichotomously branched, e.g., *Riccia*, *Marchantia*) or foliose (leafy, with "leaves" in 2-3 rows, e.g., *Porella*).
    • Rhizoids are unicellular and unbranched.
    • Sporophyte is simple, often without stomata (e.g., *Riccia*).
  2. Class Anthocerotae (Hornworts):
    • Gametophyte is a simple, thalloid body.
    • Each cell typically has a single large chloroplast with a pyrenoid (an algal character).
    • Sporophyte is a unique, horn-like, long cylindrical structure that grows continuously from a basal meristem.
    • Sporophyte has stomata and a photosynthetic region.
    • Example: *Anthoceros*.
  3. Class Musci (Mosses):
    • Gametophyte is differentiated into a juvenile (protonema) stage and a mature, leafy gametophore stage.
    • "Leaves" are arranged spirally on the "stem."
    • Rhizoids are multicellular, branched, and have oblique septa.
    • Sporophyte is highly differentiated into a foot, seta, and capsule.
    • Capsule has a sophisticated mechanism for spore dispersal (e.g., peristome teeth).
    • Example: *Sphagnum*, *Polytrichum*.

Reproduction

Bryophytes reproduce both asexually and sexually.

Asexual Reproduction

  • Fragmentation: Older parts of the thallus die, and the younger branches grow into new plants.
  • Gemmae: Multicellular green bodies produced in special structures called gemma cups (e.g., *Marchantia*). When dispersed by rain, they germinate to form new gametophytes.
  • Protonema: In mosses, the protonema can fragment, and its buds can grow into new plants.

Sexual Reproduction

  1. The gametophyte (n) bears the sex organs: antheridia (male) and archegonia (female).
  2. The antheridium produces biflagellate antherozoids (sperm).
  3. The archegonium produces a single egg (oosphere).
  4. At maturity, antherozoids are released and swim through a film of water to the archegonium.
  5. Fertilization occurs in the archegonium, forming a diploid zygote (2n).
  6. The zygote is retained and develops into the embryo, which grows into the sporophyte (2n), still attached to the gametophyte.
  7. Inside the sporophyte's capsule, spore mother cells (2n) undergo meiosis to produce haploid spores (n).
  8. Spores are dispersed by wind. On a suitable substrate, a spore germinates to form a new gametophyte (n) (either directly or via a protonema), completing the cycle.

Structure of Sporophytes & Evolutionary Trends

The central theme in bryophyte evolution is the progressive sterilization of sporogenous (spore-producing) tissue. This means that as evolution progressed, more and more of the sporophyte's tissue became sterile and took on other functions (like photosynthesis, support, and spore dispersal), leading to a more complex sporophyte.

1. Riccia (A Liverwort)

  • Gametophyte: Thalloid, prostrate, dichotomously branched, forms rosettes.
  • Sporophyte: The simplest sporophyte among all bryophytes.
    • It is just a spherical capsule, with no foot or seta.
    • It remains embedded within the gametophyte thallus.
    • It lacks any sterile tissue (no elaters, no columella). The entire internal mass consists of spore mother cells.
    • Evolutionary Status: Most primitive. No sterilization of tissue. Spore dispersal is passive (by decay of the thallus).

2. Marchantia (A Liverwort)

  • Gametophyte: Thalloid, prostrate, dichotomously branched. Sex organs are borne on specialized stalks (antheridiophores and archegoniophores).
  • Sporophyte: More complex than *Riccia*.
    • Differentiated into a foot (for absorption), a short seta (for elevation), and a capsule.
    • Inside the capsule, there is sterile tissue in the form of elaters (long, spindle-shaped cells with spiral thickenings).
    • Evolutionary Status: A step up from *Riccia*. Sterilization has occurred to form elaters, which aid in spore dispersal by their hygroscopic (twisting) movements.

3. Pellia (A Liverwort)

  • Gametophyte: Simple, thalloid.
  • Sporophyte: Differentiated into foot, seta, and capsule.
    • The seta elongates rapidly at maturity, pushing the capsule out.
    • The capsule contains both spores and elaters.
    • An "elaterophore" (a bundle of fixed elaters) is present at the base of the capsule.
    • Evolutionary Status: Shows differentiation and a mechanism for elevating the capsule for better spore dispersal.

4. Porella (A "Leafy" Liverwort)

  • Gametophyte: Foliose (leafy), with "leaves" in three rows on a "stem."
  • Sporophyte: Similar to *Pellia*, with a foot, seta, and capsule.
    • The capsule wall is several layers thick.
    • It contains spores and elaters.
    • Evolutionary Status: The gametophyte is more complex (leafy), but the sporophyte follows the typical liverwort pattern of having elaters for spore dispersal.

5. Anthoceros (A Hornwort)

  • Gametophyte: Simple, thalloid.
  • Sporophyte: Highly advanced and unique.
    • Consists of a bulbous foot and a long, cylindrical, horn-like capsule (no seta).
    • Has an intercalary meristem at its base, allowing it to grow continuously.
    • The capsule wall is photosynthetic and has stomata (like higher plants).
    • A central column of sterile tissue, the columella, runs through the capsule.
    • Pseudo-elaters (multicellular, unlike true elaters) are present and aid in dispersal.
    • Evolutionary Status: Very advanced. Shows significant sterilization to form a photosynthetic wall, stomata, and a columella. The indeterminate growth is a unique feature. It represents a different evolutionary line.

6. Sphagnum (A Moss - "Peat Moss")

  • Gametophyte: Unique, with "branches" in clusters and specialized water-holding cells (hyaline cells).
  • Sporophyte:
    • Has a foot and a globose capsule, but the seta is very short.
    • The capsule is elevated by a stalk of gametophytic tissue called the pseudopodium.
    • Contains a central columella.
    • Lacks peristome teeth. Spore dispersal is explosive: as the capsule dries, it builds up pressure and the lid (operculum) is blown off.
    • Evolutionary Status: Advanced, with a columella, but has a unique (non-homologous) method of elevating the capsule and a different dispersal mechanism from other mosses.

7. Polytrichum (A Moss - "Haircap Moss")

  • Gametophyte: Highly developed, with "leaves" showing complex lamellae for photosynthesis and a "stem" with a central conducting strand.
  • Sporophyte: The most complex sporophyte among bryophytes.
    • Differentiated into a foot, a long seta, and a capsule.
      • * The capsule is highly complex, with a photosynthetic region (apothem), stomata, and a central columella.
    • Spore dispersal is controlled by a ring of peristome teeth that respond to humidity (closing when wet, opening when dry).
    • Evolutionary Status: Peak of bryophyte evolution. Shows maximum sterilization of tissue to form a complex capsule with an efficient, hygroscopic dispersal mechanism. The sporophyte is also photosynthetic, making it less dependent on the gametophyte.
Evolutionary Trend Summary: The "progressive sterilization theory" is key.
Riccia (no sterile tissue) -> Marchantia (sterile elaters) -> Anthoceros (sterile columella, stomata, photosynthetic wall) -> Polytrichum (sterile columella, seta, and complex peristome).
This shows a trend from a simple sporophyte (all fertile) to a complex sporophyte (mostly sterile tissue) dedicated to support, photosynthesis, and efficient spore dispersal.

Economic Importance of Bryophytes (with reference to Sphagnum)

General Importance:

  • Ecological Role: As pioneer species, they prevent soil erosion and are bioindicators of pollution (as discussed in Unit 1).

Specific Importance of Sphagnum (Peat Moss):

Sphagnum is by far the most economically important bryophyte.

  1. Formation of Peat: In cool, acidic bogs, *Sphagnum* grows continuously from the top while the lower parts die and accumulate without fully decomposing (due to acidity and anaerobic conditions). This accumulated, partially carbonized material is peat.
    • As a Fuel: Dried peat is used as a fuel source in many countries (e.g., Ireland, Finland) for heating and electricity.
    • As a Carbon Sink: Peat bogs store vast amounts of carbon, more than all the world's forests combined, playing a crucial role in regulating the global climate.
  2. Horticultural Use:
    • Soil Conditioner: Peat moss is added to garden soil to increase its water-holding capacity and acidity, which is ideal for acid-loving plants (e.g., blueberries, rhododendrons).
    • Packing Material: Dried *Sphagnum* is extremely absorbent and lightweight. It is used to pack live plants, bulbs, and flowers for shipping, as it retains moisture and prevents fungal growth (due to its acidic, antiseptic properties).
  3. Absorbent & Antiseptic Properties:
    • Surgical Dressings: *Sphagnum* has high absorbency and mild antiseptic properties. It was used extensively during World War I as a first-aid dressing for wounds when cotton was scarce.
