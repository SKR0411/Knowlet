General Characteristics, Classification, and Origin

General Characteristics

Pteridophytes (e.g., ferns, horsetails, club mosses) are the first terrestrial vascular plants (Tracheophytes). They are often called "vascular cryptogams" (vascular plants without seeds).

  • Sporophyte-Dominant Life Cycle: The main plant body is the diploid sporophyte (2n), which is independent and differentiated into true roots, stem (often a rhizome), and leaves (megaphylls or microphylls).
  • Vascular Tissues: They are the first plants to have true xylem (for water transport) and phloem (for food transport).
  • Independent Gametophyte: The haploid gametophyte (n), called a prothallus, is small, short-lived, and independent (photosynthetic).
  • Reproduction:
    • The sporophyte produces haploid spores (n) by meiosis in sporangia. Sporangia are often clustered on leaves called sporophylls (e.g., in cones/strobili or on the underside of fern fronds).
    • Spores germinate to form the prothallus.
    • The prothallus bears antheridia and archegonia.
  • Water for Fertilization: Like bryophytes, they require water for the motile sperm to swim to the archegonium.
  • Homosporous vs. Heterosporous:
    • Homosporous: Produce one type of spore, which grows into a bisexual gametophyte (e.g., *Lycopodium*, *Pteris*).
    • Heterosporous: Produce two types of spores: small microspores (male) and large megaspores (female). This is a crucial step towards seed evolution (e.g., *Selaginella*, *Marsilea*).

Classification (Simple overview)

Pteridophytes are classified into four main divisions:

  1. Psilopsida: (e.g., *Psilotum*). Most primitive. Roots are absent (rhizoids present). Stem is dichotomously branched.
  2. Lycopsida: (e.g., *Lycopodium*, *Selaginella*). "Club mosses." Have microphylls (small leaves with a single vein). Sporangia are borne on sporophylls, often in a strobilus (cone).
  3. Sphenopsida: (e.g., *Equisetum*). "Horsetails." Stems are jointed, with whorls of scale-like leaves.
  4. Pteropsida: (e.g., *Pteris*, *Marsilea*). "True ferns." Have large, complex leaves (fronds or megaphylls), often divided.

Early Land Plants (Cooksonia and Rhynia)

These are extinct fossil plants from the Silurian and Devonian periods, representing the earliest known vascular plants.

  • Cooksonia: The oldest known plant with a stem and vascular tissue. It was a simple, small plant with dichotomously branching stems, no leaves or true roots (only rhizoids), and sporangia at the tips of the branches.
  • Rhynia: A well-preserved fossil from the Rhynie Chert (Scotland). It was more advanced than *Cooksonia*.
    • Had a creeping rhizome with rhizoids.
    • Upright, cylindrical, dichotomously branched stems.
    • No leaves. Stems were photosynthetic.
    • Had a simple protostele (a solid core of xylem) in the stem.
    • Bore elliptical sporangia at the tips of branches.
  • Significance: They show us the first steps in the evolution of a dominant, vascular sporophyte. Their simple, leafless, dichotomously branching structure is the "raw material" from which more complex plant bodies evolved (see Telome Theory).

Structure and Reproduction of Representative Genera

1. Psilotum (Whisk Fern)

  • Structure: A "living fossil." Has an underground rhizome with rhizoids (no true roots). Aerial stems are green, photosynthetic, and dichotomously branched. Leaves are tiny, scale-like.
  • Reproduction: Sporangia are fused in groups of three (a synangium) in the axils of small, forked "leaves." It is homosporous.

2. Lycopodium (Club Moss)

  • Structure: Plant body has true roots, a branching stem (creeping or erect), and is covered in small, simple leaves (microphylls).
  • Reproduction: Homosporous. Sporangia are borne on sporophylls (modified leaves), which are often aggregated into a cone-like strobilus at the stem tips.

3. Selaginella (Spike Moss)

  • Structure: Similar to *Lycopodium*, but often has leaves of two different sizes (dimorphic). A small, tongue-like appendage called a ligule is present on each leaf (a key difference).
  • Reproduction: Heterosporous. The strobilus contains two types of sporangia:
    • Microsporangia: Produce many small microspores (male).
    • Megasporangia: Produce few (usually 4) large megaspores (female).
    This is a critical step in the evolution of the "seed habit."

4. Equisetum (Horsetail)

  • Structure: Has an underground rhizome. Aerial stems are jointed (nodes and internodes) and have characteristic ridges and grooves. Stems are photosynthetic. Leaves are reduced to small, non-photosynthetic scales, fused in a whorl at each node.
  • Reproduction: Homosporous. A large strobilus at the stem tip contains unique, umbrella-shaped sporangiophores that bear sporangia. Spores have 4 hygroscopic "elaters" that aid in dispersal.

5. Marsilea (Water Fern)

  • Structure: An aquatic fern with a creeping rhizome. Leaves are long-petioled with (usually) four leaflets at the top, resembling a "four-leaf clover."
  • Reproduction: Heterosporous. Spores are produced in a hard, bean-like, resistant structure called a sporocarp, which is a modified leaf. The sporocarp contains both microsporangia and megasporangia.

6. Pteris (A True Fern)

  • Structure: Typical fern. Has a rhizome with adventitious roots. Leaves (fronds) are large (megaphylls), pinnately compound, and show circinate vernation (uncoil as they grow, like a "fiddlehead").
  • Reproduction: Homosporous. Sporangia are not in a strobilus, but are grouped into sori on the underside of leaflets. In *Pteris*, the sori are in a continuous line along the leaflet margin, protected by the in-rolled margin (a "false indusium").

Apogamy and Apospory

These are two types of asexual reproduction (vegetative reproduction) that bypass the normal life cycle.

  • Apogamy: (Gamos = marriage; Apo = without). The development of a sporophyte (2n) directly from a gametophyte (n), without fertilization. The resulting sporophyte is haploid (n). This can be induced in ferns by stress conditions.
  • Apospory: (Spory = spore; Apo = without). The development of a gametophyte (n) directly from the sporophyte's vegetative tissue (2n) (e.g., from a leaf), without meiosis and spore formation. The resulting gametophyte is diploid (2n).
Mnemonic:
  • Apo-GAMY (no gametes): Gametophyte -> Sporophyte (no fertilization)
  • Apo-SPORY (no spores): Sporophyte -> Gametophyte (no meiosis)

Heterospory and Seed Habit

Heterospory

As mentioned, this is the production of two distinct types of spores (e.g., in *Selaginella* and *Marsilea*).

  • Microspores: Small, numerous. Germinate to form a highly reduced male gametophyte (which produces sperm).
  • Megaspores: Large, few. Contain abundant food. Germinate to form a female gametophyte (which produces eggs).

Seed Habit

A true seed (like in Gymnosperms) is an ovule containing an embryo. "Seed habit" refers to the set of evolutionary prerequisites that led to the formation of the seed. *Selaginella* is a perfect example of a plant exhibiting these "pre-seed" characteristics.

Prerequisites for the Seed Habit (all seen in Selaginella):

  1. Heterospory: Production of two spore types. This is the first and most important step.
  2. Reduction of Gametophytes: The gametophytes are extremely small and simple.
  3. Retention and Germination of Megaspore: The megaspore is not dispersed. It is retained inside the megasporangium (which is part of the parent sporophyte).
  4. Endosporic Development: The female gametophyte develops inside the megaspore wall, protected and nourished by the food stored in it.
  5. Development of the Embryo: Fertilization occurs (sperm swims to the archegonium on the retained gametophyte), and the new embryo develops in situ, still retained within the female gametophyte, which is itself retained within the megasporangium on the parent sporophyte.

This entire structure (megasporangium + retained megaspore + female gametophyte + embryo) is essentially a "prototype" seed. The only thing missing is an integument (a protective outer layer that would form a seed coat) and a resting stage for the embryo.

Telome Theory

Proposed by Zimmerman, this theory explains the evolution of the complex plant bodies of higher plants (stems, leaves, sporangiophores) from the simple, dichotomously branching, leafless "telomes" of early fossil plants like *Rhynia*.

Telome: A single, terminal, ultimate branch in the dichotomous system of an early plant. It can be fertile (with a sporangium) or sterile (photosynthetic).

The theory states that all complex plant organs evolved through a series of "elementary processes" acting on these telomes:

  1. Overtopping: One branch of a dichotomy grows more strongly than the other, creating a central "main stem" and a "side branch."
  2. Planation: The dichotomous branches, which were in various 3D planes, all shift to grow in a single 2D plane.
  3. Webbing (or Syngenesis): Parenchyma tissue grows in (like webbing between fingers) to connect the flattened branches, forming a "leaf blade."
  4. Reduction: The shortening of a telome (e.g., to form a small microphyll or a fertile sporangiophore).
  5. Curvation: The bending of telomes (e.g., to move sporangia from a terminal to a ventral position on a leaf).
  • Evolution of a Megaphyll (Fern Leaf): Explained by Overtopping + Planation + Webbing.
  • Evolution of a Microphyll (Lycopodium Leaf): Explained by a single, simple Reduction of a small, sterile telome.
  • Evolution of Sporangiophores (Equisetum): Explained by Reduction and Curvation of fertile telomes.

Stelar Structure and Evolution

The stele is the central core of the stem and root, containing the vascular tissues (xylem and phloem) and associated ground tissue (pericycle, pith).

The evolution of the stele shows a trend from a simple, solid core to a more complex, dissected cylinder, which provides better support and more efficient transport.

1. Protostele (Simplest, most primitive)

A solid core of xylem surrounded by phloem. There is no pith (central core of parenchyma). Found in *Rhynia*, *Lycopodium*.

  • Haplostele: Simple, circular core of xylem (e.g., *Rhynia*).
  • Actinostele: Xylem core is star-shaped (e.g., *Psilotum*).
  • Plectostele: Xylem is broken into separate plates or bands (e.g., *Lycopodium* stem).

2. Siphonostele

A more advanced stele where the vascular tissue forms a cylinder around a central pith. Thought to have evolved from a protostele by the "hollowing out" of the center.

  • Ectophloic Siphonostele: Phloem is present only on the outside of the xylem cylinder (e.g., *Equisetum*).
  • Amphiphloic Siphonostele: Phloem is present on both the outside and the inside of the xylem cylinder (e.g., *Marsilea* rhizome).

Leaf Gaps and Branch Gaps: When a vascular strand diverges from the stele to enter a leaf (leaf trace) or branch (branch trace), it creates a "break" or "gap" in the vascular cylinder.

  • In siphonosteles, if these gaps are small and do not overlap, the stele is called a Solenostele.
  • In most ferns, the leaves are large and close together, so the leaf gaps are large and overlapping. This dissects the vascular cylinder into a network of separate strands. This advanced type of stele is called a Dictyostele.

3. Dictyostele (Most advanced in Pteridophytes)

A siphonostele with large, overlapping leaf gaps. The vascular cylinder is dissected into a network of individual vascular strands called meristeles. This is characteristic of most modern ferns (e.g., *Pteris*).

Economic Importance

  • Ornamentals: Many ferns (e.g., Boston fern, Maidenhair fern) are grown as ornamental plants for their attractive fronds.
  • Food: The young, coiled "fiddleheads" of some ferns (e.g., *Pteridium*) are eaten as a vegetable in many parts of the world. *Marsilea* is also eaten in some places.
  • Medicine: Extracts from some ferns (e.g., *Dryopteris*) have been used as traditional medicines, particularly to expel tapeworms.
  • Biofertilizer: The aquatic fern *Azolla* hosts a symbiotic, nitrogen-fixing cyanobacterium (*Anabaena azollae*) and is used as a natural biofertilizer in rice paddies.
  • Fossil Fuels: As mentioned in Unit 1, the giant Pteridophytes of the Carboniferous period formed the coal deposits we use today.
  • Scouring: *Equisetum* stems are rich in silica and have a rough texture. They were historically used for scrubbing and cleaning pots and pans, earning them the name "scouring rush."
