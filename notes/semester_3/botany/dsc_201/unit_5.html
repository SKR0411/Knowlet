Distinguishing Features

Gymnosperms (from Greek: gymnos = "naked", sperma = "seed") are seed-bearing plants (Spermatophytes) that are the next evolutionary step up from Pteridophytes.

Key Feature: Their ovules are "naked", meaning they are not enclosed within an ovary (as they are in Angiosperms/flowering plants). The ovules are borne exposed on the surface of modified leaves (megasporophylls).
  • Dominant Sporophyte: The plant body is a diploid sporophyte (2n), which is large, perennial, woody (trees or shrubs), and differentiated into true roots, stem, and leaves.
  • Vascular Tissue: Xylem lacks true vessels (except in Gnetales), and phloem lacks companion cells (instead, they have albuminous cells).
  • Reduced Gametophyte: The gametophyte generation is extremely reduced and is completely dependent on the sporophyte.
    • Male Gametophyte: The pollen grain.
    • Female Gametophyte: A small, multicellular body retained inside the ovule.
  • Reproduction:
    • Pollen: They produce pollen grains (microspores) which are transferred to the ovule, usually by wind (anemophily).
    • No Water for Fertilization: A pollen tube grows from the pollen grain and delivers the male gametes directly to the egg. This eliminates the need for water for fertilization.
    • Ovule: The megasporangium (nucellus) enclosed by a protective layer (integument). After fertilization, the ovule develops into a seed.
    • Endosperm: The nutritive tissue in the seed is the haploid (n) female gametophyte. (Note: This is before fertilization. In Angiosperms, the endosperm is triploid (3n) and forms after fertilization).

Classification

Living gymnosperms are typically classified into four major divisions (or phyla):

  1. Division Cycadophyta: (e.g., *Cycas*). Palm-like plants with large, pinnate leaves. Dioecious (separate male and female plants). Have motile, flagellated sperm (a primitive trait).
  2. Division Ginkgophyta: (e.g., *Ginkgo biloba*). Only one living species. Has unique, fan-shaped leaves. Also dioecious and has motile sperm.
  3. Division Coniferophyta: (e.g., *Pinus*, *Cedrus*, *Picea*). "Conifers." The largest and most common group. Typically have needle-like or scale-like leaves. Bear seeds in cones. Non-motile sperm.
  4. Division Gnetophyta: (e.g., *Gnetum*, *Ephedra*, *Welwitschia*). A strange group with some Angiosperm-like features (e.g., vessels in their xylem). Non-motile sperm.

Structure and Reproduction of Representative Genera

(Syllabus states "excluding developmental features," so we will focus on mature structure and life cycle.)

1. Cycas (a Cycad)

  • Structure: Palm-like, woody stem (unbranched) with a crown of large, pinnately compound leaves. Also has small, brown scale leaves. Roots are of two types: normal taproots and coralloid roots (specialized, upward-growing roots containing symbiotic, nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria like *Anabaena*).
  • Reproduction: Dioecious.
    • Male Plant: Produces a large, central male cone (strobilus) made of microsporophylls that bear microsporangia (pollen sacs).
    • Female Plant: Does not produce a true cone. It produces a loose cluster of megasporophylls in the center of the crown. These are large, leaf-like structures that bear several large, naked ovules along their margins.
    • Fertilization: Has motile, flagellated sperm (a primitive, Pteridophyte-like character), which are the largest in the plant kingdom.

2. Pinus (a Conifer)

  • Structure: A large, branching tree. Stem has two types of branches: long shoots and dwarf shoots. Leaves are also of two types: scale leaves (on long shoots) and needles (borne in clusters of 2, 3, or 5 on the dwarf shoots). Needles are adapted for xerophytic (dry) conditions (sunken stomata, thick cuticle).
  • Reproduction: Monoecious (both male and female cones on the same tree).
    • Male Cones: Small, produced in clusters, simple. Consist of spiral microsporophylls bearing microsporangia. Produce winged pollen grains.
    • Female Cones: Large, woody, and complex. Composed of ovuliferous scales (which are modified dwarf shoots) in the axil of bract scales. Each ovuliferous scale bears two naked ovules on its upper surface.
    • Fertilization: Has non-motile sperm, which are delivered to the egg by the pollen tube. The seed is winged and dispersed by wind.

3. Ginkgo (The "Maidenhair Tree")

  • Structure: A "living fossil." A large, deciduous (loses leaves in winter) tree. Has fan-shaped, dichotomously veined leaves. Also has long and dwarf shoots.
  • Reproduction: Dioecious.
    • Male Plant: Produces pollen in small, catkin-like strobili.
    • Female Plant: Produces ovules in pairs on a long stalk (peduncle).
    • Fertilization: Has motile, flagellated sperm (like *Cycas*). The mature seed has a fleshy, foul-smelling outer coat.

4. Gnetum (a Gnetophyte)

  • Structure: Mostly woody vines (lianas) or small trees. Atypical for a gymnosperm, it has broad, flat leaves with reticulate (net-like) venation, making it look like an Angiosperm.
  • Reproduction: Has highly complex, flower-like strobili. Ovules are enclosed by multiple envelopes.
    • Angiosperm-like features: 1) Presence of vessels in its xylem. 2) Broad, net-veined leaves. 3) The way its strobili are organized.

Economic Importance

  • Timber: Conifers (*Pinus*, *Cedrus*) are the world's most important source of softwood timber for construction, furniture, and paper pulp.
  • Resins: Pines produce resin, which is distilled to make turpentine (a solvent) and rosin (used by musicians, for baseball, and in inks/varnishes).
  • Food: The seeds ("pine nuts") of some *Pinus* species are eaten. "Sago" starch is extracted from the stem of *Cycas*.
  • Medicine: *Ephedra* (a gnetophyte) is the source of the drug ephedrine (a decongestant and stimulant). *Taxus* (Yew tree) is the source of Taxol, an important anti-cancer drug.
  • Ornamentals: Many gymnosperms (*Cycas*, *Ginkgo*, various conifers) are planted as ornamental trees in parks and gardens.

Fossils and Fossilization

Fossils are the preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms that lived in the past. Fossilization is the process by which these remains are preserved.

Various Forms of Fossils (Types):

  1. Impressions: A "stamp" or imprint of an organism (like a leaf) left in soft sediment, which then hardens. Shows the surface features but no internal structure.
  2. Compressions: An organism is flattened under sediment. Some organic material may remain as a thin carbon film. This is a common way for leaves and coaly fossils to form.
  3. Petrifactions (or Permineralization): The most informative type. Minerals from groundwater slowly infiltrate the tissues of the organism (e.g., wood), replacing the organic matter molecule by molecule. This preserves the detailed internal (cellular) structure. "Petrified wood" is a common example.
  4. Casts and Molds: A mold is the hollow space left in rock after an organism (like a shell or stem) decays. A cast is formed when this mold is later filled in by minerals.
  5. Amber: Fossilized tree resin that can perfectly preserve small organisms (like insects or pollen) that were trapped within it.

Theories of Fossilization (Conditions):

Fossilization is a rare event. It requires specific conditions:

  • Rapid Burial: The organism must be buried quickly by sediment (mud, sand, volcanic ash) after death. This protects it from scavengers, decay (by bacteria/fungi), and weathering.
  • Lack of Oxygen (Anaerobic Conditions): Burial in mud, deep water, or bogs slows down decomposition.
  • Presence of Hard Parts: Hard parts (like wood, bones, shells, or spores with sporopollenin) are much more likely to fossilize than soft tissues.
  • Mineral-rich Water: For petrifaction to occur, groundwater rich in dissolved minerals (like silica or calcite) must flow through the buried organism.

Fossil Flora of India

India has a rich and unique fossil record, largely due to its history as partof the Gondwana supercontinent.

  • The Gondwana Flora: This refers to the distinct flora that characterized the southern supercontinent (India, Australia, Africa, S. America, Antarctica) during the Paleozoic and Mesozoic eras.
  • Key Genus: Glossopteris: This was the dominant plant of the Permian period in Gondwana. It was a seed fern (Pteridosperm). The widespread presence of *Glossopteris* fossils across all the (now separate) southern continents was one of the strongest pieces of evidence for continental drift.
  • Key Locations: Major fossil beds are found in the Damodar and Godavari river basins (the main coal-bearing regions of India).
  • The Rajmahal Hills (Jurassic): This area in Jharkhand is world-famous for its incredible collection of Jurassic-era plant fossils, especially petrified gymnosperms (conifers, cycads). This flora is known as the Rajmahal Flora and gives a perfect snapshot of the "Age of Gymnosperms" in India.
