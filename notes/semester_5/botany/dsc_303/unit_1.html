The objective of this unit is to gain practical knowledge about advanced morphology and the diversity of vegetative and reproductive structures in plants[cite: 895].

1. Study of Leaf Phyllotaxy

 [cite_start]

Phyllotaxy refers to the mode of arrangement of leaves on the stem or branch[cite: 896]. [cite_start]The primary goal is to avoid overcrowding and ensure maximum exposure to sunlight[cite: 799].

Observations:

    [cite_start]
  • Alternate: A single leaf arises at each node in an alternating manner (e.g., Hibiscus)[cite: 799].
    • [cite_start]
  • Opposite: A pair of leaves arises at each node, standing opposite to each other[cite: 799].
      [cite_start]
    • Opposite Decussate: Successive pairs are at right angles (e.g., Calotropis)[cite: 799].
      • [cite_start]
    • Opposite Superimposed: Successive pairs are directly above one another (e.g., Guava)[cite: 799].
    • [cite_start]
  • Whorled: More than two leaves arise at a single node forming a circle or whorl (e.g., Alstonia)[cite: 799].

2. Study of Leaf Modifications

 [cite_start]

Leaves often undergo structural changes to perform specialized functions other than photosynthesis[cite: 896].

Common Practical Examples:

    [cite_start]
  • Leaf Tendrils: The whole leaf or parts like leaflets are modified into sensitive, thread-like coils for climbing (e.g., Pisum sativum/Pea)[cite: 799, 896].
    • [cite_start]
  • Leaf Spines: Leaves or parts of leaves modified into sharp, pointed structures to reduce transpiration and provide protection (e.g., Opuntia, Aloe)[cite: 799, 896].
    • [cite_start]
  • Phyllode: The petiole becomes green, fleshy, and leaf-like to perform photosynthesis when the true lamina is reduced (e.g., Australian Acacia)[cite: 799].
    • [cite_start]
  • Storage Leaves: Fleshy leaves that store food or water (e.g., Onion, Aloe)[cite: 799].

3. Study of Special Types of Inflorescence

 [cite_start]

Special inflorescences are condensed or highly modified arrangements that do not strictly follow typical racemose or cymose patterns[cite: 897].

Key Types to Identify:

    [cite_start]
  • Cyathium: Characteristics include a cup-shaped involucre enclosing a single female flower and multiple male flowers (e.g., Euphorbia)[cite: 801, 897].
    • [cite_start]
  • Verticillaster: A cluster of sessile flowers forming a false whorl at the nodes (e.g., Ocimum/Tulsi)[cite: 801, 897].
    • [cite_start]
  • Hypanthodium: A fleshy, pear-shaped receptacle with an internal cavity housing small flowers (e.g., Ficus/Fig)[cite: 801, 897].
[cite_start]Exam Tip: In practical exams, use live specimens or photographs as directed[cite: 897]. When identifying inflorescences, look for the presence of an Involucre in Cyathium and the False Whorl in Verticillaster to distinguish them quickly.

Practical Checklist

    [cite_start]
  • Observe the phyllotaxy and note the leaf arrangement[cite: 896].
    • [cite_start]
  • Identify leaf modifications and state their functional significance[cite: 799, 896].
    • [cite_start]
  • Examine special inflorescences using a hand lens to see minute floral parts[cite: 897].
