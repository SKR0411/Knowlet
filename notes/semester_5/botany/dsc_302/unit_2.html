1. Photosynthesis: Light and Dark Reactions

 [cite_start]

Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants transform light energy into chemical energy[cite: 861]. It occurs in two main stages:

    [cite_start]
  • Light-Dependent Reactions: These occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts[cite: 868]. [cite_start]They involve the absorption of light and the production of energy-rich molecules, ATP and NADPH[cite: 868, 869].
    • [cite_start]
  • Dark Reactions (Light-Independent): These occur in the stroma of chloroplasts[cite: 868]. [cite_start]They utilize the ATP and NADPH produced in the light reactions to reduce Carbon Dioxide (CO2) into carbohydrates[cite: 868, 869].

2. Photosystem I and II: Electron Transport

 [cite_start]

The light reaction utilizes two photosystems to capture light energy[cite: 868]:

    [cite_start]
  • Photosystem II (PS II): P680 reaction center; absorbs light energy to split water (photolysis), releasing oxygen and electrons[cite: 868].
    • [cite_start]
  • Photosystem I (PS I): P700 reaction center; receives electrons through an electron transport chain and reduces NADP+ to NADPH[cite: 868].
    • [cite_start]
  • Electron Transport: Can be Cyclic (only PS I involved, producing only ATP) or Non-Cyclic (both PS I and PS II involved, producing ATP, NADPH, and O2)[cite: 868].
    • [cite_start]
  • Photophosphorylation: The process of ATP synthesis during the light reactions, driven by a proton gradient[cite: 869].

4. Factors Affecting Photosynthesis and Kranz Anatomy

 [cite_start]

The rate of photosynthesis is influenced by several external and internal factors[cite: 869]:

    [cite_start]
  • External Factors: Light intensity, CO2 concentration, and temperature[cite: 869].
    • [cite_start]
  • Kranz Anatomy: A specialized leaf structure found in C4 plants[cite: 869]. [cite_start]It features two types of photosynthetic cells: Mesophyll cells and large Bundle Sheath cells arranged in a "wreath" like manner[cite: 869].

5. Respiration: Aerobic and Anaerobic

 [cite_start]

Respiration is the metabolic process by which cells break down organic molecules to release energy[cite: 861, 869].

    [cite_start]
  • Aerobic Respiration: Occurs in the presence of oxygen, completely oxidizing glucose into CO2, H2O, and a large amount of ATP[cite: 869].
    • [cite_start]
  • Anaerobic Respiration: Occurs in the absence of oxygen, resulting in partial breakdown of glucose (fermentation) and less energy[cite: 869].
    • [cite_start]
  • Respiratory Quotient (RQ): The ratio of CO2 evolved to O2 consumed during respiration[cite: 870].

6. Glycolysis, Krebs Cycle, and ETS

 [cite_start]

The breakdown of glucose in aerobic respiration involves multiple sequential steps[cite: 870]:

    [cite_start]
  • Glycolysis: Occurs in the cytosol; breaks glucose into two molecules of pyruvate[cite: 870].
    • [cite_start]
  • Krebs (TCA) Cycle: Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix; oxidizes acetyl-CoA into CO2, producing NADH and FADH2[cite: 870].
    • [cite_start]
  • Electron Transport System (ETS): Occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane; uses high-energy electrons from NADH/FADH2 to create a proton gradient[cite: 870].
    • [cite_start]
  • Oxidative Phosphorylation: The final stage where the energy from the proton gradient is used to synthesize ATP[cite: 870].

7. Photorespiration and Source-Sink Relationship
    [cite_start]
  • Photorespiration: A seemingly wasteful process where RuBisCO binds with Oxygen instead of CO2, leading to CO2 loss; common in C3 plants under high light/heat[cite: 870].
    • [cite_start]
  • Source-Sink Relationship: The movement of carbohydrates from photosynthetic parts (sources like leaves) to non-photosynthetic or storage parts (sinks like roots and fruits)[cite: 870].
Exam Tip: For respiration, be ready to calculate RQ. If glucose is oxidized, RQ = 1.0. If fats are oxidized, RQ < 1.0. [cite_start]If organic acids are oxidized, RQ > 1.0[cite: 870].
Common Mistake: Confusing the locations of metabolic cycles. [cite_start]Remember: Glycolysis is in the cytoplasm, but Krebs Cycle and ETS are strictly in the mitochondria[cite: 870].

Frequently Asked Questions

  • Q: Why do C4 plants have Kranz anatomy? [cite_start]
    A: To physically separate the initial carbon fixation (mesophyll) from the Calvin Cycle (bundle sheath), allowing them to concentrate CO2 and avoid photorespiration[cite: 869].
  • Q: What is the primary function of photosystems? [cite_start]
    A: To absorb light energy and convert it into chemical energy (ATP/NADPH) through electron transport[cite: 868].
