1. Nomenclature and Classification of Enzymes

 [cite_start]

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being consumed in the process[cite: 842, 843]. [cite_start]Most enzymes are proteins, though some RNA molecules (ribozymes) also exhibit catalytic activity[cite: 843, 844].

Classification (IUBMB System)

[cite_start]

Enzymes are classified into six major classes based on the type of reaction they catalyze[cite: 843]:

[cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start]
Class Reaction Catalyzed Example
1. Oxidoreductases Oxidation-reduction reactions.Dehydrogenase[cite: 843].
2. Transferases Transfer of functional groups (e.g., methyl, phosphate).Kinase[cite: 843].
3. Hydrolases Hydrolysis (breaking bonds using water).Amylase[cite: 843].
4. Lyases Addition or removal of groups to form double bonds without hydrolysis.Decarboxylase[cite: 843].
5. Isomerases Rearrangement of atoms within a molecule.Phosphoglucoisomerase[cite: 843].
6. Ligases Joining two molecules using ATP energy.DNA Ligase[cite: 843].

2. The Active Site and Models of Enzyme Action

 [cite_start]

The active site is a specific region on the enzyme where the substrate binds and the reaction occurs[cite: 843]. [cite_start]It is usually a small, 3D pocket or cleft[cite: 843].

Mechanism of Enzyme Action

[cite_start]

Enzymes work by lowering the activation energy required for a reaction to proceed[cite: 843]. Two main models describe substrate binding:

    [cite_start]
  • Lock-and-Key Model: Suggests the enzyme's active site is a rigid, perfect match for the substrate[cite: 843].
    • [cite_start]
  • Induced-Fit Model: Suggests the active site is flexible and changes shape to wrap more tightly around the substrate upon binding[cite: 843].

3. Enzyme Kinetics

 [cite_start]

Enzyme kinetics is the study of the rates of enzyme-catalyzed reactions[cite: 844].

Michaelis-Menten Equation

[cite_start]

This fundamental equation relates the reaction velocity (v) to the substrate concentration ([S])[cite: 1001]:

v = (Vmax * [S]) / (Km + [S])
    [cite_start]
  • Vmax: The maximum velocity reached when the enzyme is saturated with substrate[cite: 1001].
    • [cite_start]
  • Km (Michaelis Constant): The substrate concentration at which the velocity is half of Vmax[cite: 1001]. [cite_start]A low Km indicates high affinity for the substrate[cite: 1001].

4. Factors Affecting Activity and Allosteric Regulation

 [cite_start]

Enzyme activity is highly sensitive to the environment[cite: 843]:

    [cite_start]
  • Temperature: Each enzyme has an optimum temperature; high heat causes denaturation[cite: 843].
    • [cite_start]
  • pH: Changes in pH can alter the ionization state of the active site[cite: 843].
    • [cite_start]
  • Substrate Concentration: Increasing [S] increases velocity until Vmax is reached[cite: 1001].

Allosteric Regulation

[cite_start]

Allosteric enzymes have a "regulatory site" (other than the active site) where an effector binds[cite: 843]. [cite_start]Binding can either increase (activator) or decrease (inhibitor) the enzyme's activity[cite: 843].

5. Enzyme Inhibition: Types and Mechanisms

 [cite_start]

Inhibitors are substances that reduce the rate of enzyme-catalyzed reactions[cite: 844].

    [cite_start]
  • Competitive Inhibition: The inhibitor resembles the substrate and competes for the active site[cite: 844]. [cite_start]Km increases, but Vmax remains unchanged[cite: 1001].
    • [cite_start]
  • Non-competitive Inhibition: The inhibitor binds to a site other than the active site, changing the enzyme's shape[cite: 844]. [cite_start]Vmax decreases, but Km remains unchanged[cite: 1001].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uncompetitive Inhibition: The inhibitor binds only to the Enzyme-Substrate (ES) complex[cite: 844]. [cite_start]Both Vmax and Km decrease[cite: 1001].

6. Cofactors, Prosthetic Groups, and Ribozymes

 [cite_start]

Some enzymes require non-protein components to function[cite: 844, 1000]:

    [cite_start]
  • Apoenzyme: The protein part of the enzyme (inactive)[cite: 1000].
    • [cite_start]
  • Holoenzyme: The complete, active enzyme (Apoenzyme + Cofactor)[cite: 1000].
    • [cite_start]
  • Cofactors: Can be inorganic metal ions (e.g., Mg2+, Zn2+) or organic molecules (Coenzymes)[cite: 844, 1000].
    • [cite_start]
  • Prosthetic Group: A cofactor that is tightly or covalently bound to the enzyme[cite: 844, 1000].
    • [cite_start]
  • Ribozymes: RNA molecules that act as enzymes, such as those in the ribosome involved in peptide bond formation[cite: 844].

7. Exam Focus: Critical Concepts & FAQs

Exam Tip: Be ready to explain how competitive inhibition can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration, whereas non-competitive inhibition cannot. This is a very common conceptual question.

Common Mistakes

  • Mistake: Confusing Km with Vmax. Correction: Km is a concentration; Vmax is a rate.
  • Mistake: Thinking all enzymes are proteins. Correction: Ribozymes are non-protein RNA catalysts.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is activation energy?
A: It is the minimum energy required to start a chemical reaction. [cite_start]Enzymes decrease this energy barrier, allowing reactions to happen faster at body temperature[cite: 843].

Q: How does an allosteric inhibitor differ from a competitive one?
[cite_start]A: A competitive inhibitor fights for the active site, while an allosteric inhibitor binds elsewhere to change the active site's shape[cite: 843, 844].
