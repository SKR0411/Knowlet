1. Classification and Properties of Fatty Acids

 [cite_start]

Lipids are a diverse group of organic compounds that are insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents[cite: 835, 836]. [cite_start]Fatty acids are the simplest lipids, consisting of a hydrocarbon chain with a terminal carboxyl group[cite: 836].

Saturated vs. Unsaturated Fatty Acids

    [cite_start]
  • Saturated Fatty Acids: Contain no double bonds between carbon atoms in the chain[cite: 836]. [cite_start]They are generally solid at room temperature (e.g., Palmitic acid)[cite: 836].
    • [cite_start]
  • Unsaturated Fatty Acids: Contain one or more double bonds[cite: 836]. [cite_start]These double bonds create "kinks" in the chain, usually making them liquid at room temperature (e.g., Oleic acid)[cite: 836].
    • [cite_start]
  • Essential Fatty Acids: These are fatty acids that the body cannot synthesize and must be obtained from the diet, such as Linoleic acid[cite: 836].

2. Phospholipids, Glycolipids, and Steroids

 [cite_start]

Complex lipids serve as structural components of cell membranes and signaling molecules[cite: 836].

    [cite_start]
  • Phospholipids: Consist of two fatty acids, a glycerol backbone, and a phosphate group[cite: 836]. [cite_start]They are amphipathic, meaning they have a hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails, forming the basis of the lipid bilayer[cite: 836].
    • [cite_start]
  • Glycolipids: Lipids with a carbohydrate attached by a glycosidic bond[cite: 836]. [cite_start]They are crucial for cell recognition and stability of the cell membrane[cite: 836].
    • [cite_start]
  • Steroids: Characterized by a carbon skeleton with four fused rings[cite: 836]. [cite_start]Cholesterol is a key steroid in animal membranes, while plants contain phytosterols[cite: 836].

3. Nucleic Acids: Nucleosides and Nucleotides

 [cite_start]

Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) are polymers of nucleotides that store and transmit genetic information[cite: 835, 837].

Components

    [cite_start]
  • Pentose Sugar: Ribose in RNA and Deoxyribose in DNA[cite: 837].
  • Nitrogenous Bases:
      [cite_start]
    • Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G)[cite: 837].
      • [cite_start]
    • Pyrimidines: Cytosine (C), Thymine (T - in DNA only), and Uracil (U - in RNA only)[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • Phosphate Group: Attached to the 5' carbon of the sugar[cite: 837].
Nucleoside = Nitrogenous Base + Sugar
[cite_start]Nucleotide = Nucleoside + Phosphate group [cite: 837]

4. Double Helical Model of DNA

 [cite_start]

Proposed by Watson and Crick in 1953, the DNA structure is described as a double helix[cite: 837].

Salient Features

    [cite_start]
  • Two Polynucleotide Chains: The chains run anti-parallel (one 5' to 3', the other 3' to 5')[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sugar-Phosphate Backbone: Located on the outside, with bases projecting inward[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • Base Pairing: Adenine pairs with Thymine (2 hydrogen bonds), and Guanine pairs with Cytosine (3 hydrogen bonds)[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • Helical Dimensions: One full turn is 3.4 nm long and contains 10 base pairs[cite: 837].

5. Physical Properties and Types of DNA

Physical and Chemical Properties

    [cite_start]
  • Denaturation (Melting): Separation of the two strands due to high temperature or extreme pH[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • Renaturation (Annealing): Re-association of separated strands when conditions return to normal[cite: 837].
    • [cite_start]
  • UV Absorption: DNA absorbs UV light strongly at 260 nm[cite: 837].

6. Exam Focus: Comparison Tables & Tips

Exam Tip: Remember Chargaff's Rule: In any double-stranded DNA, the amount of Adenine equals Thymine (A=T) and Guanine equals Cytosine (G=C). This is a frequent numerical question topic!

Common Mistakes

    [cite_start]
  • Mistake: Forgetting that Uracil is only in RNA and Thymine is only in DNA[cite: 837].
  • Mistake: Thinking that steroids are composed of fatty acids. [cite_start]Correction: Steroids are non-saponifiable lipids and do not contain fatty acids[cite: 836].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the biological significance of the 3 hydrogen bonds between G and C?
A: GC pairs are more stable and harder to separate than AT pairs (which have only 2 bonds). DNA with high GC content has a higher melting temperature (Tm).

Q: Why is DNA the preferred genetic material over RNA?
A: The absence of the 2'-OH group in deoxyribose makes DNA chemically more stable and less prone to hydrolysis than RNA.
