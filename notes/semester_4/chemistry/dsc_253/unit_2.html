1. Principles of Organic Preparation

Organic preparation involves the synthesis of a specific organic compound through chemical reactions like substitution, oxidation, or condensation[cite: 762].

  • Theoretical Yield: The maximum amount of product that can be formed from the given reactants[cite: 762].
  • Percentage Yield: Calculated as (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) x 100[cite: 762].
  • Crude Product: The initial product obtained before any purification steps are performed[cite: 774].

2. Key Organic Preparations

The syllabus lists several fundamental organic reactions used for synthesis [cite: 764-770].

Nitration Reactions

The introduction of a nitro group (-NO2) into an organic molecule[cite: 764].

  • Examples: Nitration of acetanilide, nitrobenzene, or salicylic acid[cite: 764].
  • Reagent: Typically a mixture of concentrated Nitric Acid (HNO3) and Sulfuric Acid (H2SO4)[cite: 764].

Bromination Reactions

The substitution of hydrogen atoms with bromine atoms[cite: 765].

  • Examples: Bromination of phenol or aniline[cite: 765].
  • Observation: Often results in the formation of white precipitates (e.g., 2,4,6-tribromophenol)[cite: 765].

Iodoform Preparation

The haloform reaction of acetone or ethanol with iodine and alkali[cite: 770].

  • Product: Yellow crystals of Iodoform (CHI3) with a characteristic medicinal smell[cite: 770].

3. Specialized Reactions

Some preparations involve structural rearrangements or condensation [cite: 766-769].

  • Azomethyne: Formation of Schiff bases through the condensation of amines and aldehydes[cite: 766].
  • Benzil from Benzoin: An oxidation reaction using concentrated nitric acid[cite: 767].
  • Benzilic Acid from Benzil: A classic rearrangement reaction involving the addition of strong alkali (KOH) to benzil[cite: 768].

4. Purification Techniques: Recrystallization

Recrystallization is the most common method for purifying solid organic compounds based on differences in solubility[cite: 771].

Steps in Recrystallization

  1. Selection of a suitable solvent where the solute is soluble at high temperature but insoluble at low temperature [cite: 775-777].
  2. Preparation of a saturated solution in hot solvent[cite: 775].
  3. Hot filtration to remove insoluble impurities[cite: 771].
  4. Cooling the filtrate to allow crystal formation[cite: 771].
  5. Cold filtration to collect pure crystals and washing[cite: 771].

Specific Applications

  • Decolorization: Use of animal charcoal to remove colored impurities from crude sulphanilic acid[cite: 774].
  • Solvents: Boiling water is used for acetanilide; ethanol is used for naphthalene or m-dinitrobenzene[cite: 776, 777].

5. Purification Techniques: Sublimation

Sublimation is used for substances that pass directly from solid to vapor phase upon heating[cite: 778].

  • Compounds: Naphthalene, camphor, and phthalic acid[cite: 778].
  • Process: The crude solid is heated in a porcelain dish covered with a perforated filter paper and an inverted funnel to collect pure "sublimate" on the cool walls of the funnel[cite: 778].

6. Exam Focus: Laboratory Tips & Viva

Practical Exam Tips

  • Safety: Always perform nitration in a fume hood or well-ventilated area due to acid fumes[cite: 764].
  • Drying: Ensure crystals are completely dry before weighing them to calculate the yield[cite: 762].
  • Melting Point: The purity of your purified product should be checked by determining its melting point; a sharp melting point indicates high purity[cite: 329].

Common Viva Questions

Q: Why is concentrated H2SO4 used in nitration?
A: It acts as a dehydrating agent and helps in the generation of the nitronium ion (NO2+), which is the active electrophile[cite: 764].

Q: What is the role of animal charcoal in recrystallization?
A: It adsorbs high molecular weight colored impurities from the solution[cite: 774].

Q: Define a Schiff Base.
A: It is an azomethine compound formed by the condensation of a primary amine with a carbonyl compound[cite: 766].
