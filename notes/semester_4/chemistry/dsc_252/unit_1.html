1. Electrolytes and Ionization

Ionic equilibrium involves the equilibrium between unionized molecules and ions in solution[cite: 692].

Classification of Electrolytes

  • Strong Electrolytes: Substance that ionizes almost completely in aqueous solution (e.g., HCl, NaOH, NaCl)[cite: 692].
  • Weak Electrolytes: Substance that ionizes only to a small extent (e.g., CH3COOH, NH4OH)[cite: 692, 693].

Degree of Ionization (α)

The fraction of the total number of molecules of an electrolyte that ionizes in solution[cite: 692]. It is affected by:

  • Nature of solute and solvent[cite: 692].
  • Concentration (α increases with dilution)[cite: 692].
  • Temperature (α increases with temperature)[cite: 692].
  • Common Ion Effect: Suppression of degree of ionization of a weak electrolyte by adding a strong electrolyte containing a common ion[cite: 693].

2. pH Scale and Ionic Product of Water

Ionic Product of Water (Kw)

Water is a very weak electrolyte that undergoes self-ionization[cite: 692].

Kw = [H+][OH-] = 1.0 x 10^-14 (at 298 K)

The pH Scale

A logarithmic scale used to specify the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution[cite: 693].

pH = -log [H+]

At 298 K: pH + pOH = 14[cite: 693].

3. Salt Hydrolysis

Salt hydrolysis is the reaction of an anion or cation of a salt with water to produce acidity or alkalinity[cite: 694].

Salt Type Nature of Solution Example
Strong Acid + Strong Base Neutral (pH = 7) NaCl, KNO3
Weak Acid + Strong Base Alkaline (pH > 7) CH3COONa
Strong Acid + Weak Base Acidic (pH < 7) NH4Cl
Weak Acid + Weak Base Depends on Ka/Kb CH3COONH4

4. Buffer Solutions & Henderson Equation

A buffer solution resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added[cite: 694].

Types of Buffers

  • Acidic Buffer: Mixture of a weak acid and its salt with a strong base (e.g., CH3COOH + CH3COONa)[cite: 694].
  • Basic Buffer: Mixture of a weak base and its salt with a strong acid (e.g., NH4OH + NH4Cl)[cite: 694].

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

Used to calculate the pH of a buffer solution[cite: 695].

pH = pKa + log ([Salt] / [Acid])

Applications of Buffers

  • Analytical Chemistry: To maintain pH for precipitation of cations[cite: 695].
  • Biochemistry: Maintaining blood pH (approx. 7.4) in the human body[cite: 695].

5. Acid-Base Titrations and Indicators

Titration curves show the change in pH as a titrant is added[cite: 696].

Acid-Base Indicators

Weak organic acids or bases that change color within a specific pH range[cite: 696].

  • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid, pink in base (Range: 8.3 - 10.0)[cite: 696].
  • Methyl Orange: Red in acid, yellow in base (Range: 3.1 - 4.4)[cite: 696].

6. Exam Corner: FAQs & Tips

Exam Tips

  • Formula Mastery: Be ready to calculate pH for strong/weak acids and buffer solutions using the Henderson equation[cite: 693, 695].
  • Common Ion Effect: This is a favorite theoretical question; explain it using Le Chatelier's Principle[cite: 690, 693].
  • Indicator Selection: Know which indicator to use for different types of titrations (e.g., Phenolphthalein for strong base titrations)[cite: 697].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the buffer capacity?
A: It is a measure of the effectiveness of a buffer in resisting pH changes[cite: 695].

Q: Define Salt Hydrolysis.
A: It is the interaction of salt ions with water, leading to an excess of H+ or OH- ions[cite: 694].

Q: Why does NH4Cl give an acidic solution?
A: NH4+ ions undergo hydrolysis to produce H3O+ ions, making the solution acidic[cite: 694].
