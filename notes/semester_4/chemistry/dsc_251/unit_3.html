1. Gibbs Phase Rule

[cite_start]

The phase rule is a general relation used to determine the number of independent variables (degrees of freedom) required to define the state of a system in equilibrium[cite: 682].

F = C - P + 2
    [cite_start]
  • P (Phases): A physically distinct, homogeneous, and mechanically separable part of a system[cite: 682, 683].
    • [cite_start]
  • C (Components): The minimum number of independent chemical constituents needed to express the composition of every phase[cite: 682, 683].
    • [cite_start]
  • F (Degrees of Freedom): The number of intensive variables (like P, T, concentration) that can be changed independently without altering the number of phases[cite: 682, 683].

2. Clausius-Clapeyron Equation

[cite_start]

This equation relates the temperature and pressure dependence of phase equilibria[cite: 683].

dP / dT = ΔH / (T * ΔV)
[cite_start]

Applications: Used to calculate the enthalpy of vaporization, sublimation, or fusion, and to predict how boiling and melting points change with pressure[cite: 683].

Fugacity and Activity

[cite_start]

For non-ideal systems, pressure (P) is replaced by Fugacity (f) and concentration is replaced by Activity (a) to maintain the form of ideal thermodynamic equations[cite: 683].

3. One Component Systems

[cite_start]

In a one-component system (C=1), the phase rule becomes F = 3 - P[cite: 683].

Water (H2O) System

[cite_start]

The phase diagram of water features three areas (Liquid, Solid, Gas), three curves (Vaporization, Fusion, Sublimation), and a Triple Point where all three phases coexist (P=3, F=0)[cite: 683].

[Image of the phase diagram of water]

Carbon Dioxide (CO2) System

[cite_start]

Unlike water, the fusion curve for CO2 has a positive slope, and its triple point occurs at pressures higher than atmospheric (5.11 atm), explaining why it exists as "dry ice" (solid to gas) at 1 atm[cite: 683].

4. Two Component Systems

[cite_start]

For binary systems, pressure is often kept constant, leading to the "Reduced Phase Rule": F' = C - P + 1[cite: 684].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a Triple Point?
[cite_start]A: It is the specific Temperature and Pressure at which the solid, liquid, and gas phases of a substance coexist in equilibrium[cite: 683].

Q: What is the degree of freedom at a eutectic point?
[cite_start]A: Since C=2 and P=3 (Liquid + Solid A + Solid B), F' = 2 - 3 + 1 = 0 (Invariant)[cite: 684].

Q: Why is the slope of the ice-water curve negative?
[cite_start]A: Because the volume of ice is greater than the volume of liquid water (ΔV is negative), which is an anomaly[cite: 683].
