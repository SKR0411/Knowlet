1. Second Law of Thermodynamics & Entropy

[cite_start]

While the First Law focuses on energy conservation, the Second Law determines the direction of spontaneous change[cite: 676].

Limitations of the First Law

[cite_start]

The First Law cannot explain why certain processes occur spontaneously in one direction but not the reverse (e.g., heat flowing from hot to cold)[cite: 676].

Concept of Entropy (S)

[cite_start]

Entropy is a measure of the molecular disorder or randomness of a system[cite: 676]. [cite_start]It is a state function[cite: 678].

[cite_start]Mathematical expression for entropy change: dS = dq_rev / T [cite: 677]

For a reversible process, the entropy change of the universe is zero. [cite_start]For an irreversible (spontaneous) process, the entropy of the universe increases[cite: 677].

2. Clausius Inequality

[cite_start]

The Clausius Inequality provides a mathematical criterion for the spontaneity of a process[cite: 677].

[cite_start]dS ≥ dq / T [cite: 677]
    [cite_start]
  • dS = dq_rev / T: For a reversible process[cite: 677].
    • [cite_start]
  • dS > dq_irrev / T: For an irreversible/spontaneous process[cite: 677].

3. Free Energy Functions (Gibbs & Helmholtz)

[cite_start]

To predict spontaneity without checking the entire universe, we use system-specific functions: Gibbs Free Energy (G) and Helmholtz Free Energy (A)[cite: 678].

Function Definition Condition
Helmholtz Energy (A)A = U - TS [cite: 678]Constant Temperature and Volume [cite: 678]
Gibbs Energy (G)G = H - TS [cite: 678]Constant Temperature and Pressure [cite: 678]

4. Spontaneity and Equilibrium

[cite_start]

The criteria for spontaneity based on free energy changes are vital for chemical reactions[cite: 679].

    [cite_start]
  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous process[cite: 679].
    • [cite_start]
  • ΔG = 0: System at equilibrium[cite: 679].
    • [cite_start]
  • ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous process[cite: 679].

Gibbs-Helmholtz Equation

[cite_start]

This equation relates the temperature dependence of Gibbs energy to enthalpy[cite: 679].

[cite_start][d(G/T) / dT]_P = -H / T² [cite: 679]

5. Maxwell Relations & Thermodynamic Equations

[cite_start]

Maxwell relations allow us to relate non-measurable quantities (like entropy) to measurable ones (P, V, T)[cite: 679].

[cite_start]

Common Maxwell Relations derived from exact differentials[cite: 679]:

    [cite_start]
  1. (dS/dV)_T = (dP/dT)_V [cite: 679]
    2. [cite_start]
  2. (dS/dP)_T = -(dV/dT)_P [cite: 679]

6. Joule-Thomson Coefficient (μ_JT)

[cite_start]

The Joule-Thomson effect describes the temperature change of a real gas when it expands through a porous plug into a lower pressure region under adiabatic conditions[cite: 679].

[cite_start]μ_JT = (dT / dP)_H [cite: 679]
[cite_start]

For an ideal gas, μ_JT = 0 because there are no intermolecular forces to overcome[cite: 679].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why is the Second Law necessary if we have the First Law?
A: The First Law only tracks energy quantity. [cite_start]The Second Law tracks energy quality and directionality[cite: 676].

Q: What is the physical significance of ΔA?
[cite_start]A: It represents the maximum work (reversible work) a system can perform at constant T and V[cite: 678].

Common Mistakes
    [cite_start]
  • Using ΔG to predict spontaneity for processes not occurring at constant T and P[cite: 679].
    • [cite_start]
  • Forgetting that entropy must increase for the Universe (System + Surroundings), not just the system[cite: 677].
