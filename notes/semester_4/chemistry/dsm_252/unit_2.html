1. Review of Laws of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the study of energy, heat, work, and their interconversions in chemical systems[cite: 878].

  • First Law: Energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another[cite: 879].
  • Second Law: In any spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe always increases[cite: 879].
  • Third Law: The entropy of a perfectly crystalline substance at absolute zero (0 K) is exactly zero[cite: 879].

2. Principles of Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry focuses on the heat energy absorbed or released during chemical reactions[cite: 880].

Key Definitions

  • System: The specific part of the universe under study[cite: 880].
  • Surroundings: Everything outside the system[cite: 880].
  • Exothermic Reaction: A reaction that releases heat to the surroundings (ΔH is negative)[cite: 880].
  • Endothermic Reaction: A reaction that absorbs heat from the surroundings (ΔH is positive)[cite: 880].

3. Standard Enthalpies of Formation and Solution

To standardize measurements, thermodynamic properties are reported under "Standard State" conditions[cite: 880].

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔHf°)

The enthalpy change when one mole of a substance is formed from its elements in their most stable states under standard conditions[cite: 880].

Enthalpies of Solution and Dilution

  • Integral Enthalpy of Solution: The heat change when a specific amount of solute is dissolved in a solvent to form a solution of a specific concentration[cite: 880].
  • Differential Enthalpy of Solution: The heat change when one mole of solute is added to a very large volume of solution so that the concentration remains virtually unchanged[cite: 880].
  • Enthalpy of Dilution: The heat change that occurs when more solvent is added to a solution[cite: 880].

4. Bond Energy and Resonance Energy

Thermochemical data allows for the calculation of the energy required to break chemical bonds[cite: 881].

Bond Energy and Bond Dissociation Energy

  • Bond Dissociation Energy: The energy required to break one mole of a specific bond in a gaseous molecule[cite: 881].
  • Bond Energy: Often used as the average value of bond dissociation energies for similar bonds in different molecules[cite: 881].

Resonance Energy

Resonance energy is the difference between the actual (experimental) enthalpy of formation of a compound and the value calculated for its most stable Lewis structure[cite: 881].

Resonance Energy = ΔH (experimental) - ΔH (calculated) [cite: 881]

5. Temperature Dependence: Kirchhoff’s Equation

Kirchhoff’s equation describes how the enthalpy change (ΔH) of a chemical reaction varies with temperature[cite: 882].

ΔH2 - ΔH1 = ΔCp (T2 - T1) [cite: 882]

Where ΔCp is the difference in molar heat capacities at constant pressure between products and reactants[cite: 882]:

ΔCp = Σ Cp (products) - Σ Cp (reactants)[cite: 882].

6. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tips:
  • State Functions: Remember that Enthalpy (H) and Internal Energy (U) are state functions, meaning they depend only on the initial and final states, not the path taken[cite: 880].
  • Standard State: In calculations, the standard enthalpy of formation for elements in their stable form (e.g., O2 gas, C graphite) is taken as zero[cite: 880].
  • Kirchhoff's Equation: This is a common derivation; ensure you understand the relationship between ΔH and heat capacity[cite: 882].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the difference between integral and differential enthalpy of solution?
A: Integral enthalpy refers to dissolving a whole amount of solute into a solvent, while differential enthalpy refers to adding a tiny amount of solute to an existing solution[cite: 880].

Q: How is bond energy calculated from thermochemical data?
A: It is calculated using Hess’s Law by comparing the enthalpies of formation of gaseous atoms and the molecule itself[cite: 881].

Q: Define standard state.
A: It is the most stable physical state of a substance at 1 bar pressure and a specified temperature (usually 298.15 K)[cite: 880].
