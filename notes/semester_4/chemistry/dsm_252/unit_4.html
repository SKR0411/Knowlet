1. Alkanes: Preparation and Reactions

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons containing only single C-C bonds[cite: 888].

Methods of Preparation

  • Catalytic Hydrogenation: Addition of hydrogen to alkenes or alkynes in the presence of catalysts like Ni, Pt, or Pd[cite: 889].
  • Wurtz Reaction: Reaction of alkyl halides with sodium metal in dry ether to form higher alkanes[cite: 889].
  • Kolbe's Synthesis: Electrolysis of aqueous solutions of sodium or potassium salts of fatty acids[cite: 889].
  • From Grignard Reagent: Treatment of RMgX with water or alcohols to yield alkanes[cite: 889].

Chemical Reactions

  • Free Radical Substitution (Halogenation): Alkanes react with halogens (like Cl2) in the presence of UV light or heat[cite: 889].

2. Alkenes and Alkynes: Synthesis and Addition

These are unsaturated hydrocarbons containing double or triple bonds[cite: 890].

Preparation Methods

  • Elimination Reactions: Dehydrogenation of alkanes or dehydrohalogenation of alkyl halides following Saytzeff's rule[cite: 890].
  • Acetylene Production: Prepared industrially from Calcium Carbide (CaC2) reacting with water[cite: 891].

Key Reactions

  • Hydrogenation and Halogenation: Addition of H2 or Br2 (trans-addition)[cite: 891].
  • Hydration: Addition of water to form alcohols or carbonyl compounds[cite: 891].
  • Ozonolysis: Cleavage of double/triple bonds with ozone to identify the position of unsaturation[cite: 891].
  • Formation of Metal Acetylides: Terminal alkynes react with ammoniacal AgNO3 or CuCl to form precipitates, highlighting their acidic nature[cite: 891].

3. Rules of Addition

When unsymmetrical reagents add to unsymmetrical alkenes, specific rules govern the orientation[cite: 891].

Markownikoff's Rule

In the addition of HX to an alkene, the hydrogen atom attaches to the carbon with more hydrogen atoms, while the halogen attaches to the carbon with fewer hydrogens[cite: 891].

Anti-Markownikoff's (Peroxide) Effect

In the presence of organic peroxides, the addition of HBr (only) to alkenes occurs in the opposite direction via a free radical mechanism[cite: 891].

4. Aromatic Hydrocarbons: Benzene

Benzene (C6H6) is the simplest aromatic hydrocarbon, exhibiting unique stability[cite: 892].

Preparation of Benzene

  • From Phenol: Reduction of phenol by heating with Zinc dust[cite: 892].
  • Decarboxylation: Heating sodium benzoate with soda lime[cite: 892].
  • Cyclic Polymerization: Passing acetylene through a red-hot iron tube[cite: 892].
  • From Benzene Sulphonic Acid: Hydrolysis with superheated steam[cite: 892].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why do terminal alkynes show acidic character?
A: The hydrogen attached to the sp-hybridized carbon has high s-character, making the C-H bond polar and the hydrogen easily removable by strong bases[cite: 891].

Q: What is the purpose of Ozonolysis?
A: It is used to locate the position of a double or triple bond in a molecule by analyzing the resulting carbonyl fragments[cite: 891].

Q: Define the Wurtz Reaction.
A: It is a method to prepare higher symmetrical alkanes by reacting two moles of alkyl halides with sodium metal in dry ether[cite: 889].
