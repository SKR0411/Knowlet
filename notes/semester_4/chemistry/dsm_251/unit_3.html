1. Surface Tension Determination

Surface tension is the property of a liquid surface that causes it to act like a stretched elastic membrane[cite: 113].

  • Experiment: To determine the surface tension of glycerol or acetic acid solutions at various concentrations[cite: 842].
  • Method: Usually performed using a Stalagmometer (drop number or drop weight method)[cite: 154].
  • Data Analysis: A graph is constructed to show the variation of surface tension with the concentration of the solute[cite: 842].

2. Viscosity Measurement

Viscosity is the measure of a fluid's resistance to flow[cite: 113].

  • Experiment: To determine the viscosity of glycerol or acetic acid solutions at different concentrations[cite: 844].
  • Instrument: Performed using an Ostwald Viscometer[cite: 154].
  • Procedure: The time taken for a fixed volume of liquid to flow between two marks on the viscometer is measured and compared to a reference liquid (usually water)[cite: 154].
  • Graphing: A plot is made showing the relationship between viscosity and concentration[cite: 845].

3. Instrumental Titrations

Instrumental methods offer higher precision and can be used for colored or very dilute solutions where visual indicators fail.

pH-metric Titration

  • Reaction: Strong acid (e.g., HCl) vs. Strong base (e.g., NaOH)[cite: 847].
  • Principle: Measurement of the pH change as the titrant is added to determine the equivalence point[cite: 786].

Conductometric Titration

  • Reaction: Strong acid vs. Strong base[cite: 849].
  • Principle: Based on the change in electrical conductivity of the solution as ions are replaced during the reaction[cite: 1001].
  • Observation: For a strong acid-strong base titration, the conductance initially decreases sharply as highly mobile H+ ions are replaced by less mobile cations, then increases after the equivalence point as excess OH- ions are added[cite: 1232].

4. Solubility and Enthalpy of Dissolution

The solubility of a substance is temperature-dependent, which allows for the calculation of thermodynamic properties.

  • Experiment: To determine the solubility of benzoic acid at different temperatures[cite: 851].
  • Goal: To determine the ΔH (Enthalpy) of the dissolution process[cite: 852].
  • Method: Saturated solutions are titrated at different temperatures, and the results are analyzed using the Van't Hoff equation[cite: 782, 783].

5. Exam Focus: Tips and Viva Questions

Practical Exam Tips

  • Cleanliness: For surface tension and viscosity experiments, the glassware must be absolutely clean (grease-free) as impurities significantly affect the results[cite: 214].
  • Temperature: Record the room temperature accurately, as physical properties like viscosity are highly sensitive to temperature changes[cite: 155].
  • Consistency: Take at least three readings for flow time or drop numbers to ensure the reliability of your data[cite: 203].

Common Viva Questions

Q: How does temperature affect the viscosity of a liquid?
A: For liquids, viscosity generally decreases as temperature increases because the kinetic energy of molecules increases, overcoming intermolecular forces[cite: 155].

Q: What is the unit of viscosity?
A: The SI unit is Pascal-second (Pa·s), but it is commonly expressed in Poise or Centipoise[cite: 113].

Q: Why do we use NH4Cl in Group III cation analysis?
A: To suppress the ionization of NH4OH via the common ion effect, ensuring the hydroxide ion concentration is only high enough to precipitate Group III cations[cite: 731].
