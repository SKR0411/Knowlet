Justice

[cite_start]

Justice is a core concern of social and political philosophy, dealing with the fair treatment of individuals and the equitable distribution of resources and opportunities[cite: 288, 300].

Dimensions of Justice

    [cite_start]
  • Distributive Justice: Concerns the fair allocation of goods and wealth in a society[cite: 311].
    • [cite_start]
  • Procedural Justice: Focuses on the fairness of the processes and rules that determine outcomes[cite: 313].
    • [cite_start]
  • Social Justice: Aims for a society that provides for the basic needs and rights of all its members, regardless of their social standing[cite: 307].

Liberty

[cite_start]

Liberty refers to the state of being free within society from oppressive restrictions imposed by authority on one's way of life, behavior, or political views[cite: 300].

Types of Liberty

    [cite_start]
  • Negative Liberty: Freedom from interference; the absence of obstacles or constraints on an individual's actions[cite: 303].
    • [cite_start]
  • Positive Liberty: The freedom to act in such a way as to realize one's fundamental purposes or potential; often linked to self-mastery[cite: 304].
    • [cite_start]
  • Civil Liberty: Protections provided by the state to ensure individuals can exercise their basic rights without arbitrary interference[cite: 303].

Equality

[cite_start]

Equality signifies that all individuals are considered equal in status, rights, and opportunities[cite: 300]. It is closely linked to justice and liberty.

[cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start]
Type of Equality Core Concept
Legal EqualityEvery citizen is subject to the same laws without discrimination[cite: 313].
Social EqualityAll people in a society have equal social status and opportunities[cite: 309].
Economic EqualityA reduction in wealth gaps and provision of equal opportunities for economic advancement[cite: 311].

Contemporary Challenges: Terrorism and Insurgency

[cite_start]

Social and political philosophy must address modern threats to the stability and ethical fabric of the state, specifically Terrorism and Insurgency[cite: 301].

1. Terrorism

[cite_start]

Terrorism involves the use of intentional violence, typically against civilians, to create fear for political, religious, or ideological goals[cite: 301]. [cite_start]Philosophically, it raises questions about the legitimacy of political violence and the balance between security and civil liberties[cite: 303].

2. Insurgency

[cite_start]

Insurgency is a rebellious act against an established government or authority, often involving armed conflict by a group not recognized as a belligerent[cite: 301]. [cite_start]Unlike terrorism, it usually seeks to seize territorial control or political power through a broader social movement[cite: 303, 312].

Exam Focus: Unit 5 Enhancements

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the relationship between Liberty and Equality?
A: They are often seen as complementary but sometimes in tension. [cite_start]Complete liberty for some can lead to inequality, while strict equality enforced by the state can restrict personal liberty[cite: 300, 313].

Q: How is Insurgency different from a Revolution?
[cite_start]A: While both involve resistance to authority, a revolution typically aims for a total structural change of the entire system, whereas an insurgency might be more localized or limited in its initial political goals[cite: 296, 301].

Exam Tip

[cite_start]

When discussing Justice, referencing its relation to Ethics and Sociology (as studied in Unit 1) will gain extra marks by showing inter-unit consistency[cite: 292].

Common Mistakes

Do not treat Terrorism and Insurgency as the same. [cite_start]Terrorism is primarily a tactic of fear, whereas Insurgency is a political-military strategy used to contest the state's sovereignty over a population[cite: 301, 312].
