Anumana and Concomitance (Vyapti)

[cite_start]

In the Tarkasamgraha, Anumana (Inference) is the second source of valid knowledge[cite: 277, 281]. [cite_start]It is defined as knowledge that follows other knowledge—specifically, the knowledge of a sign (Hetu) that leads to the knowledge of the thing signified (Sadhya)[cite: 277].

Constituents of Anumana

    [cite_start]
  • Sadhya: The major term or the thing to be proved (e.g., Fire)[cite: 277].
    • [cite_start]
  • Hetu (or Linga): The middle term or the reason/sign (e.g., Smoke)[cite: 277].
    • [cite_start]
  • Paksa: The minor term or the location where the Sadhya is to be proved (e.g., The Hill)[cite: 277].
[cite_start]"Anumiti is the knowledge produced by the consideration of a sign (paramarsa). Anumana is the instrument of that knowledge." [cite: 277, 285]

Understanding Vyapti (Concomitance)

[cite_start]

Vyapti is the logical ground of inference[cite: 277]. [cite_start]It is the invariable, unconditional relation between the Hetu and the Sadhya[cite: 277]. [cite_start]It ensures that wherever the Hetu (Smoke) is present, the Sadhya (Fire) must also be present[cite: 277].

Types of Vyapti

    [cite_start]
  • Anvaya-vyapti: Positive concomitance—the presence of Hetu is followed by the presence of Sadhya (Wherever there is smoke, there is fire)[cite: 277, 285].
    • [cite_start]
  • Vyatireka-vyapti: Negative concomitance—the absence of Sadhya is followed by the absence of Hetu (Wherever there is no fire, there is no smoke)[cite: 277, 285].

Classification of Inference

[cite_start]

Inference in Indian Logic is classified from multiple perspectives—psychological, causal, and logical[cite: 278].

1. Psychological Classification

Type Purpose Structure
Svarthanumana For oneself. Informal; requires only the presence of the three constituents in the mind.
Pararthanumana For others. Formal; consists of the five-membered syllogism (Panchavayava Nyaya).

2. Causal Classification (Based on the relationship between Cause and Effect)

    [cite_start]
  • Purvavat: Inference of an unperceived effect from a perceived cause (e.g., Inferring rain from dark clouds)[cite: 278].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sesavat: Inference of an unperceived cause from a perceived effect (e.g., Inferring rain occurred upstream from a muddy, rising river)[cite: 278].
    • [cite_start]
  • Samanyatodrsta: Inference based on common observation rather than direct causal relation (e.g., Inferring the motion of the sun from its changing position in the sky)[cite: 278].

Exam Tips

    [cite_start]
  • Paramarsa: Understand that Paramarsa is the crucial "link" where we realize the Hetu exists in the Paksa AND that the Hetu is invariably related to the Sadhya[cite: 285].
    • [cite_start]
  • The Five Members (Panchavayava): Memorize the order: Pratijna (Proposition), Hetu (Reason), Udaharana (Example), Upanaya (Application), and Nigamana (Conclusion)[cite: 285].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the difference between a Western Syllogism and an Indian Syllogism?
[cite_start]A: Western logic is purely formal, while Indian logic is formal-material, meaning it requires a real-world example (Udaharana) to be valid[cite: 285].

Q: Can Anumana be valid without Vyapti?
A: No. Vyapti is the very "breath" of inference. [cite_start]Without a universal relationship between the sign and the thing signified, no certain knowledge can be inferred[cite: 277].
