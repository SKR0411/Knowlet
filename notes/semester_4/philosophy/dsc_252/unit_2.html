Definition of Pratyaksa

[cite_start]

In the Tarkasamgraha, Pratyaksa is identified as the first and most fundamental Pramana (source of knowledge)[cite: 273, 280]. [cite_start]It is defined as knowledge produced by the contact of a sense-organ with its object (Indriyartha-sannikarsa-janyam jnanam)[cite: 271, 273].

[cite_start]"Perception is that knowledge which arises from the contact of the senses with their objects." [cite: 273]

For a perceptual experience to occur, the following chain of contact is necessary:

  • The Atman (Self) contacts the Manas (Mind).
  • The Manas contacts the Indriya (Sense-organ).
  • The Indriya contacts the Artha (Object).

Savikalpaka and Nirvikalpaka Stages

[cite_start]

Annambhatta distinguishes between two psychological stages or types of perception based on the level of conceptual clarity[cite: 274]:

1. Nirvikalpaka (Indeterminate Perception)

[cite_start]

This is the primary, immediate, and non-judgmental stage of perception[cite: 274]. It is the awareness of an object without its names, classes, or attributes. [cite_start]We perceive the "that" (existence) but not the "what" (identity)[cite: 274]. It is pure, raw sensory data before the mind processes it into a concept.

2. Savikalpaka (Determinate Perception)

[cite_start]

This is the secondary, reflective, and judgmental stage where the object is perceived along with its attributes (Vishesana), such as its name, genus, and quality[cite: 274]. [cite_start]Here, the object is recognized as "This is a jar" or "This man is fair"[cite: 274].

Classification of Pratyaksa

[cite_start]

Perception is broadly classified into two categories based on the nature of the sense-object contact[cite: 275]:

1. Laukika (Ordinary) Perception

[cite_start]

This occurs through the normal functioning of the five external senses (Sight, Smell, Taste, Touch, Hearing) and the internal sense (Manas/Mind)[cite: 275]. It is further divided into:

    [cite_start]
  • Bahya (External): Perceived through external sense organs. [cite: 275]
    • [cite_start]
  • Abhyantara (Internal): Perception of mental states like pleasure or pain through the Mind. [cite: 275]

2. Alaukika (Extraordinary) Perception

[cite_start]

This involves contact that transcends ordinary sensory limits[cite: 275]. [cite_start]It is typically subdivided in the Nyaya tradition into Samanyalaksana (perception of universals), Jnanalaksana (complicated perception), and Yogaja (intuitive perception of yogis)[cite: 275].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Savikalpaka perception always valid?
A: Not necessarily. [cite_start]While it is a stage of perception, if the attributes applied are incorrect (like mistaking a rope for a snake), it becomes Viparyaya (invalid knowledge). [cite: 270, 274]

Q: What is the role of Manas in Pratyaksa?
[cite_start]A: Manas acts as an internal sense-organ for perceiving internal states and serves as a coordinator that prevents multiple sensations from overwhelming the Atman at once. [cite: 275]
