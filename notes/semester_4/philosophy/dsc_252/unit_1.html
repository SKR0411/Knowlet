Prama and Aprama: Definition

[cite_start]

In Indian Epistemology, specifically in Tarkasamgraha, knowledge (Buddhi) is divided into two main categories: Prama and Aprama[cite: 269].

1. Prama (Valid Knowledge)

[cite_start]

Prama is defined as Yathartha Anubhava[cite: 269]. It is knowledge that represents an object exactly as it is in reality. For knowledge to be considered Prama, it must fulfill three conditions:

    [cite_start]
  • It must be New (Anadhigata): It shouldn't be a mere memory. [cite: 269]
    • [cite_start]
  • It must be True/Certain (Abadhita): It shouldn't be contradicted by further experience. [cite: 269]
    • [cite_start]
  • It must be Unwavering (Nishchaya): It shouldn't be doubtful. [cite: 269]

2. Aprama (Invalid Knowledge)

[cite_start]

Aprama is defined as Ayathartha Anubhava[cite: 269]. This is knowledge that does not correspond to the actual nature of the object. It is essentially non-valid experience.

Samsaya, Viparyaya, and Tarka

[cite_start]

Annambhatta classifies non-valid knowledge (Ayathartha Anubhava) into three specific types[cite: 270]:

Term English Translation Definition & Example
Samsaya Doubt Knowledge where the mind wavers between two or more conflicting possibilities regarding the same object. [cite_start]
Example: Seeing a tall object in the distance and wondering, "Is that a post or a man?" [cite: 270]
Viparyaya Error / Illusion Definite but false knowledge of an object. It is taking one thing for another. [cite_start]
Example: Mistaking a piece of shell (mother-of-pearl) for silver. [cite: 270]
Tarka Hypothetical Reasoning Indirect proof used to support a claim by pointing out the absurdity of its opposite. It is not knowledge of the thing itself but a way to remove doubt. [cite_start]
Example: "If there were no fire, there would be no smoke." [cite: 270]

Pramana: Definition

[cite_start]"Pramana is the instrument (Karana) of valid knowledge (Prama)." [cite: 271]
[cite_start]

In this context, Pramana refers to the means or source by which we acquire correct knowledge[cite: 271]. For every valid piece of knowledge, there must be an instrument that produced it. For example, if we see a pot (Prama), the eyes are the instrument (Pramana).

Exam Focus: Unit 1 Enhancements

Key Distinctions for Exams

  • Anubhava vs. Smriti: Anubhava is presentative knowledge (direct experience), while Smriti is representative knowledge (memory). [cite_start]Prama is always a type of Anubhava. [cite: 269, 270]
  • Samsaya vs. Viparyaya: In Samsaya, the mind is undecided. [cite_start]In Viparyaya, the mind is decided, but it is wrong. [cite: 270]

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is memory (Smriti) considered Prama?
A: Generally, no. In Tarkasamgraha, Prama is Anubhava (direct experience). [cite_start]Memory is a reproduction of previous experience and is usually excluded from the primary definition of valid knowledge. [cite: 269]

Q: Why is Tarka classified under non-valid knowledge?
A: Tarka is not a direct source of knowledge but a supportive psychological tool to confirm the validity of an inference or to remove doubts. [cite_start]It does not provide new knowledge of an object on its own. [cite: 270]
