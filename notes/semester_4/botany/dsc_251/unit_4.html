Therapeutic Drugs

[cite_start]

Therapeutic drugs are substances derived from plants used to treat or prevent diseases[cite: 661, 662].

1. Rauwolfia (Rauwolfia serpentina)

    [cite_start]
  • Common Name: Sarpagandha[cite: 578].
  • Family: Apocynaceae.
    • [cite_start]
  • Morphology: An erect perennial undershrub with tuberous roots[cite: 662].
  • Active Principle: Reserpine.
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Primarily used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension) and mental disorders like schizophrenia[cite: 217, 662].

2. Cinchona (Cinchona officinalis)

    [cite_start]
  • Family: Rubiaceae[cite: 693].
    • [cite_start]
  • Part Used: Dried bark of the stem or root[cite: 662].
  • Active Principle: Quinine.
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Famous as an anti-malarial drug; also used as a bitter tonic[cite: 662].

3. Withania (Withania somnifera)

    [cite_start]
  • Common Name: Ashwagandha[cite: 220].
    • [cite_start]
  • Family: Solanaceae[cite: 693].
    • [cite_start]
  • Morphology: An erect branching undershrub with fleshy roots[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Used to boost immunity, reduce stress, and improve memory and physical strength[cite: 217, 220, 662].

4. Digitalis (Digitalis purpurea)

  • Common Name: Foxglove.
    • [cite_start]
  • Part Used: Dried leaves[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Used to treat congestive heart failure and cardiac arrhythmias by strengthening heart muscle contractions[cite: 217, 662].

5. Aloe vera

  • Family: Xanthorrhoeaceae (Liliaceae).
    • [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Succulent plant with thick, fleshy leaves containing gel[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Used in treating burns, skin diseases, and as a laxative[cite: 217, 662].

6. Taxus (Taxus baccata)

  • Common Name: Yew.
    • [cite_start]
  • Part Used: Bark and leaves[cite: 662].
  • Active Principle: Taxol.
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses: Potent anti-cancer drug used specifically in breast and ovarian cancer treatment[cite: 662].

Habit-forming Drugs

[cite_start]

These plants contain psychoactive substances that can lead to physical or psychological dependence if misused[cite: 661, 662].

1. Opium Poppy (Papaver somniferum)

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: An annual herb with large, showy flowers; the fruit is a capsule[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Processing: Incisions are made in the unripe capsules to collect the milky latex (opium)[cite: 662].
  • Alkaloids: Morphine, Codeine, and Papaverine.
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses/Hazards: Used medically as a powerful painkiller (analgesic); however, it is highly addictive and can lead to severe respiratory depression[cite: 662].

2. Cannabis (Cannabis sativa)

  • Common Names: Marijuana, Ganja, Bhang.
    • [cite_start]
  • Morphology: An erect annual herb with palmate leaves[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Processing: Dried leaves, flowering tops, or resin (charas) are extracted[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uses/Hazards: Used to treat glaucoma and nausea in chemotherapy; recreational use causes hallucinations, impaired judgment, and potential long-term cognitive issues[cite: 662].

Tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum)

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: An annual herb with large, sticky leaves and tubular flowers[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Processing: Leaves undergo curing (slow drying), fermentation, and aging to develop flavor and aroma[cite: 662].
  • Active Principle: Nicotine.
  • Uses: Smoking, chewing, or snuff.
  • Health Hazards: Causes lung cancer, heart disease, chronic bronchitis, and emphysema; [cite_start]Nicotine is highly addictive[cite: 662].
Exam Tips
    [cite_start]
  • Match the Plant to Use: Questions often ask to match plants like Taxus (Cancer) or Digitalis (Heart) to their clinical use[cite: 662].
    • [cite_start]
  • Botanical Names: Be sure to use the full scientific names for higher marks (e.g., Rauwolfia serpentina instead of just Rauwolfia)[cite: 578].
    • [cite_start]
  • Processing: For Tobacco, the term "Curing" is vital; for Opium, the term "Unripe capsule latex" is a key phrase[cite: 662].
Common Pitfalls
  • Confusing Reserpine (from Rauwolfia) with Quinine (from Cinchona).
    • [cite_start]
  • Forgetting that Ashwagandha belongs to the family Solanaceae (the potato family)[cite: 693].
Real-world Application

Many modern pharmaceutical drugs are simply synthetic versions of these plant extracts. [cite_start]For example, Aspirin was originally derived from Willow bark, showing how vital plant genetic resources are for modern medicine[cite: 212, 644].
