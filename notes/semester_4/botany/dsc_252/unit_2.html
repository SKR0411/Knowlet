Principles and Rules of ICN

[cite_start]

The International Code of Nomenclature for algae, fungi, and plants (ICN) is the set of rules and recommendations dealing with the formal botanical names that are given to plants[cite: 356, 686]. [cite_start]Its intent is that each taxonomic group of plants has only one correct name that is accepted worldwide[cite: 356, 686].

Core Principles of ICN

    [cite_start]
  • Independence: Botanical nomenclature is independent of zoological and bacteriological nomenclature[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Typification: The application of names of taxonomic groups is determined by means of nomenclatural types[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Priority: The nomenclature of a taxonomic group is based upon priority of publication[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Uniqueness: Each taxonomic group with a particular circumscription, position, and rank can bear only one correct name[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Scientific Names: Scientific names of taxonomic groups are treated as Latin regardless of their derivation[cite: 356, 686].

Ranks and Names

[cite_start]

Taxonomic groups are treated in a hierarchical system of ranks[cite: 356, 686]. [cite_start]The primary ranks are Kingdom, Division, Class, Order, Family, Genus, and Species[cite: 682, 686].

Suffixes for Specific Ranks

Rank Standard Suffix Example
Order -ales Rosales
Family -aceae Rosaceae
Subfamily -oideae Rosoideae

Typification and Author Citation

Typification

[cite_start]

Typification is the process of designating a specific specimen (the type) to which the name of a taxon is permanently attached[cite: 356, 686].

    [cite_start]
  • Holotype: The single specimen designated by the author at the time of publication[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Isotype: A duplicate specimen of the holotype, collected at the same time and place[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Lectotype: A specimen chosen from the original material to serve as the type when no holotype was designated or is lost[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Neotype: A specimen chosen to serve as the type when all original material is missing[cite: 356, 686].

Author Citation

[cite_start]

For a name to be complete and accurate, it should be followed by the name of the author who first validly published it[cite: 356, 686]. [cite_start]Example: Solanum tuberosum L. (where 'L.' stands for Linnaeus)[cite: 356, 686].

Valid Publication and Principle of Priority

Valid Publication

[cite_start]

To be validly published, a name must be[cite: 356, 686]:

    [cite_start]
  1. Effectively published (distributed in printed matter to the public/institutions)[cite: 356, 686].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Accompanied by a description or diagnosis of the taxon[cite: 356, 686].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Formulated according to the rules of the ICN[cite: 356, 686].

Principle of Priority

[cite_start]

If a taxon has been given multiple names, the oldest validly published name is the correct one[cite: 356, 686]. [cite_start]This principle starts from May 1, 1753 (Linnaeus's Species Plantarum)[cite: 356, 686]. [cite_start]Limitations: Priority does not apply to ranks above family[cite: 686].

Naming of Hybrids

[cite_start]

Hybrids are named using an 'x' sign[cite: 356, 687].

    [cite_start]
  • Interspecific: Fragaria x ananassa (Hybrid between two species)[cite: 356, 687].
    • [cite_start]
  • Intergeneric: x Triticosecale (Hybrid between Triticum and Secale)[cite: 356, 687].

Taxonomic Documentation

[cite_start]

Documentation is essential for storing and retrieving information about plant diversity[cite: 687].

    [cite_start]
  • Flora: A book describing the plants of a specific geographical area (e.g., Flora of British India)[cite: 353, 687].
    • [cite_start]
  • Monograph: A comprehensive study of a single taxonomic group (usually a genus or family) across its entire global range[cite: 687].
    • [cite_start]
  • Journals: Periodical publications where new taxa are described and taxonomic research is shared[cite: 687].

Taxonomic Keys

[cite_start]

Keys are artificial devices used for the rapid identification of plants[cite: 353, 687].

    [cite_start]
  • Single Access (Dichotomous) Keys: Provide two contrasting choices (couplets) at each step[cite: 687].
    • Indented Key: Each pair of choices is indented under the previous pair.
    • Bracketeded Key: The two choices of a couplet are placed on adjacent lines.
    • [cite_start]
  • Multi-access (Polyclave) Keys: Allow the user to choose characters in any order, often used in computer-based identification systems[cite: 687].
Exam Tips
  • Typification: Be very clear on the differences between Holotype, Isotype, and Lectotype. [cite_start]This is a common objective/short note topic[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Priority: Remember the starting date (1753) for botanical priority[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Keys: Be prepared to write a simple dichotomous key for a few common plants[cite: 687].
Common Mistakes
    [cite_start]
  • Confusing **Effective Publication** (distribution) with **Valid Publication** (meeting all ICN requirements)[cite: 356, 686].
    • [cite_start]
  • Forgetting the 'x' when writing the scientific name of a hybrid[cite: 356, 687].
Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a diagnosis in nomenclature?
[cite_start]A: A diagnosis is a statement that sets the taxon apart from its most closely related relatives by highlighting its unique features[cite: 356, 686].

Q: Can a name be valid if it doesn't have a type?
[cite_start]A: No, the principle of typification requires that a name be associated with a physical type specimen for it to be validly applied[cite: 356, 686].
