Photosynthetic Pigments

[cite: 776] [cite_start]

Photosynthetic pigments are chemical compounds that absorb light energy and convert it into chemical energy[cite: 776]. [cite_start]In plants, these are primarily found in the chloroplasts[cite: 776].

    [cite_start]
  • Chlorophyll a (Chl a): The primary photosynthetic pigment present in all oxygen-evolving photosynthetic organisms[cite: 776]. [cite_start]It acts as the reaction center[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Chlorophyll b (Chl b): An accessory pigment that broadens the spectrum of light that can be used for photosynthesis[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Xanthophylls: Yellowish accessory pigments that also provide photoprotection to the plant by dissipating excess light energy[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Carotenes: Orange-colored accessory pigments that assist in light harvesting and protect chlorophyll from photo-oxidation[cite: 776].
[cite_start]

Photosystem I and II

[cite: 776] [cite_start]

Photosystems are functional and structural units involved in photosynthesis that absorb light and transfer electrons[cite: 776].

    [cite_start]
  • Photosystem I (PS I): Contains a reaction center chlorophyll a molecule known as P700, which has an absorption peak at 700 nm[cite: 776]. [cite_start]It is primarily involved in the production of NADPH[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Photosystem II (PS II): Contains a reaction center chlorophyll a molecule known as P680, with an absorption peak at 680 nm[cite: 776]. [cite_start]It is responsible for the photolysis of water and the evolution of oxygen[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Antenna Molecules: These are accessory pigments that capture light energy and funnel it toward the reaction center[cite: 776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reaction Center: The specific site within a photosystem where the energy of a photon is converted into the movement of an electron[cite: 776].
[cite_start]

Electron Transport and ATP Synthesis

[cite: 777] [cite_start]

The movement of electrons through the photosystems creates the energy needed for ATP synthesis[cite: 777].

    [cite_start]
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC): Electrons move from PS II to PS I through a series of carriers (Plastoquinone, Cytochrome b6f, Plastocyanin)[cite: 777].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mechanism of ATP Synthesis: According to the chemiosmotic hypothesis, the movement of electrons leads to the accumulation of protons in the thylakoid lumen[cite: 777]. [cite_start]The flow of these protons back into the stroma through the ATP synthase enzyme drives the synthesis of ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate[cite: 777].

Photorespiration

[cite: 777] [cite_start]

Photorespiration is a process where the enzyme Rubisco binds with oxygen instead of carbon dioxide, leading to a loss of fixed carbon and energy[cite: 777].

    [cite_start]
  • It occurs primarily in C3 plants under high temperature and high oxygen conditions[cite: 777].
    • [cite_start]
  • It is considered a "wasteful" process because it produces no ATP or sugar[cite: 777].
    • [cite_start]
  • C4 plants have evolved to minimize photorespiration by concentrating CO2 in bundle sheath cells[cite: 777].

Glycolysis, Anaerobic Respiration, and TCA Cycle

[cite: 778] [cite_start]

Respiration is the process by which plants break down food to release energy[cite: 778].

[cite_start]

Glycolysis

[cite: 778] [cite_start]

Occurs in the cytosol and is common to both aerobic and anaerobic respiration[cite: 778]. [cite_start]One molecule of glucose is broken down into two molecules of pyruvic acid, with a net gain of 2 ATP[cite: 778].

[cite_start]

Anaerobic Respiration

[cite: 778] [cite_start]

Occurs in the absence of oxygen[cite: 778]. [cite_start]Pyruvate is converted into either ethanol and CO2 (alcoholic fermentation) or lactic acid (lactic acid fermentation)[cite: 778].

[cite_start]

TCA Cycle (Krebs Cycle)

[cite: 778] [cite_start]

Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix[cite: 778]. [cite_start]Pyruvate enters the cycle after being converted to Acetyl-CoA[cite: 778]. [cite_start]It produces CO2, ATP, NADH, and FADH2[cite: 778].

[cite_start]

Advanced Respiratory Pathways

[cite: 778]
    [cite_start]
  • Oxidative Phosphorylation: Occurs in the inner mitochondrial membrane where NADH and FADH2 are oxidized to produce ATP via the electron transport system and ATP synthase[cite: 778].
    • [cite_start]
  • Glyoxylate Cycle: A variation of the TCA cycle that allows plants (especially in fatty seeds) to convert fats into carbohydrates[cite: 778].
    • [cite_start]
  • Oxidative Pentose Phosphate Pathway (OPPP): An alternative pathway to glycolysis for glucose oxidation that generates NADPH and five-carbon sugars for biosynthetic reactions[cite: 778].
Exam Tips
    [cite_start]
  • Z-Scheme: Be ready to draw the Z-scheme of electron transport, labeling the carriers correctly[cite: 777].
    • [cite_start]
  • C3 vs C4: Comparison between C3 and C4 pathways is a very common essay-type question[cite: 777].
    • [cite_start]
  • ATP Gains: Memorize the net ATP gain in glycolysis (2 ATP) and the total yield in aerobic respiration[cite: 778].
Common Mistakes
    [cite_start]
  • Confusing the location of cycles: Glycolysis is in the cytosol, TCA cycle is in the mitochondria, and Calvin cycle is in the chloroplast stroma[cite: 777, 778].
  • Mistaking photorespiration for regular respiration. [cite_start]Photorespiration depends on light and Rubisco activity, while regular respiration occurs in both light and dark[cite: 777, 778].
Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Why is Rubisco called a "dual enzyme"?
[cite_start]A: Because it can act as both a carboxylase (fixing CO2) and an oxygenase (fixing O2 in photorespiration)[cite: 777].

Q: What is the significance of the Glyoxylate cycle?
[cite_start]A: It allows germinating seeds to use stored fats to produce sugars for energy before they start photosynthesis[cite: 778].
