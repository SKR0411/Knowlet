Pteridophytes: General Account and Classification

[cite_start]

Pteridophytes are known as "Vascular Cryptogams" because they are the first terrestrial plants to possess vascular tissues (xylem and phloem) while remaining seedless[cite: 147].

General Characteristics

    [cite_start]
  • Dominant Phase: The sporophyte is the dominant, independent, and vascularized phase of the life cycle[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Differentiation: The plant body is differentiated into true roots, stem, and leaves[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: They reproduce through spores produced in sporangia, which are often grouped into structures called strobili or sori[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Habitat: They prefer cool, damp, and shady places, though some are aquatic[cite: 147].

Classification and Economic Importance

    [cite_start]
  • Classification: Classified into four major groups: Psilopsida, Lycopsida, Sphenopsida, and Pteropsida[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Economic Importance: Used as ornamentals, soil binders, and for medicinal purposes[cite: 147].

Early Land Plants: Rhynia

[cite_start]

Rhynia is a fossil genus representing one of the simplest and earliest known land plants[cite: 148].

    [cite_start]
  • Structure: It lacked true roots and leaves[cite: 148]. [cite_start]The plant body consisted of a creeping rhizome and dichotomously branched aerial shoots[cite: 148].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sporangia: Large, oval sporangia were borne at the tips of the aerial branches[cite: 148].

Heterospory, Seed Habit, and Stelar Organization

Heterospory and Seed Habit

[cite_start]

Heterospory is the production of two types of spores: small microspores (male) and large megaspores (female)[cite: 147].

    [cite_start]
  • It is considered a significant evolutionary step toward the seed habit found in higher plants[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • The retention of the megaspore within the megasporangium allows for the development of the female gametophyte in a protected environment[cite: 147].

Stelar Organization

[cite_start]

The stele is the central vascular cylinder of the sporophyte[cite: 147]. [cite_start]Pteridophytes exhibit various stelar types, evolving from simple protostele (solid core of xylem) to complex siphonostele (pith present) and dictyostele[cite: 147].

Morphology and Reproduction: Lycopodium and Selaginella

1. Lycopodium (Club Moss)

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Sporophyte with a creeping or erect stem covered with small leaves (microphylls)[cite: 148].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: It is homosporous (produces one type of spore)[cite: 148]. [cite_start]Sporangia are borne on sporophylls which often form terminal strobili[cite: 148].

2. Selaginella (Spike Moss)

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Characterized by a creeping stem and the presence of a unique structure called the rhizophore[cite: 148].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: It is heterosporous, producing both microspores and megaspores in the same strobilus[cite: 148].

Gymnosperms: General Account and Classification

[cite_start]

Gymnosperms are "Naked Seeded" plants because their ovules are not enclosed by an ovary wall[cite: 149].

General Characteristics

    [cite_start]
  • Wood: Usually perennial, woody plants with well-developed taproot systems[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Leaves: Often xerophytic, adapted to withstand extreme environmental conditions (e.g., needle-like leaves in Pinus)[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: They are heterosporous and produce male and female cones (strobili)[cite: 149]. [cite_start]Pollination is primarily by wind (anemophily)[cite: 149].

Classification and Economic Importance

    [cite_start]
  • Classification: Major groups include Cycadales, Coniferales, Ginkgoales, and Gnetales[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Economic Importance: Source of timber, resin, turpentine, and edible seeds (e.g., Chilgoza)[cite: 149].

Morphology and Reproduction of Gymnosperm Genera

1. Cycas

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Palm-like appearance with a stout stem and a crown of pinnately compound leaves[cite: 149]. [cite_start]It possesses unique coralloid roots associated with nitrogen-fixing cyanobacteria[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: Dioecious; male plants produce large cones, while female plants produce clusters of megasporophylls (no true female cone)[cite: 149].

2. Pinus

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Tall, branched tree with a "pyramidal" habit[cite: 149]. [cite_start]Leaves are of two types: scale leaves and needle-like foliage leaves borne on dwarf shoots[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: Monoecious; both male and female cones are found on the same plant[cite: 149].

3. Ginkgo (Maidenhair Tree)

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Known as a "Living Fossil" with characteristic fan-shaped leaves[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reproduction: Dioecious; the seeds are large and fleshy[cite: 149].

4. Gnetum

    [cite_start]
  • Morphology: Often climbers or small trees with broad, angiosperm-like leaves[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Evolutionary Link: It is considered the most advanced gymnosperm, showing many similarities to angiosperms (e.g., presence of vessels in xylem)[cite: 149].
Exam Tips
    [cite_start]
  • Rhizophore vs. Root: For Selaginella, understand the morphological nature of the rhizophore as it is a common debate in exams[cite: 148].
    • [cite_start]
  • Evolutionary Trends: Focus on how Heterospory in Pteridophytes led to the Seed Habit in Gymnosperms[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Coralloid Roots: Always mention the symbiotic association with cyanobacteria when discussing Cycas[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Stele Types: Practice drawing the basic diagrams of protostele and siphonostele[cite: 147].
Common Mistakes
    [cite_start]
  • Calling Pteridophytes "Seed Plants"; they produce spores, not seeds[cite: 147].
    • [cite_start]
  • Forgetting that Gymnosperms do not have fruits (because they lack an ovary)[cite: 149].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mixing up Lycopodium (homosporous) and Selaginella (heterosporous)[cite: 148].
