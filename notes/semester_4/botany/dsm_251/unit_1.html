Viruses: Characteristics and Importance

[cite_start]

Viruses are ultramicroscopic, non-cellular organisms that exist at the threshold between living and non-living worlds[cite: 43, 133]. [cite_start]They are obligate intracellular parasites, meaning they can only replicate inside a living host cell[cite: 41, 133].

Key Characteristics

    [cite_start]
  • Acellular Structure: They lack a cellular organization and cytoplasm[cite: 43].
    • [cite_start]
  • Genetic Material: They contain either DNA or RNA, but never both, surrounded by a protein coat called a capsid[cite: 47, 133].
    • [cite_start]
  • Metabolic Inertness: Outside a host cell, they are inert and can be crystallized like non-living matter[cite: 133].

Economic Importance

[cite_start]

Viruses play a dual role in our world, primarily known for causing diseases but also having beneficial applications[cite: 47, 133]:

    [cite_start]
  • Harmful: They cause severe diseases in plants (e.g., Mosaic disease), animals (e.g., Rabies), and humans (e.g., Influenza, HIV)[cite: 64, 1170].
    • [cite_start]
  • Beneficial: Used as vectors in genetic engineering to transfer genes[cite: 1028]. [cite_start]Bacteriophages are used to control harmful bacterial populations (phage therapy)[cite: 47].

T-phage and TMV Structure

T-phage (Bacteriophage)

[cite_start]

T-phages are viruses that infect bacteria, specifically E. coli[cite: 47, 133].

    [cite_start]
  • Structure: They typically have a "tadpole" shape with a head and a tail[cite: 47].
    • [cite_start]
  • Head: A protein capsid containing the double-stranded DNA[cite: 47].
    • [cite_start]
  • Tail: A complex structure used for attaching to the host and injecting the viral DNA[cite: 47].

Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV)

[cite_start]

TMV was the first virus to be discovered and primarily infects tobacco plants[cite: 47, 133].

    [cite_start]
  • Structure: It is a rod-shaped virus[cite: 47].
    • [cite_start]
  • Composition: It consists of a single-stranded RNA molecule spirally coiled and enclosed in a cylindrical protein coat made of capsomeres[cite: 47].

Lytic and Lysogenic Cycles

[cite_start]

These are the two primary mechanisms by which viruses replicate within a host cell[cite: 133].

Lytic Cycle

[cite_start]

In the lytic cycle, the virus takes over the host's machinery to produce new viruses, eventually causing the host cell to burst (lyse)[cite: 133].

    [cite_start]
  1. Adsorption: Attachment of virus to the host cell surface[cite: 133].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Penetration: Injection of viral genetic material into the host[cite: 133].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Biosynthesis: Viral DNA directs the host cell to produce viral components[cite: 133].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Maturation: Assembly of new viral particles[cite: 133].
    5. [cite_start]
  5. Lysis: Release of new viruses by breaking the host cell[cite: 133].
[Image of lytic cycle of a bacteriophage]

Lysogenic Cycle

[cite_start]

In the lysogenic cycle, the viral DNA integrates into the host's chromosome and remains dormant as a "prophage"[cite: 133].

    [cite_start]
  • The viral genome is replicated along with the host's DNA during cell division[cite: 133].
    • [cite_start]
  • The prophage can be triggered by environmental stress to enter the lytic cycle[cite: 133].

Bacteria: Characteristics and Importance

[cite_start]

Bacteria are prokaryotic, microscopic, unicellular organisms found in almost every environment on Earth[cite: 48, 134].

General Characteristics

    [cite_start]
  • Prokaryotic: They lack a well-defined nucleus and membrane-bound organelles[cite: 48, 134].
    • [cite_start]
  • Nutrition: Diverse nutritional groups including autotrophs and heterotrophs[cite: 44].

Economic Importance

    [cite_start]
  • Beneficial: Nitrogen fixation in soil (e.g., Rhizobium), antibiotic production (e.g., Streptomyces), and fermentation in food industry (e.g., Lactobacillus)[cite: 50, 52, 57].
    • [cite_start]
  • Harmful: Pathogenic bacteria cause diseases like Tuberculosis, Tetanus, and Citrus Canker[cite: 64, 1170].

Bacterial Cell Structure

[cite_start]

The bacterial cell is characterized by a relatively simple but highly efficient organization[cite: 49, 134].

    [cite_start]
  • Cell Wall: A rigid outer layer typically composed of peptidoglycan, providing shape and protection[cite: 49, 134].
    • [cite_start]
  • Cytoplasmic Membrane: Regulates the passage of materials in and out of the cell[cite: 49].
    • [cite_start]
  • Nucleoid: A region containing the circular, double-stranded DNA molecule[cite: 49, 134].
    • [cite_start]
  • Flagella: Hair-like structures used for locomotion (in some species)[cite: 49].
    • [cite_start]
  • Pili/Fimbriae: Used for attachment to surfaces or other cells during conjugation[cite: 49].

Reproduction in Bacteria

[cite_start]

Bacteria reproduce primarily through asexual methods but can also exchange genetic material[cite: 49, 134].

Vegetative and Asexual Reproduction

    [cite_start]
  • Binary Fission: The most common method where a single cell divides into two identical daughter cells[cite: 134, 311].
    • [cite_start]
  • Endospore Formation: Production of highly resistant spores under unfavorable conditions for survival[cite: 134, 311].

Genetic Recombination

[cite_start]

While not true sexual reproduction, these processes lead to genetic diversity[cite: 134]:

    [cite_start]
  • Conjugation: Direct transfer of DNA between two bacteria via a conjugation tube or pilus[cite: 134, 311].
    • [cite_start]
  • Transformation: Uptake of "naked" DNA from the surrounding environment[cite: 134].
    • [cite_start]
  • Transduction: Transfer of bacterial DNA from one cell to another by a bacteriophage[cite: 134].
Exam Tips
  • Viral Comparison: Be ready to compare the Lytic and Lysogenic cycles; use a diagram to illustrate the differences.
  • Bacterial DNA Transfer: Clearly distinguish between Conjugation (cell-to-cell contact), Transformation (environment), and Transduction (viral vector).
  • Economic Importance: Always mention both the positive (biotechnology, nitrogen fixation) and negative (diseases) aspects.
Common Mistakes
    [cite_start]
  • Thinking viruses can grow on artificial media; they MUST have a living host[cite: 133].
    • [cite_start]
  • Confusing the rod-shaped structure of TMV with the tadpole shape of T-phages[cite: 47].
Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What is a prophage?
[cite_start]A: It is the viral DNA that has integrated into the bacterial host's chromosome during the lysogenic cycle[cite: 133].

Q: Why are endospores significant?
[cite_start]A: They allow bacteria to survive extreme heat, chemicals, and radiation for very long periods until conditions improve[cite: 134, 311].
