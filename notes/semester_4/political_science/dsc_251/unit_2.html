1. Basic Features of the US Constitution

[cite_start]

The US Constitution is the supreme law of the United States of America[cite: 636, 645]. [cite_start]It serves as the foundation for its political and legal systems[cite: 636, 649].

Written and Rigid Constitution

[cite_start]

The US Constitution is a written document[cite: 636, 645]. [cite_start]It is also rigid, meaning the process to amend it is intentionally difficult to ensure stability and protect the rights of states[cite: 636, 650].

Federalism

[cite_start]

The US operates under a federal system, where power is divided between the national (central) government and individual state governments[cite: 636, 649].

Separation of Powers

[cite_start]

This principle ensures that the legislative, executive, and judicial functions are held by different branches to prevent the concentration of power[cite: 636, 650].

[Image of the three branches of the US government: Legislative, Executive, and Judicial]

Checks and Balances

[cite_start]

Each branch has the power to limit the actions of the others[cite: 650]. [cite_start]For example, the President can veto legislation passed by Congress, while Congress can override a veto[cite: 636, 645].

Bill of Rights

[cite_start]

The first ten amendments, known as the Bill of Rights, guarantee fundamental individual liberties such as freedom of speech and religion[cite: 650, 651].

2. The US President: Powers and Functions

[cite_start]

The President is the head of the executive branch and serves as both head of state and head of government[cite: 636, 645].

Election and Term

[cite_start]

The President is elected for a four-year term through an Electoral College system[cite: 636, 645]. [cite_start]A person is limited to serving two terms[cite: 650].

Executive Powers

    [cite_start]
  • Commander-in-Chief: Holds supreme command over the US military[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • Chief Executive: Responsible for enforcing federal laws and managing the federal bureaucracy[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • Power of Appointment: Appoints Cabinet members, ambassadors, and federal judges, subject to Senate approval[cite: 636, 645].

Legislative and Diplomatic Roles

    [cite_start]
  • Veto Power: Can reject bills passed by Congress[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • Treaty Making: Negotiates international treaties, which require ratification by the Senate[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • State of the Union: Delivers periodic messages to Congress regarding the national agenda[cite: 636, 645].
The President "shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed."

4. The US Supreme Court: Composition and Powers

[cite_start]

The Supreme Court is the highest judicial body in the United States[cite: 636, 645].

Composition

[cite_start]

It typically consists of a Chief Justice and eight Associate Justices[cite: 636, 645]. [cite_start]Justices are appointed for life to ensure judicial independence[cite: 636, 645].

Judicial Review

[cite_start]

The most significant power is Judicial Review, the authority to declare acts of the legislature or executive unconstitutional[cite: 636, 645, 650].

Jurisdiction

    [cite_start]
  • Original Jurisdiction: Hearing cases for the first time, such as disputes between states[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • Appellate Jurisdiction: Reviewing decisions from lower federal courts or state supreme courts[cite: 636, 645].

5. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tips

    [cite_start]
  • Compare and Contrast: Be prepared to compare the US Presidential system with the UK Parliamentary system[cite: 639, 648].
    • [cite_start]
  • Federalism Focus: Understand how power is divided; this is a core theme of US politics[cite: 636, 649].
  • Marbury v. Madison: Mention this case when discussing Judicial Review for extra credit.

Common Mistakes

  • Do not say the US President is a member of Congress. [cite_start]They are in separate branches[cite: 636, 645].
  • Avoid confusing the "Senate" with the "House." [cite_start]Remember their different term lengths and roles[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • Do not forget that the US has a written constitution, unlike the UK[cite: 634, 636, 644, 645].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long is a US Supreme Court Justice's term?

[cite_start]

A: They serve for life ("during good behavior")[cite: 636, 645].

Q: Who is the "Commander-in-Chief"?

[cite_start]

A: The President of the United States[cite: 636, 645].
