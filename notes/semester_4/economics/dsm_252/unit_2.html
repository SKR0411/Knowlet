1. Monopoly

[cite_start]

A monopoly exists when a single firm is the sole producer of a product with no close substitutes[cite: 278].

Core Characteristics:

    [cite_start]
  • Single Seller: The firm and the industry are synonymous[cite: 278].
    • [cite_start]
  • Price Maker: The firm exercises significant control over the market price[cite: 278].
    • [cite_start]
  • Barriers to Entry: Strong barriers prevent new competitors from entering the market[cite: 278].

Profit Maximization: A monopolist maximizes profit by producing at the level where Marginal Revenue (MR) equals Marginal Cost (MC). Because the demand curve is downward-sloping, the price is higher than MR.

2. Price Discrimination

[cite_start]

Price discrimination is the practice of charging different prices to different consumers for the same good, where price differences do not reflect cost differences[cite: 278].

Types of Price Discrimination:

  • First-Degree: Charging each consumer the maximum price they are willing to pay (captures all consumer surplus).
  • Second-Degree: Charging different prices based on the quantity consumed (e.g., volume discounts).
    • [cite_start]
  • Third-Degree: Charging different prices to different groups of consumers based on their price elasticity of demand (e.g., senior citizen discounts)[cite: 278].

3. Monopolistic Competition: Price and Output Determination

[cite_start]

Monopolistic competition involves many firms selling differentiated products that are close substitutes but not identical[cite: 278].

Equilibrium Analysis:

    [cite_start]
  • Product Differentiation: Firms use branding and quality to gain a limited degree of market power[cite: 278].
  • Short-Run: Firms can earn supernormal profits or incur losses, reaching equilibrium where MR = MC.
    • [cite_start]
  • Long-Run: Ease of entry and exit leads to a situation where firms earn only normal profits, though they produce at a point where Average Cost is still falling (excess capacity)[cite: 278].

4. Oligopoly

[cite_start]

Oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a few large firms that are strategically interdependent[cite: 278].

Key Features:

    [cite_start]
  • Interdependence: Each firm's decisions regarding price and output significantly impact its rivals[cite: 278].
    • [cite_start]
  • Barriers to Entry: Significant obstacles make it difficult for new firms to enter[cite: 278].
  • Non-Price Competition: Firms often compete through advertising and product improvements rather than price cuts to avoid price wars.

5. Government Intervention

[cite_start]

Governments often intervene in imperfect markets to address inefficiencies and protect consumer interests[cite: 273, 278].

  • Antitrust Laws: Regulations intended to prevent monopolies and encourage competition.
    • [cite_start]
  • Price Ceilings: Setting a maximum price to prevent firms from overcharging consumers[cite: 278].
  • Public Ownership: In some cases, the government may take control of an industry (e.g., utilities) to ensure fair service and pricing.

Exam Tips: Imperfect Competition

  • Comparative Advantage: Be prepared to compare Perfect Competition (Price = MC) with Monopoly (Price > MC).
    • [cite_start]
  • Profit Conditions: Remember that while monopolists can earn supernormal profit in the long run, firms in monopolistic competition cannot due to free entry[cite: 278].
  • Welfare Loss: Understand that imperfect competition often results in "Deadweight Loss" due to lower output and higher prices than a competitive market.
