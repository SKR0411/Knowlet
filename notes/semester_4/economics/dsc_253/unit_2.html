1. Random Experiment, Sample Spaces, and Events

[cite_start]

A Random Experiment is a process where the exact outcome cannot be predicted with certainty, even if the experiment is repeated under the same conditions[cite: 248].

Sample Space (S)

[cite_start]

The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment[cite: 248]. For example, in a coin toss, S = {Heads, Tails}.

Events

[cite_start]

An Event is a subset of the sample space[cite: 248].

    [cite_start]
  • Simple Event: An event with only one outcome. [cite: 248]
    • [cite_start]
  • Compound Event: An event with more than one outcome. [cite: 248]
    • [cite_start]
  • Mutually Exclusive Events: Events that cannot happen at the same time. [cite: 248]

2. Probability Axioms and Properties

[cite_start]

Modern probability is built on three fundamental axioms[cite: 248]:

    [cite_start]
  1. Non-negativity: The probability of any event A is always greater than or equal to zero (P(A) ≥ 0). [cite: 248]
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Certainty: The probability of the entire sample space S is 1 (P(S) = 1). [cite: 248]
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Additivity: For mutually exclusive events, the probability of their union is the sum of their individual probabilities. [cite: 248]
[cite_start]Property: The probability of an event always lies between 0 and 1 (0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1). [cite: 248]

3. Addition and Multiplication Theorems

Addition Theorem

[cite_start]

Used to find the probability of at least one of two events occurring (A or B)[cite: 249].

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)
[cite_start]

If A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A ∩ B) = 0, so P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B). [cite: 249]

Multiplication Theorem

[cite_start]

Used to find the probability of two events occurring together (A and B)[cite: 249].

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B|A)

4. Conditional Probability and Independence

[cite_start]

Conditional Probability is the probability of event A occurring given that event B has already occurred[cite: 248].

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), where P(B) > 0

Independence of Events

[cite_start]

Two events A and B are Independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other[cite: 248].

[cite_start]Condition for Independence: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B) [cite: 248]

5. Bayes Theorem

[cite_start]

Bayes Theorem describes the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions that might be related to the event[cite: 249]. It is a way to "reverse" conditional probabilities.

P(Ai|B) = [P(Ai) * P(B|Ai)] / [Σ P(Ak) * P(B|Ak)]
[cite_start]

In economics, this is often used to update forecasts as new data (B) becomes available for various economic scenarios (Ai)[cite: 243, 249].

Exam Corner: Common Pitfalls

  • Mutually Exclusive vs Independent: These are NOT the same. [cite_start]If events are mutually exclusive, they cannot be independent (if one happens, the other definitely won't). [cite: 248]
    • [cite_start]
  • Total Probability: Always check if the denominator in Bayes theorem (the total probability) sums up all possible mutually exclusive paths. [cite: 249]
    • [cite_start]
  • Formula Tip: P(A) + P(Not A) = 1. Using the complement is often easier than calculating complex events directly. [cite: 248]
