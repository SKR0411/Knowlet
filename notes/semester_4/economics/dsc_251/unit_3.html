1. Monopolistic Market: Price & Output Determination

Monopolistic competition is a market structure characterized by many firms selling differentiated products[cite: 188].

Short-run Equilibrium

In the short run, a firm under monopolistic competition behaves like a monopoly[cite: 188]. It produces at a level where Marginal Revenue (MR) = Marginal Cost (MC), provided price is greater than Average Variable Cost (AVC)[cite: 188].

Long-run Equilibrium

Due to the free entry and exit of firms, any supernormal profits are eroded in the long run[cite: 188]. Firms only earn normal profits where the demand curve (AR) is tangent to the Long-run Average Cost (LAC) curve[cite: 188].

2. Collusive vs Non-Collusive Oligopoly

Oligopoly is a market with a few large sellers[cite: 188]. The key feature is interdependence between firms[cite: 188].

  • Non-Collusive Oligopoly: Firms compete with each other and act independently based on their assumptions about rivals' reactions[cite: 188].
  • Collusive Oligopoly: Firms cooperate to reduce competition, often forming a cartel to maximize joint profits[cite: 188].

3. Cournot Model

The Cournot model assumes that each firm chooses its quantity produced, assuming the rival's output remains constant[cite: 188].

Equilibrium Point: The intersection of the Reaction Curves of the two firms[cite: 188].

In a duopoly (two firms), each firm ends up producing 1/3 of the total market capacity, leading to a total output of 2/3 of the competitive level[cite: 188].

4. Bertrand Model

The Bertrand model assumes that firms compete on price rather than quantity[cite: 188].

  • Firms assume the rival will keep its price constant[cite: 188].
  • If products are homogeneous, a price war occurs until Price = MC[cite: 188].
  • Outcome: Even with only two firms, the market reaches a competitive equilibrium result[cite: 188].

5. Stackelberg's Model

This is a "Leader-Follower" model[cite: 188]. One firm (the leader) moves first and chooses its output, anticipating how the follower will respond[cite: 188].

  • The Leader earns higher profits than in the Cournot model[cite: 188].
  • The Follower earns lower profits than in the Cournot model[cite: 188].

6. Kinked Demand Curve

Developed by Paul Sweezy, this explains price rigidity in oligopolies[cite: 188].

  • Assumption: If a firm increases its price, rivals will not follow. If it decreases its price, rivals will follow immediately[cite: 188].
  • This creates a "kink" in the demand curve at the prevailing price[cite: 188].
  • The MR curve becomes discontinuous, meaning MC can shift within a range without changing the equilibrium price or output[cite: 188].

7. Introduction to Game Theory (Duopoly)

Game theory provides a mathematical framework for analyzing strategic interactions[cite: 188].

Two-Person Zero-Sum Game

A game where the gain of one player is exactly equal to the loss of the other[cite: 188]. The sum of gains and losses is zero[cite: 188].

Key Term Definition
Strategy A complete plan of action for a player[cite: 188].
Payoff Matrix A table showing the gains/losses for each strategy combination[cite: 188].
Nash Equilibrium A situation where no player can benefit by changing strategy unilaterally[cite: 188].

Exam Tips

  • Compare the models: Competitive Output > Bertrand > Cournot > Stackelberg > Monopoly[cite: 188].
  • The Kinked demand curve explains why prices are stable, but not how the initial price was determined[cite: 188].
Common Pitfall: Do not confuse the Cournot model (Quantity competition) with the Bertrand model (Price competition). They lead to very different market outcomes[cite: 188].
