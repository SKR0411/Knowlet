2. Comparison of Executive Organs

 [cite_start]

This section explores how executive power is structured and exercised across these four diverse political models[cite: 640].

Types of Executive Systems

    [cite_start]
  • UK (Parliamentary): Dual executive with a titular head (The Crown) and a real head (Prime Minister) who is a member of the legislature[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • USA (Presidential): Single executive who is both head of state and government, elected separately from the legislature[cite: 636].
    • [cite_start]
  • Switzerland (Plural): A collegiate body (Federal Council) where seven members share equal power, avoiding the concentration of power in one person[cite: 637].
    • [cite_start]
  • China (State Council): An executive body that functions under the strict administrative and policy guidelines of the Communist Party[cite: 638].

3. Comparison of Legislative Organs

 [cite_start]

The structure and power of the legislative branch differ based on the principle of sovereignty[cite: 640].

Bicameralism vs. Unicameralism

  • UK Parliament: Bicameral; [cite_start]The House of Commons is dominant over the House of Lords[cite: 634].
  • US Congress: Bicameral; [cite_start]The Senate is exceptionally powerful compared to other upper houses, maintaining a balance with the House of Representatives[cite: 636].
  • Swiss Federal Assembly: Bicameral; [cite_start]Both the National Council and the Council of States have exactly equal powers[cite: 637].
  • National People's Congress (China): Unicameral; [cite_start]Technically the "highest organ of state power," though it follows Party leadership[cite: 638, 640].

4. Comparison of Judicial Systems

 [cite_start]

The role of courts in interpreting the law and checking other branches varies significantly[cite: 640].

  • USA: Strongest judicial review in the world; [cite_start]Supreme Court can strike down laws of Congress[cite: 636, 645].
    • [cite_start]
  • UK: Traditionally no judicial review of primary legislation due to Parliamentary Sovereignty[cite: 634, 644].
    • [cite_start]
  • Switzerland: Federal Supreme Court ensures Cantonal laws match Federal law, but its power to review federal laws is limited[cite: 637].
    • [cite_start]
  • China: Courts are specialized administrative organs; judicial review of the state's political direction does not exist[cite: 638, 647].

6. Direct Democracy and Federalism

 [cite_start]

The relationship between the center and the regions, and the role of the individual citizen[cite: 637, 639].

    [cite_start]
  • Switzerland: Unique for its Direct Democracy (Referendum and Initiative) and a highly decentralized federal structure where Cantons are powerful[cite: 637, 646].
    • [cite_start]
  • USA: Traditional Federalism; a strong division of powers between the Federal government and the States[cite: 636].
    • [cite_start]
  • UK & China: Both are Unitary States; though the UK has devolved power, the center remains legally sovereign[cite: 634, 638].

7. Exam Focus and FAQs

Exam Tips

  • Matrix Thinking: Always try to visualize these countries as a matrix. [cite_start]If a question asks for a comparison, pick a theme (e.g., Executive) and compare all four[cite: 648].
    • [cite_start]
  • Key Distinctions: Remember that Switzerland is the only "Direct Democracy" and "Plural Executive" in the group[cite: 646].
    • [cite_start]
  • Party Role: In any question about China, you MUST mention the Communist Party as the ultimate decision-maker[cite: 647].

Common Mistakes

  • Do not call the UK system "Presidential" or the US system "Parliamentary."
    • [cite_start]
  • Avoid stating that China has a "Separation of Powers"; it explicitly uses "Democratic Centralism"[cite: 638].
    • [cite_start]
  • Don't forget that the US Senate is an Upper House, unlike the unicameral NPC of China[cite: 636, 638].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Which of these systems is the most rigid?

[cite_start]

A: The USA and Switzerland are both rigid, but the US system is often considered the most difficult to formally amend[cite: 645].

Q: Is there any commonality between all four?

[cite_start]

A: All four possess a formal structure of government (Executive, Legislative, Judicial), though they operate under vastly different philosophies[cite: 649].

Mnemonics

U-U-S-C: The "Four Countries" - UK (Sovereignty), USA (Separation), Switzerland (Direct), China (Centralism).
