1. Characteristics of the UK Constitution

[cite_start]

The Constitution of the United Kingdom is unique because it is not contained in a single document[cite: 634]. [cite_start]It has evolved over centuries through a combination of statutes, conventions, and judicial decisions[cite: 634].

Uncodified Nature

Unlike the US or Indian constitutions, the UK constitution is uncodified. [cite_start]This means there is no supreme document that holds higher legal status than regular Acts of Parliament[cite: 634].

Evolutionary Growth

[cite_start]

The British political system is the result of gradual historical development rather than a revolutionary break from the past[cite: 634]. [cite_start]It has transitioned from an absolute monarchy to a constitutional monarchy and a parliamentary democracy[cite: 634].

Unitary State

[cite_start]

The UK is a unitary state, where supreme power resides with the central Parliament at Westminster[cite: 634]. [cite_start]While there has been significant devolution of power to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, legal sovereignty remains centralized[cite: 634].

Parliamentary Sovereignty

This is the cornerstone of the British Constitution. [cite_start]It implies that Parliament can make or unmake any law, and no person or body is recognized by the law as having a right to override or set aside the legislation of Parliament[cite: 634, 635].

Rule of Law

The constitution is governed by the principle that every citizen is subject to the law, including lawmakers themselves. [cite_start]This ensures that the exercise of arbitrary power is prevented[cite: 634].

2. The Crown: Powers and Functions

[cite_start]

In the UK, a distinction is made between the Monarch (the individual) and The Crown (the institution)[cite: 634]. [cite_start]The Crown represents the sum total of governmental powers and is the symbolic head of the executive, legislature, and judiciary[cite: 634].

Constitutional Monarchy

The UK is a Constitutional Monarchy. [cite_start]This means the Monarch "reigns but does not rule"[cite: 634]. [cite_start]The actual exercise of power is carried out by the Prime Minister and the Cabinet in the name of the Crown[cite: 634].

Executive Powers

    [cite_start]
  • Appointment of the Prime Minister and other ministers[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Granting of Royal Assent to bills passed by Parliament to make them law[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Representing the nation in international relations and signing treaties[cite: 634].

Legislative and Judicial Role

[cite_start]

The Monarch summons, prorogues, and dissolves Parliament[cite: 634]. [cite_start]Judicially, the Monarch is the "fountain of justice," although justice is administered by independent courts[cite: 634].

Formula: The King/Queen can do no wrong (Legal maxim implying the Monarch acts only on advice).

3. The Prime Minister: Role and Functions

[cite_start]

The Prime Minister (PM) is the real executive head of the UK government[cite: 634]. [cite_start]The office is based largely on constitutional convention rather than law[cite: 634].

Appointment and Position

[cite_start]

The Monarch appoints the leader of the party that has a majority in the House of Commons as the PM[cite: 634]. [cite_start]The PM is the "keystone of the Cabinet arch"[cite: 634].

Main Functions

    [cite_start]
  • Formation of the Cabinet: The PM selects ministers and has the power to dismiss them[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Presiding over Cabinet: The PM coordinates the policies and activities of various government departments[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Leader of the House of Commons: The PM acts as the primary spokesperson for the government in Parliament[cite: 634].
    • [cite_start]
  • Link between the Crown and Parliament: The PM keeps the Monarch informed about government affairs[cite: 634].

Powers of Patronage

[cite_start]

The PM recommends the appointment of senior judges, bishops, and life peers to the Monarch, giving the PM significant influence over various national institutions[cite: 634].

4. The Parliament: Structure and Powers

[cite_start]

The UK Parliament is a bicameral legislature, consisting of two houses: the House of Commons and the House of Lords[cite: 635].

House of Commons

[cite_start]

The House of Commons is the lower house but the most powerful one[cite: 635]. [cite_start]It consists of 650 Members of Parliament (MPs) elected by the people[cite: 635].

    [cite_start]
  • Controls the national finances (The "Power of the Purse")[cite: 635].
    • [cite_start]
  • Holds the government accountable through questioning and debates[cite: 635].
    • [cite_start]
  • Can force the government to resign through a Vote of No Confidence[cite: 635].

House of Lords

[cite_start]

The House of Lords is the upper house[cite: 635]. [cite_start]It consists of life peers, hereditary peers, and bishops[cite: 635].

    [cite_start]
  • Acts as a revising chamber to review and suggest amendments to legislation[cite: 635].
    • [cite_start]
  • Can delay non-money bills for up to one year but cannot block them permanently[cite: 635].

The Legislative Process

[cite_start]

For a bill to become an Act, it must pass through several stages in both houses (First Reading, Second Reading, Committee Stage, Report Stage, Third Reading) and receive Royal Assent[cite: 634, 635].

5. Exam-Oriented Preparation

Exam Tips

  • Understand Conventions: Many aspects of the UK system (like the PM's powers) are not written in law. Use the word "Convention" frequently in your answers.
  • Bicameralism: Be ready to explain the relationship between the two houses and why the House of Commons is more powerful.
  • Parliamentary Sovereignty: This is a favorite exam topic. Memorize the definition given by A.V. Dicey.

Common Mistakes

  • Do not say the Monarch has no power. They have "latent" powers that are exercised on advice.
  • Do not confuse the "Monarch" with the "Crown." The Crown is a permanent institution, while the Monarch is a person.
  • Avoid saying the UK has no constitution. It has an uncodified one, not none.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the "Royal Prerogative"?

A: These are powers historically held by the Monarch that are now exercised by the government, such as declaring war or issuing passports.

Q: Can the House of Lords stop a tax bill?

A: No. Under the Parliament Acts of 1911 and 1949, the House of Lords has no power to block "Money Bills."
