1. Platyhelminthes: General Characters and Taenia solium

[cite_start]Platyhelminthes are commonly known as flatworms[cite: 125]. [cite_start]They are triploblastic, acoelomate, and bilaterally symmetrical animals[cite: 125].

General Characters

    [cite_start]
  • Dorso-ventrally flattened body[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Organ-system level of organization[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Presence of hooks and suckers in parasitic forms for attachment[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Excretion and osmoregulation occur through specialized cells called flame cells[cite: 125].

Classification

[cite_start]

Classification up to classes includes[cite: 125]:

    [cite_start]
  • Turbellaria: Mostly free-living (e.g., Planaria)[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Trematoda: Ecto- or endoparasites, commonly called flukes (e.g., Fasciola)[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Cestoda: Endoparasites, commonly called tapeworms (e.g., Taenia)[cite: 125].

Life History of Taenia solium

[cite_start]Taenia solium (Pork Tapeworm) is a digenetic parasite[cite: 125].

    [cite_start]
  • Primary Host: Human (where the adult lives in the small intestine)[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Secondary Host: Pig (where larvae develop)[cite: 125].
    • [cite_start]
  • Infection: Humans are infected by eating "measly pork" containing cysticercus larvae[cite: 125].

2. Nemathelminthes: General Characters and Ascaris lumbricoides

[cite_start]Nemathelminthes (Aschelminthes) are known as roundworms[cite: 126]. [cite_start]They are triploblastic, bilaterally symmetrical, and pseudocoelomate[cite: 126].

General Characters

    [cite_start]
  • Long, cylindrical body tapering at both ends[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • The body is covered by a tough, resistant cuticle[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • Alimentary canal is complete with a well-developed muscular pharynx[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sexes are separate (dioecious), and females are often longer than males[cite: 126].

Classification

[cite_start]

Classified into classes such as Aphasmidia and Phasmidia based on the presence of phasmids (sensory organs)[cite: 126].

Life History of Ascaris lumbricoides

[cite_start]Ascaris lumbricoides is a monogenetic parasite (requires only one host)[cite: 126].

    [cite_start]
  • Host: Humans[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • Habitat: Small intestine[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • Infection: Occurs by swallowing embryonated eggs through contaminated food or water[cite: 126].
    • [cite_start]
  • Migration: Larvae undergo a complex migration through the liver, heart, and lungs before returning to the intestine[cite: 126].

3. Annelida: General Characters and Digestive System of Leech

[cite_start]Annelids are true coelomate animals characterized by a segmented body (metamerism)[cite: 127].

General Characters

    [cite_start]
  • Triploblastic, bilaterally symmetrical, and truly coelomate[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Locomotion by setae or parapodia[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Closed circulatory system (except in leeches)[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Excretion by nephridia[cite: 127].

Classification

[cite_start]

Classes include[cite: 127]:

    [cite_start]
  • Polychaeta: Many setae, parapodia present (e.g., Nereis)[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Oligochaeta: Few setae, no parapodia (e.g., Pheretima - Earthworm)[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Hirudinea: Fixed number of segments, suckers present (e.g., Hirudinaria - Leech)[cite: 127].

Digestive System of Leech

[cite_start]The leech (Hirudinaria granulosa) is a sanguivorous (blood-sucking) animal[cite: 127].

    [cite_start]
  • Mouth: Triradiate opening surrounded by the anterior sucker[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Pharynx: Muscular, used for sucking blood[cite: 127]. [cite_start]It contains salivary glands that secrete hirudin (an anticoagulant)[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Crop: The largest part of the alimentary canal with 10 pairs of diverticula for storing huge quantities of blood[cite: 127].
    • [cite_start]
  • Stomach and Intestine: Where slow digestion and absorption occur[cite: 127].
