1. Laboratory Objectives & Overview

[cite_start]The primary objective of this course is to provide students with hands-on experience in using thermal measurement instruments and estimating various physical parameters[cite: 448]. [cite_start]Students will learn to perform experiments related to thermal physics, analyze results, and report findings with accuracy[cite: 448, 473].

2. Mechanical Equivalent of Heat (J)

[cite_start]This experiment involves determining the value of 'J' using either Joule's Calorimeter or Callender and Barne’s constant flow method[cite: 451]. The goal is to establish the ratio of work done to the heat produced.

Principle: When work is converted into heat, the amount of heat produced is proportional to the work done.
W = J * Q

3. Thermal Conductivity of a Good Conductor (Searle’s Method)

[cite_start]The coefficient of thermal conductivity of a good conductor like Copper (Cu) is measured using Searle's Apparatus[cite: 452]. In this method, heat flows through a rod under steady-state conditions, and the temperature gradient along the rod is measured.

Key Procedure:

    [cite_start]
  • Steam is passed through one end of the rod while water flows through a coil at the other end[cite: 452].
    • [cite_start]
  • Temperature is measured at four points using thermometers once steady-state is reached[cite: 452].

4. Coefficient of Linear Expansion

[cite_start]The coefficient of linear expansion of a metal rod is determined using the optical lever method[cite: 453]. This method allows for the measurement of extremely small increases in length as the temperature of the rod increases.

α = (ΔL) / (L * ΔT)

5. Thermal Conductivity of a Bad Conductor (Lee’s Disc Method)

[cite_start]For bad conductors (like ebonite or glass), the Lee and Charlton’s disc method is used[cite: 454]. Since bad conductors do not allow for easy steady-state heat flow, this method uses a thin specimen disc between a steam chamber and a metallic base disc.

6. Platinum Resistance Thermometer (PRT)

[cite_start]This experiment focuses on determining the Temperature Coefficient of Resistance using a PRT[cite: 455]. It utilizes the principle that the electrical resistance of a platinum wire increases linearly with temperature.

Rt = R0(1 + αt)

7. Variation of Thermo-emf with Temperature

[cite_start]Students study the variation of Thermo-emf of a Thermocouple with the temperature difference between its two junctions[cite: 457]. This experiment explores the Seebeck effect and establishes the relationship between temperature and generated voltage.

8. Specific Heat of a Liquid (Method of Cooling)

[cite_start]The specific heat of a liquid is determined by comparing its rate of cooling to that of a known liquid (usually water) under identical conditions[cite: 458]. This is based on Newton’s Law of Cooling.

Lab Exam Focus Corner

Frequently Asked Questions

    [cite_start]
  • What is 'Steady State' in conductivity experiments?
    It is the state when the temperature at every point of the rod remains constant with time[cite: 473].
  • Why is Lee's Disc method used specifically for bad conductors?
    Standard methods like Searle's require high heat flow, which bad conductors cannot maintain. [cite_start]Lee's method uses a thin disc to minimize thermal resistance[cite: 454].

Common Mistakes

  • Parallax Error: Not reading thermometers at eye level, leading to inaccurate temperature gradients.
  • Steady State Assumption: Starting observations before the apparatus has truly reached thermal equilibrium.

Lab Tips

[cite_start]Tip: Always ensure the cooling calorimeter is well-polished to maintain constant emissivity when performing the method of cooling[cite: 458]. [cite_start]For PRT, make sure all connections are tight to avoid contact resistance[cite: 455].
