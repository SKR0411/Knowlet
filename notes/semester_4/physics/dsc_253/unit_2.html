1. Laboratory Objectives

[cite_start]The objective of this part is to provide students with hands-on knowledge and an overview of various instruments used in analog electronics[cite: 473]. [cite_start]Students will learn to perform experiments related to semiconductor devices and report findings accurately[cite: 448].

2. PN Junction & Zener Diode Characteristics

[cite_start]These experiments involve studying the Volt-Ampere (V-I) relationship of semiconductor diodes[cite: 460, 461].

    [cite_start]
  • PN Junction Diode: Students plot the current against the voltage in both forward and reverse bias to understand the unilateral behavior[cite: 460].
    • [cite_start]
  • Zener Diode: The focus is on the reverse breakdown region to understand its role as a voltage regulator[cite: 461].

Gain (Non-Inverting) = 1 + (Rf / Ri)

Lab Exam Focus Corner

Frequently Asked Questions

    [cite_start]
  • Why is voltage divider bias used for transistors?
    It provides the best stability for the Q-point against variations in temperature and transistor parameters[cite: 415].
    • [cite_start]
  • What is 'Knee Voltage' in a diode?
    It is the forward voltage at which the current starts to increase rapidly[cite: 1025].

Common Mistakes

  • Biasing Polarity: Connecting the Zener diode in forward bias when trying to observe regulation. [cite_start]Remember, regulation occurs in reverse breakdown[cite: 461].
  • Clipping: In Op-amp circuits, expecting a gain that results in an output voltage higher than the supply voltage (+Vcc / -Vee). The output will saturate.

Lab Tips

[cite_start]Tip: When plotting the frequency response of an amplifier, use semi-log graph paper (logarithmic scale for frequency) to clearly see the flat mid-band region and the roll-off at low and high frequencies[cite: 465].
