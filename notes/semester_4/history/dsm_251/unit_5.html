1. Aurangzeb: His Religious Policy

[cite_start]

Aurangzeb (1658–1707) reversed many of the liberal policies of his predecessors, particularly Akbar[cite: 1742]. His religious policy was characterized by a strict adherence to Islamic orthodoxy.

Key Actions and Impacts

    [cite_start]
  • Reintroduction of Jizya (1679): He brought back the poll tax on non-Muslim subjects, which alienated the Rajputs, Marathas, and Hindus[cite: 1742].
    • [cite_start]
  • Temple Destructions: He ordered the destruction of several prominent temples (e.g., Vishwanath temple in Kashi) during times of political rebellion, though he also issued land grants to others[cite: 1742].
    • [cite_start]
  • Strict Moral Codes: He appointed Muhtasibs to enforce Sharia law and regulate public moral conduct[cite: 1742].
    • [cite_start]
  • Impact: These policies created widespread resentment and contributed to the rise of regional resistances among the Sikhs, Jats, and Satnamis[cite: 1748].

2. Rise of Maratha Power under Shivaji

[cite_start]

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj emerged as a symbol of regional pride and resistance against Mughal dominance in the Deccan[cite: 1743].

Factors Behind the Rise

    [cite_start]
  • Geographical Advantage: The rugged terrain of the Western Ghats was perfect for Shivaji’s Ganimi Kava (Guerrilla Warfare)[cite: 1743].
    • [cite_start]
  • Military Innovation: Shivaji focused on building a strong infantry and a naval fleet, earning him the title "Father of the Indian Navy"[cite: 1743].
    • [cite_start]
  • Spirit of Swarajya: He successfully mobilized the Maratha peasantry and warrior clans under the ideal of Hindavi Swarajya (Self-rule)[cite: 1743].

3. Shivaji's Administration

[cite_start]

Shivaji’s administration was highly systematic and aimed at public welfare rather than just military conquest[cite: 1744].

The Ashta Pradhan Council

[cite_start]

He was assisted by a council of eight ministers known as the Ashta Pradhan[cite: 1744].

Revenue System

    [cite_start]
  • Chauth: A tax amounting to 1/4th of the land revenue, collected from neighboring territories in exchange for protection from Maratha raids[cite: 1744].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sardeshmukhi: An additional 10% tax claimed as the hereditary head (Deshmukh) of the Maratha nation[cite: 1744].

4. Downfall of the Mughal Empire

[cite_start]

The death of Aurangzeb in 1707 signaled the beginning of the rapid decline of the Mughal Empire[cite: 1745].

Primary Causes of Decline

    [cite_start]
  • Religious and Rajput Policies: Alienation of loyal Rajput allies and other groups weakened the state's foundation[cite: 1742, 1748].
    • [cite_start]
  • The Deccan Ulcer: Aurangzeb’s 25-year-long war in the Deccan depleted the imperial treasury and exhausted the military[cite: 1745].
    • [cite_start]
  • Weak Successors: Later Mughals were incompetent and became puppets in the hands of powerful nobles (the "Sayyid Brothers")[cite: 1745].
    • [cite_start]
  • Economic Crisis: Failure of the Jagirdari system and excessive taxation led to peasant distress[cite: 1745].
    • [cite_start]
  • Foreign Invasions: The raids by Nadir Shah (1739) and Ahmad Shah Abdali shattered the central authority and prestige of Delhi[cite: 1745].
Exam Corner: Tips & Tricks [cite_start]

Exam Tip: Always contrast Aurangzeb’s Deccan policy with Akbar's Rajput policy to show the shift in the empire’s stability[cite: 1748].

[cite_start]

Mnemonic for Ashta Pradhan: Remember P.A.S.S. (Peshwa, Amatya, Senapati, Sumant) to cover the top four ministers in Shivaji's council[cite: 1744].
