This unit examines the struggle between the conservative forces that sought to restore the old order in Europe after Napoleon's defeat and the rising tide of liberalism and nationalism [cite: 1768-1772].

1. Main Provisions of the Vienna Congress

[cite_start]

The Congress of Vienna (1814-1815) was a meeting of ambassadors from European states chaired by Austrian statesman Klemens von Metternich[cite: 1780]. [cite_start]Its primary goal was to provide a long-term peace plan for Europe by settling critical issues arising from the French Revolutionary Wars and the Napoleonic Wars[cite: 1780].

Key Principles and Provisions:

    [cite_start]
  • Legitimacy: Restoring the "legitimate" monarchs who had been deposed by Napoleon to their thrones, such as the Bourbons in France, Spain, and Naples[cite: 1780].
    • [cite_start]
  • Balance of Power: Ensuring that no single power could ever dominate Europe again by redistributing territories among the major powers[cite: 1780].
    • [cite_start]
  • Containment of France: Establishing strong "buffer states" around France to prevent future French aggression[cite: 1780].
    • [cite_start]
  • Compensation: Rewarding countries that had contributed most to Napoleon's defeat with new territories[cite: 1780].

2. The Concert of Europe

[cite_start]

The Concert of Europe was a system of dispute resolution adopted by the major conservative powers of Europe (Austria, Prussia, Russia, and Great Britain) to maintain the status quo and suppress revolutionary movements[cite: 1781].

    [cite_start]
  • Collective Security: The powers agreed to meet in periodically scheduled congresses to discuss and resolve European problems collectively[cite: 1781].
    • [cite_start]
  • Interventionism: Under the "Metternich System," the major powers claimed the right to intervene in other states to crush liberal or nationalistic revolts[cite: 1781].
    • [cite_start]
  • Decline: The system eventually weakened due to conflicting interests among the powers, particularly Britainâ€™s move toward a policy of non-intervention[cite: 1781].

3. July Revolution (1830): Causes & Effects

[cite_start]

The July Revolution in France, also known as the "Three Glorious Days," saw the overthrow of the Bourbon King Charles X and the ascent of his cousin Louis-Philippe[cite: 1782].

Causes:

    [cite_start]
  • The July Ordinances: Charles X issued strict decrees that suspended the liberty of the press, dissolved the Chamber of Deputies, and reduced the number of voters[cite: 1782].
    • [cite_start]
  • Reactionary Policies: Attempting to restore the absolute power and privileges of the nobility and clergy[cite: 1782].

Effects:

    [cite_start]
  • Establishment of Constitutional Monarchy: Louis-Philippe became the "Citizen King" under a revised constitution[cite: 1782].
    • [cite_start]
  • Spread of Revolution: Sparked similar revolts across Europe, most notably leading to the independence of Belgium from the Netherlands[cite: 1782].

4. February Revolution (1848): Causes & Effects

[cite_start]

The February Revolution ended the constitutional monarchy of Louis-Philippe and led to the creation of the French Second Republic[cite: 1783].

Causes:

    [cite_start]
  • Economic Distress: A severe industrial crisis and crop failures led to unemployment and hunger among the working class[cite: 1783].
    • [cite_start]
  • Political Corruption: The government's refusal to extend voting rights to the middle and working classes[cite: 1783].
    • [cite_start]
  • The Reform Banquets: When the government banned a large political gathering (banquet), riots broke out in Paris[cite: 1783].

Effects:

    [cite_start]
  • The "Springtime of Peoples": Sparked a wave of revolutions across Europe, including in Germany, Italy, and the Austrian Empire[cite: 1783].
    • [cite_start]
  • Rise of Louis-Napoleon: Eventually led to the election of Louis-Napoleon Bonaparte (later Napoleon III) as President[cite: 1783].
Exam Tip: Remember that the Revolution of 1830 was primarily a middle-class victory for constitutional monarchy, whereas the 1848 Revolution saw a greater involvement of the working class and demanded republicanism and socialism.
Common Pitfall: Do not confuse the July Revolution (1830) with the February Revolution (1848). One changed the king; the other abolished the monarchy entirely.
