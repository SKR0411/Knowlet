Unit 1 of HISDSM252 provides a foundational understanding of the transformation of Europe from absolute monarchies to modern nation-states, triggered by the cataclysmic events in France starting in 1789[cite: 1771, 1772].

1. Causes of the French Revolution

[cite_start]

The revolution was not the result of a single event but a combination of long-standing systemic issues[cite: 1774].

Political and Social Causes

    [cite_start]
  • Despotic Monarchy: The Bourbon kings (Louis XIV, XV, and XVI) exercised absolute power without accountability to the people[cite: 1774].
    • [cite_start]
  • The Three Estates: French society was divided into the Clergy (First Estate), Nobility (Second Estate), and the Commoners (Third Estate)[cite: 1774].
    • [cite_start]
  • Social Inequality: The first two estates owned most of the land and were exempt from taxes, while the Third Estate bore the entire financial burden[cite: 1774].

Economic and Intellectual Causes

    [cite_start]
  • Financial Bankruptcy: Wars and royal extravagance left France in deep debt[cite: 1774].
    • [cite_start]
  • Famine: Successive crop failures led to bread shortages and widespread hunger[cite: 1774].
    • [cite_start]
  • Enlightenment Philosophers: Thinkers like Montesquieu, Voltaire, and Rousseau challenged the "Divine Right of Kings" and inspired the public with ideas of liberty and equality[cite: 1774].

2. Results of the French Revolution

[cite_start]

The 1789 revolution fundamentally altered the course of human history[cite: 1775].

    [cite_start]
  • End of Absolutism: The principle of popular sovereignty replaced the absolute power of the monarch[cite: 1775].
    • [cite_start]
  • Declaration of Rights: The "Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen" established basic civil liberties[cite: 1775].
    • [cite_start]
  • Abolition of Feudalism: The centuries-old feudal system and its privileges were dismantled[cite: 1775].
    • [cite_start]
  • Rise of Nationalism: The revolution fostered a sense of national identity that soon spread across the European continent[cite: 1775].

3. The Continental System of Napoleon

[cite_start]

Failing to defeat Britain militarily at sea (Battle of Trafalgar), Napoleon launched a massive economic war known as the Continental System[cite: 1776].

Objectives and Mechanisms

    [cite_start]
  • Economic Blockade: Napoleon issued the Berlin (1806) and Milan (1807) decrees, forbidding European nations from trading with Great Britain[cite: 1776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Goal: To destroy Britain's commercial economy and force it to sue for peace[cite: 1776].

Why it Failed

    [cite_start]
  • British Naval Supremacy: Britain launched a counter-blockade, cutting off European access to essential colonial goods like sugar and coffee[cite: 1776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Smuggling: European nations, dependent on British goods, engaged in massive smuggling despite Napoleon's threats[cite: 1776].
    • [cite_start]
  • Internal Resentment: The blockade caused high prices and scarcity within Europe, turning Napoleon's allies and subjects against him[cite: 1776].
