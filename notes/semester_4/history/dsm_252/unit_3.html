1. Unification of Italy

[cite_start]

The unification of Italy, also known as the Risorgimento (Resurgence), was a movement to liberate the Italian peninsula from foreign (primarily Austrian) control and unify several states under one government[cite: 1787].

Key Figures

    [cite_start]
  • Giuseppe Mazzini: The "Soul" of unification; he founded Young Italy to inspire national consciousness[cite: 1787].
    • [cite_start]
  • Count Camillo di Cavour: The "Brain" of unification; the Prime Minister of Piedmont-Sardinia who used diplomacy and alliances (specifically with France) to expel Austria[cite: 1787].
    • [cite_start]
  • Giuseppe Garibaldi: The "Sword" of unification; leader of the Red Shirts who conquered the Kingdom of the Two Sicilies in the south[cite: 1787].
    • [cite_start]
  • Victor Emmanuel II: The first King of a unified Italy[cite: 1787].

The Process

    [cite_start]
  1. War with Austria (1859): Piedmont-Sardinia, with French help, liberated Lombardy[cite: 1787].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Southern Conquest (1860): Garibaldi’s "Thousand" Red Shirts invaded Sicily and Naples[cite: 1787].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Final Integration: Venetia was added in 1866, and Rome was annexed in 1870, completing the unification[cite: 1787].

2. Unification of Germany

[cite_start]

Prussia led the movement to unify the German states into a single empire under the leadership of King William I and his Chancellor, Otto von Bismarck[cite: 1786].

Bismarck’s Strategy: "Blood and Iron"

3. Home Policy of Otto von Bismarck

[cite_start]

After unification in 1871, Bismarck sought to consolidate the new empire and suppress internal divisions[cite: 1788].

  • Kulturkampf (Struggle for Culture): An attempt to weaken the influence of the Catholic Church by subjecting it to state control. [cite_start]It was largely unsuccessful and later abandoned[cite: 1788].
    • [cite_start]
  • Anti-Socialist Laws: Bismarck feared the rise of socialism and banned socialist meetings and newspapers, though he simultaneously introduced social welfare to win over workers[cite: 1788].
    • [cite_start]
  • Social Welfare System: To counter socialist appeal, he introduced the world’s first national sickness insurance, accident insurance, and old-age pensions[cite: 1788].

4. Foreign Policy of Otto von Bismarck

[cite_start]

Bismarck’s foreign policy aimed to maintain European peace and isolate France to protect the newly unified Germany[cite: 1788].

    [cite_start]
  • Alliance System: He created a complex web of alliances, including the Three Emperors' League (Germany, Austria, Russia) and the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria, Italy)[cite: 1788].
    • [cite_start]
  • Berlin Congress (1878): Bismarck acted as an "honest broker" to resolve the Balkan crisis and prevent a general European war[cite: 1792].
    • [cite_start]
  • Maintenance of Status Quo: He declared Germany a "saturated power" with no further territorial ambitions in Europe[cite: 1788].
Exam Tip: When discussing Bismarck, always use the term Realpolitik. It refers to a system of politics based on practical rather than moral or ideological considerations.

5. Exam Focus: Frequently Asked Questions

  • Q: What was the Ems Telegram?
    A: A dispatch edited by Bismarck to provoke France into declaring war on Prussia, which served as the immediate cause of the Franco-Prussian War.
  • Q: Why did Bismarck introduce social welfare?
    A: To "kill socialism with kindness"—meaning he wanted to provide for the workers so they wouldn't feel the need to support the Socialist Party.
  • Q: Who were the "Red Shirts"?
    A: The volunteer army of Giuseppe Garibaldi that played a crucial role in unifying Southern Italy.
