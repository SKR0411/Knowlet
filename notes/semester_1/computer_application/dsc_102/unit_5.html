Basic Concepts of Graph Theory

[cite_start]

A graph consists of a set of vertices (nodes) and edges that connect them[cite: 82].

    [cite_start]
  • Incidence: An edge is incident to the vertices it connects[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Degree: The number of edges incident to a vertex[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Sub-graphs: A graph whose vertices and edges are subsets of another graph[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Union of Graphs: A graph containing all vertices and edges from two or more graphs[cite: 82].

Isomorphism and Homomorphism

    [cite_start]
  • Isomorphism: Two graphs are isomorphic if there is a one-to-one correspondence between their vertices and edges that preserves adjacency[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Homomorphism: A mapping between two graphs that preserves the structure but does not necessarily require a one-to-one correspondence[cite: 82].

Types of Graphs

[cite_start]

Graphs can be categorized by their specific properties and constraints[cite: 82]:

    [cite_start]
  • Multigraphs: Graphs that can have multiple edges between the same two vertices[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Weighted Graphs: Graphs where each edge is assigned a numerical value or "weight"[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Planar Graphs: Graphs that can be drawn in a plane such that no edges cross each other[cite: 82].

Walks, Paths, and Circuits

[cite_start]

These terms describe movement within a graph[cite: 82]:

    [cite_start]
  • Walk: A sequence of vertices and edges[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Path: A walk where all vertices (and thus all edges) are distinct[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Circuit: A closed walk where the start and end vertices are the same[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Connectedness: A graph is connected if there is a path between every pair of vertices[cite: 82].

Eulerian and Hamiltonian Graphs

    [cite_start]
  • Eulerian Graph: A graph containing a circuit that uses every edge exactly once[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Hamiltonian Graph: A graph containing a circuit that visits every vertex exactly once[cite: 82].

Complete, Regular, and Bipartite Graphs

    [cite_start]
  • Complete Graph: A graph where every pair of distinct vertices is connected by a unique edge[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Regular Graph: A graph where every vertex has the same degree[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Bipartite Graph: A graph whose vertices can be divided into two disjoint sets such that no two vertices within the same set are adjacent[cite: 82].

Cut-sets and Cut-vertices

    [cite_start]
  • Cut-vertex: A vertex whose removal increases the number of connected components in the graph[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Cut-set: A set of edges whose removal disconnects the graph[cite: 82].

Exam Focus & Tips

    [cite_start]
  • Exam Tip: To prove a graph is Eulerian, check if all vertices have an even degree[cite: 82].
  • Common Mistake: Confusing Eulerian (Edges) with Hamiltonian (Vertices). [cite_start]Euler = Edges[cite: 82].
    • [cite_start]
  • Practical Tip: Weighted graphs are frequently used in shortest-path problems like GPS navigation[cite: 82].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What makes a graph "Planar"?
[cite_start]A: It can be drawn on a flat surface without any edges intersecting[cite: 82].

Q: What is a Bipartite graph?
[cite_start]A: A graph where vertices are split into two groups, and edges only connect vertices from different groups[cite: 82].
