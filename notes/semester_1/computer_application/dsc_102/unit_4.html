Basic Concepts of Tree

[cite_start]

In discrete mathematics, a tree is a connected graph that contains no circuits (cycles)[cite: 59, 77]. It is one of the most important structures used to represent hierarchical relationships.

[Image of a basic tree graph structure]
    [cite_start]
  • Connectedness: There is a path between every pair of vertices[cite: 59, 77].
    • [cite_start]
  • Acyclic: There are no loops or closed paths in the structure[cite: 59, 77].

Properties of Trees

[cite_start]

Trees have several mathematical properties that distinguish them from other types of graphs[cite: 59, 77]:

    [cite_start]
  • Edge-Vertex Relationship: A tree with n vertices always has exactly n - 1 edges[cite: 59, 77].
    • [cite_start]
  • Unique Path: There is exactly one unique path between any two vertices in a tree[cite: 59, 77].
    • [cite_start]
  • Minimally Connected: Removing any edge from a tree results in a disconnected graph (it becomes a "forest")[cite: 59, 77].

Pendant Vertices and Centre of a Tree

[cite_start]

Special vertices in a tree help define its structure and shape[cite: 59, 77].

Pendant Vertices

[cite_start]

A pendant vertex (also known as a leaf) is a vertex of degree 1[cite: 59, 77]. [cite_start]Every tree with more than one vertex has at least two pendant vertices[cite: 59, 77].

Centre of a Tree

[cite_start]

The centre of a tree consists of one or two vertices that have the minimum maximum distance to all other vertices in the tree[cite: 59, 77]. [cite_start]This is found by repeatedly removing all pendant vertices until only one or two remain[cite: 59, 77].

Rooted Binary Trees

[cite_start]

A rooted tree is a tree in which one specific vertex is designated as the root[cite: 59, 77].

Rooted Binary Tree

[cite_start]

A binary tree is a rooted tree where every internal vertex has at most two children[cite: 59, 77]. [cite_start]Each child is typically designated as either a "left child" or a "right child"[cite: 59, 77].

Complete and Extended Binary Trees

[cite_start]

Depending on how the nodes are filled, binary trees are categorized into specific types[cite: 59, 77]:

    [cite_start]
  • Complete Binary Tree: A binary tree in which every level, except possibly the last, is completely filled, and all nodes are as far left as possible[cite: 59, 77].
    • [cite_start]
  • Extended Binary Tree: Also known as a 2-tree, it is a binary tree where every node has either zero or two children[cite: 59, 77]. [cite_start]Empty spots are often represented by special "external nodes"[cite: 59, 77].

Exam Focus & Tips

  • Exam Tip: Remember the formula Edges = V - 1. [cite_start]If a question asks for edges in a tree with 15 vertices, the answer is 14[cite: 59, 77].
  • Common Mistake: Confusing a general tree with a binary tree. [cite_start]A general tree can have any number of children per node, whereas a binary tree is limited to two[cite: 59, 77].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mnemonic: "No Cycles, No Problems" - A tree must never have a loop[cite: 59, 77].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a pendant vertex?
[cite_start]A: A vertex with a degree of 1, effectively a "leaf" of the tree[cite: 59, 77].

Q: How many roots does a rooted tree have?
[cite_start]A: Exactly one[cite: 59, 77].
