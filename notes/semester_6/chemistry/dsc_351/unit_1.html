1. Definition of Nanoparticles

[cite_start]

Nanoscience and nanotechnology involve the study and manipulation of matter at the atomic and molecular scale[cite: 1111, 1112].

[cite_start]Nanoparticle: A microscopic particle with at least one dimension less than 100 nanometres[cite: 1112].
[cite_start]

The emergence of this field presents both unique challenges and opportunities due to the drastic change in properties when transitioning from bulk materials to the nanoscale[cite: 1112].

3. New Forms of Carbon (CNT to Graphene)

[cite_start]

Carbon at the nanoscale exhibits extraordinary mechanical, electrical, and thermal properties[cite: 1112].

    [cite_start]
  • Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs): Cylindrical molecules consisting of rolled-up sheets of single-layer carbon atoms (graphene)[cite: 1112].
    • [cite_start]
  • Graphene: A single, two-dimensional layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice[cite: 1112].
    • [cite_start]
  • Metal Oxides and Semiconductors: These also form nanostructures like nanowires and quantum dots with unique optical and electronic traits[cite: 1112].

4. Size and Surface Effects

[cite_start]

The transition from micro/macro scale to the nano scale introduces two fundamental effects[cite: 1112]:

Large Surface to Volume Ratio

[cite_start]

As the size of a particle decreases, a much higher percentage of its atoms are located on the surface rather than in the bulk[cite: 1112]. [cite_start]This significantly increases the surface energy and chemical reactivity[cite: 1112].

Quantum Size Effects

[cite_start]

When the size of a material reaches the nanoscale, its electronic and optical properties are governed by quantum mechanics[cite: 1112]. [cite_start]For example, the band gap of semiconductors increases as the particle size decreases[cite: 1112].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the primary difference between bulk and nanomaterials?
[cite_start]A: Bulk materials have constant physical properties regardless of size, while nanomaterials exhibit size-dependent properties due to surface and quantum effects[cite: 1112].

Q: Define a Quantum Dot.
[cite_start]A: It is a 0D semiconductor nanostructure that confines electrons in all three spatial dimensions, leading to discrete energy levels[cite: 1112].

Q: Why are nanomaterials more reactive?
[cite_start]A: Due to the very large surface-to-volume ratio, leaving many surface atoms with "dangling bonds" ready to participate in reactions[cite: 1112].
