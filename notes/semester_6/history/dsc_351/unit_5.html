1. 2nd World War: Causes and Consequences

[cite_start]

The Second World War (1939-1945) was a global conflict that pitted the Allied Powers against the Axis Powers[cite: 722].

Causes

    [cite_start]
  • Failures of the Treaty of Versailles: The harsh terms imposed on Germany created long-term resentment[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Rise of Totalitarianism: The aggressive expansionist policies of Hitler (Germany), Mussolini (Italy), and Imperial Japan[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Failure of the League of Nations: Its inability to act against aggression in Manchuria and Abyssinia[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Immediate Cause: The German invasion of Poland on September 1, 1939[cite: 722].

Consequences

    [cite_start]
  • Human Loss: Over 60 million people died, including millions in the Holocaust[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Rise of Superpowers: The emergence of the USA and the USSR as the dominant world powers, leading to the Cold War[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Decolonization: The weakening of European powers (Britain, France) led to the independence of colonies in Asia and Africa[cite: 722].
    • [cite_start]
  • Establishment of the UNO: A new international organization was formed to maintain peace[cite: 722, 724].

2. War-time Conferences

[cite_start]

During the war, leaders of the Allied powers (the "Big Three") met multiple times to plan military strategy and decide the future of the post-war world[cite: 723].

[cite_start]
Conference Participants Key Decisions
Yalta Conference (1945) Stalin, Roosevelt, Churchill Agreed to divide Germany into occupation zones; [cite_start]USSR promised to enter war against Japan[cite: 723].
Potsdam Conference (1945) Stalin, Truman, AttleeFinalized the administration of Germany; issued an ultimatum to Japan for unconditional surrender[cite: 723].

3. UNO: Aims, Organizations, and Achievements

[cite_start]

The United Nations Organization (UNO) was established on October 24, 1945, to succeed the failed League of Nations[cite: 724].

Aims and Objectives

    [cite_start]
  • Maintain International Peace: To prevent future wars through collective security[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • Human Rights: To promote and encourage respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • International Cooperation: To achieve cooperation in solving economic, social, and cultural problems[cite: 724].

Main Organizations

    [cite_start]
  • General Assembly: The main deliberative assembly where all member nations have a seat[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • Security Council: Responsible for maintaining peace; consists of 5 permanent members with veto power[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • Secretariat: The administrative body headed by the Secretary-General[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • International Court of Justice (ICJ): The principal judicial organ[cite: 724].

Achievements

    [cite_start]
  • Conflict Resolution: Successfully mediated in various regional conflicts (e.g., Korea, Suez Canal)[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • Humanitarian Work: Massive contributions to global health (WHO) and child welfare (UNICEF)[cite: 724].
    • [cite_start]
  • Environmental Protection: Leading international efforts to combat climate change[cite: 724].

4. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tip: Always distinguish between the League of Nations (Post-WWI) and the United Nations (Post-WWII). [cite_start]The UN's inclusion of a Security Council with a military mandate is its key difference[cite: 700, 724].

Frequently Asked Question: Why did the League of Nations fail but the UNO succeeded?
[cite_start]A: The UNO included all major powers (including the USA) and had a more robust enforcement mechanism through the Security Council[cite: 700, 724].

Warning: Don't forget the role of War-time Conferences. [cite_start]They weren't just about winning the war; they were the blueprints for the Cold War divide[cite: 723].
