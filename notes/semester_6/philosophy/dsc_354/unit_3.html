This unit examines the various perspectives on how religion originated in human history, focusing primarily on Anthropological and Psychological models[cite: 486].

1. Anthropological Theory

[cite_start]

Anthropological theories seek to explain the origin of religion by studying the development of human cultures and primitive belief systems[cite: 486].

Animism (E.B. Tylor)

Edward Burnett Tylor proposed that religion began with Animism—the belief that spirits or souls inhabit both living and non-living things.

    [cite_start]
  • Origin in Dreams: Primitive humans observed that in dreams, they could travel or interact with the dead, leading them to believe in a separate "soul" or spirit[cite: 646].
    • [cite_start]
  • Extension to Nature: This belief was extended to natural phenomena like trees, rivers, and mountains, which were thought to be controlled by spirits[cite: 646].
  • Evolution: Over time, these numerous spirits were organized into a hierarchy, eventually leading to Polytheism and then Monotheism.

Totemism and Magic

Other anthropologists like James Frazer focused on the transition from Magic to Religion. [cite_start]They argued that when humans realized they could not control nature through magic rituals (taboos and myths), they turned to supernatural beings (Gods) for help[cite: 647, 648].

2. Psychological Theory

[cite_start]

Psychological theories argue that religion originates from the internal mental and emotional needs of the human psyche rather than external observations of nature[cite: 486].

Religion as Wish-Fulfillment (Sigmund Freud)

[cite_start]

Freud viewed religion as a "collective neurosis" or a wish-fulfillment mechanism[cite: 517].

  • Infantile Helplessness: Just as a child looks to a father for protection, adults create a "Heavenly Father" (God) to cope with the frightening and unpredictable forces of nature and the inevitability of death.
    • [cite_start]
  • Guilt and Repression: Freud also linked the origin of religion to primal guilt associated with the "Oedipus Complex" and the repression of instincts[cite: 517].

The Collective Unconscious (Carl Jung)

Unlike Freud, Carl Jung viewed religion more positively. [cite_start]He argued that religious symbols and ideas arise from the Collective Unconscious[cite: 518].

    [cite_start]
  • Archetypes: These are universal, inherited patterns of thought (like the "Mother," the "Hero," or the "Divine") that manifest in religious myths and rituals[cite: 518].
  • Individuation: Religion serves as a tool for "individuation"—the process of integrating the self to achieve psychological wholeness.

4. Exam Focus: Tips & FAQs

Exam Tip: When discussing Freud, use the term "Universal Obsessional Neurosis" to describe his view of religion. [cite_start]For Tylor, emphasize "Animism" as the most primitive form of religious belief[cite: 646, 517].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is the Anthropological theory purely historical?
A: Primarily, yes. [cite_start]It looks at the "childhood of humanity" to see how early humans developed rituals and myths to cope with their environment[cite: 539, 648].

Q: Does the Psychological theory claim God doesn't exist?
A: Not necessarily. [cite_start]It explains why humans believe in God, but the existence of God is a separate metaphysical question that psychologists often leave to philosophers[cite: 1104].
