1. Fate of Germ Layers - Fate Map and Organiser Concept

[cite_start]

Following gastrulation, the three primary germ layers differentiate into specific tissues and organs[cite: 1066].

Fate Map

[cite_start]

A Fate Map is a diagrammatic representation showing the future destination of various cells or areas of a blastula or early gastrula[cite: 1066].

  • Ectoderm: Gives rise to the nervous system, epidermis of the skin, and sense organs.
  • Mesoderm: Develops into the circulatory system, muscles, bones, and excretory system.
  • Endoderm: Forms the lining of the digestive tract, respiratory system, and associated glands (liver, pancreas).

Organiser Concept

[cite_start]

The Organiser (specifically the Spemann-Mangold Organiser in amphibians) refers to a region of the embryo that can induce the development of the primary embryonic axis and coordinate the differentiation of surrounding tissues[cite: 1066].

2. Extra-embryonic Membranes in Birds and Mammals

[cite_start]

These are specialized membranes that develop outside the embryo proper to provide protection, nutrition, and waste management[cite: 1067].

Membrane Primary Function

[cite_start]

Implantation is the process by which the blastocyst attaches to and embeds within the endometrium of the mother's uterus[cite: 1068].

4. Placenta: Structure, Types, and Functions

[cite_start]

The placenta is a temporary organ formed by the association of maternal and fetal tissues for physiological exchange[cite: 1069].

Structure and Functions

    [cite_start]
  • Nutrition: Transfer of nutrients from mother to fetus[cite: 1069].
    • [cite_start]
  • Respiration: Exchange of Oxygen and Carbon dioxide[cite: 1069].
    • [cite_start]
  • Excretion: Removal of fetal metabolic wastes[cite: 1069].
    • [cite_start]
  • Endocrine: Secretes hormones like hCG, Estrogen, and Progesterone to maintain pregnancy[cite: 1069].

Classification of Placenta

    [cite_start]
  1. Based on Extra-embryonic membranes: Yolk sac placenta or Chorio-allantoic placenta[cite: 1069].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Based on Distribution of Villi: Diffuse, Cotyledonary, Intermediate, or Discoid[cite: 1069].
  3. Based on Histology (Maternal-Fetal Barriers):
    • Epitheliochorial: Least invasive (e.g., Pig).
    • Endotheliochorial: Villi contact maternal blood vessel endothelium (e.g., Carnivores).
      • [cite_start]
    • Hemochorial: Villi are bathed directly in maternal blood (e.g., Humans)[cite: 1069].

Exam-Oriented Focus

[cite_start]Exam Tip: For classifications of the placenta, always remember that Humans have a Discoid, Hemochorial, and Deciduate placenta[cite: 1069]. These terms are frequently asked in objective and short-answer questions.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

  1. Q: What are the three germ layers?
    [cite_start]A: Ectoderm, Mesoderm, and Endoderm[cite: 1066].
  2. Q: Which extra-embryonic membrane protects against shock?
    [cite_start]A: The Amnion[cite: 1067].
  3. Q: What is a "Hemochorial" placenta?
    [cite_start]A: A type of placenta where fetal villi are in direct contact with maternal blood[cite: 1069].
Common Mistake: Do not confuse the Yolk Sac in birds and humans. [cite_start]In humans, the yolk sac is vestigial (does not contain yolk) but remains important for early blood cell formation[cite: 1067].

Mnemonics

  • E-M-E: Ectoderm (External - Skin), Mesoderm (Middle - Muscles/Blood), Endoderm (Entirely inside - Gut).
