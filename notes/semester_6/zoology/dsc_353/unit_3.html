1. Types of Eggs and Egg Membranes

[cite_start]

Animal eggs vary significantly based on the amount and distribution of yolk, which determines the subsequent pattern of development[cite: 1061].

Classification based on Yolk Quantity

    [cite_start]
  • Alecithal: Eggs with negligible yolk (e.g., placental mammals)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Microlecithal: Small amount of yolk (e.g., Amphioxus, sea urchin)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mesolecithal: Moderate amount of yolk (e.g., amphibians)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Macrolecithal/Megalecithal: Large amount of yolk (e.g., birds, reptiles)[cite: 1061].

Classification based on Yolk Distribution

    [cite_start]
  • Isolecithal: Yolk is uniformly distributed throughout the cytoplasm[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Telolecithal: Yolk is concentrated at one pole (Vegetal pole)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Centrolecithal: Yolk is concentrated in the center (common in insects)[cite: 1061].

Egg Membranes

[cite_start]

Membranes protect the embryo and facilitate physiological exchange[cite: 1061].

    [cite_start]
  • Primary Membrane: Produced by the oocyte itself (e.g., Vitelline membrane, Zona pellucida in mammals)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Secondary Membrane: Produced by the ovary follicle cells (e.g., Corona radiata)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • Tertiary Membrane: Produced by the oviduct or other maternal organs (e.g., Albumin, shell of a bird's egg)[cite: 1061].

2. Fertilization: External and Internal

[cite_start]

Fertilization is the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote[cite: 1062].

Process and Changes in Gametes

    [cite_start]
  • External Fertilization: Occurs outside the body, usually in water (e.g., frogs, most fish)[cite: 1062].
    • [cite_start]
  • Internal Fertilization: Occurs inside the female reproductive tract (e.g., mammals, birds)[cite: 1062].
    • [cite_start]
  • Capacitation: Physiological maturation of sperm within the female tract required for fertilization[cite: 1057, 1059].
    • [cite_start]
  • Acrosome Reaction: Release of enzymes to penetrate the egg membranes[cite: 1062].

Blocks to Polyspermy

[cite_start]

Mechanisms to ensure only one sperm fertilizes the egg[cite: 1062].

    [cite_start]
  1. Fast Block: Rapid change in the membrane potential of the egg (depolarization) immediately after sperm contact[cite: 1062].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Slow Block (Cortical Reaction): Release of cortical granules that harden the vitelline membrane into a fertilization envelope[cite: 1062].

3. Planes and Patterns of Cleavage and Blastulation

[cite_start]

Cleavage is a series of rapid mitotic divisions where the zygote is converted into a multicellular blastula[cite: 1063].

Planes of Cleavage

    [cite_start]
  • Meridional: Plane passes through the animal-vegetal axis[cite: 1063].
    • [cite_start]
  • Equatorial: Plane passes at right angles to the animal-vegetal axis, dividing it into two halves[cite: 1063].

Patterns of Cleavage

[cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start]
Type Description Example
HoloblasticComplete division of the egg[cite: 1063].Mammals, Amphibians[cite: 1063].
MeroblasticIncomplete division (due to high yolk)[cite: 1063].Birds, Reptiles, Fishes[cite: 1063].

Blastulation

[cite_start]

The stage following cleavage where a fluid-filled cavity called the blastocoel is formed within the embryo (now called a blastula)[cite: 1063].

4. Early Development of Chick up to Gastrulation

[cite_start]

Bird eggs are highly telolecithal, leading to a specific pattern of development[cite: 1064].

    [cite_start]
  • Cleavage: Meroblastic discoidal cleavage occurs only in the small disc of cytoplasm (blastodisc)[cite: 1064].
    • [cite_start]
  • Blastula Stage: Formation of the blastoderm, which separates into two layers: the epiblast (upper) and hypoblast (lower)[cite: 1064].
    • [cite_start]
  • Primitive Streak: A thickening of the epiblast that serves as the site for the beginning of gastrulation[cite: 1101].
    • [cite_start]
  • Gastrulation: The process of cell migration where the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, and endoderm) are formed[cite: 1064].

Exam-Oriented Focus

Exam Tip: Be ready to explain the relationship between yolk quantity and cleavage type. [cite_start]High yolk (macrolecithal) always leads to incomplete (meroblastic) cleavage[cite: 1061, 1063].

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

  1. Q: What is the significance of the cortical reaction?
    [cite_start]A: It acts as a permanent (slow) block to polyspermy, ensuring the zygote remains diploid[cite: 1062].
  2. Q: What defines a 'blastula'?
    [cite_start]A: The presence of a blastocoel (fluid-filled cavity) and the completion of the cleavage phase[cite: 1063].
  3. Q: Where does the primitive streak form in chicks?
    [cite_start]A: In the epiblast of the blastoderm during the onset of gastrulation[cite: 1064, 1101].
Common Mistake: Do not confuse holoblastic with isolecithal. [cite_start]One describes the process of division, the other describes the distribution of yolk[cite: 1061, 1063].

Mnemonics

    [cite_start]
  • M-E-G-A Yolk: Megalecithal eggs have Mega (huge) amounts of yolk (like birds)[cite: 1061].
    • [cite_start]
  • S-L-O-W block: The Slow block involves Secretion (of cortical granules)[cite: 1062].
