1. Recording of Simple Muscle Twitch

[cite_start]

A simple muscle twitch is a rapid, jerky response of a muscle to a single threshold stimulus[cite: 641, 715, 1115]. [cite_start]It is studied through electrical stimulation[cite: 715, 1115].

Phases of a Muscle Twitch

    [cite_start]
  • Latent Period: The time between the application of the stimulus and the beginning of the contraction[cite: 641].
    • [cite_start]
  • Contraction Phase: Period of shortening and tension development in the muscle[cite: 640].
    • [cite_start]
  • Relaxation Phase: Period when the muscle returns to its original length as calcium ions are pumped back[cite: 641].

2. Study of Unconditioned Reflex Action (Knee Jerk)

[cite_start]

The knee jerk reflex is a classic example of an unconditioned (innate), monosynaptic reflex arc[cite: 717, 1116].

Mechanism

    [cite_start]
  1. Striking the patellar tendon stretches the quadriceps muscle[cite: 717, 1116].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Stretch receptors (muscle spindles) send an impulse via sensory neurons to the spinal cord[cite: 659, 717].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. In the spinal cord, the sensory neuron synapses directly with a motor neuron[cite: 646, 659].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. The motor neuron sends an impulse back to the quadriceps, causing it to contract and the leg to kick forward[cite: 659, 717].

3. Study of Permanent Slides of Mammalian Tissues

[cite_start]

This section involves the microscopic identification of various organ tissues based on their characteristic structural features[cite: 1116].

A. Integumentary and Connective Tissues

    [cite_start]
  • Mammalian Skin: Identified by layers of stratified squamous epithelium (epidermis) and dense irregular connective tissue (dermis) containing sweat glands and hair follicles[cite: 718, 1116].
    • [cite_start]
  • Cartilage (Hyaline): Clear, glassy matrix with chondrocytes located in lacunae[cite: 718, 1116].
    • [cite_start]
  • Bone (TS): Characterized by the Haversian System (osteons), concentric lamellae, and a central Haversian canal[cite: 718, 1116].

B. Nervous System Tissues

    [cite_start]
  • Spinal Cord (TS): Butterfly-shaped inner gray matter surrounded by outer white matter; contains a central canal[cite: 718, 1116].
    • [cite_start]
  • Brain Tissue (Cerebrum/Cerebellum): Cerebellum is identified by the highly branched Purkinje cell layer[cite: 718, 1116].

Exam Focus: Identification Keys and Viva Questions

Exam Tip: When identifying the Spinal Cord vs. Brain, remember: In the Spinal Cord, gray matter is internal (butterfly shape). In the Brain (Cerebrum), gray matter is external (cortex).
Common Mistake: Do not confuse Hyaline Cartilage with Bone. [cite_start]Cartilage lacks the circular Haversian canals and calcified lamellae seen in bone tissue[cite: 636, 718].

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

  1. Q: What is the "Haversian Canal"?
    [cite_start]A: It is the central canal in a bone osteon that contains blood vessels and nerves[cite: 636].
  2. Q: What does the "colloid" in thyroid follicles represent?
    [cite_start]A: It is the stored precursor of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4)[cite: 802].
  3. Q: Why is the knee jerk called a "monosynaptic" reflex?
    [cite_start]A: Because there is only one synapse in the central nervous system between the sensory neuron and the motor neuron[cite: 646].
