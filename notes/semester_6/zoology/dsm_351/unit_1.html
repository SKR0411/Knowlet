1. Preparation of Temporary Stained Squash for Mitosis

[cite_start]

Mitosis is the process of somatic cell division resulting in two genetically identical daughter cells[cite: 1110, 1111]. [cite_start]Onion root tips are ideal for this study as they contain actively dividing meristematic cells[cite: 1111].

Materials Required

    [cite_start]
  • Onion root tips (fixed in Carnoy's solution)[cite: 1111].
    • [cite_start]
  • 1N HCl (for hydrolysis)[cite: 1111].
    • [cite_start]
  • Acetocarmine or Aceto-orcein stain[cite: 1111].
    • [cite_start]
  • Slides, coverslips, and spirit lamp[cite: 1111].

Step-by-Step Procedure

    [cite_start]
  1. Hydrolysis: Place the fixed root tips in 1N HCl at 60 degrees Celsius for 5-10 minutes to soften the cell walls[cite: 1111].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Staining: Transfer the hydrolyzed tips to a slide and add a drop of Acetocarmine[cite: 1111]. Warm gently over a flame (do not boil).
  3. Squashing: Place a coverslip over the tip. [cite_start]Tap gently with a needle or use thumb pressure to spread the cells into a single layer[cite: 1111].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Observation: Examine under a microscope to identify Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, and Telophase[cite: 1111].

2. Study of Various Stages of Meiosis

[cite_start]

Meiosis is the reductional division occurring in germ cells to produce haploid gametes[cite: 1110, 1112]. [cite_start]It involves two successive divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II[cite: 1112].

Key Stages for Identification

    [cite_start]
  • Prophase I: Includes specialized sub-stages: Leptotene, Zygotene, Pachytene, Diplotene, and Diakinesis[cite: 1112].
    • [cite_start]
  • Metaphase I: Homologous pairs (bivalents) align at the equatorial plate[cite: 1112].
    • [cite_start]
  • Anaphase I: Homologous chromosomes move to opposite poles (Reductional step)[cite: 1112].
    • [cite_start]
  • Meiosis II: Similar to mitosis, resulting in the separation of sister chromatids[cite: 1112].

3. Preparation of Permanent Slide for Barr Body

[cite_start]

A Barr body is an inactivated X chromosome found in the somatic cells of female mammals[cite: 1113]. [cite_start]It appears as a small, densely stained structure near the nuclear envelope[cite: 1113].

Objective

[cite_start]

To demonstrate the presence of a Barr body in human female cells, specifically from hair follicles[cite: 1113].

Procedure

    [cite_start]
  1. Pluck a fresh hair from a human female, ensuring the hair follicle (bulb) is intact[cite: 1113].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Fix the follicle in a suitable fixative (e.g., Ethanol-Acetic acid)[cite: 1113].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Stain with a DNA-specific stain such as Acetocarmine or Feulgen stain[cite: 1113].
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Prepare a permanent mount using DPX or a similar mounting medium[cite: 1113].
    5. [cite_start]
  5. Under high power, look for a dark, condensed mass attached to the inner surface of the nuclear membrane[cite: 1113].
Lyon's Hypothesis: Only one X chromosome is active in the somatic cells of females; the other is heterochromatinized and becomes the Barr Body.

Exam Focus: Tips and Common Practical Pitfalls

Exam Tip: When drawing Mitosis stages, pay close attention to the position of centromeres during Anaphase. [cite_start]They should be leading toward the poles while the arms of the chromosomes trail behind[cite: 1111].
[cite_start]Common Mistake: Over-staining or under-hydrolyzing the root tip often leads to a dark mass where individual chromosomes cannot be seen[cite: 1111]. Always ensure the root tip is soft enough to "spread" easily.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

  1. Q: Why is 1N HCl used in the mitosis squash?
    [cite_start]A: To break down the middle lamella (pectin) between cells, allowing them to separate easily during the squash[cite: 1111].
  2. Q: Can Barr bodies be found in males?
    [cite_start]A: Generally no, unless the male has a chromosomal abnormality like Klinefelter Syndrome (XXY)[cite: 1113].
  3. Q: What is the best tissue to study meiosis?
    [cite_start]A: In animals, the testis is commonly used; in plants, young flower buds (anthers) are used[cite: 1112].
