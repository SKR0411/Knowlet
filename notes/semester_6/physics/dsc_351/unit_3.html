1. Liquid Drop Model

[cite_start]The Liquid Drop Model, proposed by George Gamow and developed by Niels Bohr and John Wheeler, treats the nucleus as a drop of incompressible nuclear fluid[cite: 653]. This model is based on the similarities between a nucleus and a liquid drop.

Similarities between Nucleus and Liquid Drop:

    [cite_start]
  • Constant Density: Both have a density that is independent of their size[cite: 644].
    • [cite_start]
  • Binding Energy: The binding energy per nucleon is constant, similar to the latent heat of vaporization of a liquid[cite: 644].
  • Surface Tension: Nucleons on the surface are pulled inward, creating a surface tension effect.
  • Saturation: Nuclear forces are short-ranged and saturate, much like the intermolecular forces in a liquid.

2. Nuclear Fission & Bohr-Wheeler Theory

[cite_start]Nuclear Fission is the process where a heavy nucleus splits into two lighter nuclei after capturing a neutron[cite: 653]. The Bohr-Wheeler Theory explains this using the Liquid Drop Model.

The Process:

  1. The nucleus absorbs a neutron and enters an excited state.
  2. It begins to oscillate and deforms from a spherical shape to an ellipsoidal "dumbbell" shape.
    3. [cite_start]
  3. If the excitation energy is high enough, the surface tension cannot overcome the Coulomb repulsion, and the nucleus splits at the "neck"[cite: 653].

3. Semi-Empirical Mass Formula

[cite_start]The Semi-Empirical Mass Formula (SEMF), also known as the Weizsäcker formula, predicts the binding energy of a nucleus[cite: 654].

B.E. = av*A - as*A^(2/3) - ac*(Z^2 / A^(1/3)) - aa*((A - 2Z)^2 / A) +/- δ
[cite_start]

Significance of Terms[cite: 654]:

  • Volume Energy (av*A): Represents the strong nuclear force attraction between all nucleons. It is proportional to the volume (A).
  • Surface Energy (-as*A^(2/3)): Corrects for nucleons on the surface who have fewer neighbors to interact with.
  • Coulomb Energy (-ac*(Z^2 / A^(1/3))): Accounts for the electrostatic repulsion between protons.
  • Asymmetry Energy (-aa*((A - 2Z)^2 / A)): Reflects the fact that nuclei with N=Z are more stable.
  • Pairing Energy (+/- δ): Nuclei with even N and even Z are more stable (positive), while odd-odd nuclei are less stable (negative).

4. Conditions of Nuclear Stability

[cite_start]A nucleus is stable if its mass is lower than the sum of any possible decay products[cite: 654].

  • Binding Energy per Nucleon: High B.E./A values indicate stability (maximum near A=56).
    • [cite_start]
  • N/Z Ratio: For light nuclei, N/Z ≈ 1. For heavy nuclei, N/Z increases to ≈ 1.5 to balance Coulomb repulsion[cite: 644].
    • [cite_start]
  • Magic Numbers: Nuclei with specific numbers of protons or neutrons are exceptionally stable[cite: 655].

5. Nuclear Shell Model

[cite_start]The Nuclear Shell Model assumes that nucleons move independently in a central potential field, similar to electrons in an atom[cite: 655].

[cite_start]

Basic Assumptions[cite: 655]:

  • Nucleons move in orbits with discrete energy levels.
  • Each nucleon is subject to a Strong Spin-Orbit Coupling.
  • Nucleons obey the Pauli Exclusion Principle.
[cite_start]

Evidences for the Shell Model[cite: 655]:

    [cite_start]
  • Magic Numbers: Nuclei with Z or N = 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126 are exceptionally stable[cite: 655].
  • Energy Discontinuities: Sudden changes in binding energy and separation energy occur at magic numbers.
  • Nuclear Spin: The model successfully predicts the ground-state spin and parity of many nuclei.

6. Nuclear Force & Its Properties

[cite_start]The Nuclear Force is the strong interaction that holds nucleons together within the nucleus[cite: 656].

[cite_start]

Properties[cite: 656]:

  • Short Range: Effective only within distances of ≈ 1-2 fm.
  • Strongest Force: Much stronger than electromagnetic or gravitational forces.
  • Charge Independent: The n-n, p-p, and n-p forces are nearly identical.
  • Saturation: A nucleon only interacts with its immediate neighbors.
  • Spin Dependent: The force depends on the relative alignment of nucleon spins.
  • Non-Central Component: Contains a tensor force that depends on the orientation of the spins relative to the radius vector.

7. Meson Theory of Nuclear Force

[cite_start]Proposed by Hideki Yukawa in 1935, this theory explains nuclear forces as the exchange of particles called mesons (specifically pi-mesons or pions)[cite: 656].

[cite_start]

Core Concepts[cite: 656]:

  • Nucleons constantly emit and absorb virtual pions.
  • The exchange of these pions creates the attractive force between nucleons.
  • Range: The finite range of the force is related to the mass of the pion via the Uncertainty Principle (R ≈ h / 2πmc).

Exam Focus Corner

Frequently Asked Questions

    [cite_start]
  • What are Magic Numbers? [cite: 655] These are specific counts of protons or neutrons (2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126) that lead to closed shells and high stability.
    • [cite_start]
  • Why do we need the Asymmetry term in SEMF? [cite: 654] Because the Pauli Principle prevents all nucleons from occupying the lowest energy states if N and Z are very different.

Common Mistakes

  • Mixing Models: Don't use Shell Model logic to explain Fission (which is a collective, Liquid Drop effect).
  • Coulomb Term: Forgetting that the Coulomb term decreases binding energy (it's a negative term in the SEMF).

Exam Tips

Tip: If asked to compare the two models, remember: the Liquid Drop Model explains collective properties like Binding Energy and Fission, while the Shell Model explains individual properties like Spin and Magic Numbers.
