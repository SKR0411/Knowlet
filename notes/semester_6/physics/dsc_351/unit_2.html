1. Beta Decay: Kinematics and Spectrum

Beta decay is a radioactive process in which a nucleus transforms by emitting an electron (beta-minus) or a positron (beta-plus). [cite_start]This process occurs to adjust the neutron-to-proton ratio for better stability[cite: 12, 644, 1711].

Energy Kinematics

In beta-minus decay, a neutron transforms into a proton, an electron, and an antineutrino. [cite_start]The energy released (Q-value) is shared among these three particles[cite: 12, 648, 650].

Q = [M(parent) - M(daughter)] * c^2

The Beta Spectrum

[cite_start]Unlike alpha particles, which have discrete energies, beta particles are emitted with a continuous range of energies[cite: 12, 648, 1711]. The energy varies from zero up to a maximum value (the endpoint energy), which corresponds to the total Q-value of the reaction.

2. Positron Emission and Electron Capture

[cite_start]These are alternative modes of beta decay primarily observed in proton-rich nuclei[cite: 12, 648].

    [cite_start]
  • Positron Emission (Beta-plus): A proton inside the nucleus converts into a neutron, a positron, and a neutrino[cite: 12, 648].
    • [cite_start]
  • Electron Capture: An inner-shell electron (usually from the K-shell) is captured by the nucleus, combining with a proton to form a neutron and emitting a neutrino[cite: 12, 648].

3. Neutrino Hypothesis and Detection

[cite_start]The continuous nature of the beta spectrum and the apparent violation of energy and angular momentum conservation led Wolfgang Pauli to propose the Neutrino Hypothesis in 1930[cite: 12, 648, 1711].

Definition: A neutrino is an elementary particle with extremely small (or zero) mass, no electric charge, and spin 1/2. [cite_start]It carries away the "missing" energy and momentum in beta decay[cite: 12, 648, 664].

Detection of Neutrino

Due to their weak interaction with matter, neutrinos are incredibly difficult to detect. [cite_start]The Rein & Cowans experiment (1956) provided the first experimental evidence of neutrinos using a large tank of water and detecting the results of inverse beta decay[cite: 12, 648].

4. Gamma Decay and Internal Conversion

After alpha or beta decay, the daughter nucleus is often left in an excited state. [cite_start]It reaches the ground state by emitting high-energy electromagnetic radiation called Gamma Rays[cite: 12, 645, 649].

Gamma Ray Emissions

[cite_start]Gamma rays are emitted with discrete energies corresponding to the difference between nuclear energy levels[cite: 12, 649].

Internal Conversion

Instead of emitting a gamma ray, the excited nucleus may transfer its energy directly to an inner orbital electron (K or L shell), which is then ejected from the atom. [cite_start]These are called Conversion Electrons[cite: 12, 649].

5. Nuclear Reactions: Types and Conservation Laws

[cite_start]A nuclear reaction occurs when a target nucleus is bombarded by a projectile particle, resulting in a change in the nucleus[cite: 12, 650].

Types of Reactions

    [cite_start]
  • Elastic Scattering: Projectile and target remain unchanged; only kinetic energy is redistributed[cite: 12, 650].
    • [cite_start]
  • Inelastic Scattering: The target is left in an excited state[cite: 12, 650].
    • [cite_start]
  • Transmutation: The identity of the nucleus changes[cite: 12, 650].
    • [cite_start]
  • Fission & Fusion: Splitting or joining of nuclei[cite: 12, 653, 1712].

Conservation Laws

[cite_start]

In every nuclear reaction, the following must be conserved[cite: 12, 650, 664]:

  1. Total Mass-Energy
  2. Linear and Angular Momentum
  3. Electric Charge (Z)
  4. Nucleon Number (A)
    5. [cite_start]
  5. Parity and Isospin (in certain cases) [cite: 12, 664]

6. Kinematics and Q-Value Analysis

[cite_start]The Q-value is the net energy released or absorbed in a nuclear reaction[cite: 12, 650].

Q = (Mass of reactants - Mass of products) * c^2
    [cite_start]
  • Exoergic (Exothermic): Q > 0, energy is released[cite: 12, 650].
    • [cite_start]
  • Endoergic (Endothermic): Q < 0, energy is absorbed[cite: 12, 650]. For these reactions, the projectile must have a minimum Threshold Energy to proceed.

7. Expression of Scattering Cross-Section

[cite_start]The Scattering Cross-Section (σ) is a measure of the probability that a specific nuclear reaction will occur when a target is hit by a beam of particles[cite: 12, 650].

Concept: It is imagined as an "effective area" presented by the target nucleus to the incoming projectile. [cite_start]Its unit is the barn (1 barn = 10^-28 m^2)[cite: 12, 650].

Exam Focus Corner

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

    [cite_start]
  • Why is the beta spectrum continuous? Because the available energy is shared between three particles: the daughter nucleus, the beta particle, and the (anti)neutrino[cite: 12, 648].
    • [cite_start]
  • What is the difference between gamma emission and internal conversion? Gamma emission is the release of energy as a photon, while internal conversion involves the ejection of an orbital electron[cite: 12, 649].

Common Mistakes

    [cite_start]
  • Missing the Neutrino: Forgetting to include the (anti)neutrino in the beta decay equation, which is essential for energy conservation[cite: 12, 648].
  • Q-Value Units: Using grams instead of atomic mass units (amu). [cite_start]Remember 1 amu ≈ 931.5 MeV[cite: 12, 650].

Exam Tips

[cite_start]Note on Threshold Energy: For endoergic reactions, the threshold energy is always slightly higher than the absolute value of Q because some energy must go into the kinetic energy of the products to conserve momentum[cite: 12, 650].
