1. Recombinant DNA: Concept and Process

 [cite_start]

Recombinant DNA (rDNA) is a form of artificial DNA that is created by combining two or more sequences that would not normally occur together[cite: 1032, 1036]. It is the cornerstone of modern genetic engineering and gene cloning.

The General Process of Creating rDNA

    [cite_start]
  1. Isolation: Extracting the target DNA (gene of interest) and the vector DNA[cite: 1033, 1036].
    2. [cite_start]
  2. Digestion: Cutting both the target and vector DNA with the same Restriction Endonuclease to create compatible "sticky" or "blunt" ends[cite: 1033].
    3. [cite_start]
  3. Ligation: Joining the two DNA molecules together using the enzyme DNA Ligase to form a single recombinant molecule[cite: 1036].

2. Bacterial Transformation and Selection of Clones

 [cite_start]

Once the recombinant DNA is created, it must be introduced into a host cell, usually E. coli, to be copied (cloned)[cite: 1036].

Bacterial Transformation

[cite_start]

This is the process by which bacteria take up foreign DNA from their environment[cite: 1036]. Cells are made "competent" through chemical treatment (e.g., Calcium Chloride) followed by a brief Heat Shock, which creates temporary pores in the cell membrane.

Selection of Recombinant Clones

Not every bacterial cell will take up a plasmid, and not every plasmid will contain the target gene. [cite_start]Selection is necessary to identify the successful clones[cite: 1036, 1040].

    [cite_start]
  • Antibiotic Resistance: Using antibiotic-containing media to kill cells that did not take up the plasmid (which carries resistance genes like ampR)[cite: 1033].
    • [cite_start]
  • Blue-White Screening: A technique where successful insertion of the target gene disrupts a reporter gene (like lacZ), causing successful recombinants to appear as white colonies instead of blue[cite: 1036, 1040].

3. PCR Mediated Gene Cloning

 [cite_start]

The Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) can be used to specifically amplify a gene of interest before it is inserted into a vector, making the cloning process much more efficient[cite: 1036, 1037].

Steps in PCR

  • Denaturation: Heating the DNA to ~94°C to separate the strands.
  • Annealing: Cooling to ~55°C to allow primers to bind to specific sequences.
  • Extension: Heating to ~72°C for Taq Polymerase to synthesize new DNA strands.
[cite_start]

In cloning, primers are often designed to include restriction sites, allowing the amplified product to be easily digested and ligated into a vector[cite: 1036].

4. Construction of Genomic and cDNA Libraries

 [cite_start]

A DNA library is a collection of cloned DNA fragments that together represent an entire genome or the expressed genes of a cell[cite: 1037].

Genomic Library

    [cite_start]
  • Source: Total genomic DNA[cite: 1037].
  • Content: Contains all DNA sequences, including exons, introns, and non-coding regions.
    • [cite_start]
  • Construction: Genomic DNA is digested and fragments are ligated into high-capacity vectors like BACs or YACs[cite: 1034, 1037].

cDNA Library

    [cite_start]
  • Source: Messenger RNA (mRNA) extracted from a specific tissue[cite: 1037].
  • Content: Represents only the genes being expressed (active) at that time; does not contain introns.
    • [cite_start]
  • Construction: mRNA is converted into double-stranded "complementary DNA" (cDNA) using the enzyme Reverse Transcriptase before being cloned[cite: 1037].

5. Screening DNA Libraries

 [cite_start]

Once a library is created, it must be searched (screened) to find the specific clone containing the gene of interest[cite: 1037].

    [cite_start]
  • Genetic Selection: Designing the experiment so that only cells containing the desired gene can survive (e.g., providing a gene that fixes a nutritional deficiency in the host)[cite: 1037].
  • Colony Hybridization: Using a labeled DNA or RNA "probe" that is complementary to the target gene. [cite_start]The probe binds (hybridizes) to the target DNA in the specific colony that contains it[cite: 1037].
    • [cite_start]
  • PCR Screening: Using gene-specific primers to identify which pool of clones contains the target sequence[cite: 1037].

6. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tip: Understand the difference between Genomic and cDNA libraries clearly. [cite_start]Genomic libraries are the same for every cell in an organism, but a cDNA library varies depending on the tissue and the time it was collected[cite: 1037].

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistake: Thinking PCR creates recombinant DNA. [cite_start]Correction: PCR only amplifies DNA; ligation is needed to make it recombinant[cite: 1036].
  • Mistake: Assuming a cDNA library contains all the organism's genes. [cite_start]Correction: It only contains genes that were being transcribed into mRNA at the time of collection[cite: 1037].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a "probe" in colony hybridization?
[cite_start]A: A probe is a short, single-stranded DNA or RNA fragment that is labeled (with radioactivity or fluorescence) and has a sequence complementary to the gene being sought[cite: 1037].

Q: Why is Reverse Transcriptase used for cDNA libraries?
A: Because libraries are made of DNA, but the starting material for a cDNA library is RNA. [cite_start]Reverse transcriptase is the only enzyme that can synthesize DNA using an RNA template[cite: 1037].
