1. Biosynthesis of Plant Hormones

 [cite_start]

Plant hormones (Phytohormones) are signal molecules produced within the plant that occur in extremely low concentrations[cite: 1008]. [cite_start]This unit focuses on the biosynthesis of the four major growth regulators[cite: 1008]:

    [cite_start]
  • Auxin: Primarily synthesized from the amino acid Tryptophan[cite: 1008]. Synthesis mainly occurs in rapidly growing tissues like apical buds and young leaves.
    • [cite_start]
  • Gibberellin: Synthesized via the terpenoid pathway in plastids, membranes, and the endoplasmic reticulum[cite: 1008].
  • Cytokinin: Primarily synthesized in roots and transported to shoots. [cite_start]They are derived from adenine[cite: 1008].
    • [cite_start]
  • Abscisic Acid (ABA): Synthesized in chloroplasts and other plastids via the carotenoid (indirect) pathway[cite: 1008].

2. Transport and Mode of Action of Plant Hormones

 [cite_start]

Phytohormones must be transported to target cells where they trigger specific physiological responses[cite: 1008].

    [cite_start]
  • Transport: Auxins exhibit polar transport (from apex to base)[cite: 1008]. [cite_start]Others like Gibberellins and Cytokinins move primarily through the xylem and phloem[cite: 1008].
    • [cite_start]
  • Mode of Action: Hormones bind to specific protein receptors[cite: 1008]. [cite_start]This binding initiates a signal transduction cascade, leading to changes in gene expression or enzyme activity[cite: 1010].

3. Physiological and Biochemical Effects of Hormones

 [cite_start]

Each hormone has unique roles in plant development[cite: 1008]:

[cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start]
Hormone Primary Physiological Roles
AuxinCell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation, and phototropism[cite: 1008].
GibberellinStem elongation (bolting), seed germination, and flowering[cite: 1008].

4. Biological Nitrogen Fixation (BNF)

 [cite_start]

Biological nitrogen fixation is the process by which atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) is converted into ammonia (NH₃) by certain microorganisms[cite: 1009].

    [cite_start]
  • Symbiotic Fixation: Occurs in the root nodules of leguminous plants through an association with Rhizobium bacteria[cite: 1009]. [cite_start]Non-leguminous symbiotic systems (e.g., Frankia) also exist[cite: 1009].
    • [cite_start]
  • Non-symbiotic Fixation: Carried out by free-living bacteria like Azotobacter and certain cyanobacteria[cite: 1009].
    • [cite_start]
  • Biochemistry: The reaction is catalyzed by the Nitrogenase enzyme complex, which requires high ATP and a highly anaerobic environment to function[cite: 1009].

5. Nitrate and Ammonia Assimilation

 [cite_start]

Plants absorb nitrogen mostly in the form of nitrate (NO₃⁻), which must be converted into amino acids[cite: 1009].

    [cite_start]
  • Nitrate Assimilation: Nitrate is reduced to Nitrite (NO₂⁻) by Nitrate Reductase and then further reduced to Ammonia (NH₃) by Nitrite Reductase[cite: 1009].
    • [cite_start]
  • Ammonia Assimilation: To avoid toxicity, ammonia is rapidly incorporated into organic molecules (amino acids) via the GS-GOGAT (Glutamine Synthetase - Glutamate Synthase) pathway[cite: 1009].

6. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tip: Be prepared to discuss the antagonistic roles of hormones, such as Gibberellins (promote germination) vs. ABA (promotes dormancy). [cite_start]Also, ensure you can name the Nitrogenase enzyme and its requirement for oxygen-free conditions in BNF[cite: 1009].

Common Mistakes

  • Mistake: Assuming nitrogenase can fix nitrogen in the presence of oxygen. [cite_start]Correction: Nitrogenase is extremely sensitive to oxygen; plants often use Leghaemoglobin to scavenge oxygen in nodules[cite: 1009].
  • Mistake: Confusing nitrate reduction and nitrogen fixation. [cite_start]Correction: Fixation converts atmospheric N₂ gas to NH₃, while nitrate reduction converts soil NO₃⁻ to NH₃[cite: 1009].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the precursor for Auxin biosynthesis?
[cite_start]A: The amino acid Tryptophan is the most common precursor[cite: 1008].

Q: Why is ammonia assimilation necessary?
[cite_start]A: Ammonia is toxic to plant cells at high concentrations because it can dissipate proton gradients; therefore, it must be quickly converted into Glutamate or Glutamine[cite: 1009].
