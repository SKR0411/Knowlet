1. Synthesis and Catabolism of Starch

 [cite_start]

Starch is the primary storage polysaccharide in plants, existing as amylose and amylopectin[cite: 1006].

    [cite_start]
  • Synthesis: Occurs in plastids (chloroplasts and amyloplasts) using ADP-glucose as the precursor[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Catabolism: Involves enzymes like amylases and starch phosphorylase to mobilize stored energy during darkness or germination[cite: 1006].

2. Glycolysis and its Regulation

 [cite_start]

Glycolysis is the sequence of reactions that converts glucose into pyruvate while producing energy in the form of ATP and NADH[cite: 1006].

[Image of the glycolysis pathway steps]
    [cite_start]
  • Location: Occurs in the cytosol[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Key Steps: Includes phosphorylation, cleavage, and energy-harvesting phases[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Regulation: Controlled by key enzymes such as phosphofructokinase (PFK) and hexokinase, which respond to the energy status (ATP/ADP levels) of the cell[cite: 1006].

3. Pentose Phosphate Pathway (PPP)

 [cite_start]

This pathway is an alternative to glycolysis for glucose oxidation[cite: 1006].

    [cite_start]
  • Primary Functions: Generates NADPH for biosynthetic reactions and provides pentose sugars (like ribose-5-phosphate) for nucleic acid synthesis[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Phases: Consists of an oxidative phase (irreversible) and a non-oxidative phase (reversible)[cite: 1006].

4. TCA Cycle and Krebs Cycle Regulation

 [cite_start]

The Tricarboxylic Acid (TCA) cycle, or Krebs cycle, is a series of chemical reactions used by all aerobic organisms to generate energy[cite: 1006].

    [cite_start]
  • Location: Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Input: Acetyl-CoA derived from pyruvate[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Anaplerotic Reactions: Reactions that replenish cycle intermediates to maintain metabolic flux[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Regulation: Regulated by the availability of substrates and the energy charge of the cell (inhibited by high ATP and NADH)[cite: 1006].

5. Mitochondrial Electron Transport and Oxidative Phosphorylation

 [cite_start]

The final stage of aerobic respiration where most of the ATP is produced[cite: 1006].

    [cite_start]
  • Electron Transport Chain (ETC): A series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane that transfer electrons from NADH and FADH2 to oxygen[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • Proton Gradient: The transfer of electrons drives the pumping of protons across the membrane, creating a gradient[cite: 1006].

6. Anaerobic Respiration and Mechanism of ATP Synthesis

 [cite_start]

In the absence of oxygen, plants can switch to anaerobic respiration to sustain energy production[cite: 1006].

    [cite_start]
  • Anaerobic Respiration: Involves fermentation processes (alcoholic or lactic acid fermentation) to regenerate NAD+ for glycolysis[cite: 1006].
    • [cite_start]
  • ATP Synthesis: Driven by ATP synthase through the process of chemiosmosis, where the flow of protons back across the membrane powers the synthesis of ATP from ADP and Pi[cite: 1006].
[Image of ATP synthase mechanism in the mitochondria]

7. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

Exam Tip: Be prepared to differentiate between aerobic and anaerobic respiration yields. Aerobic respiration is far more efficient, yielding much more ATP per glucose molecule compared to fermentation.

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistake: Thinking the TCA cycle produces the most ATP. Correction: The TCA cycle produces high-energy electron carriers (NADH, FADH2); the ETC/Oxidative Phosphorylation phase produces the most ATP.
  • Mistake: Forgetting the location of different processes. Correction: Glycolysis (Cytosol), TCA Cycle (Mitochondrial Matrix), ETC (Inner Mitochondrial Membrane).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What are anaplerotic reactions?
[cite_start]A: They are reactions that form intermediates of a metabolic pathway, such as the TCA cycle, to replace those that have been diverted for other biosynthetic purposes[cite: 1006].

Q: Why is the Pentose Phosphate Pathway important if it doesn't produce ATP?
[cite_start]A: It is essential for providing NADPH, which is used in reductive biosynthesis like fatty acid synthesis, and ribose sugars for making DNA and RNA[cite: 1006].
