1. Types and Significance of Chemical Bonds

 [cite_start]

The stability and function of biomolecules depend on the chemical bonds that hold them together[cite: 994, 995]. [cite_start]These are categorized based on their strength and nature[cite: 996]:

    [cite_start]
  • Covalent Bonds: Strong bonds formed by the sharing of electron pairs (e.g., C-C, C-H bonds in organic backbones)[cite: 996].
    • [cite_start]
  • Ionic Bonds: Electrostatic attractions between oppositely charged ions[cite: 996].
    • [cite_start]
  • Hydrogen Bonds: Weak attractions between a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to an electronegative atom and another electronegative atom; crucial for DNA and protein stability[cite: 996].
    • [cite_start]
  • Van der Waals Interactions: Weak, short-range attractions between non-polar molecules[cite: 996].

2. Structure of Water, pH, and Buffers

 [cite_start]

Water is the medium of life, and its unique properties are essential for biological systems[cite: 996].

    [cite_start]
  • Water Structure: A polar molecule that forms extensive hydrogen bond networks, leading to high cohesion, surface tension, and specific heat[cite: 996].
    • [cite_start]
  • pH: A measure of the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution (pH = -log[H+])[cite: 996].
    • [cite_start]
  • Buffers: Solutions that resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added; essential for maintaining metabolic stability in living systems[cite: 996].

3. Structure and Classification of Biomolecules

 [cite_start]

Biomolecules are the organic molecules that make up living organisms[cite: 997].

Carbohydrates

[cite_start]

Classified into Monosaccharides, Disaccharides, and Polysaccharides based on the number of sugar units[cite: 997]. [cite_start]They serve as primary energy sources and structural components (e.g., cellulose)[cite: 997].

Proteins

[cite_start]

Polymers of amino acids[cite: 997]. [cite_start]Classification is based on structure (primary, secondary, tertiary, quaternary) and function (enzymes, transport, structural)[cite: 997].

Lipids

[cite_start]

Insoluble in water but soluble in organic solvents[cite: 997]. [cite_start]Includes fats, oils, phospholipids (membrane structure), and steroids[cite: 997].

4. Laws of Thermodynamics and Bioenergetics

 [cite_start]

Biological systems must obey the physical laws of the universe regarding energy transfer[cite: 998].

    [cite_start]
  • First Law: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed (Energy conservation)[cite: 998].
    • [cite_start]
  • Second Law: Every energy transfer increases the entropy (disorder) of the universe[cite: 998].
    • [cite_start]
  • Free Energy (G): The portion of a system's energy that can perform work[cite: 998]. [cite_start]The change in free energy (ΔG) determines if a reaction happens spontaneously[cite: 998].

5. Endergonic, Exergonic, and Redox Reactions

 [cite_start]

Metabolism involves the coupling of energy-releasing and energy-requiring reactions[cite: 998].

[cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start] [cite_start]
Reaction Type Energy Change Spontaneity
ExergonicReleases free energy (-ΔG)[cite: 998].Spontaneous[cite: 998].
EndergonicAbsorbs free energy (+ΔG)[cite: 998].Non-spontaneous[cite: 998].
    [cite_start]
  • Coupled Reactions: The use of an exergonic process to drive an endergonic one (often using ATP)[cite: 998].
    • [cite_start]
  • Redox Reactions: Coupled oxidation (loss of electrons) and reduction (gain of electrons) reactions that drive energy production in respiration and photosynthesis[cite: 998].

6. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

[cite_start]Exam Tip: Always relate the structure of a biomolecule to its function[cite: 1010]. [cite_start]For example, the amphipathic nature of phospholipids is what allows them to form cell membranes[cite: 997]. Also, memorize the ΔG conditions: ΔG < [cite_start]0 is spontaneous[cite: 998].

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistake: Assuming "spontaneous" reactions happen instantly. [cite_start]Correction: Spontaneous only means energy-favorable; rate depends on enzymes[cite: 998, 1001].
  • Mistake: Confusing oxidation and reduction. [cite_start]Correction: Use OIL RIG (Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain of electrons)[cite: 998].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Why is water a "Universal Solvent"?
[cite_start]A: Its polarity allows it to surround and dissolve many ionic and polar biological molecules[cite: 996].

Q: What is a buffer?
[cite_start]A: It is a mixture of a weak acid and its conjugate base that minimizes pH fluctuations in a cell[cite: 996].
