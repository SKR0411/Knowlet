1. Principles of Phytogeography

 [cite_start]

Phytogeography (or plant geography) is the branch of biogeography that studies the geographical distribution of plant species and their influence on the earth's surface[cite: 960, 972].

Core Principles:

    [cite_start]
  • Descriptive Phytogeography: Focuses on the description of floral regions[cite: 973].
    • [cite_start]
  • Interpretative (Historical) Phytogeography: Explains the reasons for the distribution patterns based on past events and evolutionary history[cite: 973].

2. Continental Drift and Theory of Tolerance

The current distribution of plants is heavily influenced by geological history and the environmental limits of the plants themselves.

Continental Drift

[cite_start]

This theory explains how landmasses moved over time, causing the isolation of plant populations or creating "land bridges" that allowed migration[cite: 973]. [cite_start]This movement explains why closely related plant species are sometimes found on different continents[cite: 973].

Theory of Tolerance

[cite_start]

This theory states that a plant's distribution is limited by its range of tolerance for various environmental factors (like temperature, moisture, and light)[cite: 973]. [cite_start]A species will only exist in areas where the environmental conditions fall within its physiological limits[cite: 973].

3. Concept of Endemism

 [cite_start]

Endemism refers to the condition of a plant species being unique to a specific geographic location, such as a specific mountain range, island, or country, and not found naturally anywhere else[cite: 973].

    [cite_start]
  • Causes: Physical barriers (mountains, oceans), climate changes, or recent evolution in a specific spot[cite: 973].
    • [cite_start]
  • Significance: Endemic species are often highly vulnerable to extinction due to their restricted range[cite: 973].

5. Phytogeographical Divisions of India

 [cite_start]

India is divided into several phytogeographical regions based on varied climatic and topographic features[cite: 974]. [cite_start]These divisions typically include the Western Himalayas, Eastern Himalayas, Indus Plain, Gangetic Plain, Central India, Malabar (Western Ghats), Deccan, and the Andaman/Nicobar Islands[cite: 974].

6. Vegetation of NE India and Assam

 [cite_start]

North-East India is a significant biodiversity hotspot with a wide array of vegetation types[cite: 974].

Vegetation Types in NE India:

    [cite_start]
  • Tropical Evergreen and Semi-evergreen Forests: Found in areas with very high rainfall[cite: 974].
    • [cite_start]
  • Deciduous Forests: Common in the plains and foothills of Assam[cite: 974].
    • [cite_start]
  • Grasslands: Found in riverine areas and high-altitude plateaus[cite: 974].
    • [cite_start]
  • Alpine Vegetation: Restricted to the high-altitude regions of the Eastern Himalayas[cite: 974].
[cite_start]

Special Reference to Assam: The vegetation is dominated by tropical evergreen, semi-evergreen, and moist deciduous forests, with significant riverine grasslands in areas like Kaziranga[cite: 974].

7. Exam Focus: Tips and FAQs

[cite_start]Exam Tip: When discussing the Phytogeographical divisions of India, emphasize the Eastern Himalayas/North-East India section, as it is a global biodiversity hotspot with high levels of endemism[cite: 973, 974].

Common Pitfalls

  • Mistake: Confusing a "Biome" with an "Ecosystem." [cite_start]Correction: A biome is a large regional unit based on climate/vegetation; an ecosystem is a smaller functional unit of interaction[cite: 961, 973].
  • Mistake: Thinking endemic species are just "rare." [cite_start]Correction: Endemic species are rare because they are restricted to a specific area; not all rare species are endemic[cite: 973].

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is the significance of Continental Drift in phytogeography?
[cite_start]A: It explains the "disjunct distribution" of plant families, where identical or related plants are found in widely separated parts of the world[cite: 973].

Q: Why is NE India called a biodiversity hotspot?
[cite_start]A: Due to its unique position at the junction of three biogeographical realms, leading to exceptionally high species richness and endemism in its vegetation[cite: 974].
