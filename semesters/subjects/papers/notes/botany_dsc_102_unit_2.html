Cyanophyta (Cyanobacteria)

This group is also known as Blue-Green Algae (BGA). They are prokaryotic, meaning they lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (like chloroplasts, mitochondria).

  • Pigments: Chl-a and Phycobilins (Phycocyanin, Phycoerythrin), which are located in the cytoplasm (not in chloroplasts).
  • Reserve Food: Cyanophycean starch.
  • Motility: Completely lack flagella at all stages.
  • Cell Wall: Peptidoglycan (like bacteria).

Nostoc

  • Ecology: Terrestrial (on moist soil) or freshwater.
  • Thallus Organization: A colonial alga. The filaments (called trichomes) are unbranched and look like a string of beads. Many filaments are embedded in a common, gelatinous sheath, forming a ball-like colony.
  • Cell Structure: Filaments contain two cell types:
    1. Vegetative Cells: Normal photosynthetic cells.
    2. Heterocysts: Large, thick-walled, pale-yellow, empty-looking cells. Their sole function is nitrogen fixation (they contain the oxygen-sensitive enzyme nitrogenase).
  • Reproduction:
    • Fragmentation/Hormogonia: Filaments break into small pieces (hormogonia) that form new colonies.
    • Akinetes: Thick-walled, dormant resting spores that survive harsh conditions.
    • Sexual reproduction is completely absent.

Oscillatoria

  • Ecology: Freshwater (often in polluted water) and on moist soil.
  • Thallus Organization: A simple, unbranched, uniseriate (single row) filament (trichome). No gelatinous sheath.
  • Key Feature: Exhibits a characteristic "oscillating" (swaying) and gliding movement.
  • Reproduction: Exclusively by hormogonia formation. The filament breaks at special "separation discs" (dead cells) to release short, motile hormogonia.
  • Note: Lacks heterocysts and akinetes. Sexual reproduction is absent.

Chlorophyta (Green Algae)

A large, diverse group of eukaryotic algae. Believed to be the ancestors of higher plants.

  • Pigments: Chlorophyll-a and Chlorophyll-b (in the same ratio as plants), carotenes, and xanthophylls. Pigments are in chloroplasts.
  • Reserve Food: True Starch, stored inside chloroplasts around a structure called the pyrenoid.
  • Motility: Motile cells (zoospores, gametes) are common, typically with 2 or 4 whiplash flagella.
  • Cell Wall: Cellulose.

Chlamydomonas

  • Ecology: Freshwater ponds and ditches.
  • Thallus: Unicellular, motile, pear-shaped. Has two anterior whiplash flagella, a single cup-shaped chloroplast, one pyrenoid, and a red eyepot (stigma) for light detection.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By zoospores. The parent cell loses flagella, its protoplast divides (2, 4, 8, or 16), and each new cell develops flagella, forming miniature copies that are released.
    • Sexual: Can be isogamous, anisogamous, or oogamous (depending on species). Gametes fuse to form a diploid zygote (2n), which develops a thick wall (zygospore). The zygospore undergoes meiosis to form 4 haploid (n) new individuals.
  • Life Cycle: Haplontic (the dominant organism is haploid (n), and the zygote is the only diploid (2n) stage).

Volvox

  • Ecology: Freshwater, forms green rolling balls visible to the naked eye.
  • Thallus: A highly advanced motile colony called a coenobium. It's a hollow sphere of a fixed number of Chlamydomonas-like cells (500 to 60,000) held in a gelatinous matrix. Cells are connected by cytoplasmic strands. Shows division of labor (vegetative cells for motility, reproductive cells).
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: Specialized cells in the posterior end divide to form daughter colonies, which are released by inversion.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy. Some cells become non-motile eggs. Others divide to form packets of motile antherozoids (sperm). Fertilization produces a zygote, which undergoes meiosis to form a new haploid colony.

Oedogonium

  • Ecology: Freshwater, attached to surfaces in ponds.
  • Thallus: Unbranched filament. Cells are cylindrical. The basal cell is a holdfast (for attachment).
  • Key Feature: Cells divide in a unique way, leaving "rings" at the top of the cell called cap cells.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By zoospores. A single, large zoospore with a "crown" of many flagella is formed per cell.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy.
      • Oogonium: A large, swollen cell containing a single egg.
      • Antheridium: Short, flat cells that produce two small, multiflagellate sperm.
      • Some species are macrandrous (normal-sized male filaments).
      • Some are nannandrous (tiny "dwarf male" filaments (nannandria) grow on or near the oogonium from a special spore).

Coleochaete

  • Ecology: Freshwater, grows as an epiphyte on aquatic plants.
  • Thallus: A heterotrichous (branched filament system with both prostrate/creeping and erect parts) or discoid (flat disc) thallus. Cells are uninucleate and have a single chloroplast. Some cells bear a long, bristle-like seta.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By zoospores.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy. The zygote is retained on the parent thallus and is covered by a layer of sterile cells (a cortex), forming a spermocarp. This retention of the zygote is a very advanced feature, showing an evolutionary link to higher plants (bryophytes).

Xanthophyta (Yellow-Green Algae)

Distinguished from green algae by their pigments and food reserve.

  • Pigments: Chl-a, Chl-c, and carotenes. They lack Chl-b and fucoxanthin.
  • Reserve Food: Oil and Leucosin (not starch).
  • Cell Wall: Pectic compounds, often with silica.
  • Key Feature: Many are coenocytic (multinucleate).

Vaucheria

  • Ecology: Freshwater or terrestrial ("water felt") on moist soil.
  • Thallus: A sparsely branched, filamentous, siphonaceous thallus. It is one long, tube-like coenocyte with no cross-walls (septa) in the vegetative state. Contains many nuclei and many small, discoid chloroplasts.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By forming a single, large, multiflagellate synzoospore in a sporangium at the filament tip.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy.
      • Oogonium: A globular structure with one egg.
      • Antheridium: A curved, tube-like structure producing many small, biflagellate sperm.
      • Both sex organs are often found together on a short branch.

Phaeophyta (Brown Algae)

A group of almost exclusively marine, complex, multicellular algae (seaweeds, kelps).

  • Pigments: Chl-a, Chl-c, and a large amount of the xanthophyll Fucoxanthin, which gives them their characteristic olive-green to brown color.
  • Reserve Food: Laminarin and Mannitol (not starch).
  • Motility: Motile cells (zoospores, gametes) have two unequal, lateral flagella (one tinsel, one whiplash).
  • Cell Wall: Cellulose and alginic acid.

Ectocarpus

  • Ecology: Marine, grows attached to rocks.
  • Thallus: A simple, branched, heterotrichous filament (has both prostrate and erect systems).
  • Life Cycle: Shows a perfect isomorphic alternation of generations.
    1. Sporophyte (2n): The diploid plant. It produces two types of sporangia:
      • Plurilocular Sporangia (Asexual): Multicellular. Produce diploid (2n) zoospores that grow into new sporophytes (asexual reproduction).
      • Unilocular Sporangia (Sexual): Single-celled. Meiosis occurs inside. Produces haploid (n) zoospores.
    2. Gametophyte (n): The haploid (n) zoospores grow into a haploid plant (gametophyte) that looks identical to the sporophyte.
      • The gametophyte produces plurilocular gametangia, which release haploid (n) gametes.
      • Gametes fuse (isogamy/anisogamy) to form a diploid zygote (2n), which grows into the sporophyte (2n), completing the cycle.

Rhodophyta (Red Algae)

A group of mostly marine algae, often found in deeper water.

  • Pigments: Chl-a, and Phycobilins (especially Phycoerythrin), which masks the green chlorophyll and allows them to absorb blue-green light that penetrates deep water.
  • Reserve Food: Floridean Starch (stored in the cytoplasm).
  • Motility: Completely absent. No flagellated cells (zoospores or gametes) are formed at any stage.
  • Cell Wall: Cellulose, pectins, and agar or carrageenan.
  • Key Feature: Highly complex sexual reproduction.
