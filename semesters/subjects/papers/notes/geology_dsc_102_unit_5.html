Concept of a Mineral

This definition is critical and often a major exam question. A substance must meet all five criteria to be considered a true mineral.

Definition of a Mineral: A mineral is a (1) naturally occurring, (2) inorganic, (3) solid, with a (4) definite (but not fixed) chemical composition and a (5) ordered internal (crystalline) structure.

Breakdown of the Definition:

  1. Naturally Occurring: Must be formed by natural, geological processes. Man-made equivalents (e.g., synthetic diamonds, synthetic Bismuth) are not "minerals."
  2. Inorganic: Not formed by living organisms (e.g., not part of a shell, bone, or plant tissue).
    • Exception: Shells made of calcite are not minerals, but *after* they are buried, compressed, and recrystallized into limestone, the calcite *is* a mineral.
  3. Solid: Must be a solid at standard Earth surface temperatures. Water (ice) is a mineral, but liquid water is not. Mercury is not a mineral.
  4. Definite (but not fixed) Chemical Composition: The chemical formula is specific, but can allow for some variation (substitution).
    • Definite: Olivine is (Mg, Fe)₂SiO₄. It *must* have Mg and/or Fe, Silicon, and Oxygen in that ratio.
    • Not Fixed: The ratio of Mg to Fe can vary. This is called solid solution.
  5. Ordered Internal (Crystalline) Structure: The atoms are arranged in a repeating, 3D lattice. This is what *makes* it a crystal.
    • Substances that lack this internal order (like volcanic glass or Opal) are called mineraloids.

Classification of Minerals (Rock-forming vs. Ore-forming)

This is a broad, practical classification based on a mineral's abundance and economic use.

Rock-forming Minerals

These are the most common minerals that make up the vast majority (over 90%) of the Earth's crust. They are the "building blocks" of most rocks.

  • Primary Examples:
    • Silicates: The most dominant group. Includes Quartz, Feldspar (Plagioclase and K-Feldspar), Mica (Muscovite, Biotite), Amphibole (e.g., Hornblende), Pyroxene (e.g., Augite), and Olivine.
    • Carbonates: Calcite and Dolomite (which form limestone and dolostone).

Ore-forming Minerals

These are minerals that contain a valuable metal or element that can be extracted for a profit. They are often less common than rock-forming minerals but are concentrated by geological processes into "ore deposits."

  • Examples:
    • Iron (Fe): Hematite (Fe₂O₃), Magnetite (Fe₃O₄)
    • Copper (Cu): Chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂)
    • Lead (Pb): Galena (PbS)
    • Zinc (Zn): Sphalerite (ZnS)
    • Aluminum (Al): Bauxite (a rock composed of several aluminum hydroxide minerals)

Chemical Classification of Minerals

This is the standard scientific classification system for minerals. It is based on the dominant anion (negatively charged ion) or anionic group (a complex ion like (SO₄)²⁻) in the mineral's chemical formula. Minerals in the same class share similar chemical and physical properties.

Sulphides (Anion: S²⁻)

Compounds of a metal (or semi-metal) with sulfur. Many are important ore minerals.

  • Pyrite: FeS₂ (Fool's Gold)
  • Galena: PbS (Ore of lead)
  • Sphalerite: ZnS (Ore of zinc)
  • Chalcopyrite: CuFeS₂ (Ore of copper)

Oxides / Hydroxides (Anion: O²⁻ / (OH)⁻)

Compounds of a metal with oxygen (oxides) or the hydroxyl group (hydroxides). Also includes many important ores.

  • Oxides:
    • Hematite: Fe₂O₃ (Ore of iron, red streak)
    • Magnetite: Fe₃O₄ (Ore of iron, magnetic)
    • Corundum: Al₂O₃ (Gem varieties: Ruby and Sapphire)
    • Rutile: TiO₂ (Ore of titanium)
  • Hydroxides:
    • Goethite: FeO(OH) (A common rust, yellowish-brown streak)
    • Bauxite: (A mixture, but rich in Al-hydroxides like Gibbsite)

Halides (Anion: Cl⁻, F⁻, Br⁻)

Compounds with a halogen element. They are often soft and water-soluble.

  • Halite: NaCl (Rock salt)
  • Fluorite: CaF₂
  • Sylvite: KCl

Carbonates (Anionic Group: (CO₃)²⁻)

Minerals built with the carbonate (CO₃)²⁻ group. Their key diagnostic feature is that they fizz (effervesce) in dilute acid (HCl).

  • Calcite: CaCO₃ (The primary mineral in limestone and marble)
  • Dolomite: CaMg(CO₃)₂ (Primary mineral in dolostone; fizzes weakly)
  • Aragonite: CaCO₃ (A polymorph of calcite)

Sulphates (Anionic Group: (SO₄)²⁻)

Minerals built with the sulphate (SO₄)²⁻ group. They are often soft and found in evaporite deposits.

  • Gypsum: CaSO₄·2H₂O (Used to make drywall/plaster)
  • Anhydrite: CaSO₄ (Gypsum without the water)
  • Barite: BaSO₄ (Used in drilling mud due to high density)

Phosphates (Anionic Group: (PO₄)³⁻)

Minerals built with the phosphate (PO₄)³⁻ group. The most important mineral is Apatite.

  • Apatite: Ca₅(PO₄)₃(F,Cl,OH) (The mineral that makes up our bones and teeth; a source of phosphorus for fertilizer)

Silicate Minerals

Definition and Silica Tetrahedron

The silicate minerals are the largest, most abundant, and most important class of minerals, forming ~90% of the Earth's crust.

The fundamental building block of all silicate minerals is the Silica Tetrahedron.

  • It consists of one small, central Silicon atom (Si⁴⁺) bonded to four larger Oxygen atoms (O²⁻).
  • This forms a strong, stable, pyramid-like shape.
  • The overall charge of this group is: (+4) + 4*(-2) = -4.
  • Formula: (SiO₄)⁴⁻

This (SiO₄)⁴⁻ unit has a large negative charge, so it must bond with positive ions (cations) like Mg²⁺, Fe²⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, etc. OR, it can bond with *other* silica tetrahedra by sharing oxygen atoms.

Classification Based on Silicate Structure

The method of sharing oxygens (polymerization) is the basis for the classification of silicates. This is a very important exam topic.

Classification of Silicate Structures
Class (Name) Structure (How Tetrahedra Link) Si:O Ratio Example Mineral(s)
Nesosilicates (Ortho-) Independent, isolated tetrahedra. (No shared O) 1:4 Olivine, Garnet, Kyanite, Staurolite
Sorosilicates (Di-) Two tetrahedra sharing one oxygen. (Double units) 2:7 Epidote, Hemimorphite
Cyclosilicates (Ring) Tetrahedra share two oxygens each to form 3, 4, or 6-membered rings. 1:3 Beryl (Emerald), Tourmaline
Inosilicates (Single Chain) Tetrahedra share two oxygens each to form a continuous single chain. 1:3 Pyroxene Group (e.g., Augite, Diopside)
Inosilicates (Double Chain) Two single chains linked together by sharing oxygens. 4:11 Amphibole Group (e.g., Hornblende)
Phyllosilicates (Sheet) Tetrahedra share three oxygens each to form continuous sheets. 2:5 Mica Group (Muscovite, Biotite), Clay Minerals (Kaolinite), Talc
Tectosilicates (Framework) Tetrahedra share all four oxygens each to form a 3D framework. 1:2 Quartz (SiO₂), Feldspar Group (Plagioclase, Orthoclase)

Related Mineralogical Concepts

Isomorphism ("Same Form")

This describes a series of minerals that have the same crystal structure but different chemical compositions. This occurs when ions of similar size and charge can substitute for each other.

  • Example: The Olivine Series.
    • Forsterite (Mg₂SiO₄) and Fayalite (Fe₂SiO₄) have the same orthorhombic structure.
    • The Mg²⁺ and Fe²⁺ ions are very similar in size and can substitute freely.
    • This creates a solid solution series, (Mg,Fe)₂SiO₄.

Polymorphism ("Many Forms")

This describes a single chemical compound that can exist as two or more different minerals with different crystal structures. The structure that forms depends on the conditions of temperature (T) and pressure (P) during crystallization.

  • Example 1: Carbon (C)
    • Graphite: Soft, grey, sheet structure (Phyllosilicate-like). Forms at low P, T.
    • Diamond: Hardest mineral, framework structure. Forms at very high P.
  • Example 2: Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃)
    • Calcite: Trigonal system, stable at most surface P, T.
    • Aragonite: Orthorhombic system, stable at higher P.
