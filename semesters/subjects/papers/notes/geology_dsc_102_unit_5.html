1. Concept of Mineral

The term "mineral" is precise. To be classified as a mineral, a substance must meet five specific criteria.

Definition of a Mineral: A mineral is a...
  1. Naturally Occurring: It must be formed by natural geological processes, not man-made (e.g., synthetic diamond is not a mineral).
  2. Inorganic: It must not be formed from living organisms or their remains (e.g., coal, which comes from plants, is not a mineral).
  3. Solid: It must be a solid substance at standard Earth surface temperatures (e.g., liquid water is not a mineral, but ice is).
  4. Definite Chemical Composition: Its chemical makeup is fixed or varies within a specific, limited range (e.g., Quartz is always SiO‚ÇÇ, Halite is always NaCl).
  5. Ordered Internal Structure: The atoms within it are arranged in a repeating, orderly, three-dimensional pattern. This is what makes it crystalline.

Substances that have a chemical composition but lack an ordered internal structure (like volcanic glass) are called mineraloids.

Confusing minerals and rocks. A rock is an aggregate (a mixture) of one or more minerals or mineraloids. For example, the rock Granite is composed of the minerals Quartz, Feldspar, and Mica. A mineral is a single, pure "ingredient."
This five-part definition is a classic exam question. Be prepared to list and explain all five criteria.

2. Classification: Rock-Forming vs. Ore-Forming Minerals

Minerals can be broadly grouped based on their abundance and economic value.

Rock-Forming Minerals

These are the minerals that make up the vast majority (over 90%) of the Earth's crust. They are the common "building blocks" of most rocks.

  • Primary Examples: The Silicates are the most dominant group.
    • Quartz (SiO‚ÇÇ)
    • Feldspar Group (e.g., Orthoclase, Plagioclase)
    • Mica Group (e.g., Muscovite, Biotite)
    • Amphibole Group (e.g., Hornblende)
    • Pyroxene Group (e.g., Augite)
    • Olivine
  • Other Common Groups: Carbonates (like Calcite) and Oxides are also important rock-formers in specific environments.

Ore-Forming Minerals

These are minerals from which a metal or other valuable element can be extracted profitably. The key distinction is economic viability.

  • The element of interest is present in high concentration.
  • The mineral can be processed relatively easily to extract the element.
  • A deposit of this mineral is called an ore deposit.

4. Physical Properties of Minerals and Their Significance

Physical properties are the primary tools used to identify minerals in the field (hand-specimen identification). These properties are a direct expression of the mineral's chemical composition and internal crystalline structure.

Color

The first thing you notice, but often the least reliable property.

  • Idiochromatic: Minerals that have a consistent, characteristic color (e.g., Malachite is always green, Azurite is always blue).
  • Allochromatic: Minerals that come in many colors due to tiny impurities (e.g., Quartz can be clear, white, pink, purple, or black).

Streak

The color of a mineral's powder when scraped against an unglazed porcelain plate (a "streak plate").

  • Significance: Far more reliable than color. A mineral's color may vary, but its streak is almost always consistent.
  • Example: Hematite can be black, gray, or red, but it *always* leaves a cherry-red streak. Pyrite ("Fool's Gold") is gold-colored, but its streak is greenish-black.

Luster

The way a mineral's surface reflects light. This is independent of color.

  • Metallic: Looks like polished metal (e.g., Galena, Pyrite).
  • Non-metallic:
    • Vitreous: Glassy (e.g., Quartz)
    • Pearly: Iridescent, like a pearl (e.g., Talc, some Micas)
    • Resinous: Looks like resin or tree sap (e.g., Sphalerite)
    • Silky: Has a fibrous sheen (e.g., Gypsum-satin spar)
    • Waxy / Greasy: Looks like it's coated in oil (e.g., Serpentine)
    • Dull / Earthy: No shine at all (e.g., Kaolinite clay)

Hardness

A mineral's resistance to being scratched. This is measured using Mohs' Scale (see next section).

Crystal Form / Habit

The characteristic external shape a mineral takes if it grows unimpeded. This is the outward expression of its internal atomic arrangement.

  • Examples: Cubic (Pyrite, Halite), Prismatic (Quartz, Tourmaline), Tabular (Mica, Barite), Acicular (needle-like), Botryoidal (grape-like clusters), Fibrous (Asbestos).

Cleavage

The tendency of a mineral to break along flat, parallel planes of weakness in its crystal structure.

  • Description: Described by the number of directions and their angles.
  • Quality: Perfect (Mica), Good (Feldspar), Poor (Beryl).
  • Examples:
    • 1 direction (Basal): Mica (Muscovite, Biotite) splits into thin, flat sheets.
    • 2 directions at ~90¬∞: Feldspar.
    • 3 directions at 90¬∞ (Cubic): Halite, Galena.
    • 3 directions not at 90¬∞ (Rhombohedral): Calcite.

Fracture

How a mineral breaks when it does not break along cleavage planes.

  • Conchoidal: Smooth, curved surfaces like broken glass (e.g., Quartz, Obsidian).
  • Uneven: Rough, irregular surface.
  • Fibrous / Splintery: Breaks into splinters.

Specific Gravity (SG)

The ratio of a mineral's density to the density of water (i.e., how "heavy" it feels for its size).

  • Example: Galena (SG ‚âà 7.5) feels much heavier than Quartz (SG ‚âà 2.65) of the same size. This is a key identifier for metallic ores.

Other Properties

  • Magnetism: Attracted to a magnet (e.g., Magnetite).
  • Reaction to Acid: Fizzes (effervesces) with dilute HCl (e.g., Calcite).
  • Taste: Salty taste (e.g., Halite - *Caution: never taste unknown minerals*).
  • Feel: Greasy or soapy feel (e.g., Talc).

5. Mohs' Scale of Hardness

Developed by Friedrich Mohs, this is a relative scale of hardness from 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest). It's not a linear scale; the difference in hardness between 9 (Corundum) and 10 (Diamond) is greater than the difference between 1 and 9.

The test is simple: a harder mineral will scratch a softer one.

7. Isomorphism

Iso = "same", Morph = "form"

Isomorphism describes a relationship where two or more different minerals have different chemical compositions but the same crystal structure.

  • How it works: This happens when atoms or ions of similar size and charge can freely substitute for one another in the crystal lattice without changing the overall structure.
  • Result: This often creates an isomorphous series or solid solution (see Topic 10).
  • Classic Example: The Olivine Group: (Mg,Fe)‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÑ.
    • Forsterite (Mg‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÑ) and Fayalite (Fe‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÑ) are the two end-members.
    • The Mg¬≤‚Å∫ and Fe¬≤‚Å∫ ions are very similar in size and have the same charge, so they can substitute for each other in any proportion.
    • All minerals in this series (from pure Mg to pure Fe) have the same orthorhombic crystal structure.

8. Polymorphism

Poly = "many", Morph = "form"

Polymorphism is when a single chemical substance (same composition) can exist in two or more different crystal structures. These different structures are called polymorphs.

  • How it works: The specific crystal structure that forms depends on the pressure (P) and temperature (T) conditions during crystallization. Each polymorph is stable in a specific P-T range.
  • Classic Example 1: Carbon (C)
    • Graphite: Hexagonal structure, very soft. Stable at low pressure.
    • Diamond: Cubic structure, very hard. Stable at very high pressure.
  • Classic Example 2: Al‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÖ (Important in metamorphic rocks)
    • Kyanite: Triclinic, stable at high pressure.
    • Andalusite: Orthorhombic, stable at low pressure.
    • Sillimanite: Orthorhombic, stable at high temperature.
Don't confuse Isomorphism and Polymorphism.
  • Isomorphism: Same Structure, Different Chemistry.
  • Polymorphism: Same Chemistry, Different Structure.

9. Pseudomorphism

Pseudo = "false", Morph = "form"

A pseudomorph is a mineral that has the outward crystal form (shape) of one mineral, but is actually composed of a different mineral that has replaced it. It's a "false form."

  • How it works: This occurs through chemical alteration where the original mineral is dissolved and removed, and a new mineral is simultaneously deposited in its place, perfectly preserving the original's shape.
  • Types of Replacement:
    • Alteration: Only partial replacement (e.g., Anhydrite (CaSO‚ÇÑ) altering to Gypsum (CaSO‚ÇÑ¬∑2H‚ÇÇO) by adding water).
    • Substitution: Atom-by-atom replacement (e.g., fossilized wood, where silica replaces the wood fibers).
  • Example: Goethite (FeO(OH)) after Pyrite (FeS‚ÇÇ). You might find a perfect cube, which is Pyrite's crystal form, but the mineral itself is dull, brown Goethite, not shiny, brassy Pyrite. The Pyrite has chemically altered, but its "ghost" shape remains.
This is not just one mineral coating another. It's a complete replacement of the internal substance while retaining the original external shape.

10. Solid Solution

A solid solution refers to a range of chemical compositions in a mineral, resulting from the substitutional replacement of one type of ion for another in the crystal lattice.

Instead of a fixed formula (like Quartz, SiO‚ÇÇ), minerals in a solid solution series have a variable formula written with parentheses.

  • Conditions for Substitution:
    1. The substituting ions must have similar ionic radii (size).
    2. The substituting ions must have the same or similar ionic charge. If charges are different, another substitution must happen elsewhere to maintain charge neutrality.
  • Types:
    • Complete Solid Solution (Isomorphous Series): Substitution can occur in any proportion (from 0% to 100%). Example: The Olivine series (Mg¬≤‚Å∫, Fe¬≤‚Å∫)‚ÇÇSiO‚ÇÑ.
    • Partial (Limited) Solid Solution: Substitution is only possible up to a certain percentage.
  • Classic Example: The Plagioclase Feldspar Series. This is a complex substitution.
    • Albite (Na-rich): NaAlSi‚ÇÉO‚Çà
    • Anorthite (Ca-rich): CaAl‚ÇÇSi‚ÇÇO‚Çà

    Here, a coupled substitution of (Na‚Å∫ + Si‚Å¥‚Å∫) is replaced by (Ca¬≤‚Å∫ + Al¬≥‚Å∫). The size is similar, and the total charge (+5) is balanced in both cases.

11. Exsolution

Exsolution is the "un-mixing" of a solid solution that was stable at high temperatures, but becomes unstable as it cools down. The single mineral separates into two or more distinct mineral phases.

  • How it works:
    1. At high temperature, the crystal lattice is expanded and can accommodate differently-sized ions (forming a solid solution).
    2. As the mineral cools, the lattice contracts. The "misfit" ions, which are now too large or too small, are "kicked out" or segregated.
    3. These segregated ions form their own tiny, distinct layers or lenses within the host mineral. These are called lamellae.
  • Example: Perthite (an Alkali Feldspar)
    • At high-T, K-Feldspar (Orthoclase) and Na-Feldspar (Albite) can form a complete solid solution.
    • As this solution cools, it unmixes.
    • The result (Perthite) is a host crystal of K-Feldspar (Orthoclase) with intergrown lamellae (streaks) of Na-Feldspar (Albite).
    • This un-mixing is what often gives minerals like Moonstone (a variety of feldspar) its beautiful "schiller" or adularescence (play of light).
Think of solid solution and exsolution as opposites.
  • Solid Solution: "Mixing" of ions into one crystal, usually stable at high-T.
  • Exsolution: "Un-mixing" of ions into two phases upon cooling.
