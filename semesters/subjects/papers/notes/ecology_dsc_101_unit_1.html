1. Introduction to Ecology

Ecology is the scientific study of the relationships and interactions between organisms and their environment. The term "ecology" was coined by the German biologist Ernst Haeckel in 1866, derived from the Greek words Oikos (meaning 'house' or 'dwelling') and Logos (meaning 'study').

The environment includes:

  • Biotic factors: All the living components, such as plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria.
  • Abiotic factors: All the non-living chemical and physical components, such as sunlight, temperature, water, soil, and wind.

Ecology seeks to understand the distribution, abundance, and interactions of organisms, and the flow of energy and materials within ecosystems.

2. Levels of Organization

Ecology studies life at various levels of complexity. These levels are hierarchical, meaning each level builds upon the one before it.

Diagram: A pyramid or flowchart showing the levels of ecological organization, starting from the individual organism and building up to the biosphere.
  1. Organism (Individual): The fundamental unit of ecology. It studies how an individual organism adapts to its environment (morphology, physiology, behavior).
  2. Population: A group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time. Ecology at this level focuses on population size, density, growth, and regulation.
  3. Community: A group of different populations (different species) interacting in a specific area. This level focuses on species interactions (e.g., predation, competition).
  4. Ecosystem: The community of living organisms (biotic) interacting with their non-living (abiotic) environment as a system. This level focuses on energy flow and nutrient cycling.
  5. Biome: A large collection of ecosystems that share similar climatic conditions and have a dominant type of vegetation. Examples include tundra, desert, rainforest, and grasslands.
  6. Biosphere: The highest level of organization. It is the sum of all parts of the Earth where life exists, including land, water, and the atmosphere.
Exam Tip: Be able to clearly define each level and explain the key difference between them. For example, a population is single-species, while a community is multi-species. An ecosystem adds the abiotic factors to the community.

3. Habitat and Ecological Niche

These two concepts are fundamental but often confused. They describe an organism's place in the environment.

Habitat

The habitat is the physical place or environment where an organism lives. It's the organism's "address." It provides all the resources the organism needs to survive, such as food, water, and shelter.

  • Example: The habitat of a polar bear is the Arctic sea ice. The habitat of a pitcher plant is a nutrient-poor bog.

Ecological Niche

The ecological niche is a more complex concept. It describes the functional role and position of a species within an ecosystem. It includes:

  • What it eats (its trophic level).
  • Who eats it.
  • Its relationships with other species (e.g., competition, mutualism).
  • Its tolerance to abiotic factors (e.g., temperature range, humidity).

In short, the niche is the organism's "profession" or "lifestyle."

G.E. Hutchinson (1957) defined the niche as an "n-dimensional hypervolume," where each dimension represents an environmental factor or resource required by the species.

Types of Niches:

  • Fundamental Niche: The full range of environmental conditions and resources an organism could *potentially* occupy and use, in the absence of competition from other species.
  • Realized Niche: The part of the fundamental niche that an organism *actually* occupies as a result of limiting factors, especially competition. The realized niche is always smaller than or equal to the fundamental niche.
Diagram: Two overlapping circles. One large circle represents the Fundamental Niche of Species A. A smaller circle inside it, partially overlapped by the Fundamental Niche of Species B, represents the Realized Niche of Species A.
Common Question: Differentiate between habitat and niche.
  • Habitat = Address (Where it lives)
  • Niche = Profession (What it does, its role, and its resource requirements)
  • Analogy: A single habitat (like an apartment building) can contain many different niches (plumber, teacher, artist), just as a single tree (habitat) can support many insect species (niches).

4. Ecosystem Stability: Resistance & Resilience

Ecosystem stability refers to the ability of an ecosystem to maintain its structure and function over time, especially when faced with disturbances (like fires, floods, or pollution).

Stability has two main components:

  • Resistance: The ability of an ecosystem to avoid displacement or change when faced with a disturbance. A highly resistant ecosystem changes very little.
    • Example: A mature forest with large trees may be highly resistant to a mild windstorm.
  • Resilience: The ability of an ecosystem to recover quickly and return to its original state *after* it has been disturbed.
    • Example: A grassland may not be resistant to fire (it burns easily), but it is very resilient and grows back quickly within a few weeks or months.
Diagram: A graph of "Ecosystem State" vs. "Time."
1. Resistant system: A disturbance occurs (a dip), but the dip is very shallow.
2. Resilient system: A disturbance occurs (a deep dip), but the line quickly returns to the original level.
3. Non-stable system: A disturbance occurs (a deep dip), and the line settles at a new, lower level (a "regime shift").

An ecosystem can be resistant but not resilient, or resilient but not resistant. For example, a coral reef is resistant to small waves but has very low resilience if it gets bleached by high temperatures (it takes decades to recover).

5. Branches of Ecology: Autecology & Synecology

Ecology can be broadly divided based on the level of organization being studied.

Autecology (Species Ecology)

This is the study of a single species (or individual organism) and its relationship with its environment. It focuses on the adaptations, life history, and population dynamics of that one species.

  • Questions asked: What is the temperature tolerance of this plant? How does this animal find a mate? What factors limit the population size of this specific butterfly?

Synecology (Community Ecology)

This is the study of groups of organisms (communities) and their relationships with each other and their environment. It focuses on the composition, structure, and development of communities.

  • Questions asked: How do predators affect the diversity of prey species in this forest? What are the patterns of energy flow in this lake? How does succession proceed after a volcanic eruption?
Feature Autecology Synecology
Unit of Study Individual or Population (single species) Community or Ecosystem (multiple species)
Focus Adaptation, life history, population dynamics Species interactions, community structure, energy flow
Example A study of the water needs of a saguaro cactus. A study of all plant and animal interactions in the Sonoran Desert.

6. Major Terrestrial Biomes

Biomes are very large ecological areas on the Earth's surface, defined by their dominant vegetation and adaptations to the regional climate. Climate, particularly temperature and precipitation, is the primary factor determining a biome.

Diagram: A graph showing the distribution of major biomes based on "Average Annual Precipitation" (x-axis) and "Average Annual Temperature" (y-axis).
Biome Climate Dominant Vegetation Key Characteristics
Tropical Rainforest Hot and very wet (high rainfall) all year. Broadleaf evergreen trees, vines, epiphytes. Highest biodiversity. Multi-layered canopy. Nutrient-poor soil (nutrients are in the biomass).
Savanna (Tropical Grassland) Hot, with distinct wet and dry seasons. Grasses with scattered trees (e.g., Acacia). Supports large grazing mammals. Fire is an important abiotic factor.
Desert Very dry (low precipitation). Can be hot or cold. Succulents (e.g., cacti), drought-tolerant shrubs. Organisms have water-saving adaptations (e.g., nocturnal behavior, waxy leaves).
Temperate Grassland (Prairie/Steppe) Moderate precipitation, with hot summers and cold winters. Grasses and forbs (non-woody flowering plants). Very fertile soil. Adapted to grazing and fire.
Temperate Deciduous Forest Moderate precipitation, with four distinct seasons. Broadleaf deciduous trees (e.g., oak, maple, beech). Trees lose leaves in winter. Rich soil from decomposing leaf litter.
Taiga (Boreal Forest) Cold, with short summers and long, severe winters. Coniferous evergreen trees (e.g., spruce, fir, pine). Largest terrestrial biome. Acidic, nutrient-poor soil (needles decompose slowly).
Tundra Extremely cold and dry (like a "cold desert"). Low-growing mosses, lichens, sedges, dwarf shrubs. Permafrost (permanently frozen subsoil) is a key feature. Very short growing season.
