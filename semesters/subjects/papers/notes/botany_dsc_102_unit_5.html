Role of fungi in biotechnology

Fungi are "metabolic factories" that are easily grown in large bioreactors. This makes them ideal for industrial biotechnology.

  • Enzyme Production: Fungi are a major source of industrial enzymes.
    • Amylases (from Aspergillus oryzae) break down starch. Used in baking and brewing.
    • Pectinases (from Aspergillus niger) break down pectin. Used to clarify fruit juices.
    • Cellulases (from Trichoderma) break down cellulose. Used in animal feed, stone-washed jeans, and biofuel production.
  • Organic Acid Production:
    • Citric Acid: Produced by Aspergillus niger. Used as a flavoring and preservative in soft drinks, jams, and candies.
  • Genetic Engineering: Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) is a key model organism in molecular biology. As a simple eukaryote, it is used as a "host" to produce valuable recombinant proteins like the Hepatitis B vaccine and human insulin.

Application of fungi in food industry

Flavour & processing

Fungal enzymes are key to processing many foods.

  • Soy Sauce: A paste of soybeans and wheat is fermented by Aspergillus oryzae. The fungus's enzymes (proteases, amylases) break down proteins and starches, creating the characteristic flavor, color, and aroma.
  • Tempeh: Soybeans are fermented with Rhizopus oligosporus, which binds the beans together into a solid cake.

Mushroom

Mushrooms are the edible fruiting bodies (basidiocarps) of fungi (like Agaricus). They are cultivated worldwide as a food source, valued for their protein content, vitamins (B vitamins), and umami flavor.

Yeast

Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most economically important fungus.

  • Baking: Yeast ferments sugars in dough, producing Carbon Dioxide (CO2) and ethanol. The CO2 gas gets trapped, causing the bread to rise. The ethanol evaporates during baking.
  • Brewing (Beer & Wine): Yeast ferments the sugars (from grapes or barley) into Ethanol (alcohol) and CO2.

Cheese

Fungi are essential for ripening many "gourmet" cheeses.

  • Blue Cheese (e.g., Roquefort, Stilton): Spores of Penicillium roqueforti are added to the cheese curds. The fungus grows, producing the characteristic blue-green veins and strong flavor.
  • Soft-Ripened Cheese (e.g., Brie, Camembert): The surface of the cheese is treated with Penicillium camemberti, which forms a white, moldy rind and breaks down proteins, giving a soft, creamy texture.

Fermentation

This is the general metabolic process (anaerobic breakdown of sugar) that fungi, especially yeasts, use in baking, brewing, and industrial alcohol production. It is the foundation of their role in the food and beverage industry.

Pharmaceutical applications

Fungi produce a wide range of "secondary metabolites" to compete with other microbes. Humans have harnessed these chemicals as medicines.

Pharmaceutical Preparations

  • Antibiotics:
    • Penicillin: The first antibiotic, discovered by Alexander Fleming. It is produced by the mold Penicillium chrysogenum (formerly *P. notatum*). It revolutionized medicine by allowing doctors to treat bacterial infections.
    • Griseofulvin: An anti-fungal drug (used to treat skin infections like ringworm) also produced by a species of Penicillium.
  • Alkaloids:
    • Ergot Alkaloids: Produced by Claviceps purpurea, a fungus that infects rye grain (causing "ergotism"). These toxic alkaloids, in small, purified doses, are used in medicine (e.g., Ergometrine) to control bleeding after childbirth and treat migraines.
  • Immunosuppressants:
    • Cyclosporin: A vital drug used to prevent the rejection of transplanted organs. It is produced by the fungus Tolypocladium inflatum.
  • Statins:
    • Lovastatin: A cholesterol-lowering drug, originally isolated from Aspergillus terreus.

Agriculture (Biofertilizers)

Fungi play a crucial role in soil health and plant growth, which can be leveraged in agriculture.

  • Mycorrhiza: As discussed in Unit 3, Arbuscular Mycorrhiza (AM) fungi are the single most important biofertilizer.
  • How they work: The fungal hyphae act as a massive extension to the plant's root system, "mining" the soil for nutrients, especially phosphorus.
  • Application: Farmers can buy "inoculum" of mycorrhizal spores and add it to soil or potting mix to enhance plant growth, reduce the need for chemical phosphate fertilizers, and improve drought resistance.
  • Biological Control: Some fungi (e.g., Trichoderma) are used as "biocontrol agents" because they parasitize and kill disease-causing fungi in the soil.

Mycotoxins

Mycotoxins: Toxic secondary metabolites produced by fungi (molds) that contaminate food and feed crops.

Mycotoxins are a major agricultural and health problem. They are poisonous to humans and livestock, even in very small quantities.

  • Aflatoxins: The most famous and dangerous mycotoxins.
    • Produced by: Aspergillus flavus and Aspergillus parasiticus.
    • Source: Commonly contaminate improperly stored crops like peanuts, corn (maize), and cottonseed.
    • Effect: They are potent carcinogens (cancer-causing), primarily targeting the liver.
  • Ergotism: A disease caused by ingesting ergot alkaloids from Claviceps purpurea in contaminated rye. It causes hallucinations, gangrene, and convulsions (known as "St. Anthony's Fire" in the Middle Ages).

Medical mycology

Medical Mycology: The study of fungal diseases (mycoses) in humans and animals.

While most fungi are harmless, some can cause disease, especially in individuals with weakened immune systems (immunocompromised).

  • Superficial Mycoses: Infections of the skin, hair, and nails.
    • Dermatophytes: Fungi that cause Ringworm and Athlete's Foot.
  • Subcutaneous Mycoses: Infections of the tissues beneath the skin.
  • Systemic (Deep) Mycoses: The most serious type, where the fungus spreads deep within the body and affects major organs (e.g., lungs).
    • Candidiasis: An opportunistic infection caused by the yeast Candida albicans (e.g., "thrush").
    • Aspergillosis: A lung infection caused by inhaling Aspergillus spores.

Mushroom cultivation

This is the agricultural practice of growing mushrooms (edible fungi) for food. The syllabus lists this twice, but it is a key part of the food industry application.

The general process involves:

  1. Spawn Preparation: "Spawn" is the fungal mycelium growing on a sterile carrier, like grain. This is the "seed."
  2. Substrate Preparation: A nutrient-rich medium (substrate) is prepared. This is different for each mushroom.
    • Button Mushroom (Agaricus bisporus): Requires composted manure and straw.
    • Oyster Mushroom (Pleurotus): Can grow on simple agricultural waste like rice straw or sawdust.
  3. Inoculation: The spawn is mixed with the pasteurized substrate. The mycelium is allowed to grow and colonize the substrate (spawn run).
  4. Casing: A layer of soil or peat is applied on top of the colonized substrate. This helps to trigger the formation of fruiting bodies.
  5. Fruiting & Harvesting: By controlling temperature, humidity, and CO2 levels, the mushroom (basidiocarps) are induced to form. They are then harvested in "flushes."
