Pteridophytes

Pteridophytes (e.g., ferns, clubmosses, horsetails) are the first vascular land plants (tracheophytes). They are "Archegoniates" (like Bryophytes) but are more advanced.

General characteristics, Classification (upto orders) and economic importance

General Characteristics

  • Dominant Phase: The sporophyte (2n) is the dominant, independent, photosynthetic, and long-lived phase. This is a major advancement over Bryophytes.
  • Dependent Phase: The gametophyte (n) (called a prothallus) is simple, small, free-living, and short-lived.
  • Vascular Tissue: They are the first plants to possess true vascular tissues (xylem and phloem). This allows for efficient transport of water and food, and for greater height.
  • True Roots/Stems/Leaves: The sporophyte body is differentiated into king true roots, stems (often a rhizome), and leaves (fronds).
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By spores (n), which are produced by meiosis in sporangia. Sporangia are often clustered on leaves (sporophylls).
    • Sexual: The haploid prothallus (n) bears the sex organs (antheridia and archegonia). They still require water for fertilization, as the sperm are flagellated.
  • Alternation of Generations: They show a true heteromorphic alternation of generations, with both phases being independent (at maturity).

Classification (upto orders)

Pteridophytes are classified into four main divisions:

  1. Division Psilophyta: (e.g., Rhynia, Psilotum). Most primitive. No true roots, leaves are scale-like.
    • Order: Psilophytales
  2. Division Lycophyta: (Club mosses). Have small leaves (microphylls).
    • Order: Lycopodiales (e.g., Lycopodium), Selaginellales (e.g., Selaginella)
  3. Division Sphenophyta: (Horsetails). Stems are jointed, with whorls of scale-like leaves.
    • Order: Equisetales (e.g., Equisetum)
  4. Division Pterophyta: (True Ferns). Have large, complex leaves (megaphylls or fronds).
    • Order: Filicales (e.g., Dryopteris, Pteris)

Economic Importance

  • Ornamentals: Many ferns (e.g., Boston fern) are grown as ornamental plants.
  • Soil Binders: Their rhizomes help in soil conservation.
  • Fossil Fuels: The great pteridophyte forests of the Carboniferous period formed today's coal deposits.
  • Biofertilizer: The aquatic fern Azolla hosts a nitrogen-fixing cyanobacterium (*Anabaena*) and is used as a biofertilizer in rice paddies.

Heterospory and seed habit

Homospory vs. Heterospory

  • Homospory: The production of only one type of spore. These spores are all the same size. They germinate to form a monoecious (bisexual) prothallus that bears both antheridia and archegonia. (e.g., Lycopodium, most ferns).
  • Heterospory: The production of two different types of spores in two different types of sporangia. This is a crucial evolutionary advancement. (e.g., Selaginella, *Marsilea*).
    • Microspores: Small, numerous spores produced in microsporangia. They germinate to form a small, reduced male gametophyte (n).
    • Megaspores: Large, few spores produced in megasporangia. They contain stored food and germinate to form a female gametophyte (n).

Seed Habit

A "seed" is a fertilized, protected, and nourished ovule. Selaginella is not a seed plant, but it shows several key pre-requisites for the evolution of the seed, known as the "seed habit":

  1. Heterospory: The production of two spore types (micro- and megaspores) is the first step.
  2. Retention of Megaspore: The single, functional megaspore is not dispersed. It is retained *within* the megasporangium (which is like a "nucellus").
  3. Endosporic Gametophyte: The female gametophyte develops *inside* the megaspore wall (endosporic development), while it is still in the sporangium.
  4. Fertilization in situ: Fertilization (sperm swimming to the egg) occurs *while* the female gametophyte is still attached to the parent sporophyte.
  5. Embryo Development: The new embryo (2n sporophyte) develops *inside* the female gametophyte, still attached to the old parent, receiving protection and nutrition.

What's missing for a true seed? A protective seed coat (integument) around the megasporangium, and a period of dormancy.

Early land plants - Rhynia

  • Status: A fossil pteridophyte from the Devonian period. One of the oldest and simplest known vascular plants.
  • Sporophyte (2n):
    • Body: Simple, dichotomously branched, cylindrical, leafless stem.
    • Roots: No true roots; had rhizoids for anchorage.
    • Vascular Tissue: Had a simple, solid core of xylem (a protostele).
    • Reproduction: Bore simple, oval sporangia at the tips of the branches. It was homosporous.
  • Significance: It provides a "snapshot" of what the earliest vascular land plants looked like.

Morphology, anatomy and reproduction of Lycopodium and Selaginella

Lycopodium (Club Moss)

  • Status: Homosporous pteridophyte.
  • Morphology: The sporophyte (2n) has a stem (creeping or erect) densely covered with small, simple leaves called microphylls. Has true roots.
  • Anatomy: The stem has a simple protostele (a solid core of xylem, often star-shaped).
  • Reproduction:
    • Sporangia are borne on the upper surface of specialized leaves (sporophylls).
    • In many species, the sporophylls are aggregated at the branch tips to form a cone-like strobilus.
    • All spores are of one type (homospores).
    • Spores (n) germinate (often after years, with a fungus) to form a simple, free-living, bisexual prothallus (n).
    • Fertilization requires water, zygote (2n) grows into the new sporophyte.

Selaginella (Spike Moss)

  • Status: Heterosporous pteridophyte (shows the seed habit).
  • Morphology: The sporophyte (2n) is a branching stem, typically prostrate, with four rows of leaves (two large, two small). A unique, leafless, root-bearing organ called a rhizophore grows down from the stem. A small, scale-like appendage called a ligule is present on each leaf (a defining feature).
  • Anatomy: Stem anatomy is variable, often a protostele.
  • Reproduction:
    • Reproduction is by a terminal strobilus (cone).
    • The strobilus contains two types of sporophylls:
      • Microsporophylls: Bear microsporangia containing many small microspores (n).
      • Megasporophylls: Bear megasporangia containing (usually) four large megaspores (n).
    • Gametophyte Development:
      • Male Gametophyte (n): Develops *inside* the microspore (endosporic). It is extremely reduced, consisting of just an antheridium that produces biflagellate sperm.
      • Female Gametophyte (n): Develops *inside* the megaspore (endosporic). It bursts the megaspore wall, exposing a few archegonia.
    • Fertilization: Occurs after the spores are shed (or sometimes on the plant). Sperm swim to the archegonium, forming a zygote (2n) that develops into the new sporophyte.

Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms (e.g., conifers, cycads) are "Archegoniates" that are more advanced than Pteridophytes. Their name means "naked seed" because their ovules (which become seeds) are not enclosed within an ovary (fruit).

General characteristics, Classification (upto orders) and economic importance

General Characteristics

  • Dominant Phase: The sporophyte (2n) is dominant, independent, and highly complex (e.g., shrubs or large trees).
  • Vascular Tissue: Highly developed. Xylem consists mainly of tracheids (vessels are absent, except in Gnetum). Phloem lacks companion cells.
  • Gametophyte Phase: The gametophyte (n) is extremely reduced and completely dependent on the sporophyte.
    • Male Gametophyte: Reduced to a pollen grain.
    • Female Gametophyte: Reduced to a small structure (endosperm) inside the ovule.
  • Reproduction:
    • They are heterosporous (microspores and megaspores).
    • Pollination: The transfer of pollen grains (male gametophyte) to the ovule. This is typically by wind (anemophily). Water is not required for fertilization.
    • Ovule: The megasporangium (nucellus) is enclosed by a protective layer called an integument. This entire structure is the ovule.
    • Seed: After fertilization, the ovule develops into a seed (a dormant embryo, stored food, and a seed coat).

Classification (upto orders)

Gymnosperms are classified into four main divisions:

  1. Division Cycadophyta: (Cycads). Palm-like, stout stems, large compound leaves.
    • Order: Cycadales (e.g., Cycas)
  2. Division Ginkgophyta: Only one living species. Fan-shaped leaves.
    • Order: Ginkgoales (e.g., Ginkgo)
  3. Division Coniferophyta: (Conifers). Needle-like or scale-like leaves. Cone-bearing.
    • Order: Coniferales (e.g., Pinus)
  4. Division Gnetophyta: Advanced group with some angiosperm-like features (e.g., vessels in xylem).
    • Order: Gnetales (e.g., Gnetum), Ephedrales

Economic Importance

  • Timber: Conifers (e.g., Pine, Fir, Deodar) are the most important source of softwood timber for construction, furniture, and paper pulp.
  • Resins: Conifer resin is harvested to produce turpentine and rosin (used in paints, varnishes).
  • Food: Seeds of some species are edible (e.g., "pine nuts," sago from Cycas).
  • Medicine: Ephedra produces the drug ephedrine. Taxus (Yew) produces Taxol, an anti-cancer drug.
  • Ornamentals: Many conifers and cycads are used in landscaping.

Morphology, anatomy and reproduction of Cycas, Pinus, Ginkgo and Gnetum

Cycas (Division: Cycadophyta)

  • Morphology: A "living fossil." Has a stout, unbranched, columnar stem (like a palm tree) with a crown of large, pinnately compound leaves.
  • Anatomy: Stem has a large pith and cortex with many mucilage canals. Vascular bundles are in a ring. Leaves are xerophytic (thick cuticle, sunken stomata). Has unique coralloid roots (symbiotic with N2-fixing Cyanobacteria).
