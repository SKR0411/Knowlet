Fungi: General Characteristics

Mycology: The branch of biology that deals with the study of fungi.

Fungi are a large, diverse group of eukaryotic organisms that are distinct from plants and animals.

  • Eukaryotic: Possess a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.
  • Achlorophyllous & Heterotrophic: They lack chlorophyll and cannot make their own food. They obtain nutrients by absorption.
  • Body Form (Thallus):
    • Yeast: Unicellular, non-filamentous, oval/spherical fungi (e.g., Saccharomyces).
    • Mold: Filamentous fungi. The body consists of a network of long, thread-like structures called hyphae. The entire network of hyphae is called a mycelium.
  • Hyphae Types:
    • Aseptate (or Coenocytic): Hyphae that lack cross-walls (septa). They are multinucleate (e.g., *Mucor*).
    • Septate: Hyphae that are divided by cross-walls (septa) into individual cells (e.g., *Aspergillus*, *Agaricus*).
  • Cell Wall: Composed primarily of Chitin (a nitrogen-containing polysaccharide).
  • Reserve Food: Stored as Glycogen and Oil (not starch).
  • Reproduction: By vegetative, asexual (spores), and sexual (spores) means.
Fungi vs. Plants vs. Animals
  • Like plants, they have cell walls and are non-motile (mostly).
  • Like animals, they are heterotrophic and store glycogen.
  • They are unique in having chitin cell walls and absorptive nutrition.

Nutrition in Fungi

Fungi are chemoheterotrophs. They secrete digestive enzymes into their environment (external digestion) and then absorb the resulting small organic molecules.

  1. Saprophytes (or Saprobes): Obtain nutrients from dead and decaying organic matter. They are the primary decomposers of the ecosystem (e.g., Mucor, Agaricus).
  2. Parasites: Obtain nutrients from living hosts, often causing disease.
    • Obligate Parasites: Can only grow on a living host (e.g., *Puccinia* - rust fungi).
    • Facultative Parasites: Can live as saprophytes but can also attack living hosts (e.g., *Phytophthora*).
  3. Symbionts (Mutualists): Live in a mutually beneficial relationship with another organism.
    • Lichens: Symbiosis with an alga.
    • Mycorrhiza: Symbiosis with the roots of higher plants.

Classification (Ainsworth)

G.C. Ainsworth (1973) proposed a modern classification of fungi based on their morphology, especially their reproductive structures (spores, fruiting bodies).

He divided the Kingdom Fungi into two main divisions:

Division 1: Myxomycota (Slime Molds)

Division 2: Eumycota (True Fungi)

These fungi have cell walls, (usually) a filamentous thallus, and absorptive nutrition. This division is split into five subdivisions:

Subdivision Common Name Key Characteristics
Mastigomycotina "Lower Fungi" Produce motile spores (zoospores) with flagella. Hyphae are often coenocytic. (e.g., *Phytophthora*, *Albugo*).
Zygomycotina "Conjugation Fungi" Sexual reproduction by the fusion of two gametangia to form a thick-walled resting spore called a zygospore. Hyphae are coenocytic. (e.g., *Mucor*, *Rhizopus*).
Ascomycotina "Sac Fungi" Sexual spores (ascospores) are produced *internally* in a sac-like structure called an ascus. Hyphae are septate. (e.g., Yeast, *Aspergillus*, *Penicillium*).
Basidiomycotina "Club Fungi" Sexual spores (basidiospores) are produced *externally* on a club-shaped structure called a basidium. Hyphae are septate. (e.g., Mushrooms, Rusts, Smuts).
Deuteromycotina "Fungi Imperfecti" An artificial "holding" group for fungi in which no sexual reproduction (teleomorph) has been observed. They reproduce only by asexual spores (conidia). (e.g., *Alternaria*, *Fusarium*).

Lichen

Occurrence and General Characteristics

Lichen: A composite, dual organism resulting from a symbiotic (mutualistic) association between a fungus and an alga.
  • Mycobiont: The fungal partner (usually an Ascomycete). Provides structure, protection from desiccation, and absorbs water/minerals.
  • Phycobiont: The algal partner (either a green alga like Trebouxia or a cyanobacterium like Nostoc). Provides food for both partners via photosynthesis.

Occurrence: Lichens are hardy pioneer organisms. They can grow in extreme environments where neither partner could survive alone (e.g., bare rocks, tundra, tree bark).

Thallus Forms:

  1. Crustose: A thin, flat crust, strongly attached to the substrate (e.g., on rocks).
  2. Foliose: A flat, broad, leaf-like thallus, loosely attached to the substrate (e.g., Parmelia).
  3. Fruticose: A shrubby, branched, cylindrical or ribbon-like thallus (e.g., Usnea - "Old man's beard").

Reproduction

  • Vegetative: The primary method, as it propagates *both* partners together.
    • Fragmentation: Thallus breaks into pieces.
    • Soredia: Microscopic, dust-like propagules containing a few algal cells wrapped in fungal hyphae. They are light and easily dispersed by wind.
    • Isidia: Small, finger-like outgrowths from the thallus that break off.
  • Sexual: Only the fungal partner reproduces sexually (e.g., by forming apothecia, which produce ascospores). The resulting spore must land and find a compatible algal partner to form a new lichen, which is a rare event.

Economic Importance

  • Pioneer Species: Lichens colonize bare rock and secrete acids, beginning the process of soil formation.
  • Pollution Indicators: Most lichens are extremely sensitive to air pollution, especially sulfur dioxide (SO2). Their absence in a city indicates poor air quality.
  • Food: "Reindeer moss" (Cladonia) is a staple food for reindeer in the tundra.
  • Dyes: Litmus (the pH indicator) is traditionally extracted from Roccella.
  • Perfumes: Some species (e.g., Usnea) produce aromatic compounds used in the perfume industry.

Mycorrhiza

Types and Significance

Mycorrhiza ("fungus-root"): A symbiotic (mutualistic) association between a fungus and the roots of higher plants (found in >90% of all land plants).

The Mutualism:

  • The plant provides the fungus with sugars (carbon) from photosynthesis.
  • The fungus provides the plant with mineral nutrients (especially phosphorus) and water, which it "mines" from the soil more efficiently than roots alone.

Types of Mycorrhiza

  1. Ectomycorrhiza (ECM):
    • The fungal hyphae form a dense sheath (mantle) *around* the outside of the root.
    • Hyphae also grow *between* the root cortical cells, forming a network called the Hartig Net.
    • They do not penetrate the plant cells.
    • Common on woody plants like pine, oak, and birch.
  2. Endomycorrhiza (Arbuscular Mycorrhiza - AM):
    • The fungal hyphae do not form an outer sheath.
    • They penetrate *into* the root cortical cells.
    • Inside the cells, they form highly branched, tree-like structures called arbuscules, which are the primary sites of nutrient exchange.
    • Extremely common, found in most crops and herbaceous plants.

Significance in Agriculture

  • Enhanced Nutrient Uptake: The fine hyphae act as an extension of the root system, drastically increasing the absorption of phosphorus (which is often immobile in soil).
  • Drought Tolerance: The hyphal network helps plants absorb and retain water.
  • Pathogen Resistance: The fungal association can help protect the plant from root pathogens.
  • Biofertilizers: AM fungi are now cultured and sold as biofertilizers to improve crop yield with less chemical fertilizer input.

Role of fungi with algae and plants

This is a summary of the symbiotic roles of fungi.

  • With Algae (Lichens): The fungus (mycobiont) provides a protected, stable environment (structure, water retention) for the alga (phycobiont), allowing it to live in harsh terrestrial habitats. The alga provides photosynthetic food. This is a classic example of mutualism.
  • With Plants (Mycorrhiza): The fungus (mycorrhiza) acts as an extension of the plant's root system, enhancing nutrient (especially P) and water uptake. The plant provides the fungus with sugars. This symbiosis was critical for the colonization of land by early plants.
  • With Plants (Endophytes): These are fungi that live *inside* plant tissues (leaves, stems) without causing any visible disease. They often produce alkaloids that can protect the host plant from being eaten by insects or grazing animals.
