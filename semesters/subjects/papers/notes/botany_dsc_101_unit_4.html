Microbial spoilage of foods

Food spoilage is the process where a food product becomes unsuitable for consumption. This is caused by the metabolic activities of microorganisms (bacteria, yeasts, molds) as they grow on the food, producing undesirable byproducts like gases, acids, and toxins, which change the food's odor, taste, texture, and appearance.

Examples:

  • Molds growing on bread or cheese.
  • Bacteria causing milk to sour (producing lactic acid).
  • Yeasts fermenting sugars in fruit juices.

Preservation of foods (food poisoning)

Food preservation techniques aim to stop or slow down microbial growth, extending the shelf life of food and preventing spoilage and foodborne illness.

Methods of Food Preservation

  • High Temperature:
    • Canning: Food is sealed in an airtight can and heated (sterilized) to kill all microbes, including endospores.
    • Pasteurization: (See next topic). Kills pathogens but not all microbes.
  • Low Temperature:
    • Refrigeration: Slows down microbial metabolism and reproduction.
    • Freezing: Stops microbial growth completely (but doesn't necessarily kill them).
  • Dehydration (Drying): Removing water. Microbes need water to grow. (e.g., dried fruits, beef jerky).
  • Chemical Preservation:
    • High Sugar/Salt: Creates a hypertonic environment (high osmotic pressure) that draws water out of microbial cells, inhibiting growth (e.g., jams, pickles).
    • Acidification: Adding acid (like vinegar) lowers the pH, inhibiting most microbes (e.g., pickled vegetables).
    • Preservatives: Chemicals like sodium benzoate or nitrates.

Food Poisoning (Foodborne Illness)

This is illness caused by consuming contaminated food. There are two main types:

  1. Food Infection: Caused by ingesting living pathogens, which then grow in your gut and cause disease (e.g., Salmonella, Listeria).
  2. Food Intoxication: Caused by ingesting pre-formed toxins produced by microbes growing in the food (e.g., Staphylococcus aureus toxin, Clostridium botulinum toxin). The live microbe may even be dead, but the toxin remains.

Pasteurization of milk

Pasteurization: A process of mild heating applied to liquids (like milk, juice) to kill pathogenic (disease-causing) microbes and reduce the number of spoilage microbes.
Crucial Point: Pasteurization is NOT sterilization. It does not kill all microbes (especially not thermoduric bacteria or endospores), which is why pasteurized milk still needs to be refrigerated and will eventually spoil.

Common methods for milk:

  • HTST (High-Temperature Short-Time): The most common method. Milk is heated to 72°C for 15 seconds, then rapidly cooled.
  • UHT (Ultra-High-Temperature): Milk is heated to 138-150°C for 1-2 seconds. This sterilizes the milk, giving it a very long shelf life at room temperature (until opened).

Fermented foods (Cheese, Yogurt, Bread)

Fermentation is a metabolic process where microbes (bacteria or yeast) convert carbohydrates (like sugar) into alcohol, gases, or organic acids in the absence of oxygen.

Cheese

Cheese is a way to preserve milk proteins.

  1. Curdling: Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) (e.g., Lactococcus) are added to milk. They ferment lactose into lactic acid, which lowers the pH. The enzyme rennet (chymosin) is then added to curdle the milk protein (casein), separating it into solid curds and liquid whey.
  2. Processing: The curds are cut, cooked, and pressed to remove more whey.
  3. Ripening: The solid curd is aged (ripened) for weeks, months, or years. Other microbes (bacteria or fungi like Penicillium for blue cheese) grow and produce enzymes that break down proteins and fats, creating the final flavor and texture.

Yogurt

Yogurt is made by fermenting milk with a specific starter culture containing two bacteria in a symbiotic relationship:

  • Streptococcus thermophilus
  • Lactobacillus bulgaricus

These bacteria ferment lactose into lactic acid, which curdles the milk and gives yogurt its tart flavor and thick texture.

Bread

Bread making relies on the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae (baker's yeast).

  1. Yeast is mixed with flour (starch), sugar, and water.
  2. The yeast performs alcoholic fermentation on the sugars.
  3. Reaction: C6H12O6 → 2 C2H5OH (Ethanol) + 2 CO2 (Carbon Dioxide)
  4. The CO2 gas gets trapped in the dough, causing it to "rise." The ethanol evaporates during baking.

Alcohol (Ethanol) fermentation

This is the same process used in bread making, but the goal is to harvest the ethanol. It is carried out by yeast, primarily Saccharomyces cerevisiae (brewer's or distiller's yeast), under anaerobic (oxygen-free) conditions.

The substrate (starting sugar) determines the product:

  • Grapes (sugar) → Wine
  • Barley (starch) → Beer (starch is first broken into sugar)
  • Corn, Sugarcane (starch/sugar) → Biofuel (Ethanol)

The process is limited because yeast typically dies when the alcohol concentration reaches 12-18%.

Bioreactors

Bioreactor (or Fermenter): A large, sterile vessel designed to provide an optimal, controlled environment for growing microorganisms (or cells) to produce a desired product (e.g., antibiotics, enzymes, ethanol).

A typical stirred-tank bioreactor includes:

  • Sterile Environment: Must be able to be sterilized (e.g., with steam) and maintained.
  • Control Systems: Probes and sensors to monitor and control pH, temperature, dissolved oxygen, and nutrient levels.
  • Agitator (Impeller): A stirring mechanism to keep the microbes suspended and to mix nutrients and oxygen evenly.
  • Sparger: A device that bubbles sterile air/oxygen into the broth for aerobic microbes.
  • Cooling Jacket: A jacket around the vessel to remove excess heat generated by microbial metabolism.

Microbial production

This section details the large-scale industrial production of various compounds using bioreactors.

Enzymes

Microbes are "enzyme factories." They are grown in large fermenters, and the enzymes are then extracted.

  • Examples: Proteases and Amylases (from Bacillus and Aspergillus) are used in laundry detergents, food processing, and for converting starch to sugar for ethanol production.

Alcohol (Ethanol)

As described in topic 5, this is a major industrial process. Large bioreactors (fermenters) are filled with a sugar source (like corn mash or molasses) and Saccharomyces cerevisiae. The fermentation is run anaerobically, and the resulting ethanol is then purified by distillation.

Organic acids

  • Citric Acid:
    • Organism: The mold Aspergillus niger.
    • Process: Grown in high-sugar, low-pH media with high aeration. The mold is "tricked" into overproducing citric acid.
    • Use: A major food additive as a flavoring (tartness) and preservative (acidulant) in soft drinks and candies.
  • Lactic Acid:
    • Organism: Lactic acid bacteria (e.g., Lactobacillus sp.).
    • Process: Fermentation of sugars.
    • Use: Food preservative, and as a monomer for making polylactic acid (PLA), a biodegradable plastic.

Antibiotics

Most antibiotics are secondary metabolites, meaning they are produced by the microbe during the stationary phase of growth (not during active growth). Production is done in large bioreactors with carefully controlled nutrient feeds.

  • Penicillin:
    • Organism: The mold Penicillium chrysogenum.
    • Process: Grown in a deep-tank fermenter using a nutrient-limited broth to stress the mold and maximize penicillin output.
  • Streptomycin:
    • Organism: The bacterium Streptomyces griseus (an Actinomycete).
    • Process: Similar fermentation process, also a secondary metabolite.

Vitamins (B12) and vaccines

  • Vitamin B12:
    • Organism: Bacteria like Pseudomonas denitrificans or Propionibacterium. (Plants and animals cannot make B12).
    • Process: Produced by large-scale bacterial fermentation.
    • Use: Dietary supplements and food fortification.
  • Vaccines:
    • Process: Modern vaccines (like the Hepatitis B vaccine) are often recombinant vaccines.
    • The gene for a viral surface protein (an antigen) is inserted into a microbe (like E. coli or yeast).
    • This microbe is grown in a large bioreactor, where it produces large quantities of the harmless viral protein.
    • This protein is then purified and used as the vaccine, which triggers an immune response without any risk of infection.
