Microorganisms in soil

Soil is a complex, living ecosystem teeming with a vast diversity of microorganisms. The region of soil directly influenced by plant roots is called the rhizosphere, which is particularly rich in microbial life.

Major groups include:

  • Bacteria: The most numerous inhabitants. Key players in decomposition and nutrient cycling (e.g., Bacillus, Pseudomonas, Azotobacter, Rhizobium, Streptomyces).
  • Fungi: Dominate by biomass. Excellent decomposers of complex plant material like cellulose and lignin (e.g., Aspergillus, Penicillium). Many form mycorrhizal associations.
  • Actinomycetes: A group of bacteria (like Streptomyces) that grow in fungal-like filaments. They are major decomposers and produce the characteristic "earthy" smell of soil (geosmin).
  • Algae: Photosynthetic; live on the soil surface. They contribute organic matter and help stabilize soil.
  • Protozoa: Unicellular eukaryotes that graze on bacteria, helping to control bacterial populations and release nutrients.

Role of microorganisms in biogeochemical cycles

Microorganisms are the primary drivers of biogeochemical cycles, converting elements between their organic and inorganic forms, making them available to plants and other organisms.

Nitrogen Cycle

This is a critical, microbe-driven cycle.

[Diagram: The Nitrogen Cycle, labeling all steps and key microbes]
  1. Nitrogen Fixation: Conversion of atmospheric N2 gas (unusable by most organisms) into ammonia (NH3). Done by N-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium, Azotobacter).
  2. Ammonification: Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down organic nitrogen (from dead organisms, waste) into ammonium (NH4+).
  3. Nitrification: A two-step process performed by specialized bacteria:
    • (a) Nitrosomonas oxidizes ammonium (NH4+) to nitrite (NO2-).
    • (b) Nitrobacter oxidizes nitrite (NO2-) to nitrate (NO3-). (Nitrate is the main form of N plants absorb).
  4. Denitrification: Conversion of nitrate (NO3-) back into N2 gas, which returns to the atmosphere. Done by denitrifying bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas) in anaerobic (low-oxygen) conditions.

Carbon Cycle

  • Carbon Fixation: Photosynthetic microbes (algae, cyanobacteria) and plants convert atmospheric CO2 into organic compounds.
  • Respiration/Decomposition: Heterotrophic microbes (bacteria, fungi) break down organic compounds, releasing CO2 back into the atmosphere.

Phosphorous Cycle

Microbes do not change the oxidation state of phosphorus, but they are crucial for its availability.

  • Solubilization: Most soil phosphate is locked in insoluble minerals. Many bacteria and fungi produce organic acids that solubilize this phosphate, converting it to a usable form (PO43-) for plants.

Biofertilizers: Types, production and uses

Biofertilizers: Preparations containing living microorganisms that, when applied to seeds, soil, or plants, colonize the rhizosphere and promote growth by increasing the supply or availability of primary nutrients.

Types of Biofertilizers

  • Nitrogen-Fixing:
    • Symbiotic: Rhizobium (for legumes), Frankia (for non-legumes).
    • Free-living: Azotobacter, Clostridium.
    • Associative: Azospirillum (associates with grass roots).
    • Blue-Green Algae (BGA): Cyanobacteria like Anabaena, Nostoc (especially for rice paddies).
  • Phosphate-Solubilizing: Microbes that dissolve insoluble soil phosphates (e.g., Pseudomonas, Bacillus).
  • Phosphate-Mobilizing (Mycorrhiza): Fungi that act as an extension of the root system to "mine" for phosphorus.

Production

Production involves mass-culturing the specific microbe in a sterile nutrient broth using a bioreactor (fermenter). The high-density microbial broth is then mixed with a solid carrier (like lignite, charcoal, or peat) or formulated as a liquid, packaged, and stored under cool conditions until use.

Uses

  • Applied by seed treatment (coating seeds), soil inoculation (mixing with soil), or seedling dip.
  • Reduces dependence on chemical fertilizers, which are costly and cause pollution.
  • Improves soil health and fertility over time.
  • Environmentally friendly and sustainable.

Nitrogen fixation

Nitrogen fixation is the biological conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N2) to ammonia (NH3), a form usable by plants. This process is exclusively carried out by prokaryotes (bacteria and archaea).

Types of Nitrogen Fixation

  • Non-symbiotic (Free-living): The microbes fix nitrogen for their own use, and it is released into the soil when they die.
    • Aerobic: Azotobacter, Beijerinckia.
    • Anaerobic: Clostridium.
  • Symbiotic: A close, mutualistic relationship where the microbe fixes nitrogen directly for a host plant.
    • Legume-Rhizobium: Rhizobium bacteria induce the formation of root nodules on legume plants (peas, beans, clover). The plant provides sugars, and the bacteria provide fixed nitrogen.
  • Associative Symbiosis: A looser association where N-fixing bacteria (like Azospirillum) live in the rhizosphere or on the root surface of grasses (e.g., sugarcane, corn), providing some fixed nitrogen.

Mycorrhiza: Types and role

Mycorrhiza ("fungus-root"): A symbiotic association between a fungus and the roots of a plant. This is a mutualistic relationship: the plant provides the fungus with sugars, and the fungus enhances the plant's nutrient and water uptake.

Types of Mycorrhiza

  1. Ectomycorrhiza (ECM):
    • The fungal hyphae form a dense sheath (mantle) around the outside of the root.
    • They also form a network between the root cortical cells, called the Hartig net.
    • They do not penetrate the plant cells.
    • Common on woody plants like pine, oak, and birch.
  2. Endomycorrhiza (Arbuscular Mycorrhiza - AM):
    • The fungal hyphae penetrate the root cortical cells.
    • They do not form an outer sheath.
    • Inside the cells, they form highly branched, tree-like structures called arbuscules, which are the primary sites of nutrient exchange.
    • Many also form balloon-like storage structures called vesicles (hence their other name, VAM - Vesicular-Arbuscular Mycorrhiza).
    • Extremely common; found in over 80% of all land plants (including most crops).

Role in Agriculture and Horticulture

  • Nutrient Uptake: The fungal hyphae act as a vast extension of the root system, exploring the soil more efficiently, especially for immobile nutrients like phosphorus.
  • Water Absorption: The hyphal network helps plants absorb and retain water, improving drought tolerance.
  • Pathogen Resistance: The fungal mantle (in ECM) provides a physical barrier, and both types can induce plant defense responses.
  • Soil Structure: Fungal hyphae help bind soil particles together, improving soil aggregation and stability.

Mechanism of Biological Nitrogen fixation

This process is extremely energy-intensive and is catalyzed by a sophisticated enzyme complex called nitrogenase.

The Nitrogenase Enzyme

The nitrogenase complex consists of two main protein components:

  • Dinitrogenase reductase: An iron (Fe) protein.
  • Dinitrogenase: A molybdenum-iron (Mo-Fe) protein. This is where N2 actually binds.

The Reaction

The overall reaction is:

This shows that it requires a huge amount of energy (16 ATP) to fix just one molecule of N2.

The Oxygen Problem

The nitrogenase enzyme is extremely sensitive to oxygen and is irreversibly inactivated by it. Nitrogen-fixing organisms have evolved various strategies to solve this:

  • Anaerobic growth: Clostridium simply lives in anaerobic environments.
  • Slime layers: Azotobacter has a very high respiration rate and produces slime layers to consume O2 at the cell surface.
  • Leghaemoglobin: In Rhizobium root nodules, the plant produces a special O2-binding protein called leghaemoglobin (Legume Hemoglobin). This protein has a high affinity for oxygen. It "scavenges" O2, delivering it to the bacteria for respiration (to make ATP) while keeping the free O2 concentration low enough to protect the nitrogenase enzyme. It gives active nodules their characteristic pink/red color.

Biopesticides and biological control

Biological Control: The use of living organisms (natural enemies) to control pests (insects, weeds, pathogens).
Biopesticides: Pesticides derived from natural materials like animals, plants, bacteria, and certain minerals.

Microbial Insecticides (Bio-insecticides)

These are microbes that are pathogenic to specific insects.

  • Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt): This is the most famous and widely used.
    • It's a Gram-positive soil bacterium.
    • During sporulation, it produces a crystal protein called Cry-toxin (or delta-endotoxin).
    • When an insect (like a caterpillar) ingests the spore/crystal, the alkaline conditions of its gut dissolve the crystal, activating the toxin.
    • The toxin binds to the gut wall, creates pores, and lyses the cells, leading to paralysis and death.
    • The gene for Bt-toxin is the one used in "Bt crops" (e.t., Bt cotton, Bt corn).
  • Baculoviruses: Viruses that specifically infect insects. They are very host-specific and safe for non-target organisms.

Microbial Herbicides (Bio-herbicides)

Using microorganisms (usually fungi) to control weeds.

  • Example: Using the fungus Phytophthora palmivora to control the "strangler vine" weed in citrus groves.
Advantages of Biopesticides:
  • Highly specific to target pests, safe for humans and beneficial insects (like bees).
  • Biodegradable; do not cause environmental pollution.
  • Can be used in organic farming.
