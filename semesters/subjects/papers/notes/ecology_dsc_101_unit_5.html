1. Introduction to Biogeochemical Cycles

Unlike energy, which flows one-way through an ecosystem and is lost as heat, matter (chemical elements) is cycled. Nutrients are finite and must be recycled for life to continue.

Biogeochemical Cycle: The pathway by which a chemical element (like Carbon, Nitrogen, Phosphorus) moves through the bio- (living), geo- (earth, rocks, soil), and chemo- (chemical) components of an ecosystem.

Each cycle has two main parts:

  • Reservoir Pool: The large, slow-moving, often abiotic part of the cycle where the nutrient is stored. (e.g., the atmosphere for Carbon, rocks for Phosphorus).
  • Cycling Pool: The smaller, more active, biotic part of the cycle where the nutrient moves quickly between organisms and their immediate environment.

Types of Cycles:

  1. Gaseous Cycles: The main reservoir is the atmosphere or ocean. These cycles are global.
    • Examples: Carbon, Nitrogen, Sulphur, Water.
  2. Sedimentary Cycles: The main reservoir is the Earth's crust (rocks and soil). These cycles are slower and more localized.
    • Example: Phosphorus.

2. The Hydrological (Water) Cycle

This is the continuous movement of water (H₂O) on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. It is a physical process, not a chemical one, driven by solar energy.

Key Processes:

  • Evaporation: Solar energy heats liquid water, turning it into water vapor (gas) and rising into the atmosphere.
  • Condensation: Water vapor in the air cools and changes back into liquid water, forming clouds.
  • Precipitation: Water is released from clouds as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  • Collection/Runoff: Water that falls on land flows over the surface (surface runoff) into rivers, lakes, and oceans, or soaks into the ground.
  • Infiltration/Percolation: Water soaks into the soil and moves downward to become groundwater.
  • Transpiration: The release of water vapor from plants into the atmosphere (a form of biological evaporation).

3. The Carbon Cycle (Gaseous)

Carbon is the basis of all organic molecules. The main reservoir of carbon is carbon dioxide (CO₂) in the atmosphere and dissolved in the oceans.

Key Processes:

  1. Photosynthesis: Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria pull CO₂ from the atmosphere and use it to make organic compounds (sugar/biomass). This is the key process that "fixes" carbon.
  2. Respiration: All living organisms (plants, animals, decomposers) break down organic compounds for energy, releasing CO₂ back into the atmosphere.
  3. Decomposition: Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organic matter, releasing CO₂ through respiration.
  4. Consumption: Carbon is transferred from producers to consumers when they are eaten.
  5. Combustion: The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) and wood releases vast amounts of stored carbon into the atmosphere as CO₂. This is the main driver of modern climate change.
  6. Sedimentation: Over millions of years, dead organic matter that fails to decompose can be buried and converted into fossil fuels, locking carbon away. Shells and skeletons (calcium carbonate) can form limestone.

4. The Nitrogen Cycle (Gaseous)

Nitrogen is essential for making proteins and nucleic acids (DNA, RNA). The main reservoir is N₂ gas in the atmosphere (which makes up ~78% of the air). However, most organisms cannot use N₂ gas directly.

Problem: N₂ gas is "unavailable" because of its strong triple bond. It must be "fixed" into a usable form, like ammonia (NH₃) or nitrate (NO₃⁻).

Key Processes (driven by bacteria):

  1. Nitrogen Fixation: The conversion of N₂ gas into ammonia (NH₃).
    • Biological: Done by specific bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in the root nodules of legumes like beans and peas; Azotobacter in the soil).
    • Atmospheric: Lightning has enough energy to split N₂ gas, forming nitrogen oxides.
    • Industrial: The Haber-Bosch process manufactures fertilizer.
  2. Nitrification: A two-step process, done by nitrifying bacteria, converting ammonia into nitrates, which plants can easily absorb.
    • Step 1: Nitrosomonas bacteria convert Ammonia (NH₃) → Nitrite (NO₂⁻).
    • Step 2: Nitrobacter bacteria convert Nitrite (NO₂⁻) → Nitrate (NO₃⁻).
  3. Assimilation: Plants absorb usable nitrogen (nitrates or ammonia) from the soil and incorporate it into their own proteins and DNA. Animals get nitrogen by eating plants.
  4. Ammonification: Decomposers (bacteria, fungi) break down dead organic matter (waste, dead organisms) and convert the organic nitrogen back into ammonia (NH₃).
  5. Denitrification: The conversion of nitrates (NO₃⁻) back into N₂ gas, which returns to the atmosphere. This is done by denitrifying bacteria (e.g., Pseudomonas) in anaerobic (low-oxygen) conditions, like waterlogged soil.

5. The Phosphorus Cycle (Sedimentary)

Phosphorus is essential for DNA, RNA, ATP (the energy molecule), and cell membranes.

Main Reservoir: The main reservoir is in phosphate rock (sedimentary rock) on land and in the ocean floor. There is no significant gaseous component (it does *not* go into the atmosphere).

Key Processes:

  1. Weathering: Rain and erosion slowly break down phosphate rocks, releasing phosphate ions (PO₄³⁻) into the soil and water. This is a very slow process.
  2. Assimilation (Uptake): Plants absorb dissolved phosphate from the soil. Animals get phosphorus by eating plants.
  3. Decomposition (Mineralization): Decomposers break down dead organic matter, returning phosphate to the soil.
  4. Sedimentation: Phosphate that washes into the oceans can settle to the bottom and, over millions of years, form new sedimentary rock. This "locks away" the phosphorus for a very long time.
  5. Geological Uplift: The process of mountain-building that brings the deep ocean sediments and rocks back to the surface, making the phosphate available for weathering again (this takes eons).

    Note: Because this cycle is so slow and depends on weathering, phosphorus is often a limiting nutrient for plant growth, both on land and in freshwater. This is why fertilizers (N-P-K) are rich in phosphorus.

6. The Sulphur Cycle (Gaseous)

Sulphur is important for some amino acids (proteins). This cycle is complex, having both gaseous and sedimentary components.

Reservoirs: Rocks, soils, and the atmosphere (as Sulphur Dioxide, SO₂).

Key Processes:

  1. Weathering/Decomposition: Sulphur in rocks (like pyrite) is weathered, and decomposition of organic matter releases sulphur, often as hydrogen sulfide (H₂S - rotten egg smell).
  2. Assimilation: Plants absorb sulphate ions (SO₄²⁻) from the soil and incorporate them into proteins.
  3. Volcanic Activity: Volcanoes are a major natural source of atmospheric Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂).
  4. Atmospheric Conversion: In the atmosphere, SO₂ and H₂S react with oxygen and water to form sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄).
  5. Precipitation: The sulphuric acid falls to Earth as acid rain, which can harm forests and lakes.
  6. Human Impact (Combustion): The burning of fossil fuels (especially coal) releases massive amounts of SO₂ into the atmosphere, and is the primary cause of industrial acid rain.

7. Nutrient Cycling in Ecosystems

This topic describes the processes that control the movement and storage of nutrients *within* an ecosystem.

Input of Nutrients in Ecosystem

Nutrients must first enter the ecosystem. This happens through:

  • Atmospheric Input:
    • Wet deposition: Nutrients dissolved in rain and snow (e.g., nitrogen and sulphur from acid rain).
    • Dry deposition: Nutrients that fall as dust or airborne particles.
    • Gaseous fixation: e.g., Nitrogen fixation by bacteria.
  • Weathering: The breakdown of parent rock (the "geological" part) releases nutrients like phosphorus and calcium into the soil.
  • Biological Input: Migrating animals may bring in nutrients (e.g., salmon migrating upstream, dying, and decomposing).
