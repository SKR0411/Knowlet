General Characteristics, Classification, Ecology and Economic Importance

General Characteristics

Phycology (or Algology): The branch of botany that deals with the study of algae.

Algae are a large, diverse group of simple, autotrophic organisms. They are thallophytes, meaning their body (a thallus) is not differentiated into true roots, stems, or leaves.

  • Habitat: Primarily aquatic (both freshwater and marine), but also found in moist terrestrial habitats (soil, rocks).
  • Nutrition: Overwhelmingly photosynthetic (autotrophic). They contain chlorophyll-a and other pigments.
  • Cell Type: Can be prokaryotic (Cyanobacteria/Blue-Green Algae) or eukaryotic (all other groups).
  • Thallus Organization: Shows great diversity: unicellular, colonial, filamentous, siphonaceous (coenocytic), and parenchymatous (multicellular, tissue-like).
  • Reproduction: Reproduce by vegetative, asexual (spores), and sexual means.
    • Sexual Reproduction: Can be isogamous, anisogamous, or oogamous.
    • Reproductive Organs: Unicellular. If multicellular, they lack a sterile jacket of cells (a key difference from bryophytes).
    • Embryo: No embryo is formed after fertilization.

Classification (Overview)

Algae are classified into major groups (divisions) based on key criteria:

  1. Pigment composition (e.g., Chl-a/b, Chl-a/c, Phycobilins).
  2. Nature of reserve food (e.g., Starch, Laminarin, Floridean starch).
  3. Flagella type and number (e.g., whiplash, tinsel; 2 or many).
  4. Cell wall composition (e.g., Cellulose, Algin, Agar).

Major divisions include: Cyanophyta (prokaryotic), Chlorophyta (Green algae), Phaeophyta (Brown algae), Rhodophyta (Red algae), Xanthophyta (Yellow-green algae), etc.

Ecology

  • Plankton: Microscopic algae floating in water.
    • Phytoplankton: Form the base of all aquatic food webs and are the "primary producers" of the aquatic world.
  • Benthos: Algae attached to the bottom of water bodies (e.g., seaweeds).
  • Terrestrial Algae: Grow on moist soil, rocks, tree bark.
  • Symbiotic Algae:
    • Lichens: Symbiosis with fungi.
    • Zoochlorellae: Algae living inside animals (e.g., Hydra).
  • Algal Blooms: Rapid, massive growth of algae (often Cyanobacteria) in nutrient-rich water (eutrophication). These can be toxic and deplete oxygen, killing fish.

Economic Importance

Positive Importance (Beneficial)
  • Oxygen Production: Phytoplankton produce 50-70% of the Earth's oxygen.
  • Food Source: Eaten directly (e.g., Porphyra - "Nori" for sushi; Spirulina, Chlorella as protein supplements).
  • Industrial Gums (Phycocolloids):
    • Agar-Agar: From red algae (e.g., Gelidium). Used as a solidifying agent in culture media, jellies, and cosmetics.
    • Alginates: From brown algae (e.g., Laminaria). Used as thickeners in ice cream, paints, and toothpaste.
    • Carrageenan: From red algae ("Irish moss"). Used as a stabilizer in milk products.
  • Diatomaceous Earth: Fossilized silica shells of diatoms. Used in filters, as a mild abrasive, and in dynamite.
  • Biofertilizers: Nitrogen-fixing Blue-Green Algae (e.g., Nostoc, Anabaena) enrich rice paddy fields.
  • Sewage Treatment: Algae in oxidation ponds help oxygenate the water and break down sewage.
Negative Importance (Harmful)
  • Algal Blooms: Cause eutrophication, oxygen depletion, and "dead zones."
  • Toxins: Some blooms ("red tides" by dinoflagellates, "toxic blooms" by Cyanobacteria) produce toxins lethal to fish and humans.
  • Water Fouling: Clog water filters and give undesirable taste/odor to drinking water.

Nostoc (Class: Cyanophyta)

  • Morphology: A colonial, prokaryotic alga. Forms gelatinous balls (colonies) visible to the naked eye, on moist soil or in water. Inside the mucilage are numerous unbranched filaments (trichomes).
  • Structure: The trichome is like a string of beads. It has two cell types:
    1. Vegetative Cells: Smaller, spherical, photosynthetic.
    2. Heterocysts: Large, thick-walled, pale-yellow, empty-looking cells. They are the site of nitrogen fixation.
  • Reproduction:
    • Vegetative (Hormogonia): The trichome breaks into small, motile pieces called hormogonia, which crawl out of the mucilage and form new colonies.
    • Asexual (Akinetes): Vegetative cells enlarge, fill with food, and develop a thick wall to become akinetes (resting spores). They survive harsh conditions and germinate later.
    • Sexual Reproduction: Completely absent in Cyanophyta.

Volvox (Class: Chlorophyta)

  • Morphology: A freshwater, colonial, eukaryotic alga. Forms a large, motile, spherical colony called a coenobium (a colony with a fixed number of cells).
  • Structure: The coenobium is a hollow sphere of 500-60,000 cells. Each cell is Chlamydomonas-like, with two flagella pointing outwards, a chloroplast, and an eyespot. Cells are interconnected by cytoplasmic strands. Shows division of labor: most cells are vegetative (motility), but a few larger cells in the posterior are for reproduction.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: Reproductive cells (gonidia) divide, invaginate, and form a daughter colony inside the parent. The daughter colony is released when the parent colony ruptures.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy.
      • Some reproductive cells become non-motile eggs.
      • Others divide to form a packet of motile antherozoids (sperm).
      • Sperm swim to the egg, fertilization occurs, forming a diploid (2n) zygote.
      • The zygote develops a thick, spiny wall (zygospore) and is released. It undergoes meiosis to form a new haploid (n) colony.
  • Life Cycle: Haplontic (the colony is haploid).

Oedogonium (Class: Chlorophyta)

  • Morphology: A freshwater, unbranched, filamentous alga, attached to surfaces by a holdfast (basal cell).
  • Structure: Cells are cylindrical, uninucleate, and have a large, reticulate (net-like) chloroplast with many pyrenoids. Cell division is unique, leaving "rings" at the top of the cell called cap cells.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By zoospores. A single, large, multinucleate, multiflagellate zoospore (with a "crown" of flagella) is formed in a zoosporangium, swims, settles, and grows into a new filament.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy.
      • Oogonium (female): A large, swollen cell with a single non-motile egg (n).
      • Antheridium (male): Short, flat cells that divide to produce two small, multiflagellate sperm (n).
      • Fertilization: Sperm enters the oogonium and fuses with the egg, forming a diploid (2n) zygote (oospore).
      • Meiosis: The oospore rests, then undergoes meiosis to produce 4 haploid (n) zoospores, each growing into a new filament.
      • (Note: Can be Macrandrous - normal male filaments; or Nannandrous - tiny "dwarf male" filaments).
  • Life Cycle: Haplontic (the filament is haploid).

Vaucheria (Class: Xanthophyta)

  • Morphology: Terrestrial (on moist soil, "water felt") or freshwater. A branched, filamentous alga.
  • Structure: The thallus is a siphonaceous (or coenocytic) filament. It is one long, continuous tube with no cross-walls (septa) in the vegetative state. It is multinucleate, and contains numerous small, discoid chloroplasts (lacking pyrenoids).
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By a large, multiflagellate synzoospore, formed in a sporangium at the filament tip.
    • Sexual: Advanced oogamy.
      • Oogonium (female): A large, globular structure containing one large, non-motile egg (n).
      • Antheridium (male): A curved, tube-like structure producing many small, biflagellate sperm (n).
      • Fertilization: Sperm enter the oogonium, one fuses with the egg, forming a diploid (2n) zygote (oospore).
      • Meiosis: The zygote rests, then undergoes meiosis *before* germination to form a new haploid (n) filament.
  • Life Cycle: Haplontic (the filament is haploid).

Ectocarpus (Class: Phaeophyta)

  • Morphology: A marine (brown alga), branched, filamentous alga. The thallus is heterotrichous (with both a prostrate/creeping system and an erect/branched system).
  • Structure: Cells are uninucleate with band-shaped or discoid chloroplasts. Pigment is Chl-a/c and Fucoxanthin.
  • Life Cycle: This is the classic example of an isomorphic alternation of generations. There are two morphologically identical plants, one haploid and one diploid.
    1. Sporophyte (2n): The diploid plant. It bears two types of sporangia:
      • Unilocular Sporangia: Single-celled. Meiosis occurs inside to produce haploid (n) zoospores.
      • Plurilocular Sporangia: Multicellular. Produce diploid (2n) zoospores by mitosis (for asexual reproduction, creating more 2n sporophytes).
    2. Gametophyte (n): The haploid (n) zoospores from the unilocular sporangium germinate and grow into a haploid (n) plant (the gametophyte), which looks identical to the sporophyte.
      • The gametophyte produces plurilocular gametangia, which release haploid (n) gametes.
      • Gametes (isogamous) fuse to form a diploid (2n) zygote.
      • The zygote germinates (without meiosis) and grows into the diploid (2n) sporophyte, completing the cycle.

Polysiphonia (Class: Rhodophyta)

  • Morphology: A marine (red alga), highly branched, filamentous alga.
  • Structure: The thallus is "polysiphonous" – it consists of a central filament (central siphon) surrounded by a ring of 4-20 pericentral siphons (filaments). Cells are connected by pit connections. Pigment is Chl-a and Phycoerythrin.
  • Reproduction: No motile cells (flagella) are formed at any stage.
  • Life Cycle: Extremely complex, triphasic (has three distinct "generations"). The gametophyte (n) and tetrasporophyte (2n) are isomorphic.
    1. Gametophyte (n): These are separate male and female plants.
      • Male Plant (n): Produces non-motile spermatia (n) in spermatangia.
