1. Formation of the Earth

The Earth formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from the same cloud of gas and dust (a solar nebula) that formed the Sun and the rest of our solar system. This process is explained by the Nebular Hypothesis.

  1. Accretion: The solar nebula collapsed under its own gravity. Dust, rock, and gas clumped together to form planetesimals.
  2. Collision: These planetesimals collided and stuck together, growing larger over millions of years to form a molten "proto-Earth."
  3. Differentiation: While the Earth was molten, heavier elements sank to the center, and lighter elements rose to the surface. This separation by density created the Earth's layered structure (core, mantle, crust).
Diagram: A flowchart showing a spinning gas cloud (nebula) -> flattening into a disk with a proto-sun -> accretion of planetesimals -> formation of planets in orbits.

2. Composition of Core, Mantle, and Crust

The process of differentiation resulted in three main chemical layers:

The Crust

The outermost, thinnest, and least dense layer. It is the rigid "skin" of the Earth. There are two types:

Property Continental Crust Oceanic Crust
Thickness Thick (30-70 km) Thin (5-10 km)
Density Less dense (approx. 2.7 g/cm³) More dense (approx. 3.0 g/cm³)
Composition Granitic (rich in Silicon & Aluminum - "SiAl") Basaltic (rich in Silicon & Magnesium - "SiMa")
Age Very old (up to 4 billion years) Relatively young (< 200 million years)

The Mantle

The thickest layer (about 2,900 km) located beneath the crust. It makes up about 84% of Earth's volume. It is composed of hot, dense, silicate rocks (rich in iron and magnesium, like peridotite). While it's a "solid," it flows very slowly over geologic time, like thick asphalt. This slow movement is called mantle convection and is what drives plate tectonics.

The Core

The innermost, densest layer, composed primarily of iron and nickel.

  • Outer Core: A liquid layer. The flow of this liquid metal generates the Earth's magnetic field.
  • Inner Core: A solid sphere, despite being hotter than the outer core. It is solid because of the immense pressure at the center of the Earth.

3. Formation of Atmosphere and Hydrosphere

Atmosphere

The Earth's atmosphere formed in stages:

  1. Primary Atmosphere: The first atmosphere, composed of light gases like hydrogen and helium from the solar nebula. It was stripped away by solar winds.
  2. Secondary Atmosphere: Formed by volcanic outgassing as the Earth cooled. Volcanoes released gases like water vapor (H₂O), carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrogen (N₂). There was almost no oxygen.
  3. Tertiary Atmosphere (Modern): The formation of oceans removed CO₂ from the air. Then, about 2.7 billion years ago, cyanobacteria began performing photosynthesis, releasing vast amounts of oxygen (O₂). This "Great Oxidation Event" fundamentally changed the planet, allowing for the evolution of aerobic life.

Hydrosphere

The hydrosphere (all of Earth's water) also came from two main sources:

  1. Outgassing: The vast amount of water vapor (H₂O) released from volcanoes during the Earth's early molten state.
  2. Comets and Asteroids: Water-rich comets and asteroids from the outer solar system bombarded the early Earth, delivering significant amounts of water.

As the Earth cooled, this water vapor condensed, formed clouds, and led to millions of years of rainfall, which filled the low-lying basins to become the first oceans.

4. Origin and Evolution of Life Forms

Life is thought to have begun in the early oceans ("primordial soup") around 3.5 to 4 billion years ago. The exact process is unknown, but it involved the formation of simple organic molecules that eventually combined to form self-replicating cells.

Key Milestones:

  • ~3.5 Ga (Billion years ago): First life (prokaryotes), simple single-celled organisms like bacteria. Evidence is found in stromatolites (layered mats of fossilized cyanobacteria).
  • ~2.7 Ga: Photosynthesis begins (cyanobacteria), starts releasing oxygen (O₂).
  • ~2.4 Ga: Great Oxidation Event. Oxygen builds up in the atmosphere. This was toxic to most existing (anaerobic) life, causing a mass extinction, but it paved the way for complex, oxygen-breathing life.
  • ~1.8 Ga: First complex cells (eukaryotes) appear. These cells have a nucleus and organelles.
  • ~600 Ma (Million years ago): First simple multicellular animals (e.g., sponges).
  • ~541 Ma: The Cambrian Explosion. A relatively rapid "explosion" of biological diversity, where most major animal body plans (phyla) appeared in the fossil record.

6. Radiometric Dating Methods

This is the technique used to determine the absolute age of rocks and fossils in years. It is the "clock" that adds numbers to the Geologic Time Scale.

Principle: It relies on the radioactive decay of certain isotopes. A radioactive "parent" isotope decays at a fixed, predictable rate into a stable "daughter" isotope.

Half-Life: The time it takes for half of the parent isotopes in a sample to decay into daughter isotopes.

By measuring the ratio of parent-to-daughter isotopes in a mineral, scientists can calculate how many half-lives have passed since the mineral formed, thus giving its age.

Common Dating Methods:

  • Carbon-14 (C-14):
    • Half-life: ~5,730 years.
    • Used for: Dating *organic* material (wood, bones, cloth) that was once living.
    • Range: Only useful for recent materials (< 50,000 years). It cannot be used to date rocks or dinosaurs.
  • Potassium-Argon (K-Ar):
    • Half-life: ~1.25 billion years.
    • Used for: Dating volcanic rocks (igneous). When lava cools, it traps potassium, but all argon gas escapes. Any argon found in the rock must have come from the decay of potassium *after* it cooled.
  • Uranium-Lead (U-Pb):
    • Half-life: (U-238 to Pb-206) ~4.5 billion years.
    • Used for: Dating extremely old rocks and the Earth itself. It is highly accurate and often used on zircon crystals, which are very durable.

7. Age of the Earth

The accepted age of the Earth is approximately 4.54 billion years old (plus or minus 50 million years).

How do we know?

This age was not determined from Earth rocks. The Earth's original crust has been destroyed and recycled by plate tectonics (weathering, erosion, subduction). No "birth rocks" from 4.5 Ga remain.
