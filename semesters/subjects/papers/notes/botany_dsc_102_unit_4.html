Mastigomycotina (Lower Fungi)

This group is characterized by having motile zoospores and (often) coenocytic (aseptate) hyphae. They are often aquatic or parasitic on plants.

Phytophthora

  • Classification: Mastigomycotina (Oomycetes).
  • Ecology & Significance: Parasitic. Phytophthora infestans causes the devastating "Late Blight of Potato" disease, responsible for the Irish Potato Famine.
  • Thallus: Coenocytic (aseptate), branched, hyaline hyphae that grow between host cells (intercellular). They produce knob-like haustoria to absorb nutrients from host cells.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: Branched sporangiophores emerge from the leaf stomata. They bear lemon-shaped sporangia. In cool, moist conditions, the sporangia contents divide to form several biflagellate zoospores, which swim and infect new leaves.
    • Sexual: Oogamous. A globular oogonium (female) and a club-shaped antheridium (male) form. Fertilization results in a thick-walled diploid oospore, which is a resting spore that overwinters in the soil.

Albugo

  • Classification: Mastigomycotina (Oomycetes).
  • Ecology & Significance: An obligate parasite. Albugo candida causes "White Rust of Crucifers" (e.g., on mustard, radish). It creates white, shiny "pustules" on the leaves.
  • Thallus: Coenocytic, intercellular hyphae with knob-like haustoria.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: Sporangiophores form *under* the host epidermis. They produce chains of sporangia (conidia). The pressure builds up, rupturing the epidermis to form the white pustule. Sporangia are dispersed by wind and germinate to form zoospores.
    • Sexual: Oogamous. Occurs in the host tissue, similar to Phytophthora, producing a thick-walled oospore that overwinters.

Zygomycotina (Conjugation Fungi)

This group is characterized by coenocytic hyphae and sexual reproduction by forming a zygospore.

Mucor

  • Classification: Zygomycotina.
  • Ecology & Significance: A common saprophyte ("pin mold") found on dung, bread, and decaying organic matter.
  • Thallus: A white, cottony mass of branched, coenocytic hyphae. (Note: Lacks the rhizoids and stolons seen in the related genus Rhizopus).
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By sporangiospores. Unbranched sporangiophores rise up and terminate in a globular sporangium. Inside, numerous non-motile sporangiospores are formed around a central sterile dome called the columella. The sporangium wall breaks, releasing the spores.
    • Sexual: By conjugation (gametangial copulation). Two hyphae of opposite mating strains (+ and -) grow towards each other. They form progametangia, which develop into gametangia. The walls dissolve, and the multinucleate protoplasts fuse (plasmogamy followed by karyogamy) to form a thick-walled, warty, black zygospore (2n). The zygospore rests, then undergoes meiosis to germinate and produce a new sporangium.

Ascomycotina (Sac Fungi)

This group is characterized by septate hyphae and sexual reproduction via ascospores formed inside an ascus.

Saccharomyces (Yeast)

  • Classification: Ascomycotina.
  • Ecology & Significance: Found on sugary substances (e.g., fruits). S. cerevisiae is "Baker's Yeast" or "Brewer's Yeast", essential for bread making and ethanol fermentation.
  • Thallus: A simple, unicellular, non-mycelial fungus. Cells are small, oval, or spherical.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: Primarily by budding. A small outgrowth (bud) forms on the parent cell, enlarges, and pinches off.
    • Sexual: Under stress, two haploid (n) yeast cells of opposite mating types fuse to form a diploid (2n) zygote. This diploid cell can then function as an ascus. It undergoes meiosis (and one mitosis) to form 4 or 8 haploid ascospores inside the original cell wall. These are released and return to the budding haploid state.

Aspergillus

  • Classification: Ascomycotina (sometimes placed in Deuteromycotina as its asexual "anamorph" state is most common).
  • Ecology & Significance: A common saprophytic mold ("black mold") on bread, jam, etc. Some species cause disease (aspergillosis). A. oryzae is used to make soy sauce. A. niger produces citric acid.
  • Thallus: Branched, septate hyphae.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual (Main method): By conidia. A long, non-septate conidiophore rises from a "foot cell." The tip swells into a vesicle. Small, bottle-shaped cells (phialides or sterigmata) form on the vesicle. The phialides produce long, basipetal chains of small, pigmented conidia. This entire structure is the "Aspergillus head."
    • Sexual (Teleomorph state, *Eurotium*): Two hyphae coil around each other (antheridium and ascogonium). This leads to the formation of a Cleistothecium – a simple, spherical, closed fruiting body (ascocarp). Inside, many small, globular asci are scattered, each containing 8 ascospores.

Basidiomycotina (Club Fungi)

This group is characterized by septate hyphae (with specialized "dolipore" septa and clamp connections) and sexual reproduction via basidiospores formed externally on a basidium.

Puccinia (Rust)

  • Classification: Basidiomycotina.
  • Ecology & Significance: An obligate parasite. Puccinia graminis-tritici causes "Black Stem Rust of Wheat," one of the most destructive plant diseases.
  • Key Features:
    • Heteroecious: It requires two different hosts to complete its life cycle: Wheat (primary host) and Barberry (alternate host).
    • Polymorphic: It produces 5 different types of spores.
  • Life Cycle (5 Spore Stages):
    1. On Wheat (Summer): Uredospores (n+n). Reddish-brown spores that form in "uredosori." They are the "repeating stage," re-infecting wheat all summer.
    2. On Wheat (Late Summer/Fall): Teliospores (n+n → 2n). Black, thick-walled, two-celled spores that form in "teliosori." Karyogamy (fusion of n+n nuclei) occurs, making them diploid (2n). They overwinter.
    3. On Soil (Spring): The teliospore germinates, undergoes meiosis, and forms a basidium (promycelium). The basidium produces 4 haploid basidiospores (n).
    4. On Barberry (Spring): Basidiospores infect the Barberry leaf. They form Pycnia (Spermogonia) (Stage 0), which produce pycniospores (spermatia) and receptive hyphae. Plasmogamy (mating) occurs here, re-establishing the n+n dikaryotic state.
    5. On Barberry (Late Spring): The dikaryotic (n+n) mycelium forms Aecia (Stage I) on the lower leaf surface, which produce Aeciospores (n+n) in chains.

    The aeciospores are released, infect the Wheat plant, and the cycle repeats. Control involves eradicating the alternate host (Barberry).

Ustilago (Smut)

  • Classification: Basidiomycotina.
  • Ecology & Significance: A parasite. Ustilago tritici causes "Loose Smut of Wheat."
  • Symptoms: The fungus grows systematically within the plant. At flowering, it destroys the entire grain head, replacing the seeds with a mass of black, powdery smut spores (teliospores).
  • Life Cycle (Autoecious - one host):
    1. The wind-blown teliospores (n+n → 2n) land on a healthy flower's stigma.
    2. They germinate, undergo meiosis, and form a basidium.
    3. The haploid (n) basidiospores fuse (mate) on the stigma to form a dikaryotic (n+n) hypha.
    4. This hypha infects the ovary and embryo of the new seed. The infection is embryo-borne.
    5. The seed *looks* healthy but carries the dormant mycelium. When this seed is planted, the fungus grows with the plant, finally showing symptoms at flowering.

Agaricus (Mushroom)

  • Classification: Basidiomycotina.
  • Ecology & Significance: A common saprophyte (the "field mushroom") growing in soil rich in humus, lawns, and manure. Many species are edible, some are poisonous.
  • Thallus: The "plant" is a vast, underground network of septate mycelium. The visible mushroom is only the fruiting body (basidiocarp).
  • Basidiocarp (Fruiting Body) Characteristics:
    • Stipe: The stalk.
    • Pileus: The cap.
    • Annulus: A ring on the stipe (remnant of a "partial veil" that covered the gills).
    • Gills (Lamellae): Radiating plates on the underside of the cap.
    • The gills are lined with millions of club-shaped basidia.
    • Each basidium undergoes meiosis and produces 4 basidiospores externally on small stalks (sterigmata).
