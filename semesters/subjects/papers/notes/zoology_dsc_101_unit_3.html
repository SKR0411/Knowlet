1. Phylum Platyhelminthes (Flatworms)

Platyhelminthes (from Greek: platys = flat, helmins = worm) are dorsoventrally flattened, triploblastic, acoelomate animals. They exhibit an organ-system grade of organization.

1.1 General Characters and Classification

General Characters

  • Body Form: Dorsoventrally flattened, ribbon-like or leaf-like.
  • Symmetry: Bilateral symmetry with distinct cephalization.
  • Germ Layers: Triploblastic (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm).
  • Body Cavity: Acoelomate (lack a true body cavity). The space between organs is filled with parenchyma tissue.
  • Digestive System: Incomplete, with a single opening (mouth/anus), or absent in parasitic forms like tapeworms.
  • Excretory System: Consists of specialized flame cells (protonephridia).
  • Reproduction: Mostly hermaphroditic, with complex reproductive systems. Fertilization is internal.

Classification of Platyhelminthes (Up to Classes)

Class Lifestyle Key Characteristics Examples
Turbellaria Mostly free-living Body covered with cilia. Mouth is ventral. Planaria (Dugesia)
Trematoda Parasitic (Flukes) Body unsegmented and leaf-like. Possess oral and ventral suckers for attachment. Fasciola (liver fluke), Schistosoma (blood fluke)
Cestoda Endoparasitic (Tapeworms) Body is long, ribbon-like, and divided into a scolex, neck, and proglottids. Digestive system is absent. Taenia solium (pork tapeworm), Echinococcus

1.2 Life Cycle and Pathogenicity of Taenia solium (Pork Tapeworm)

Taenia solium is a digenetic parasite, meaning it requires two hosts to complete its life cycle.

  • Primary Host: Human (where the adult worm lives in the small intestine).
  • Intermediate Host: Pig (where the larval stage develops).

Life Cycle

  1. An infected human passes gravid (egg-filled) proglottids in their feces.
  2. The eggs (oncospheres) are ingested by a pig through contaminated food or water.
  3. In the pig's intestine, the oncospheres hatch, penetrate the intestinal wall, and enter the bloodstream.
  4. They travel to striated muscles (tongue, neck, shoulder) and develop into the larval stage called Cysticercus cellulosae (also known as a bladder worm).
  5. A human becomes infected by eating raw or undercooked pork containing these cysticerci.
  6. In the human intestine, the cysticercus wall is digested, the scolex evaginates and attaches to the intestinal wall, and the neck starts budding off new proglottids, growing into an adult tapeworm.

Pathogenicity

  • Taeniasis: Caused by the adult worm in the intestine. Symptoms are usually mild and include abdominal pain, nausea, and weight loss.
  • Cysticercosis: A much more dangerous condition caused by the accidental ingestion of eggs by humans (making the human an intermediate host). The larvae migrate to various tissues, including the brain (neurocysticercosis), muscles, and eyes, forming cysts. Neurocysticercosis can cause seizures, headaches, and neurological deficits.

2. Phylum Nemathelminthes (Roundworms)

Nematodes are cylindrical, unsegmented, triploblastic, pseudocoelomate worms. They are one of the most abundant animal groups on Earth.

2.1 General Characters and Classification

General Characters

  • Body Form: Cylindrical, tapering at both ends.
  • Symmetry: Bilateral.
  • Body Wall: Covered by a tough, flexible, non-living cuticle, which is molted periodically.
  • Body Cavity: Pseudocoelomate.
  • Digestive System: Complete, with a distinct mouth and anus (a "tube-within-a-tube" body plan).
  • Circulatory & Respiratory Systems: Absent.
  • Excretory System: Consists of an H-shaped Renette cell system.
  • Reproduction: Dioecious (sexes are separate), with sexual dimorphism (males are usually smaller than females and have a curved posterior end).

Classification of Nemathelminthes (Up to Classes)

Class Key Characteristics Examples
Aphasmidia (Adenophorea) Phasmids (posterior sensory organs) are absent. Excretory system is simple or absent. Trichinella, Trichuris
Phasmidia (Secernentea) Phasmids are present. Excretory system is well-developed with lateral canals. Most parasitic nematodes belong here. Ascaris, Wuchereria, Ancylostoma (hookworm)

2.2 Life Cycle and Pathogenicity of Ascaris lumbricoides (Giant Roundworm)

Ascaris lumbricoides is a monogenetic parasite (completes its life cycle in a single host, the human).

Life Cycle

  1. An infected person passes fertilized eggs in their feces.
  2. The eggs become infective after developing into an embryonated stage in moist soil.
  3. Infection occurs when a human ingests these embryonated eggs through contaminated food or water.
  4. The eggs hatch in the small intestine, and the larvae penetrate the intestinal wall to enter the bloodstream.
  5. They undertake a remarkable migration: blood → liver → heart → lungs.
  6. In the lungs, they break into the alveoli, crawl up the trachea, and are swallowed back into the intestine.
  7. In the intestine, they mature into adult worms, mate, and produce eggs, continuing the cycle.

Pathogenicity

  • Ascariasis: The disease caused by Ascaris infection.
  • Larval Migration Phase: Can cause pneumonia-like symptoms, coughing, and fever (Loeffler's syndrome).
  • Adult Worm Phase: A heavy worm load can cause abdominal pain, malnutrition (by competing for nutrients), and even intestinal blockage, which is a medical emergency.
Exam Tip: The key to controlling Ascaris is proper sanitation and hygiene, as infection occurs via the fecal-oral route. No intermediate host is involved.

2.3 Concept of Pseudocoelom

A pseudocoelom ("false cavity") is a body cavity that is not completely lined by mesoderm. It is derived from the embryonic blastocoel. The organs lie freely within this fluid-filled cavity.
  • Formation: It is a persistent blastocoel of the embryo.
  • Lining: It is lined by mesoderm on the outer side (adjacent to the body wall) but not on the inner side (adjacent to the gut).
  • Function: The fluid-filled pseudocoelom acts as a hydrostatic skeleton, aids in circulation of substances, and provides space for organ development.
  • Found in: Phylum Nemathelminthes, Rotifera.

3. Phylum Annelida (Segmented Worms)

Annelids (from Latin: annellus = little ring) are triploblastic, coelomate animals characterized by true metameric segmentation.

3.1 General Characters and Classification

General Characters

  • Habitat: Found in marine, freshwater, and moist terrestrial environments.
  • Segmentation: Body exhibits true metamerism, both externally and internally.
  • Body Wall: Covered by a thin cuticle, with circular and longitudinal muscles that aid in locomotion.
  • Locomotory Structures: Possess chitinous setae or parapodia.
  • Body Cavity: True coelomates (schizocoelom). The coelom is well-developed and partitioned by septa.
  • Circulatory System: Closed type, with blood contained within vessels.
  • Excretory System: Consists of segmentally arranged coiled tubes called nephridia.
  • Nervous System: A pair of cerebral ganglia (brain) and a double ventral nerve cord with segmental ganglia.

Classification of Annelida (Up to Classes)

Class Key Characteristics Examples
Polychaeta Mostly marine. Have numerous setae arranged on lateral appendages called parapodia. Sexes are separate. Nereis (clam worm), Aphrodite (sea mouse)
Oligochaeta Mostly terrestrial and freshwater. Setae are few. Parapodia are absent. Hermaphroditic, with a clitellum for cocoon formation. Pheretima (earthworm), Lumbricus
Hirudinea Mostly freshwater ectoparasites. Body is dorsoventrally flattened with a fixed number of segments. Lack setae and parapodia. Have anterior and posterior suckers. Hirudinaria (leech)

3.2 Evolution of Coelom and Metamerism

Evolution of Coelom

The coelom is a fluid-filled body cavity completely lined by mesoderm.

  1. Acoelomate (e.g., Platyhelminthes): No body cavity.
  2. Pseudocoelomate (e.g., Nemathelminthes): A false coelom not fully lined by mesoderm.
  3. Eucoelomate (e.g., Annelida onwards): A true coelom. This was a major evolutionary step, providing:
    • A hydrostatic skeleton for more efficient locomotion.
    • Space for the development of complex organ systems.
    • A medium for transporting substances.
    • Isolation of the gut from body wall movements.

Evolution of Metamerism

Metamerism, or segmentation, likely evolved as an adaptation for burrowing. The compartmentalization of the coelom by septa allowed for precise, localized muscle contractions, making peristaltic movement more powerful and efficient. This innovation was so successful that it has been retained and modified in two of the most successful animal phyla: Arthropoda and Chordata.

Key Concept: The combination of a true coelom and metamerism in annelids provided the structural foundation for the evolution of larger, more complex, and more active animals.

3.3 Digestion in Annelida with special reference to Leech

Leeches (Class Hirudinea) are primarily sanguivorous (blood-sucking). Their digestive system is highly adapted for this diet.

  1. Ingestion: The leech attaches to a host using its posterior sucker. The anterior sucker surrounds the mouth, which contains three chitinous jaws. The jaws make a Y-shaped, painless incision in the host's skin.
  2. Anticoagulation: Salivary glands secrete hirudin, a powerful anticoagulant that prevents the host's blood from clotting, ensuring a steady flow. Saliva also contains an anesthetic.
  3. Digestion: The ingested blood is stored in a large crop, which has many paired diverticula (pouches). This allows the leech to store many times its own body weight in blood. Digestion is a very slow process, carried out by enzymes. A single large meal can sustain a leech for several months.
  4. Absorption & Egestion: Digested food is absorbed in the intestine, and undigested waste is egested through the anus.
