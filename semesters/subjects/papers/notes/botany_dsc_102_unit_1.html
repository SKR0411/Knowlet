General Characteristics

Phycology (or Algology): The branch of botany that deals with the study of algae.

Algae are a large, diverse group of simple, typically autotrophic organisms. They are "plant-like" but lack true stems, roots, leaves, and complex reproductive structures like flowers.

  • Thallophytes: Their body is a simple thallus, not differentiated into true roots, stems, or leaves.
  • Autotrophic: The vast majority are photosynthetic, containing chlorophyll and other pigments.
  • Aquatic: Primarily found in freshwater, marine, and moist terrestrial environments.
  • Cell Structure: Can be prokaryotic (Cyanobacteria/Blue-Green Algae) or eukaryotic (all other groups).
  • Reproductive Organs: Simple and unicellular. If multicellular, all cells are fertile (i.e., they lack a sterile jacket of cells around the gametes).
  • Embryo: No embryo is formed after fertilization (unlike in higher plants).

Ecology and Distribution

Algae are ubiquitous and can be found in almost any environment containing light and moisture.

  • Aquatic Algae:
    • Freshwater: Found in ponds, lakes, rivers (e.g., Chlamydomonas, Volvox, Spirogyra).
    • Marine: Found in seas and oceans (e.g., Kelps, *Polysiphonia*, *Ectocarpus*).
  • Plankton: Microscopic algae that float in the water column.
    • Phytoplankton: Form the base of most aquatic food webs and produce ~50% of the world's oxygen.
  • Terrestrial Algae: Grow on moist soil, rocks, and tree bark (e.g., Nostoc, Vaucheria).
  • Algae of Unusual Habitats:
    • Cryophytes: Grow on snow and ice, causing "red snow" (e.g., Chlamydomonas nivalis).
    • Thermophytes: Grow in hot springs (e.g., some Cyanobacteria).
    • Epiphytes: Grow on other plants.
    • Endophytes: Grow inside other plants.
    • Symbiotic: Live in association with other organisms (e.g., algae in Lichens, Zoochlorellae in Hydra).

Range of Thallus Organization

The algal thallus shows a great diversity of form and structure, from simple single cells to complex, plant-like bodies.

Form Description Example(s)
Unicellular Single-celled. Can be motile (flagellated) or non-motile. Chlamydomonas (motile), Chlorella (non-motile)
Colonial An aggregation of individual cells.
  • Coenobium: A colony with a fixed number of cells and definite shape.
  • Palmelloid: Cells embedded in a common gelatinous matrix.
 Volvox (Coenobium), Tetraspora (Palmelloid)
Filamentous Cells arranged in a thread-like chain. Can be unbranched or branched. Spirogyra (unbranched), Cladophora (branched)
Siphonaceous A large, tube-like thallus that is coenocytic (multinucleate without cross-walls). Vaucheria
Parenchymatous A thallus formed by cell division in multiple planes, resulting in a solid, tissue-like structure. Ulva (the "sea lettuce")
Pseudoparenchymatous A complex thallus formed by the aggregation of many interwoven filaments, appearing tissue-like. Polysiphonia (Red algae)

[Diagram: Labeled Eukaryotic Algal Cell]

Cell Wall

Provides shape and protection. The composition varies and is a key classification feature.

  • Chlorophyta (Green Algae): Primarily Cellulose.
  • Phaeophyta (Brown Algae): Cellulose and Alginic acid.
  • Rhodophyta (Red Algae): Cellulose, Pectins, and Agar or Carrageenan.
  • Diatoms: Cell wall is a silica "box" called a frustule.
  • Cyanophyta (Prokaryotic): Peptidoglycan (like bacteria).

Pigment System

Pigments capture light energy. The combination of pigments is a major characteristic for classifying algae.

  • Chlorophylls: Primary photosynthetic pigment (Chlorophyll-a is universal).
  • Carotenoids: Accessory pigments (e.g., Carotenes, Xanthophylls like Fucoxanthin).
  • Phycobilins: Accessory pigments (Phycoerythrin - red, Phycocyanin - blue). Found only in Cyanophyta and Rhodophyta.
Group Major Pigments
Cyanophyta (Blue-Green) Chl-a, Phycocyanin, Phycoerythrin
Chlorophyta (Green) Chl-a and Chl-b (same as higher plants)
Phaeophyta (Brown) Chl-a, Chl-c, Fucoxanthin (gives brown color)
Rhodophyta (Red) Chl-a, Phycoerythrin (gives red color)

Reserve Food

Excess sugar from photosynthesis is stored in various forms.

  • Chlorophyta: True Starch (stored in chloroplasts).
  • Phaeophyta: Laminarin and Mannitol.
  • Rhodophyta: Floridean Starch (stored in cytoplasm).
  • Cyanophyta: Cyanophycean Starch.

Flagella

Motile cells (zoospores, gametes) use flagella for movement. Their type and number are important.

  • Whiplash (Acronematic): Smooth surface.
  • Tinsel (Pleuronematic): Surface covered with fine, hair-like appendages (mastigonemes).

Note: Red algae (Rhodophyta) and Blue-Green Algae (Cyanophyta) are completely non-flagellated. They have no motile cells at any life cycle stage.

Methods of Reproduction

1. Vegetative Reproduction

Simple multiplication of the thallus.

  • Fragmentation: The thallus breaks into pieces, and each piece grows into a new individual (e.g., Spirogyra).
  • Hormogonia: Short, motile fragments of a filament in Cyanobacteria (e.g., Oscillatoria).
  • Akinetes: Thick-walled resting spores that survive harsh conditions (e.g., Nostoc).

2. Asexual Reproduction

Involves the formation of specialized spores.

  • Zoospores: Flagellated, motile spores (e.g., Chlamydomonas).
  • Aplanospores: Non-flagellated, non-motile spores.
  • Autospores: Non-motile spores that are miniature replicas of the parent cell (e.g., Chlorella).

3. Sexual Reproduction

Involves the fusion of gametes. It shows a clear evolutionary trend:

  1. Isogamy: Fusion of two gametes that are identical in size, shape, and structure (morphologically identical).
  2. Anisogamy: Fusion of two gametes that are morphologically similar but different in size (the female macrogamete is larger than the male microgamete).
  3. Oogamy: Fusion of a large, non-motile female gamete (egg or ovum) with a small, motile male gamete (sperm or antherozoid). This is the most advanced form. (e.g., Volvox, Oedogonium).

Classification (Fritsch)

F.E. Fritsch (one of the phycologists you need to know) proposed a comprehensive classification in his book "The Structure and Reproduction of the Algae" (1935). He is often called the "Father of Modern Phycology."

He classified algae into 11 classes based on:

  1. Pigmentation (color)
  2. Chemical nature of reserve food
  3. Type and number of flagella
  4. Cell wall composition
  5. Thallus organization

Fritsch's 11 Classes:

  1. Chlorophyceae (Green Algae)
  2. Xanthophyceae (Yellow-Green Algae)
  3. Chrysophyceae (Golden-Brown Algae)
  4. Bacillariophyceae (Diatoms)
  5. Cryptophyceae
  6. Dinophyceae (Dinoflagellates)
  7. Chloromonadineae
  8. Euglenineae (Euglenoids)
  9. Phaeophyceae (Brown Algae)
  10. Rhodophyceae (Red Algae)
  11. Myxophyceae (or Cyanophyceae - the Blue-Green Algae)

Economic Importance

Algae have vast economic and ecological importance, both positive and negative.

Positive Importance (Role in Environment, Agriculture, Industry)

  • Primary Producers: Phytoplankton form the base of all aquatic food chains.
  • Oxygen Production: Algae (especially phytoplankton) produce 50-70% of the Earth's oxygen via photosynthesis.
  • Food: Eaten directly by humans. (e.g., Porphyra - "nori" for sushi, Chlorella, Spirulina - protein supplements).
  • Fodder: Seaweeds (e.g., Laminaria) are harvested and fed to livestock.
  • Biofertilizers (Agriculture): Blue-green algae (e.g., Nostoc, Anabaena) can fix atmospheric nitrogen, enriching paddy fields. Seaweed extracts are used as liquid fertilizers.
  • Industrial Gums (Phycocolloids):
    • Agar-Agar: From red algae (e.g., Gelidium). Used as a solidifying agent in culture media (biotechnology), and in jellies/ice cream.
    • Alginates: From brown algae (e.g., Laminaria). Used as thickeners in ice cream, paint, and cosmetics.
    • Carrageenan: From red algae ("Irish moss"). Used as a stabilizer in milk products, toothpaste, and puddings.
  • Diatomaceous Earth: The fossilized silica shells of diatoms. A gritty powder used in filters (sugar refining, pools), mild abrasives (toothpaste, metal polish), and insecticide.
  • Biotechnology (Biofuel): Algae grow fast and can produce lipids (oils) that can be converted into biodiesel.

Negative Importance

  • Algal Blooms (Eutrophication): Excess nutrients (from fertilizer runoff) cause rapid growth of algae. When they die, decomposers consume all the oxygen, creating "dead zones" that kill fish.
  • Toxins: Some blooms (e.g., "red tides" by dinoflagellates) produce toxins harmful to fish, shellfish, and humans.
  • Water Fouling: Algae can clog water filters and produce undesirable tastes/odors in drinking water supplies.
