Fungi: General characteristics, Classification and Economic importance

General Characteristics

Mycology: The branch of biology that deals with the study of fungi.

Fungi are a kingdom of eukaryotic, achlorophyllous, heterotrophic organisms.

  • Thallus: Their body is a thallus. It can be unicellular (e.g., Yeasts) or filamentous (e.g., Molds).
  • Mycelium: The filamentous body is a network of thread-like structures called hyphae. The entire network is a mycelium.
  • Hyphae Type:
    • Aseptate (Coenocytic): No cross-walls; multinucleate. Found in "lower fungi" (e.g., *Rhizopus*).
    • Septate: Cross-walls (septa) divide the hyphae into cells. Found in "higher fungi" (e.g., *Penicillium*, *Puccinia*).
  • Cell Wall: Composed primarily of Chitin (not cellulose).
  • Nutrition: Heterotrophic and absorptive. They secrete digestive enzymes and absorb the nutrients.
    • Saprophytes: Decomposers of dead organic matter.
    • Parasites: Get nutrients from living hosts (e.g., *Puccinia*).
    • Symbionts: Mutualistic relationship (e.g., Lichens, Mycorrhiza).
  • Reserve Food: Glycogen and Oil (not starch).
  • Reproduction: By vegetative, asexual (spores like conidia, sporangiospores), and sexual (spores like ascospores, basidiospores, zygospores) means.

Classification (Overview)

Fungi are broadly classified into major groups (e.g., in Ainsworth's system) based on their sexual reproductive structures and hyphal morphology.

  • Mastigomycotina: "Lower fungi," often aquatic, produce motile zoospores.
  • Zygomycotina: Form a diploid zygospore as the sexual spore. Hyphae are coenocytic (e.g., *Rhizopus*).
  • Ascomycotina (Sac Fungi): Form sexual spores (ascospores) internally in a sac-like ascus. Hyphae are septate (e.g., *Penicillium*, Yeast).
  • Basidiomycotina (Club Fungi): Form sexual spores (basidiospores) externally on a club-shaped basidium. Hyphae are septate (e.g., *Puccinia*, Mushrooms).
  • Deuteromycotina (Fungi Imperfecti): An artificial group for fungi where the sexual (teleomorph) stage is not known. They reproduce only asexually (e.g., *Alternaria*).

Economic Importance

Positive Importance (Beneficial)
  • Decomposers: Fungi are the planet's primary decomposers, recycling nutrients from dead wood (lignin) and other materials.
  • Food Industry:
    • Yeast (Saccharomyces): Essential for baking (bread) and brewing (beer, wine) via fermentation.
    • Mushrooms (Agaricus): Cultivated as a food source.
    • Cheese: Penicillium species are used to ripen blue cheese and camembert.
  • Medicine (Pharmaceuticals):
    • Antibiotics: Penicillin, the first antibiotic, is from Penicillium notatum.
    • Immunosuppressants: Cyclosporin (from Tolypocladium) is vital for organ transplants.
    • Statins: Cholesterol-lowering drugs (e.g., Lovastatin) are derived from fungi.
  • Agriculture: Mycorrhizal fungi act as biofertilizers, enhancing nutrient (especially phosphorus) uptake in plants.
Negative Importance (Harmful)
  • Plant Diseases: Fungi are the most significant plant pathogens, causing huge crop losses (e.g., *Puccinia* - rusts, *Ustilago* - smuts, Late blight of potato).
  • Human Diseases (Mycoses): Cause infections like Athlete's Foot, Ringworm, and serious systemic infections (e.g., Aspergillosis) in immunocompromised individuals.
  • Food Spoilage: Molds (like *Rhizopus*, *Aspergillus*) spoil bread, fruits, and other foods.
  • Mycotoxins: Fungi like Aspergillus flavus produce Aflatoxins on crops (e.g., peanuts), which are highly carcinogenic.

Structure, morphology and life cycle of:

Rhizopus (Class: Zygomycotina)

  • Morphology: Commonly known as "bread mold." A saprophyte that grows as a cottony white (later black) mass on bread, fruits, etc.
  • Structure: The mycelium is coenocytic (aseptate) and highly branched. It consists of three types of hyphae:
    1. Stolons: Creeping horizontal hyphae that spread over the substrate.
    2. Rhizoids: Root-like, anchoring hyphae that penetrate the substrate and absorb nutrients.
    3. Sporangiophores: Erect, unbranched hyphae that rise in clusters from the nodes (where rhizoids form).
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual: By sporangiospores. The sporangiophores swell at the tip to form a large, black, globular sporangium. Inside, numerous non-motile, haploid (n) spores are formed around a central dome called the columella. The sporangium wall breaks, releasing spores.
    • Sexual: By gametangial copulation (conjugation).
      • Occurs between two hyphae of opposite mating strains (+ and -).
      • The hyphae produce swellings (progametangia) that form gametangia.
      • The gametangia fuse (plasmogamy), and their nuclei fuse (karyogamy) to form a diploid (2n) zygote.
      • The zygote develops a thick, black, warty wall and becomes a dormant zygospore.
      • Upon germination, the zygospore undergoes meiosis to produce a germ-sporangium, which releases haploid (n) spores.
  • Life Cycle: Haplontic (the mycelium is haploid).

Penicillium (Class: Ascomycotina)

  • Morphology: A common saprophytic mold ("blue/green mold") on citrus fruits, jams, and cheese. Source of the antibiotic penicillin.
  • Structure: The mycelium is a mat of branched, septate hyphae. Cells are uninucleate.
  • Reproduction:
    • Asexual (Main method): By conidia.
      • Erect, septate conidiophores rise from the mycelium.
      • The conidiophore branches at the tip in a brush-like fashion (the penicillus).
      • The branches (rami, metulae) bear bottle-shaped cells called phialides.
      • Phialides produce long, basipetal chains of small, spherical, green-pigmented, haploid (n) conidia.
    • Sexual: (Rare, found in the teleomorph state, e.g., Talaromyces).
      • Two hyphae (ascogonium and antheridium) fuse.
      • This leads to the development of a Cleistothecium – a small, spherical, closed fruiting body (ascocarp).
      • Inside the cleistothecium, many small, globular asci (sacs) are formed.
      • Meiosis (and one mitosis) occurs in each ascus to produce 8 haploid (n) ascospores.
      • The ascospores are released when the cleistothecium decays.

Puccinia (Class: Basidiomycotina)

  • Morphology: An obligate parasitic fungus that causes "rust" diseases on plants. Puccinia graminis-tritici causes "Black Stem Rust of Wheat."
  • Key Features:
    • Heteroecious: It requires two different hosts to complete its life cycle (Wheat and Barberry).
    • Polymorphic (or Pleomorphic): It produces five different types of spores in its life cycle.
    • The mycelium is dikaryotic (n+n) for most of its life on wheat.
  • Life Cycle and 5 Spore Stages:
    1. Stage 0: Pycnia (on Barberry leaf - upper):
      • Haploid (n) basidiospores (from stage 4) infect the Barberry leaf.
      • They form flask-shaped pycnia, which produce haploid pycniospores (spermatia) and receptive hyphae.
      • Plasmogamy (fusion of a pycniospore with a receptive hypha of the opposite strain) occurs, establishing the dikaryotic (n+n) state.
    2. Stage I: Aecia (on Barberry leaf - lower):
      • The (n+n) mycelium grows to the lower leaf surface and forms cup-shaped aecia.
      • Aecia produce long chains of dikaryotic (n+n) aeciospores.
    3. Stage II: Uredia (on Wheat host):
      • Aeciospores (n+n) are wind-blown, infect the Wheat host.
      • They form reddish-brown pustules (uredosori) containing dikaryotic (n+n) uredospores.
      • This is the "repeating stage" that re-infects wheat all summer.
    4. Stage III: Telia (on Wheat host):
      • Late in the season, the same sori produce black, two-celled, thick-walled, dikaryotic (n+n) teliospores (in teliosori).
      • Karyogamy (n+n → 2n) occurs in each cell, making them diploid (2n). These spores overwinter.
    5. Stage IV: Basidia (on germinating teliospore):
      • In spring, the diploid (2n) teliospore germinates. Each cell undergoes meiosis to form a 4-celled basidium (promycelium).
      • Each cell of the basidium produces one haploid (n) basidiospore.
      • These basidiospores infect the Barberry leaf, completing the cycle.

Lichens: General account and significance

General Account

Lichen: A composite, dual organism resulting from a symbiotic (mutualistic) association between a fungus (the mycobiont) and an alga or cyanobacterium (the phycobiont).
  • The Symbiosis:
    • The fungus (usually an Ascomycete) provides the structure, absorbs water/minerals, and protects the alga from desiccation and UV light.
    • The alga (e.g., Trebouxia - green alga, or Nostoc - cyanobacterium) performs photosynthesis, providing food (sugars) for both partners.
  • Thallus Forms:
    1. Crustose: A thin, flat crust, strongly attached to the substrate (e.g., on rocks).
    2. Foliose: A flat, broad, leaf-like thallus, loosely attached (e.g., Parmelia).
    3. Fruticose: A shrubby, branched, cylindrical thallus (e.g., Usnea - "Old man's beard").
  • Reproduction: Primarily by vegetative means that disperse *both* partners together:
    • Soredia: Powdery, microscopic clusters of algal cells wrapped in fungal hyphae. Dispersed by wind.
    • Isidia: Small, finger-like outgrowths from the thallus that break off.

Significance (Economic and Ecological)

  • Pioneer Species: Lichens are the first organisms to colonize bare rock. They secrete acids that break down the rock, beginning the process of soil formation.
  • Air Pollution Indicators: Most lichens are highly sensitive to sulfur dioxide (SO2) and other air pollutants. Their absence in urban areas indicates poor air quality.
