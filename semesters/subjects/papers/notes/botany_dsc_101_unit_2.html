Viruses: Stature, Classification (Baltimore)

Virus Stature

Viruses: Acellular, non-living entities that are obligate intracellular parasites. They lack their own metabolic machinery and must hijack a host cell to replicate.

They consist of a nucleic acid core (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein coat (capsid). They are "infectious particles" rather than organisms.

Classification (Baltimore)

The Baltimore Classification system (proposed by David Baltimore) is a fundamental concept. It classifies viruses into seven groups based on their type of genome and how they produce messenger RNA (mRNA) for protein synthesis.

Group Genome Type Example
Group I dsDNA (double-stranded DNA) Herpesvirus, Poxvirus, T-phages
Group II ssDNA (single-stranded DNA) Parvovirus
Group III dsRNA (double-stranded RNA) Reovirus
Group IV (+)ssRNA (Positive-sense ssRNA) Picornavirus, Flavivirus, TMV
Group V (-)ssRNA (Negative-sense ssRNA) Influenza virus, Rabies virus
Group VI ssRNA-RT (ssRNA with Reverse Transcriptase) Retroviruses (e.g., HIV)
Group VII dsDNA-RT (dsDNA with Reverse Transcriptase) Hepadnavirus (Hepatitis B)
Key Concept: (+)ssRNA (Group IV) can be directly translated by the host ribosome, acting just like mRNA. (-)ssRNA (Group V) must first be transcribed into (+)ssRNA by a viral enzyme (RNA-dependent RNA polymerase) before translation.

General Structure and Replication

General Structure

  • Genome: The genetic material, either DNA or RNA, single or double-stranded.
  • Capsid: A protein coat surrounding the genome. It's made of subunits called capsomeres. The capsid protects the genome and helps it attach to the host cell.
  • Envelope (Optional): A lipid membrane derived from the host cell that surrounds the capsid in some viruses (e.g., Influenza, HIV). Viruses without it are "naked" viruses.
[Diagram: Structure of a Naked (e.g., T-phage) and an Enveloped (e.g., Influenza) Virus]

General Replication Cycle (Lytic)

  1. Attachment: The virus binds to specific receptors on the host cell surface.
  2. Penetration/Entry: The virus or its genome enters the host cell (e.g., by injection, endocytosis, or membrane fusion).
  3. Uncoating: The capsid is removed, releasing the viral genome.
  4. Synthesis (Replication & Protein Production): The host cell's machinery is hijacked to:
    • Replicate the viral genome.
    • Transcribe viral genes into mRNA.
    • Translate the mRNA into viral proteins (capsid proteins, enzymes).
  5. Assembly (Maturation): New viral genomes are packaged into newly made capsids to form new virus particles (virions).
  6. Release: The new virions exit the host cell, often by lysis (bursting) the cell or by budding (for enveloped viruses).

Plant Viruses - TMV

Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV)

TMV is a classic example of a plant virus and was the first virus to be discovered and crystallized.

  • Structure: It is a rod-shaped, naked virus. Its capsid is a helix made of thousands of identical capsomere proteins.
  • Genome: It has a (+)ssRNA genome (Baltimore Group IV).
  • Disease: It causes Tobacco Mosaic Disease, characterized by "mosaic" patterns (light/dark green patches) and mottling on the leaves of tobacco, tomato, and other plants.
  • Transmission: It is extremely stable and transmitted mechanically (e.g., on contaminated tools, hands, or by infected plant sap). It is not typically spread by insects.

Bacteriophage (T-phage Lytic and Lysogenic cycle)

Bacteriophage (or Phage): A virus that infects and replicates within bacteria.

T-phages (e.g., T4 phage) are "T-even" phages that infect E. coli. They have a complex structure: an icosahedral "head" (containing dsDNA) and a "tail" assembly (sheath, base plate, tail fibers).

Phages can have two main life cycles:

[Diagram: Lytic vs. Lysogenic Cycles]

1. Lytic Cycle (Virulent)

This is a "slash and burn" cycle that ends in the death of the host cell.

  1. Attachment: Tail fibers attach to specific receptors on the E. coli cell wall.
  2. Penetration: The tail sheath contracts, injecting the phage's dsDNA into the bacterium. The capsid stays outside.
  3. Synthesis: The phage DNA takes over. It directs synthesis of phage DNA and phage proteins. Host DNA is degraded.
  4. Assembly: New phage heads, tails, and fibers are assembled, and DNA is packaged into the heads.
  5. Release: The phage produces an enzyme (lysozyme) that degrades the bacterial cell wall, causing the cell to lyse (burst) and release hundreds of new phages.

2. Lysogenic Cycle (Temperate)

This is a "stealth" cycle where the phage co-exists with the host.

  1. Attachment & Penetration: Same as the lytic cycle.
  2. Integration: Instead of taking over, the phage DNA integrates into the bacterial chromosome. The integrated phage DNA is now called a prophage.
  3. Replication: The prophage is dormant. It is copied and passed on to daughter cells every time the bacterium divides. The bacterium is now lysogenic (carrying the prophage).
  4. Induction: The prophage can later be "induced" (e.g., by stress like UV light) to exit the bacterial chromosome and enter the lytic cycle, leading to synthesis, assembly, and lysis.

Methods of Isolation of Plant Viruses

Viruses cannot be grown on artificial media. They must be cultured in living host cells.

  • Mechanical Inoculation: The most common method. An extract from an infected plant (sap) is prepared and simply rubbed onto the leaves of a healthy indicator plant, often with a mild abrasive (like carborundum) to create tiny wounds for the virus to enter.
  • Grafting: An infected piece of plant (scion) is grafted onto a healthy plant (stock).
  • Vector Transmission: Using the natural insect vector (e.g., aphids) to feed on an infected plant and then transfer to a healthy one.

After inoculation, the viruses are "isolated" by observing symptoms or by purifying the virus particles from the plant tissue using centrifugation and chemical precipitation.

Economic Importance of Viruses

Viruses have significant negative and positive economic impacts.

Negative Importance

  • Human Disease: Cause countless diseases (e.g., influenza, HIV/AIDS, COVID-19), leading to healthcare costs and loss of productivity.
  • Plant Disease: Major crop losses (e.g., TMV, Citrus tristeza virus).
  • Animal Disease: Impact livestock (e.g., Foot-and-mouth disease, Avian influenza).

Positive Importance

  • Genetic Engineering: Viruses (especially bacteriophages and retroviruses) are used as vectors to carry desired genes into cells for gene therapy and biotechnology.
  • Phage Therapy: Using bacteriophages to treat bacterial infections, especially those resistant to antibiotics.
  • Biological Control: Using viruses (e.comg., baculoviruses) as highly specific biopesticides to control insect pests.

Bacteria: General Characteristics and Classification

General Characteristics

  • Prokaryotic: No nucleus or membrane-bound organelles.
  • Unicellular: Most are single-celled.
  • Genetic Material: A single, circular chromosome located in the nucleoid region. May also have plasmids (small, extrachromosomal DNA rings).
  • Cell Wall: Most have a cell wall containing peptidoglycan (murein).
  • Ribosomes: Have 70S ribosomes (different from eukaryotic 80S).
  • Reproduction: Asexual, primarily by binary fission.

Classification (Major Groups)

Classification can be based on morphology (shape), Gram stain, or (most accurately) molecular data (rRNA sequencing).

  • Based on Shape:
    • Coccus (pl. cocci): Spherical (e.g., Staphylococcus - clusters, Streptococcus - chains).
    • Bacillus (pl. bacilli): Rod-shaped (e.g., E. coli, Bacillus).
    • Spirillum (pl. spirilla): Spiral-shaped (e.g., Treponema).
  • Based on Gram Stain:
    • Gram-Positive: Thick peptidoglycan wall (e.g., Bacillus, Streptomyces).
    • Gram-Negative: Thin peptidoglycan wall + outer lipid membrane (e.g., E. coli, Rhizobium).
  • Major Phyla (Molecular):
    • Proteobacteria: Large, diverse group of Gram-negatives (includes E. coli, Rhizobium).
    • Firmicutes: Gram-positives with low G+C content (includes Bacillus, Lactobacillus).
    • Actinobacteria: Gram-positives with high G+C content (includes Streptomyces).

Bacterial Cell Structure

[Diagram: Labeled Bacterial Cell Structure]
  • Cell Wall: Provides shape and protection from osmotic lysis. Composed of peptidoglycan.
    • Gram-Positive: Thick peptidoglycan layer, teichoic acids.
    • Gram-Negative: Thin peptidoglycan layer plus an outer membrane containing lipopolysaccharide (LPS), which acts as an endotoxin.
  • Flagella (sing. flagellum): Long, whip-like appendages used for motility (locomotion).
    • Arrangement: Monotrichous (single), Lophotrichous (tuft at one end), Amphitrichous (at both ends), Peritrichous (all over).
  • Pili (sing. pilus): Short, hair-like appendages. The sex pilus is used for DNA transfer during conjugation. Fimbriae are used for attachment.
  • Capsule/Slime Layer (Glycocalyx): A sticky outer layer, usually polysaccharide. Used for attachment and evading the host immune system.
  • Nucleoid: The region containing the single, circular bacterial chromosome.
  • Plasmids: Small, circular, extrachromosomal DNA molecules that carry non-essential genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance).

Genetic Recombination

Bacteria do not reproduce sexually, but they can exchange genetic material. This is called Horizontal Gene Transfer (HGT).

1. Conjugation (Bacterial "Sex")

Direct, cell-to-cell transfer of DNA (usually a plasmid) via a sex pilus.

  • F+ to F- Transfer: An F+ cell (donor) has the F (fertility) plasmid. It forms a pilus, attaches to an F- cell (recipient), and transfers a copy of the F plasmid. The F- cell becomes F+.
  • Hfr (High Frequency of Recombination): If the F plasmid integrates into the bacterial chromosome, the cell is called Hfr. When an Hfr cell conjugates, it tries to transfer its entire chromosome (with the F plasmid inside) to an F- cell.

2. Transformation

The uptake and incorporation of "naked" DNA (fragments from dead, lysed cells) from the environment. This was famously demonstrated in Griffith's experiment with Streptococcus pneumoniae.

3. Transduction

The transfer of bacterial DNA from one cell to another via a bacteriophage (virus).
