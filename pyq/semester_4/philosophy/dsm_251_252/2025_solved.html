Question 2 (a) [10 Marks]

What is meant by 'Truth' and 'Validity' in Logic? [span_10](start_span)Distinguish between them with suitable examples.[span_10](end_span)

Truth: Truth is a property of individual propositions (statements). [span_11](start_span)A statement is true if it corresponds to reality.[span_11](end_span)

Validity: Validity is a property of deductive arguments. [span_12](start_span)An argument is valid if its structure is such that if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true.[span_12](end_span)

[span_13](start_span) [span_14](start_span) [span_15](start_span)

Distinction: An argument can be valid even if its premises are false (e.g., All dogs are cats; All cats are birds; Therefore all dogs are birds). [span_17](start_span)Conversely, a set of true statements does not automatically form a valid argument if the logic is disconnected.[span_17](end_span)

Question 4 (b) [10 Marks]

Discuss the traditional square of opposition. [span_20](start_span)How does it differ from Boolean square?[span_20](end_span)

[span_21](start_span)

Traditional Square: Defines the relationships between A, E, I, and O propositions:[span_21](end_span)

  • Contradictories: A and O; [span_22](start_span)E and I (One must be true, one false).[span_22](end_span)
    • [span_23](start_span)
  • Contraries: A and E (Both cannot be true).[span_23](end_span)
    • [span_24](start_span)
  • Subcontraries: I and O (Both cannot be false).[span_24](end_span)
  • Subalternation: A to I; [span_25](start_span)E to O (Truth flows down, falsity flows up).[span_25](end_span)

Boolean Difference: George Boole argued that universal propositions (A and E) do not have existential import (they don't claim the subject exists). [span_26](start_span)Consequently, in the Boolean square, only contradictory relationships remain valid; subalternation, contraries, and subcontraries are rejected.[span_26](end_span)

Question 6 (a) [10 Marks]

What is categorical syllogism? [span_29](start_span)Explain the structure and state Copi's general rules.[span_29](end_span)

[span_30](start_span)

Definition: A deductive argument consisting of three categorical propositions (two premises and one conclusion) containing exactly three terms.[span_30](end_span)

[span_31](start_span)

Structure:[span_31](end_span)

    [span_32](start_span)
  1. Major Term (P): Predicate of the conclusion.[span_32](end_span)
    2. [span_33](start_span)
  2. Minor Term (S): Subject of the conclusion.[span_33](end_span)
    3. [span_34](start_span)
  3. Middle Term (M): Appears in both premises but not the conclusion.[span_34](end_span)
[span_35](start_span)

Copi's Rules:[span_35](end_span)

    [span_36](start_span)
  • A valid syllogism must contain exactly three terms used consistently.[span_36](end_span)
    • [span_37](start_span)
  • The middle term must be distributed in at least one premise.[span_37](end_span)
    • [span_38](start_span)
  • If a term is distributed in the conclusion, it must be distributed in the premise.[span_38](end_span)
    • [span_39](start_span)
  • No syllogism is valid if it has two negative premises.[span_39](end_span)
    • [span_40](start_span)
  • If one premise is negative, the conclusion must be negative.[span_40](end_span)

[span_42](start_span)

Question 10 (a) [10 Marks]

What is formal proof of validity? [span_48](start_span)State nine rules of inference.[span_48](end_span)

[span_49](start_span)

A formal proof of validity is a sequence of statements, each of which is either a premise or follows from preceding statements by a rule of inference, ending with the conclusion.[span_49](end_span)

[span_50](start_span)

  • Modus Ponens (M.P.): p ⊃ q; p; [span_51](start_span)∴ q[span_51](end_span)
  • Modus Tollens (M.T.): p ⊃ q; ~q; [span_52](start_span)∴ ~p[span_52](end_span)
  • Hypothetical Syllogism (H.S.): p ⊃ q; q ⊃ r; [span_53](start_span)∴ p ⊃ r[span_53](end_span)
  • Disjunctive Syllogism (D.S.): p ∨ q; ~p; [span_54](start_span)[span_55](start_span)∴ q[span_54](end_span)[span_55](end_span)
  • Constructive Dilemma (C.D.): (p ⊃ q) · (r ⊃ s); p ∨ r; [span_56](start_span)∴ q ∨ s[span_56](end_span)
  • Simplification (Simp.): p · q; [span_57](start_span)∴ p[span_57](end_span)
  • Conjunction (Conj.): p; q; [span_58](start_span)∴ p · q[span_58](end_span)
  • Addition (Add.): p; [span_59](start_span)∴ p ∨ q[span_59](end_span)
  • Absorption (Abs.): p ⊃ q; [span_60](start_span)∴ p ⊃ (p · q)[span_60](end_span)
