UNIT-I

Question 1 (a) 2 Marks

[span_1](start_span)

Define social philosophy.[span_1](end_span)

[span_2](start_span)
Social philosophy is the philosophical study of social behavior and interpretations of society and social institutions in terms of ethical values rather than empirical relations.[span_2](end_span)

Question 1 (b) 2 Marks

[span_3](start_span)

What is political philosophy?[span_3](end_span)

[span_4](start_span)

Political philosophy is the branch of philosophy that explores fundamental questions about the state, government, politics, liberty, justice, and the enforcement of a legal code by authority.[span_4](end_span)

Question 1 (c) 2 Marks

[span_5](start_span)

In what sense social philosophy is related to ethics?[span_5](end_span)

Social philosophy is deeply rooted in ethics because it evaluates social institutions and behaviors based on moral principles like justice, rights, and the "good life." [span_6](start_span)It asks what society ought to be, which is a normative ethical inquiry.[span_6](end_span)

[span_7](start_span)

Discuss the scopes and main concerns of social philosophy.[span_7](end_span)

[span_8](start_span)

The scope of social philosophy is broad, encompassing the foundational principles of social existence.[span_8](end_span) Its main concerns include:

    [span_9](start_span)
  • Nature of Society: Analyzing the origin and structure of social groups.[span_9](end_span)
    • [span_10](start_span)
  • Individual vs. Society: Examining the tension and relationship between personal autonomy and social obligation.[span_10](end_span)
    • [span_11](start_span)
  • Social Values: Investigating ideals such as justice, equality, and liberty.[span_11](end_span)
    • [span_12](start_span)
  • Social Institutions: Critically evaluating the role of family, education, and religion.[span_12](end_span)

Define Nation. Define State. [span_23](start_span)How are they related to each other?[span_23](end_span)

[span_24](start_span)

State: A political entity characterized by population, territory, government, and sovereignty.[span_24](end_span)

[span_25](start_span)

Nation: A socio-cultural entity defined by shared heritage, language, or ethnicity.[span_25](end_span)

Question 6 (a) 10 Marks

What is meant by reform? [span_27](start_span)Distinguish between reform and revolution.[span_27](end_span)

[span_28](start_span)

Reform: Gradual improvement or amendment of what is wrong, corrupt, or unsatisfactory within existing structures.[span_28](end_span)

[span_29](start_span)

Revolution: A fundamental and relatively sudden change in political power and social structure.[span_29](end_span)

[span_30](start_span)

Distinctions: Reform works within the system through legal means, whereas revolution seeks to overthrow the system, often through extra-legal or violent means.[span_30](end_span)

Question 8 (a) 10 Marks

What do you mean by social contract? [span_35](start_span)Discuss critically the social contract theory of the origin of the state.[span_35](end_span)

[span_36](start_span)

Social contract is the hypothetical agreement between individuals to leave the "state of nature" and form a governed society to ensure security and order.[span_36](end_span)

Exponents:

    [span_37](start_span)
  • Hobbes: Contract for survival; absolute sovereignty.[span_37](end_span)
    • [span_38](start_span)
  • Locke: Contract to protect natural rights (life, liberty, property).[span_38](end_span)
    • [span_39](start_span)
  • Rousseau: Contract based on the "General Will."[span_39](end_span)
[span_40](start_span)

Critique: Critics argue that the theory is unhistorical (there is no record of such a contract) and illogical, as a contract requires a legal framework that only exists after a state is formed.[span_40](end_span)

What is civil liberty?[span_41](end_span)

[span_42](start_span)

Civil liberty refers to the freedom of a citizen to exercise customary rights, such as of speech or assembly, without unwarranted or arbitrary interference by the government.[span_42](end_span)

Question 10 (b) 10 Marks

What is meant by terrorism? [span_43](start_span)Discuss briefly the causes of terrorism.[span_43](end_span)

[span_44](start_span)

Terrorism: The unlawful use of violence and intimidation, especially against civilians, in the pursuit of political, religious, or ideological aims.[span_44](end_span)

Causes:

    [span_45](start_span)
  • Political Injustice: Lack of representation or oppression by a state.[span_45](end_span)
    • [span_46](start_span)
  • Economic Deprivation: Poverty and lack of opportunity fueling resentment.[span_46](end_span)
    • [span_47](start_span)
  • Ideological Extremism: Radical interpretations of religious or political doctrines.[span_47](end_span)
    • [span_48](start_span)
  • Psychological Factors: Seeking identity or belonging through radical groups.[span_48](end_span)
