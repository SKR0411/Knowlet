Question 2(a) [Option A] 10 Marks

[span_9](start_span)

Discuss the literary sources for the history of Assam from 1228 to 1826.[span_9](end_span)

Literary sources provide the backbone for reconstructing the medieval history of Assam. They can be categorized as follows:

  • Buranjis: These are the official chronicles of the Ahom court. [span_10](start_span)They recorded political events, diplomatic relations, and administrative orders with high chronological accuracy.[span_10](end_span)
    • [span_11](start_span)
  • Vamsavalis: These were family genealogies, primarily maintained by the Koch and Kachari dynasties, providing insights into royal lineages.[span_11](end_span)
    • [span_12](start_span)
  • Biographies (Charit-Puthis): These documents, centered around the lives of Vaishnava saints like Srimanta Sankardev, offer vital information on the social, cultural, and religious life of the period.[span_12](end_span)
    • [span_13](start_span)
  • Foreign Accounts: Accounts by Persian historians (like Shihab-ud-din Talish’s Fathiyya-i-Ibriyya) and later British records provide an external perspective on Ahom-Mughal conflicts and the socio-economic state of Assam.[span_13](end_span)

Question 2(b) [Option B] 10 Marks

[span_14](start_span)

Explain the significance of Buranji in the traditional historical writing of the Ahoms.[span_14](end_span)

Buranjis represent a unique tradition of historiography in India. Their significance includes:

    [span_15](start_span)
  • Secular Nature: Unlike many medieval texts, Buranjis were largely secular, focusing on statecraft, warfare, and administration rather than religious mythology.[span_15](end_span)
    • [span_16](start_span)
  • Administrative Utility: They served as reference manuals for future kings and officials to understand past treaties, boundaries, and precedents.[span_16](end_span)
    • [span_17](start_span)
  • Chronological Precision: The Ahoms maintained a rigorous system of dating using the Lakni (60-year cycle) or Saka era, making Buranjis highly reliable historical records.[span_17](end_span)
    • [span_18](start_span)
  • Linguistic Evolution: They document the shift from the Tai-Ahom language to the development of early Assamese prose.[span_18](end_span)

Question 4(a) [Option A] 10 Marks

[span_25](start_span)

Discuss the political conditions of Assam at the beginning of the 13th century.[span_25](end_span)

At the dawn of the 13th century, Assam was a fragmented political landscape with several small and competing powers:

    [span_26](start_span)
  • Kamrupa/Kamata Kingdom: Occupying the western part of the Brahmaputra valley, it was the largest entity but faced internal instability and external threats from Bengal.[span_26](end_span)
    • [span_27](start_span)
  • The Kachari Kingdom: Located in central Assam and parts of the south bank, they were a powerful indigenous group.[span_27](end_span)
    • [span_28](start_span)
  • The Chutiya Kingdom: They ruled the northeastern region (Sadiya) and were known for their military strength and advanced metalworking.[span_28](end_span)
    • [span_29](start_span)
  • Bhuiyans: Small independent local landlords (Bhuyan chiefs) ruled pockets of territory in middle Assam.[span_29](end_span)
    • [span_30](start_span)
  • Tribal Chiefdoms: Various tribes like the Morans and Borahis held sway in the easternmost regions where Sukapha first entered.[span_30](end_span)

Question 4(b) [Option B] 10 Marks

[span_31](start_span)

Discuss the Ahom conquest and its foundation under Sukapha.[span_31](end_span)

The foundation of the Ahom kingdom was a result of Sukapha's military discipline and diplomatic sagacity:

    [span_32](start_span)
  • The Migration (1228 CE): Sukapha crossed the Patkai hills with a small band of followers, including soldiers and administrative staff.[span_32](end_span)
  • Conquest of Local Tribes: He initially subdued the Moran and Borahi tribes. [span_33](start_span)Instead of exterminating them, he treated them as equals and encouraged intermarriage.[span_33](end_span)
    • [span_34](start_span)
  • Settlement and Agriculture: Sukapha introduced wet rice cultivation, which provided the economic surplus necessary to sustain a standing army and a growing state.[span_34](end_span)
    • [span_35](start_span)
  • Establishment of Charaideo: In 1253 CE, he established Charaideo as the permanent capital, symbolizing the formal birth of the Ahom state.[span_35](end_span)

Question 6(b) [Option B] 10 Marks

[span_42](start_span)

Discuss the Ahom resistance against the Mughals under Lachit Barphukan, focusing on the Battle of Saraighat.[span_42](end_span)

The Battle of Saraighat (1671) remains the most glorious chapter of Ahom military history:

    [span_43](start_span)
  • Context: After the humiliating Treaty of Ghilajharighat, King Chakradhwaj Singha resolved to expel the Mughals and appointed Lachit as the Barphukan (Commander).[span_43](end_span)
    • [span_44](start_span)
  • Strategic Planning: Knowing the Mughals were superior on land (cavalry), Lachit chose Saraighat, where the Brahmaputra is narrowest, to force a naval confrontation.[span_44](end_span)
  • The Fortification: Overnight earthen walls (Lachit Garh) were raised. [span_45](start_span)Legend says Lachit beheaded his own uncle for negligence during this work, stating "My uncle is not greater than my country."[span_45](end_span)
  • The Battle: Despite being ill, Lachit personally led the naval charge. [span_46](start_span)The Ahom fleet used superior maneuvering to devastate the Mughal navy led by Ram Singh.[span_46](end_span)
    • [span_47](start_span)
  • Outcome: The Mughals suffered a total defeat and retreated to the Manas river, ensuring Ahom sovereignty for decades.[span_47](end_span)

[span_55](start_span)

Critically analyse the rule of the Ahom dynasty under Rudra Singha.[span_55](end_span)

Rudra Singha (1696–1714) is often regarded as the greatest Ahom monarch. His rule was marked by:

    [span_56](start_span)
  • Expansionism: He subdued the Kachari and Jaintia kingdoms, bringing them under Ahom suzerainty.[span_56](end_span)
    • [span_57](start_span)
  • Cultural Synthesis: He encouraged the study of Sanskrit and imported artists, architects, and scholars from mainland India (Bengal).[span_57](end_span)
    • [span_58](start_span)
  • Economic Growth: He established new markets (Hats) and encouraged trade with neighboring regions.[span_58](end_span)
    • [span_59](start_span)
  • Pan-Hindu Alliance: He attempted to form a confederacy of Hindu kings against the Mughal Empire, showcasing his grand political vision.[span_59](end_span)
    • [span_60](start_span)
  • Criticism: While he was a visionary, his shift toward Sanskritization began the slow alienation of traditional Ahom priestly classes.[span_60](end_span)

Question 10(b) [Option B] 10 Marks

[span_65](start_span)

Discuss the Vaishnava reform movement in Assam led by Srimanta Sankardev.[span_65](end_span)

Srimanta Sankardev (1449–1568) led the Neo-Vaishnavite movement, which transformed Assamese society:

    [span_66](start_span)
  • Eka Sarana Nama Dharma: He preached devotion to a single God (Krishna/Vishnu) through 'Nama' (chanting) rather than complex rituals or sacrifices.[span_66](end_span)
    • [span_67](start_span)
  • Social Equality: The movement was democratic and egalitarian, rejecting the caste system and inviting people from all tribes and backgrounds to the fold.[span_67](end_span)
    • [span_68](start_span)
  • Institutions (Sattras and Namghars): He established Sattras (monasteries) and Namghars (prayer halls) which served as the epicenter of social, cultural, and judicial life.[span_68](end_span)
    • [span_69](start_span)
  • Cultural Contribution: He introduced 'Borgeets' (devotional songs), 'Ankiya Naat' (one-act plays), and 'Sattriya Dance' (now a Classical dance of India).[span_69](end_span)
    • [span_70](start_span)
  • Impact: The movement unified the diverse ethnic groups of Assam under a common cultural and religious umbrella, creating a lasting identity.[span_70](end_span)
