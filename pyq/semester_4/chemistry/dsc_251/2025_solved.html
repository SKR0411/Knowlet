UNIT-I

1. (a) Define state and path functions, and give one example of each. 2

[span_7](start_span)

State Function: A thermodynamic property whose value depends only on the current state of the system (initial and final states) and is independent of the path taken to reach that state[span_7](end_span).

    [span_8](start_span)
  • Example: Internal Energy (U), Enthalpy (H), Entropy (S)[span_8](end_span).
[span_9](start_span)

Path Function: A thermodynamic property whose value depends on the specific path or process followed during the change between states[span_9](end_span).

    [span_10](start_span)
  • Example: Work (w), Heat (q)[span_10](end_span).

1. (b) State and explain the zeroth law of thermodynamics. 2

[span_11](start_span)The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, then they are in thermal equilibrium with each other[span_11](end_span).

Explanation: If system A is in thermal equilibrium with system C, and system B is also in thermal equilibrium with system C, then A and B are at the same temperature. [span_12](start_span)This law provides the basis for the measurement of temperature using thermometers[span_12](end_span).

1. (c) What are exact and inexact differentials? 2

Exact Differential: A differential of a state function (like dU or dH) where the integral depends only on the initial and final points. [span_13](start_span)Mathematically, it satisfies Euler's reciprocity relation[span_13](end_span).

Inexact Differential: A differential of a path function (like dw or dq) where the integral depends on the path taken. [span_14](start_span)These are often denoted by a cross or delta symbol (δw, δq)[span_14](end_span).

2. (a) (ii) Derive a relation between temperature and volume in reversible adiabatic expansion. 3

[span_19](start_span)

For an adiabatic process, dq = 0. From First Law: dU = dw = -P dV[span_19](end_span).

[span_20](start_span)

For 1 mole of an ideal gas, dU = Cv dT and P = RT/V[span_20](end_span).

Cv dT = -(RT/V) dV

(Cv/R) (dT/T) = -(dV/V)

Integrating both sides between (T1, V1) and (T2, V2):

(Cv/R) ln(T2/T1) = -ln(V2/V1) = ln(V1/V2)

[span_21](start_span)

Since R = Cp - Cv, then R/Cv = (Cp/Cv) - 1 = γ - 1[span_21](end_span).

T V^(γ-1) = constant

2. (a) (iii) One mole of an ideal gas expands against a constant external pressure of 1 atm from 10 dm³ to 30 dm³. Calculate work. 2

Work done in irreversible expansion against constant external pressure (Pext):

[span_22](start_span)

w = -Pext (V2 - V1)[span_22](end_span)

[span_23](start_span)

w = -1 atm * (30 dm³ - 10 dm³) = -1 atm * 20 dm³[span_23](end_span)

To convert to Joules (1 L-atm = 101.325 J):

[span_24](start_span)

w = -20 * 101.325 J = -2026.5 J[span_24](end_span)

2. (a) (iv) Derive the expression for the work done in reversible isothermal expansion. 3

[span_25](start_span)

In a reversible isothermal process, T is constant and P = nRT/V[span_25](end_span).

w = -∫ P dV (from V1 to V2)

w = -∫ (nRT/V) dV = -nRT ∫ (1/V) dV

w = -nRT [ln V] (from V1 to V2)

w = -nRT ln(V2/V1) or w = -2.303 nRT log(V2/V1)

2. (b) (ii) Deduce Kirchhoff's equation. 3

[span_27](start_span)

Kirchhoff's equation describes the variation of enthalpy of reaction with temperature[span_27](end_span).

ΔH = H(products) - H(reactants)

Differentiating with respect to T at constant pressure:

(d(ΔH)/dT)p = (dH(prod)/dT)p - (dH(react)/dT)p

Since (dH/dT)p = Cp:

[span_28](start_span)

(d(ΔH)/dT)p = ΔCp[span_28](end_span)

Integrating between T1 and T2:

ΔH2 - ΔH1 = ΔCp (T2 - T1)

UNIT-II

3. (a) Give the statement of second law of thermodynamics in terms of entropy. 2

[span_29](start_span)The entropy of the universe (an isolated system) increases in the course of any spontaneous change[span_29](end_span).
[span_30](start_span)

Mathematically, for a spontaneous process: ΔS_total > 0[span_30](end_span).

4. (a) (ii) Derive Gibbs-Helmholtz equation for a process at constant pressure. 3

We know G = H - TS. [span_31](start_span)For a process: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS[span_31](end_span).

[span_32](start_span)

From Maxwell relations, (dΔG/dT)p = -ΔS[span_32](end_span).

Substituting -ΔS in the ΔG equation:

ΔG = ΔH + T(dΔG/dT)p

Rearranging this gives the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation:

[d(ΔG/T) / dT]p = -ΔH / T²

4. (b) (ii) Show that -ΔA_T = w_max. 2

Work function A is defined as A = U - TS. [span_33](start_span)At constant temperature: ΔA = ΔU - TΔS[span_33](end_span).

[span_34](start_span)

From Second Law, for a reversible process: ΔS = q_rev / T, so TΔS = q_rev[span_34](end_span).

ΔA = ΔU - q_rev. From First Law: ΔU - q = w.

[span_35](start_span)

Therefore, ΔA = w_rev[span_35](end_span).

Since work done by the system is -w, then -ΔA = -w_rev = w_max.

[span_36](start_span)The decrease in work function (Helmholtz free energy) at constant temperature equals the maximum work obtainable from the system[span_36](end_span).

5. (a) What is triple point? 2

[span_37](start_span)

The triple point is the specific temperature and pressure at which all three phases (solid, liquid, and gas) of a substance coexist in thermodynamic equilibrium[span_37](end_span). [span_38](start_span)For water, the triple point is at 273.16 K and 0.006 atm[span_38](end_span).

6. (b) (iii) Explain the phase diagram of CO2 in detail. 3

[span_39](start_span)

The phase diagram of CO2 consists of three areas representing Solid, Liquid, and Gas phases[span_39](end_span).

  • Triple Point: Occurs at 5.11 atm and -56.6°C. [span_40](start_span)Below this pressure, liquid CO2 cannot exist[span_40](end_span).
    • [span_41](start_span)
  • Sublimation: At 1 atm, solid CO2 (dry ice) sublimes directly to gas at -78.5°C[span_41](end_span).
  • Critical Point: Occurs at 31.1°C and 73 atm. [span_42](start_span)Beyond this, CO2 exists as a supercritical fluid[span_42](end_span).
    • [span_43](start_span)
  • Slope: Unlike water, the fusion curve (solid-liquid) has a positive slope, meaning melting point increases with pressure[span_43](end_span).

8. (a) (i) Deduce Gibbs-Duhem equation. 2

[span_46](start_span)

The total Gibbs free energy of a mixture is G = Σ ni μi[span_46](end_span).

[span_47](start_span)

Differentiating G: dG = Σ ni dμi + Σ μi dni[span_47](end_span).

[span_48](start_span)

Also, from the fundamental equation at constant T and P: dG = Σ μi dni[span_48](end_span).

Comparing the two equations:

Σ ni dμi = 0
[span_49](start_span)

This shows that chemical potentials of components in a mixture are not independent[span_49](end_span).

9. (a) Define pH. Calculate pH of 10⁻⁹ M HCl. 4

[span_50](start_span)

pH Definition: pH is the negative logarithm (base 10) of the molar concentration of hydrogen ions: pH = -log[H+][span_50](end_span).

[span_51](start_span)

Calculation: Since the concentration is very dilute (10⁻⁹ M), we must include [H+] from water (10⁻⁷ M)[span_51](end_span).

[H+]_total = [H+]_acid + [H+]_water ≈ 1.01 * 10⁻⁷ M.

[span_52](start_span)

pH = -log(1.01 * 10⁻⁷) ≈ 6.99[span_52](end_span).
