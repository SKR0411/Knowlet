4. (b) (i) Derive Kirchhoff's equation. 6

[span_22](start_span)

Kirchhoff's equation describes how the enthalpy of a reaction changes with temperature[span_22](end_span).

[span_23](start_span)

For a reaction: ΔH = H(products) - H(reactants)[span_23](end_span).

Differentiating with respect to Temperature (T) at constant Pressure (P):

[span_24](start_span)

d(ΔH)/dT = dH(prod)/dT - dH(react)/dT[span_24](end_span).

Since (dH/dT)p = Cp (heat capacity at constant pressure):

d(ΔH)/dT = ΔCp

Integrating from T1 to T2:

ΔH2 - ΔH1 = ΔCp(T2 - T1)

9. (a) Why does CH3X favour SN2 reaction? 2

[span_42](start_span)

Methyl halides (CH3X) favour SN2 because they have the least steric hindrance[span_42](end_span). The nucleophile can easily attack the central carbon atom from the backside. [span_43](start_span)SN1 is not favored because the methyl carbocation is highly unstable[span_43](end_span).

10. (a) (i) Discuss SN1 mechanism. 6

[span_44](start_span)

SN1 (Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution) is a two-step process[span_44](end_span):

    [span_45](start_span)
  1. Formation of Carbocation: The leaving group departs (slowest step)[span_45](end_span).
    2. [span_46](start_span)
  2. Nucleophilic Attack: The nucleophile attacks the flat carbocation from either side[span_46](end_span).
[span_47](start_span)

Racemic Mixture: Since the carbocation intermediate is planar, the nucleophile can attack from the front or back with equal probability, leading to an equal mixture of two enantiomers (racemization)[span_47](end_span).
