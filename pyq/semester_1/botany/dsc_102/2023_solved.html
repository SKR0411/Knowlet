1. [span_9](start_span)Point out the pigments found in algae.[span_9](end_span)[2]

Algae contain a variety of photosynthetic pigments categorized into three main groups: Chlorophylls (a, b, c, d, e), Carotenoids (carotenes and xanthophylls), and Phycobilins (phycoerythrin and phycocyanin). [span_10](start_span)Chlorophyll-a is universal to all oxygenic algae.[span_10](end_span)

2. [span_11](start_span)Write briefly on the role of algae in agriculture.[span_11](end_span)[2]

Algae play a crucial role in enhancing soil fertility. Blue-green algae (Cyanobacteria) like Nostoc and Anabaena fix atmospheric nitrogen, acting as biofertilizers. [span_12](start_span)They also improve soil texture and provide growth-promoting substances like hormones.[span_12](end_span)

4. [span_15](start_span)Write a short note on the morphology of Chara.[span_15](end_span)[2]

Chara, often called "Stonewort," has a complex thallus divided into an underground rhizoidal system and an upright branched main axis. [span_16](start_span)The main axis consists of nodes and internodes, with whorls of limited growth (primary branches) arising from the nodes.[span_16](end_span)

8. [span_24](start_span)Write a short note on lichen.[span_24](end_span)[2]

Lichens are composite organisms resulting from a symbiotic association between a fungus (mycobiont) and an alga or cyanobacterium (phycobiont). [span_25](start_span)The fungus provides structure and moisture, while the alga provides nutrition through photosynthesis.[span_25](end_span)

    • [span_28](start_span)

UNIT-IV

10. [span_29](start_span)Write a short note on heterothallism.[span_29](end_span)[2]

Heterothallism is a condition in fungi where sexual reproduction occurs between two genetically different mycelia (compatible mating types). [span_30](start_span)It was first discovered by A.F. Blakeslee in Mucor.[span_30](end_span)

11. [span_31](start_span)Write briefly on parasexuality.[span_31](end_span)[2]

Parasexuality is a process in fungi where genetic recombination occurs without a standard sexual cycle (meiosis and syngamy). [span_32](start_span)It involves plasmogamy, karyogamy, and haploidization within the vegetative mycelium.[span_32](end_span)

12. [span_33](start_span)Distinguish between uredospores and teleutospores.[span_33](end_span)[2]

    [span_34](start_span)
  • Uredospores: Binucleate, thin-walled, stalked spores that function as repeating spores to spread infection in the same season.[span_34](end_span)
    • [span_35](start_span)
  • Teleutospores: Thick-walled, two-celled, black spores that serve as resting spores to survive winter.[span_35](end_span)

Fritsch's System of Classification (1935, 1945):
[span_46](start_span)F.E. Fritsch classified algae into 11 classes based on pigmentation, types of flagella, and reserve food materials.[span_46](end_span)

  • Chlorophyceae: Green algae; [span_47](start_span)Chl a and b; starch reserve.[span_47](end_span)
    • [span_48](start_span)
  • Xanthophyceae: Yellow-green algae; lack chlorophyll b; oil reserve.[span_48](end_span)
    • [span_49](start_span)
  • Chrysophyceae: Golden-brown algae.[span_49](end_span)
    • [span_50](start_span)
  • Bacillariophyceae: Diatoms; silica cell walls.[span_50](end_span)
    • [span_51](start_span)
  • Cryptophyceae: Small biflagellate algae.[span_51](end_span)
    • [span_52](start_span)
  • Dinophyceae: Dinoflagellates; two flagella in grooves.[span_52](end_span)
    • [span_53](start_span)
  • Chloromonadineae: Very small group.[span_53](end_span)
    • [span_54](start_span)
  • Euglenineae: Naked, flagellated forms.[span_54](end_span)
  • Phaeophyceae: Brown algae; Fucoxanthin pigment; [span_55](start_span)Laminarin reserve.[span_55](end_span)
  • Rhodophyceae: Red algae; Phycoerythrin pigment; [span_56](start_span)Floridean starch reserve.[span_56](end_span)
  • Myxophyceae (Cyanophyceae): Blue-green algae; prokaryotic; [span_57](start_span)Phycocyanin pigment.[span_57](end_span)

Contributions of G. M. Smith:
G.M. Smith is renowned for his classification system (1938, 1955) which separated algae into divisions. [span_58](start_span)His monumental work "Marine Algae of the Monterey Peninsula" and "Fresh-water Algae of the United States" standardized phycological study.[span_58](end_span)

18. [span_59](start_span)With necessary sketches, describe the thallus structure and asexual reproduction of Chlamydomonas.[span_59](end_span)[10]

[span_60](start_span)Chlamydomonas is a microscopic, unicellular, motile green alga.[span_60](end_span)

Thallus Structure:

    [span_61](start_span)
  • Cell Shape: Usually oval, spherical, or pyriform.[span_61](end_span)
    • [span_62](start_span)
  • Cell Wall: Composed of glycoprotein (not cellulose in many species).[span_62](end_span)
    • [span_63](start_span)
  • Flagella: Two equal, anteriorly placed whiplash flagella.[span_63](end_span)
    • [span_64](start_span)
  • Chloroplast: Distinct cup-shaped chloroplast containing a single pyrenoid (for starch synthesis).[span_64](end_span)
    • [span_65](start_span)
  • Eyespot (Stigma): A pigment spot at the anterior end used for light perception.[span_65](end_span)

Asexual Reproduction:
[span_66](start_span)Occurs primarily through the formation of zoospores under favorable conditions:[span_66](end_span)

    [span_67](start_span)
  1. The parent cell loses its flagella and the protoplast withdraws from the cell wall.[span_67](end_span)
    2. [span_68](start_span)
  2. The protoplast undergoes longitudinal division (usually 2, 4, or 8 times) to form daughter protoplasts.[span_68](end_span)
    3. [span_69](start_span)
  3. Each daughter protoplast develops its own cell wall and two flagella, becoming a zoospore.[span_69](end_span)
    4. [span_70](start_span)
  4. The parent cell wall ruptures or gelatinizes to release the new individuals.[span_70](end_span)

21. What are mycorrhizae? [span_71](start_span)Describe the various types and significance of mycorrhizal association.[span_71](end_span)[2+4+4=10]

[span_72](start_span)Definition: Mycorrhiza is a symbiotic association between a fungus and the roots of a higher plant.[span_72](end_span)

Types of Mycorrhizae:

  • Ectomycorrhizae: The fungus forms a mantle/sheath around the root surface and penetrates between cells of the cortex (Hartig net) but does not enter the cells. [span_73](start_span)Common in trees like Pine and Oak.[span_73](end_span)
  • Endomycorrhizae: The fungus penetrates inside the cortical cells. [span_74](start_span)The most common type is VAM (Vesicular Arbuscular Mycorrhiza), which forms storage vesicles and branched arbuscules within the root cells.[span_74](end_span)
    • [span_75](start_span)
  • Ectendomycorrhizae: Shows features of both types (mantle formation and intracellular penetration).[span_75](end_span)

Significance:

    [span_76](start_span)
  1. Nutrient Uptake: Greatly increases the absorption of Phosphorus, Nitrogen, and other minerals.[span_76](end_span)
    2. [span_77](start_span)
  2. Drought Resistance: Enhances the plant's ability to withstand water stress.[span_77](end_span)
    3. [span_78](start_span)
  3. Pathogen Protection: Provides a physical and chemical barrier against root pathogens.[span_78](end_span)
    4. [span_79](start_span)
  4. Growth Stimulation: Secretes growth hormones that aid plant development.[span_79](end_span)

22. [span_80](start_span)Give the general characteristic, life cycle and classification of black stem rust of wheat caused by Puccinia.[span_80](end_span)[10]

Classification:
Kingdom: Fungi | Phylum: Basidiomycota | Class: Pucciniomycetes | Genus: Puccinia | [span_81](start_span)Species: graminis tritici[span_81](end_span)

General Characteristics:
Puccinia graminis is an obligate parasite and heteroecious (requires two hosts: Wheat as primary host and Barberry as alternate host). [span_82](start_span)It produces five different types of spores.[span_82](end_span)

Life Cycle (5 Spore Stages):

  1. Uredial Stage (Stage II): Occurs on Wheat. [span_83](start_span)Uredospores are produced, spreading the infection rapidly (repeating stage).[span_83](end_span)
  2. Telial Stage (Stage III): Occurs on Wheat at the end of the season. [span_84](start_span)Teleutospores (thick-walled, 2-celled) are formed for survival.[span_84](end_span)
    3. [span_85](start_span)
  3. Basidial Stage (Stage IV): Teleutospores germinate to form Basidiospores.[span_85](end_span)
  4. Pycnidial Stage (Stage 0): Occurs on Barberry leaves. [span_86](start_span)Basidiospores infect Barberry and form Pycnidiospores (spermogonia).[span_86](end_span)
  5. Aecial Stage (Stage I): Occurs on Barberry. [span_87](start_span)Aeciospores are formed which go back to infect the Wheat crop.[span_87](end_span)

Applications of Fungi in Food Industry:

    [span_89](start_span)
  • Baking: Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Baker's yeast) is used to leaven bread through CO2 production.[span_89](end_span)
    • [span_90](start_span)
  • Cheese Production: Species like Penicillium roqueforti and P. camemberti are used for ripening and flavoring specific cheeses.[span_90](end_span)
    • [span_91](start_span)
  • Direct Consumption: Edible mushrooms (e.g., Agaricus bisporus) are a rich source of nutrients.[span_91](end_span)
    • [span_92](start_span)
  • Fermented Foods: Fungi are used in making Soy sauce, Tempeh, and Miso.[span_92](end_span)

Note on Fermentation:
Fermentation is an anaerobic metabolic process where fungi (like yeast) convert sugars into ethyl alcohol and carbon dioxide.

C6H12O6 → 2C2H5OH + 2CO2 + Energy
[span_93](start_span)In the food industry, this is exploited for both the ethanol (Brewing industry) and the carbon dioxide (Baking industry).[span_93](end_span)
