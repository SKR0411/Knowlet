1. Define Ethics. [2 Marks]

[span_1](start_span)

Ethics is a branch of philosophy that involves systematizing, defending, and recommending concepts of right and wrong conduct[span_1](end_span). [span_2](start_span)In the Indian context, it is often associated with Dharma, which refers to the moral order and duties that sustain the universe[span_2](end_span).

2. How many kinds of Rṇa are accepted in Indian ethics? Name them. [2 Marks]

[span_3](start_span)

Indian ethics generally accepts three kinds of Rṇa (debts)[span_3](end_span):

    [span_4](start_span)
  • Deva-Rṇa: Debt to the Gods[span_4](end_span).
    • [span_5](start_span)
  • Rishi-Rṇa: Debt to the sages or teachers[span_5](end_span).
    • [span_6](start_span)
  • Pitri-Rṇa: Debt to one's ancestors[span_6](end_span).

3. What is Sañcita Karma? What is Āgāmī Karma? [2 Marks]

    [span_7](start_span)
  • Sañcita Karma: The accumulated store of all past actions that have not yet reached fruition[span_7](end_span).
    • [span_8](start_span)
  • Āgāmī Karma: Actions performed in the present life that will bear fruit in the future[span_8](end_span).

4. What is Śreyas? What is Preyas? [2 Marks]

    [span_9](start_span)
  • Śreyas: That which is spiritually beneficial or good in the long run[span_9](end_span).
    • [span_10](start_span)
  • Preyas: That which is immediately pleasant or provides sensory gratification[span_10](end_span).

5. What is Moksa? [2 Marks]

[span_11](start_span)

Moksa is the ultimate liberation from the cycle of birth and death (Samsara) and the cessation of all suffering, representing the highest spiritual goal in Indian ethics[span_11](end_span).

6. What is Swadharma? [2 Marks]

[span_12](start_span)

Swadharma refers to one's own individual duty or inherent nature-based obligations, which vary according to an individual's stage of life and social position[span_12](end_span).

8. What are the Mahavratas according to Jaina ethics? [2 Marks]

[span_19](start_span)

The five "Great Vows" (Mahavratas) for Jaina monks are Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truth), Asteya (non-stealing), Brahmacharya (celibacy), and Aparigraha (non-attachment)[span_19](end_span).

9. Do the Cārvāka advocate 'hedonism'? Do they believe in the Law of Karma? [2 Marks]

    [span_20](start_span)
  • The Cārvāka school does advocate hedonism, emphasizing the pursuit of sensory pleasure in the present life[span_20](end_span).
    • [span_21](start_span)
  • They do not believe in the Law of Karma or life after death[span_21](end_span).

10. What are the five Yamas as advocated in Yoga ethics? [2 Marks]

[span_22](start_span)

The five Yamas (external restraints) are Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truthfulness), Asteya (non-stealing), Brahmacharya (continence), and Aparigraha (non-covetousness)[span_22](end_span).

11. What are the five Niyamas as advocated in Yoga ethics? [2 Marks]

[span_23](start_span)

The five Niyamas (internal observances) are Saucha (purity), Santosha (contentment), Tapas (austerity), Svadhyaya (self-study), and Ishvara Pranidhana (surrender to God)[span_23](end_span).

12. What, according to Mīmāmsā ethics, is Nitya Karma? [2 Marks]

[span_24](start_span)

Nitya Karma refers to obligatory daily duties (such as Sandhyavandanam) that must be performed regularly; their performance results in no special merit, but their neglect leads to sin[span_24](end_span).

13. What is the ethical standpoint of Swami Vivekananda? [2 Marks]

[span_25](start_span)

Vivekananda's ethics is rooted in Practical Vedanta, emphasizing the divinity of every soul and the idea that service to humanity is the highest form of worship (Shiva Jnane Jiva Seva)[span_25](end_span).

14. What, according to M. K. Gandhi, is Satyagraha? [2 Marks]

[span_26](start_span)

Satyagraha literally means "holding onto Truth"[span_26](end_span)[span_27](start_span); it is a method of non-violent resistance based on soul-force and the willingness to suffer rather than inflict suffering on others to achieve justice[span_27](end_span).

15. What is humanism according to R. N. Tagore? [2 Marks]

[span_28](start_span)[span_29](start_span)

Tagore's humanism is based on the "Religion of Man," where the divine is found within the human spirit, advocating for universal love, freedom, and the spiritual unity of all mankind[span_28](end_span)[span_29](end_span).

19. Critically examine the theory of Nişkāma Karma as found in the Bhagavad Gita. [10 Marks]

[span_39](start_span)

Nishkama Karma is the path of "selfless action" where one performs their duty without attachment to the results (phala-tyaga)[span_39](end_span).

    [span_40](start_span)
  • The Principle: The Gita states, "Your right is to work only, but never to its fruits"[span_40](end_span). [span_41](start_span)It bridges the gap between the path of action (Pravritti) and the path of renunciation (Nivritti)[span_41](end_span).
    • [span_42](start_span)
  • Psychological Impact: By detaching from outcomes, the individual remains equanimous (Sthitaprajna) in success and failure, reducing anxiety and ego[span_42](end_span).
    • [span_43](start_span)
  • Social Aspect: It promotes 'Lokasamgraha' or the welfare of the world, as actions are performed for duty's sake rather than personal gain[span_43](end_span).
    • [span_44](start_span)
  • Criticism: Critics argue that absolute detachment from results might lead to a lack of motivation or efficiency in performance[span_44](end_span).
