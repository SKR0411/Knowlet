1. Write the name of those orthodox schools which do not believe in the existence of God. [2 Marks]

2. What do you mean by the law of Karma in Indian philosophy? [2 Marks]

[span_1](start_span)

The Law of Karma is the moral law of causation which states that every action (physical or mental) produces a corresponding result, ensuring that an individual reaps what they sow across different births[span_1](end_span).

3. Why are the Carvākas called the materialists in Indian philosophy? [2 Marks]

[span_2](start_span)

They are called materialists because they recognize Matter (Bhuta) as the only ultimate reality and reject the existence of any non-material entities like the soul, God, or an after-life[span_2](end_span).

4. How do the Carvākas explain the nature of consciousness? [2 Marks]

[span_3](start_span)

The Carvākas argue that consciousness is an epiphenomenon of matter; it emerges from the combination of the four material elements (Earth, Water, Fire, Air), just as the red color emerges from the combination of betel leaf, lime, and nut[span_3](end_span).

13. Explain briefly the distinctive features of Indian philosophy. [10 Marks]

Indian philosophy (Darshana) possesses several unique characteristics that distinguish it from Western thought:

    [span_20](start_span)
  • Practical Necessity: It is not merely an intellectual exercise but arises from a practical need to overcome suffering (Dukkha)[span_20](end_span).
    • [span_21](start_span)
  • Spiritual Orientation: The ultimate goal is Moksha or liberation from the cycle of birth and death[span_21](end_span).
    • [span_22](start_span)[span_23](start_span)
  • Law of Karma: Most schools (except Carvaka) accept the moral law of cause and effect[span_22](end_span)[span_23](end_span).
    • [span_24](start_span)
  • Classification: Systems are divided into Orthodox (Astika), which accept Vedic authority, and Heterodox (Nastika), which reject it[span_24](end_span).
    • [span_25](start_span)
  • Unity of Theory and Practice: Philosophy is seen as a way of life, requiring moral discipline and meditation alongside logical reasoning[span_25](end_span).

14. Explain the epistemology of the Carvāka system. [10 Marks]

[span_26](start_span)

The Carvaka epistemology is strictly Empiricism[span_26](end_span).

    [span_27](start_span)
  • Perception as the Only Pramana: They hold that Pratyaksa (Perception) is the only valid source of knowledge because it is the only one that provides certainty[span_27](end_span).
  • Rejection of Inference (Anumana): They argue that inference relies on 'Vyapti' (universal relation), which cannot be established by perception. [span_28](start_span)Therefore, inference is only a guess and not certain knowledge[span_28](end_span).
    • [span_29](start_span)[span_30](start_span)
  • Rejection of Testimony (Sabda): They reject verbal testimony as a Pramana, arguing that words are just sounds and the Vedas were written by "buffoons, knaves, and demons"[span_29](end_span)[span_30](end_span).
    • [span_31](start_span)[span_32](start_span)
  • Metaphysical Consequences: Since only what is perceived is real, they reject God, Soul, and Heaven as they cannot be seen[span_31](end_span)[span_32](end_span).

15. Explain briefly the Anekāntavāda (relative pluralism) of Jainism. [10 Marks]

[span_33](start_span)

Anekāntavāda is the metaphysical theory that reality has infinite aspects (Ananta-dharmakam vastu)[span_33](end_span).

    [span_34](start_span)
  • Multiplicity of Truth: No single perspective can capture the absolute truth of an object because an object has infinite qualities[span_34](end_span).
  • Naya-vada: A 'Naya' is a partial truth from a particular point of view. [span_35](start_span)Errors occur when we mistake a partial truth for the whole truth[span_35](end_span).
  • Syadvada: This is the epistemological expression of Anekantavada, often called the "Theory of May-be." [span_36](start_span)It suggests that every judgment should be preceded by the word 'Syat' (somehow/in a way) to show its relativity[span_36](end_span).

16. Define Anumana. Explain the classification of Anumāna according to the Naiyāyikas. [2+8=10 Marks]

Definition: Anumāna (Inference) is "knowledge that follows other knowledge." [span_37](start_span)It is the process of arriving at a conclusion (Sadhya) regarding a subject (Paksha) based on a middle term (Hetu) and a universal relation (Vyapti)[span_37](end_span).

Classification:

    [span_38](start_span)
  • Svarthanumana vs. Pararthanumana: Inference for oneself vs. inference for convincing others through the five-membered syllogism (Pancavayava Nyaya)[span_38](end_span).
    • [span_39](start_span)
  • Purvavat: Inferring an unperceived effect from a perceived cause (e.g., seeing clouds and inferring future rain)[span_39](end_span).
    • [span_40](start_span)
  • Sesavat: Inferring an unperceived cause from a perceived effect (e.g., seeing a flooded river and inferring past rain)[span_40](end_span).
    • [span_41](start_span)
  • Samanyatodrsta: Inference based on commonality rather than a causal link (e.g., inferring the movement of the sun because it changes position)[span_41](end_span).

17. What is Prakrti? How does Samkhya prove the existence of Prakṛti? [2+8=10 Marks]

[span_42](start_span)

Definition: Prakrti is the ultimate cause of the world, characterized as the unconscious, eternal, and all-pervasive principle composed of three Gunas: Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas[span_42](end_span).

Proofs for Existence (Kārikā 15):

    [span_43](start_span)
  1. Bhedanam Parimanat: Individual objects are finite and limited; they must originate from an infinite, unlimited source[span_43](end_span).
    2. [span_44](start_span)
  2. Samanvayat: Different objects possess common qualities (the three Gunas), implying a common source[span_44](end_span).
    3. [span_45](start_span)
  3. Shaktitah Pravrttesca: Evolution is only possible if the cause has the potential energy (Shakti) to produce the effect[span_45](end_span).
    4. [span_46](start_span)
  4. Karana-karya-vibhagat: The effect is clearly distinct from the cause, but implicitly contained within it[span_46](end_span).
    5. [span_47](start_span)
  5. Avibhagat Vaishvarupyasya: The entire universe is a unified whole (Vaishvarupya) and must merge back into an undifferentiated state (Avibhaga)[span_47](end_span).

18. Discuss briefly, Śamkara's doctrine of Māyā. [10 Marks]

[span_48](start_span)

In Advaita Vedanta, Māyā is the power of Brahman that makes the one appear as many[span_48](end_span).

    [span_49](start_span)
  • Nature: It is described as Anirvacaniya (indescribable)—it is neither real (as it disappears with knowledge) nor unreal (as it is experienced in the world)[span_49](end_span).
  • Two Functions:
      [span_50](start_span)
    1. Avarana Shakti: It conceals the true nature of Brahman (like a cloud hiding the sun)[span_50](end_span).
      2. [span_51](start_span)
    2. Vikshepa Shakti: It projects the illusory world of plurality upon the concealed Brahman[span_51](end_span).
    • [span_52](start_span)
  • Locus: According to Sankara, the locus (Ashraya) of Maya is Brahman itself, though Brahman remains unaffected by it[span_52](end_span).
