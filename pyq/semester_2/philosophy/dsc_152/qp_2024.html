Section-A

1. State two points of differences between Normative ethics and Metaethics. [2 Marks]

    [span_6](start_span)
  • Focus: Normative ethics prescribes moral standards and rules for conduct, while Metaethics analyzes the nature, meaning, and origin of ethical concepts[span_6](end_span).
    • [span_7](start_span)
  • Goal: Normative ethics answers "What should we do?", whereas Metaethics answers "What does 'good' mean?"[span_7](end_span).

2. How are rights and duties correlated? [2 Marks]

[span_8](start_span)

Rights and duties are interdependent; the right of one individual implies a corresponding duty for others to respect that right, and an individual's right often comes with the duty to use it responsibly[span_8](end_span).

3. What are the different classes of virtue? [2 Marks]

[span_9](start_span)[span_10](start_span)

Virtues are generally classified into Intellectual virtues (related to reasoning and wisdom) and Moral virtues (related to habits of character and action)[span_9](end_span)[span_10](end_span).

4. What is Hedonism? [2 Marks]

[span_11](start_span)

Hedonism is an ethical theory which posits that pleasure is the ultimate good and the primary motivator of human action, making it the standard for moral judgment[span_11](end_span).

5. Mention two points of distinction between Hedonism and Utilitarianism. [2 Marks]

    [span_12](start_span)
  • Scope: Hedonism often focuses on individual pleasure, while Utilitarianism focuses on the "greatest happiness of the greatest number"[span_12](end_span).
    • [span_13](start_span)
  • Application: Hedonism is an egoistic pursuit of pleasure, whereas Utilitarianism is a social and altruistic theory[span_13](end_span).

6. How many kinds of virtue are accepted by Aristotle? Name them. [2 Marks]

[span_14](start_span)

Aristotle accepts two kinds of virtue: Intellectual virtues and Moral virtues[span_14](end_span).

7. What is Deontological Ethics? [2 Marks]

[span_15](start_span)

Deontological ethics is a normative theory that judges the morality of an action based on its adherence to rules or duties, regardless of the consequences[span_15](end_span).

8. Mention, after Kant, two maxims of morality. [2 Marks]

    [span_16](start_span)
  • Formula of Universal Law: Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law[span_16](end_span).
    • [span_17](start_span)
  • Formula of Humanity: Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end[span_17](end_span).

9. On what grounds does W. D. Ross reject Kant's deontology? [2 Marks]

W. [span_18](start_span)D. Ross rejects Kant's deontology on the grounds that moral duties are not absolute or exceptionless; he argues that duties are "prima facie" and can conflict, requiring us to choose the most pressing duty in a given situation[span_18](end_span).

10. Which philosopher holds that 'good' is indefinable? Name one book written by him. [2 Marks]

    [span_19](start_span)
  • Philosopher: G. E. Moore[span_19](end_span).
    • [span_20](start_span)
  • Book: Principia Ethica[span_20](end_span).

11. What is Naturalistic Fallacy? [2 Marks]

[span_21](start_span)

The Naturalistic Fallacy, coined by G. E. Moore, is the error of defining the indefinable property "good" in terms of natural properties like "pleasure," "desire," or "evolutionary progress"[span_21](end_span).

12. Mention two points of differences between Ayer's emotivism and Stevenson's emotivism. [2 Marks]

    [span_22](start_span)
  • Ayer views moral statements as mere expressions of emotion, whereas Stevenson emphasizes their persuasive power to influence the attitudes of others[span_22](end_span).
    • [span_23](start_span)
  • Stevenson allows for a limited role of "reasons" in ethical disagreement, while Ayer's version is more strictly non-cognitive[span_23](end_span).

13. What is Applied Ethics? [2 Marks]

[span_24](start_span)

Applied ethics is the branch of ethics that examines specific, controversial moral issues such as abortion, euthanasia, or environmental protection by applying ethical theories to real-world scenarios[span_24](end_span).

18. What is Utilitarianism? Critically examine Mill's theory of utilitarianism. [2+8=10 Marks]

[span_37](start_span)

Utilitarianism: A teleological theory holding that the moral worth of an action is determined by its contribution to overall utility, defined as the greatest happiness for the greatest number[span_37](end_span).

J.S. Mill's Theory:

    [span_38](start_span)
  • Qualitative Hedonism: Unlike Bentham, Mill distinguished between "higher" intellectual pleasures and "lower" sensual pleasures[span_38](end_span).
    • [span_39](start_span)
  • The Proof of Utility: Mill argued that the only proof that something is desirable is that people actually desire it[span_39](end_span).

Critical Examination: Critics argue that Mill's distinction between qualities of pleasure makes the theory inconsistent with pure hedonism. [span_40](start_span)Furthermore, Utilitarianism is often criticized for potentially justifying the sacrifice of individual rights for the sake of the majority[span_40](end_span).

19. What, according to Aristotle, is virtue? Discuss Aristotle's virtue ethics, with special reference to the concept of 'Golden Mean'. [2+8=10 Marks]

[span_41](start_span)[span_42](start_span)

Virtue: For Aristotle, virtue (arete) is an "excellence" of character—a disposition to act in accordance with right reason[span_41](end_span)[span_42](end_span).

[span_43](start_span)

The Golden Mean: Aristotle argued that moral virtue is always a middle ground between two extremes: deficiency and excess[span_43](end_span).

Deficiency Virtue (Mean) Excess

[span_44](start_span)

Virtue is not innate but is developed through habituation and the exercise of practical wisdom (phronesis)[span_44](end_span).

[span_45](start_span)[span_46](start_span)

Categorical Imperative: A moral command that is unconditional and binding for all rational beings, regardless of their desires or ends[span_45](end_span)[span_46](end_span).

Duty for Duty's Sake:

  • Kant argued that the only thing good without qualification is a Good Will[span_47](end_span).

  • Actions have moral worth only when performed out of respect for the moral law, not out of inclination, self-interest, or consequence[span_48](end_span).

  • This deontology emphasizes that we must do our duty simply because it is our duty[span_49](end_span).
[span_50](start_span)[span_51](start_span)

Examination: While Kant's theory provides a strong basis for human dignity, it is often criticized for being too rigid and failing to provide guidance when duties conflict[span_50](end_span)[span_51](end_span).
