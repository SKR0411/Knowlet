Section-A

(Answer any twenty questions. Each question carries 1 mark.)[span_5](end_span)

1. Is environmental ethics a branch of applied ethics?

[span_6](start_span)

Yes, environmental ethics is a major branch of applied ethics that studies the moral relationship of human beings to the environment.[span_6](end_span)

2. Mention one major challenge of environmental ethics.

[span_7](start_span)

One major challenge is balancing human developmental needs with the necessity of preserving ecological integrity.[span_7](end_span)

3. Is environmental pollution an ethical issue?

[span_8](start_span)

Yes, it is an ethical issue because it causes harm to living beings and violates the right to a healthy environment.[span_8](end_span)

4. Is man and environment interrelated?

[span_9](start_span)

Yes, humans and the environment are deeply interconnected and interdependent.[span_9](end_span)

5. Mention one adverse effect of environmental pollution.

[span_10](start_span)

Environmental pollution leads to health hazards such as respiratory diseases in humans.[span_10](end_span)

6. Who coined the term 'ecology'?

[span_11](start_span)

The term 'ecology' was coined by Ernst Haeckel.[span_11](end_span)

7. Who is called the father of social ecology?

[span_12](start_span)

Murray Bookchin is widely regarded as the father of social ecology.[span_12](end_span)

8. What are the two branches of ecology?

[span_13](start_span)

Ecology is generally divided into Autecology (study of individual species) and Synecology (study of communities).[span_13](end_span)

9. Write one principle of deep ecology.

[span_14](start_span)

One core principle is that the well-being and flourishing of non-human life on Earth have value in themselves, independent of their usefulness to humans.[span_14](end_span)

10. Mention one difference between social ecology and ecofeminism.

[span_15](start_span)

Social ecology focuses on how social hierarchies cause environmental crises, whereas ecofeminism emphasizes the link between the oppression of women and the degradation of nature.[span_15](end_span)

11. Is anthropocentrism sustainable?

[span_16](start_span)

Generally, no; pure anthropocentrism is often viewed as unsustainable as it prioritizes human consumption over long-term ecological health.[span_16](end_span)

12. Who introduced ecocentrism?

[span_17](start_span)

While influenced by many, Aldo Leopold (with his Land Ethic) is a central figure in the introduction of ecocentric thought.[span_17](end_span)

13. Which ethical theory recognizes the interconnectedness of all living beings?

[span_18](start_span)

Deep Ecology (or Ecocentrism) emphasizes this interconnectedness and the intrinsic equality of all beings.[span_18](end_span)

14. What is anthropocentric view about environment?

[span_19](start_span)

It is a human-centered view that considers humans as the sole holders of intrinsic value, viewing nature as a resource for human benefit.[span_19](end_span)

15. Does eco-centrism consider the environment to be intrinsically valuable?

[span_20](start_span)

Yes, ecocentrism holds that the environment and its ecosystems have value in and of themselves.[span_20](end_span)

16. Is animal ethics a branch of applied ethics?

[span_21](start_span)

Yes, animal ethics is a sub-discipline of applied ethics.[span_21](end_span)

17. Who wrote the book, Animal Liberation?

[span_22](start_span)

Peter Singer.[span_22](end_span)

18. What is the primary concern of animal ethics?

[span_23](start_span)

The primary concern is the moral status of non-human animals and our ethical obligations toward them.[span_23](end_span)

19. Do animals have intrinsic value according to anthropocentrism?

[span_24](start_span)

No; in strict anthropocentrism, animals only have instrumental value relative to human needs.[span_24](end_span)

20. Is ecology rightly considered as a 'holistic science'?

[span_25](start_span)

Yes, because it studies ecosystems as integrated wholes rather than just a collection of parts.[span_25](end_span)

21. Who coined the term 'bioethics'?

[span_26](start_span)

The term was coined by Fritz Jahr (1927) and later popularized by Van Rensselaer Potter (1970).[span_26](end_span)

22. Is bioethics related to issues connected to life science?

[span_27](start_span)

Yes, bioethics deals with ethical issues arising from advances in biology and medicine.[span_27](end_span)

23. Is medical ethics a sub-discipline of bioethics?

[span_28](start_span)

Yes, medical ethics is often considered a core component of the broader field of bioethics.[span_28](end_span)

24. Is biocthics a science?

[span_29](start_span)

Bioethics is primarily an interdisciplinary field of study or a branch of philosophy/ethics, rather than a "natural science" like biology.[span_29](end_span)

25. Mention one principle of bioethics.

[span_30](start_span)

One fundamental principle is Autonomy (respecting an individual's right to self-determination).[span_30](end_span)

28. What does deep ecology mean?

[span_36](start_span)

Deep ecology is an environmental philosophy that advocates for the intrinsic value of all living beings and calls for a fundamental shift in how humans interact with nature, moving beyond mere resource management.[span_36](end_span)

29. What are the four aspects of social ecology?

[span_37](start_span)

Social ecology typically examines human-nature relationships, social hierarchies, communalism, and the reconstruction of society along ecological lines.[span_37](end_span)

30. Define ecology.

[span_38](start_span)

Ecology is the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and their environment, including both biotic and abiotic components.[span_38](end_span)

31. What is the difference between ecocentrism and environmental ethics?

[span_39](start_span)

Environmental ethics is the broad field of study, while ecocentrism is a specific theory within that field that places value on entire ecosystems rather than just humans.[span_39](end_span)

32. Why do we need animal ethics? Mention two points.

    [span_40](start_span)
  • To address the moral status of animals and prevent unnecessary suffering.[span_40](end_span)
    • [span_41](start_span)
  • To evaluate the ethics of practices like factory farming and animal testing.[span_41](end_span)

33. What is environmental ethics? Discuss its scope. [2+6=8 Marks]

[span_43](start_span)

Definition: Environmental ethics is a branch of applied philosophy that studies the moral relationship between human beings and the natural environment.[span_43](end_span)

Scope:

    [span_44](start_span)
  • Nature of Value: Determining if nature has intrinsic or merely instrumental value.[span_44](end_span)
    • [span_45](start_span)
  • Obligations to Future Generations: Studying our duty to leave a livable planet for those not yet born.[span_45](end_span)
    • [span_46](start_span)
  • Animal Welfare: Evaluating the moral status of non-human animals.[span_46](end_span)
    • [span_47](start_span)
  • Policy and Law: Informing environmental regulations and global climate justice.[span_47](end_span)

34. Discuss the problem of environmental pollution from an ethical point of view. [8 Marks]

[span_48](start_span)

From an ethical perspective, pollution is not just a technical problem but a moral failure.[span_48](end_span)

    [span_49](start_span)
  • Harm Principle: Pollution violates the basic ethical principle of "doing no harm" by damaging health and ecosystems.[span_49](end_span)
    • [span_50](start_span)
  • Justice: It often leads to environmental injustice, where poor communities suffer most from the waste of the wealthy.[span_50](end_span)
    • [span_51](start_span)
  • Responsibility: It raises questions about "Polluter Pays" principles and our duty to preserve common resources like air and water.[span_51](end_span)

35. What is ecofeminism? Discuss its main concerns. [2+6=8 Marks]

[span_52](start_span)

Definition: Ecofeminism is a movement and philosophy that sees a direct connection between the exploitation of nature and the oppression of women.[span_52](end_span)

Main Concerns:

    [span_53](start_span)
  • Patriarchal Domination: It argues that the same mindset that justifies male dominance over women also justifies human dominance over nature.[span_53](end_span)
    • [span_54](start_span)
  • Dualism: It critiques Western dualisms (Culture/Nature, Male/Female) where the latter is always devalued.[span_54](end_span)
    • [span_55](start_span)
  • Sustainability: It advocates for a more nurturing, non-hierarchical relationship with the Earth.[span_55](end_span)

36. What is anthropocentrism? Distinguish between anthropocentrism and ecocentrism. [4+4=8 Marks]

[span_56](start_span)

Anthropocentrism: A human-centered viewpoint that considers human beings as the most significant entities in the universe and the only ones with intrinsic moral value.[span_56](end_span)

37. What is bioethics? Explain the nature of bioethics. [2+6=8 Marks]

[span_63](start_span)

Definition: Bioethics is the study of ethical, social, and legal issues that arise in biomedicine and biomedical research.[span_63](end_span)

Nature:

    [span_64](start_span)
  • Interdisciplinary: It combines philosophy, law, medicine, and social sciences.[span_64](end_span)
    • [span_65](start_span)
  • Pluralistic: It recognizes diverse viewpoints in a multicultural society.[span_65](end_span)
    • [span_66](start_span)
  • Practical: It aims to provide concrete guidance for medical professionals and researchers.[span_66](end_span)

Exam Focus Enhancements

  • Exam Tip: For 8-mark questions, use a clear structure (Introduction, Points, Conclusion) to maximize marks.
  • Common Mistakes: Do not confuse Social Ecology (Bookchin) with Deep Ecology (Naess). Social ecology looks at social structures.
  • Key Distinction: Anthropocentrism = Human-centered; Ecocentrism = Nature-centered.
  • Knowlet Note: Always remember that environmental ethics is a form of Applied Ethics, dealing with real-world moral problems.
