[span_19](start_span)

Gender studies emerged as an academic field in the late 20th century, evolving from Women’s Studies[span_19](end_span). Its development can be traced through several stages:

    [span_20](start_span)
  • Roots in Feminism: The field grew out of second-wave feminism in the 1960s and 70s, which sought to bring women's voices into academia[span_20](end_span).
  • Shift from Women to Gender: By the 1980s, scholars realized that analyzing women in isolation was insufficient. [span_21](start_span)They began focusing on the relationships between men and women, leading to the broader term "Gender Studies"[span_21](end_span).
    • [span_22](start_span)[span_23](start_span)
  • Interdisciplinarity: It integrated insights from sociology, history, and humanities to understand how power dynamics are gendered[span_22](end_span)[span_23](end_span).
    • [span_24](start_span)
  • Contemporary Focus: Today, it includes masculinity studies and queer theory, looking at how gender intersects with race and class[span_24](end_span).

The position of women during the Sultanate period saw a general decline compared to earlier periods, marked by increased restrictions[span_50](end_span)[span_51](end_span):

    [span_52](start_span)
  • Social Restrictions: The Purdah (veiling) system became widespread among upper-class Muslim and Hindu women[span_52](end_span).

    • [span_54](start_span)
  • Legal Status: Women had limited property rights, though Islamic law provided some inheritance rights that were often ignored in practice[span_54](end_span).
    • [span_55](start_span)
  • Exceptional Leaders: Despite restrictions, figures like Razia Sultan emerged as sovereign rulers, showing that merit could occasionally break barriers[span_55](end_span).

(a) Write a note on Age of Consent Bill.[span_80](end_span)

[span_81](start_span)

The Age of Consent Bill (1891) was a landmark legislative effort to protect young girls from early sexual consummation[span_81](end_span):

    [span_82](start_span)
  • Context: It was sparked by the tragic death of Phulmoni Dasi, a 10-year-old girl, due to marital rape[span_82](end_span).
    • [span_83](start_span)
  • Advocacy: Reformers like Behramji Malabari campaigned vigorously for the bill[span_83](end_span).
    • [span_84](start_span)
  • The Law: It raised the legal age of consent for sexual intercourse for all girls (married or unmarried) from 10 to 12 years[span_84](end_span).
    • [span_85](start_span)
  • Opposition: It faced intense opposition from conservative groups (including Bal Gangadhar Tilak) who saw it as British interference in Indian religious customs[span_85](end_span).
