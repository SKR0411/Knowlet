31. Evaluate the position of women in ancient India. [8 Marks]

[span_45](start_span)

The position of women in ancient India was complex and varied across different periods[span_45](end_span).

    [span_46](start_span)
  • Vedic Period: In the early Vedic age, women like Gargi and Maitreyi enjoyed high status, participated in philosophical debates, and had access to education[span_46](end_span).
    • [span_47](start_span)
  • Deterioration: In the later Vedic and subsequent periods, women's social standing began to decline with restricted access to property and the restriction of Upanayana (sacred thread ceremony) for girls[span_47](end_span).
    • [span_48](start_span)
  • Legal Status: Texts like the Manusmriti emphasized the subordination of women to male kin, though concepts like Stridhana (women's property) existed[span_48](end_span).

32. Review the role of women in the Bhakti Movement. [8 Marks]

[span_49](start_span)

The Bhakti Movement provided a unique space for women to challenge patriarchal constraints through spiritual devotion[span_49](end_span).

    [span_50](start_span)
  • Defiance of Norms: Women saints like Mirabai and Akka Mahadevi broke traditional household roles to pursue an individual relationship with the divine[span_50](end_span).
    • [span_51](start_span)
  • Spiritual Equality: The movement emphasized that devotion was accessible to all, regardless of gender or caste[span_51](end_span).
    • [span_52](start_span)
  • Literary Contribution: These women composed devotional poetry (bhajans/vachanas) that remains a significant part of Indian cultural heritage[span_52](end_span).

33. Narrate in brief the debate around Sati. [8 Marks]

[span_53](start_span)

The debate around 'Sati' (widow burning) was a central point of 19th-century social reform[span_53](end_span).

    [span_54](start_span)
  • Reformist View: Leaders like Raja Ram Mohan Roy argued that Sati had no scriptural sanction in the original Hindu texts and was a cruel, inhuman practice[span_54](end_span).
    • [span_55](start_span)
  • Orthodox View: Traditionalists (Dharma Sabha) argued that the practice was an essential part of religious tradition and that the British should not interfere in local customs[span_55](end_span).
    • [span_56](start_span)
  • Legal Outcome: The debate led to the Sati Abolition Act of 1829, passed by Lord William Bentinck[span_56](end_span).

34. Examine the contributions of women in the Non-Cooperation Movement. [8 Marks]

[span_57](start_span)

Women's participation in the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22) marked their large-scale entry into the public political sphere[span_57](end_span).

    [span_58](start_span)
  • Picketing: Women organized the picketing of liquor and foreign cloth shops across India[span_58](end_span).
    • [span_59](start_span)
  • Leadership: Prominent women like Basanti Devi and Sarojini Naidu addressed public meetings and led processions[span_59](end_span).
    • [span_60](start_span)
  • Charkha and Khadi: They played a vital role in popularizing the use of the spinning wheel and hand-spun cloth as symbols of self-reliance[span_60](end_span).

35. Review the role of women in the Civil Disobedience Movement. [8 Marks]

[span_61](start_span)

The Civil Disobedience Movement (1930) saw even more vigorous female participation, often described as a "awakening"[span_61](end_span).

    [span_62](start_span)
  • Salt Satyagraha: While initially kept away from the Dandi March, women like Sarojini Naidu led the raid on the Dharasana Salt Works[span_62](end_span).
    • [span_63](start_span)
  • Mass Arrests: Thousands of women were arrested for breaking salt laws and protesting against British authorities[span_63](end_span).
    • [span_64](start_span)
  • Desh Sevikas: Many women joined organizations like the 'Desh Sevikas' to provide auxiliary support to the national movement[span_64](end_span).
