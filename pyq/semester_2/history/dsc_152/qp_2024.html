16. How did Iltutmish consolidate the power and position of the Delhi Sultanate? [10 Marks]

[span_32](start_span)

Iltutmish is regarded as the real founder of the Delhi Sultanate[span_32](end_span). He consolidated power through several strategic measures:

    [span_33](start_span)
  • Suppression of Rivals: He defeated powerful rivals like Tajuddin Yildiz and Nasiruddin Qabacha to secure the western frontiers[span_33](end_span).
    • [span_34](start_span)
  • Organizing the Administration: He created the "Turkan-i-Chahalgani" (The Forty), a corps of highly loyal Turkish slave officers to assist in administration[span_34](end_span).
    • [span_35](start_span)
  • Iqta System: He institutionalized the Iqta system, dividing the empire into administrative units and assigning them to nobles for collection of revenue and maintenance of troops[span_35](end_span).
    • [span_36](start_span)
  • Currency Reform: He introduced the Silver Tanka and the Copper Jital, providing the Sultanate with a stable monetary system[span_36](end_span).
    • [span_37](start_span)
  • Caliph's Recognition: In 1229 AD, he received a 'Mansur' (letter of investiture) from the Abbasid Caliph, which gave legal and religious legitimacy to his rule[span_37](end_span).

17. Explain Ghiyas-ud-din Balban's theory of kingship. [10 Marks]

[span_38](start_span)

Balban's theory of kingship was centered on enhancing the prestige and absolute power of the Sultan to counter the influence of the Chahalgani[span_38](end_span).

    [span_39](start_span)
  • Divine Right: He believed that the King was the "Shadow of God" (Zil-i-Illahi) and the "Recipient of Divine Grace" (Niyabat-i-Khudai)[span_39](end_span).
  • Dignity of the Court: He maintained a rigorous court decorum. [span_40](start_span)He introduced the practices of Sijda (prostration) and Paibos (kissing the Sultan's feet) to demonstrate the Sultan's superiority[span_40](end_span).
    • [span_41](start_span)
  • Blood and Iron Policy: He adopted a ruthless policy of 'Blood and Iron' to suppress internal rebellions (like those of the Mewatis) and protect the Sultanate from Mongol invasions[span_41](end_span).
    • [span_42](start_span)
  • Spy System: He established an efficient network of spies (Barids) to keep a close watch on the activities of the nobles[span_42](end_span).

18. Discuss the economic policy of Alauddin Khilji. [10 Marks]

[span_43](start_span)

Alauddin Khilji introduced radical economic and market reforms primarily to maintain a large standing army at low costs[span_43](end_span).

    [span_44](start_span)
  • Market Control: He fixed the prices of all essential commodities, from food grains to cloth and cattle[span_44](end_span).
    • [span_45](start_span)
  • Separate Markets: He established different markets (Mandis) for grains, cloth (Sarai-i-Adl), and slaves/cattle[span_45](end_span).
    • [span_46](start_span)
  • Market Officers: Markets were strictly regulated by officials like the Shahna-i-Mandi and spies called Munhiyans[span_46](end_span).
  • Land Revenue Reforms: He was the first Sultan to insist on the measurement of land (Biswa) for revenue assessment. [span_47](start_span)He abolished intermediaries (Khotas and Muqaddams) to ensure direct state control over peasants[span_47](end_span).
    • [span_48](start_span)
  • Increased Taxation: He increased the land revenue (Kharaj) to 50% of the produce and introduced new taxes like Ghari (house tax) and Chari (pasture tax)[span_48](end_span).

19. Discuss the various reforms introduced by Muhammad bin-Tughluq. [10 Marks]

[span_49](start_span)

Muhammad bin Tughluq was a visionary ruler whose reforms often failed due to poor execution and lack of public support[span_49](end_span).

  • Transfer of Capital (1327): He moved the capital from Delhi to Devagiri (renamed Daulatabad) to centrally manage the Deccan. [span_50](start_span)[span_51](start_span)However, the move caused immense hardship and was eventually reversed[span_50](end_span)[span_51](end_span).
  • Token Currency (1329): He introduced bronze/copper coins as token currency to be equivalent in value to silver tankas. [span_52](start_span)Widespread counterfeiting by the public led to economic chaos, forcing him to withdraw the scheme[span_52](end_span).
  • Agricultural Reform: He established a separate department for agriculture called Diwan-i-Amir-Kohi. [span_53](start_span)He provided 'Sondhar' (agricultural loans) to farmers to bring more land under cultivation[span_53](end_span).
  • Taxation in Doab: He increased taxes in the fertile Doab region. [span_54](start_span)Unfortunately, this coincided with a severe famine, leading to a massive peasant revolt[span_54](end_span).

25. Analyse the causes behind the downfall of the Delhi Sultanate. [10 Marks]

[span_60](start_span)

The decline of the Delhi Sultanate was a gradual process caused by several internal and external factors[span_60](end_span):

    [span_61](start_span)
  • Weak Successors: After the death of Firuz Shah Tughluq, the Sultanate was ruled by weak monarchs who could not control the vast empire[span_61](end_span).
    • [span_62](start_span)
  • Nature of Government: The Sultanate was a military-centric state based on force; it failed to win the permanent loyalty of the local subjects[span_62](end_span).
    • [span_63](start_span)[span_64](start_span)[span_65](start_span)
  • Rise of Regional Kingdoms: The Deccan campaigns and administrative failures led to the emergence of independent states like Vijayanagara, Bahmani, Bengal, and Gujarat[span_63](end_span)[span_64](end_span)[span_65](end_span).
    • [span_66](start_span)[span_67](start_span)[span_68](start_span)
  • Foreign Invasions: Timur’s invasion in 1398 AD shattered the economy and prestige of the Sultanate[span_66](end_span)[span_67](end_span)[span_68](end_span).
  • Defective Military: The nobility became too powerful and often engaged in civil wars. [span_69](start_span)The Sultans also failed to modernize their military technology[span_69](end_span).
    • [span_70](start_span)[span_71](start_span)
  • Invasion of Babur: The final blow was delivered by Babur in the First Battle of Panipat (1526), which replaced the Sultanate with the Mughal Empire[span_70](end_span)[span_71](end_span).
