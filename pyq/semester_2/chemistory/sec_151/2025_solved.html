UNIT-I

1. (a) Define the term relative error. 1

[span_2](start_span)

Relative error is the ratio of the absolute error of a measurement to the true or accepted value, often expressed as a percentage or parts per thousand.[span_2](end_span)

1. (b) What is retention factor in chromatography? 1

[span_3](start_span)

The retention factor (Rf) is the ratio of the distance traveled by the solute to the distance traveled by the solvent front in a chromatographic separation.[span_3](end_span)

1. (c) What do you mean by significant figures in analytical measurements? 1

[span_4](start_span)

Significant figures are the digits in a measured value that are known with certainty plus one final digit that is somewhat uncertain or estimated.[span_4](end_span)

1. (d) What is the role of a developing reagent in chromatography? 1

[span_5](start_span)

A developing reagent is used to visualize colorless spots on a chromatogram by reacting with the separated components to form colored products.[span_5](end_span)

2. (b) What are the different types of errors in analytical chemistry? 2

[span_10](start_span)

Errors are generally classified into two types: Determinate (Systematic) Errors, which have a definite value and assignable cause (e.g., instrumental or personal errors), and Indeterminate (Random) Errors, which arise from uncontrollable variables in the measurement.[span_10](end_span)

3. (a) Explain the concept of sampling in analytical chemistry. Why is it important? 5

[span_11](start_span)

Sampling is the process of collecting a small portion of a material that is truly representative of the entire bulk (population) being studied.[span_11](end_span)

    [span_12](start_span)
  • Process: It involves defining the population, collecting a gross sample, and reducing it to a laboratory-sized sample without changing its composition.[span_12](end_span)
    • [span_13](start_span)
  • Importance: Analytical results are only as good as the sample; if the sample is not representative, even the most accurate laboratory analysis will lead to incorrect conclusions about the bulk material.[span_13](end_span)

3. (b) Describe the principle and procedure of thin layer chromatography. 5

[span_14](start_span)

Principle: TLC is based on the differential adsorption of components of a mixture between a stationary phase (usually silica gel or alumina coated on a plate) and a mobile phase (solvent) that moves up the plate by capillary action.[span_14](end_span)

[span_15](start_span)

Procedure:[span_15](end_span)

    [span_16](start_span)
  1. A small spot of the sample is applied near the bottom of the TLC plate.[span_16](end_span)
    2. [span_17](start_span)
  2. The plate is placed in a developing chamber containing a small amount of mobile phase.[span_17](end_span)
    3. [span_18](start_span)
  3. As the solvent rises, components move at different rates based on their affinity for the phases.[span_18](end_span)
    4. [span_19](start_span)
  4. Once the solvent reaches near the top, the plate is removed, dried, and visualized (using UV light or reagents).[span_19](end_span)

UNIT-II

4. (a) What are the major components of soil? 1

[span_20](start_span)

Soil is composed of mineral matter, organic matter (humus), water, and air.[span_20](end_span)

4. (b) What is the purpose of using an indicator in complexometric titrations? 1

[span_21](start_span)

The indicator (usually a metallochromic indicator) changes color at the equivalence point when it is displaced from the metal ion by the stronger chelating agent (like EDTA).[span_21](end_span)

4. (c) Name one commonly used chelating agent in complexometric titrations. 1

[span_22](start_span)

Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA) is the most common chelating agent used.[span_22](end_span)

4. (d) What is chelation? 1

[span_23](start_span)

Chelation is the formation of a stable, ring-like structure between a metal ion and a multidentate ligand (chelating agent) that provides multiple points of attachment.[span_23](end_span)

5. (a) Explain the principle and procedure of pH measurement in soil analysis. 2

[span_24](start_span)

Principle: Soil pH is measured to determine the acidity or alkalinity of the soil solution, which affects nutrient availability.[span_24](end_span)

[span_25](start_span)

Procedure: A soil-water suspension is prepared (usually in a 1:2 or 1:5 ratio), stirred thoroughly, allowed to settle, and the pH of the supernatant is measured using a calibrated pH meter.[span_25](end_span)

6. (b) What is complexometric titration and how is it applied in soil analysis? 5

[span_26](start_span)

Complexometric titration is a form of volumetric analysis where the formation of a colored complex is used to indicate the end point of a titration.[span_26](end_span)

Application in Soil: It is primarily used to determine the concentration of metal ions such as Calcium (Ca2+) and Magnesium (Mg2+), which represent soil hardness and essential plant nutrients. [span_27](start_span)[span_28](start_span)EDTA is typically titrated against a soil extract using Eriochrome Black T (EBT) as an indicator.[span_27](end_span)[span_28](end_span)

UNIT-IV

10. (a) What is food adulteration? 1

[span_39](start_span)

Food adulteration is the practice of adding inferior substances to food or removing valuable ingredients from it to increase quantity or profit, thereby lowering its quality and safety.[span_39](end_span)

11. (b) Explain the importance of checking for adulterants in food products. 2

[span_40](start_span)

Checking for adulterants is crucial to ensure food safety, prevent health hazards (like toxicity or allergies), and protect consumers from economic fraud.[span_40](end_span)

12. (b) Describe different methods used to detect adulteration in food products with examples. 5

    [span_41](start_span)
  • Physical Tests: Visual inspection or simple tests like checking for chalk in sugar by dissolving it in water.[span_41](end_span)
    • [span_42](start_span)
  • Chemical Tests: Using specific reagents, such as adding iodine to milk to detect starch (turns blue).[span_42](end_span)
    • [span_43](start_span)
  • Microscopic Analysis: Used to detect extraneous matter like insect parts or saw dust in spices.[span_43](end_span)
    • [span_44](start_span)
  • Chromatography: Advanced methods to detect prohibited synthetic dyes in oils or powders.[span_44](end_span)
