31. Illustrate the procedure for the separation of a mixture of polar and non-polar compounds by TLC. [5]

Procedure:

    [span_34](start_span)
  1. Apply a small spot of the mixture onto a TLC plate (stationary phase)[span_34](end_span).
    2. [span_35](start_span)
  2. Place the plate in a developing chamber containing a suitable solvent (mobile phase)[span_35](end_span).
  3. As the solvent rises, compounds distribute themselves between the mobile and stationary phases.
    4. [span_36](start_span)
  4. Non-polar compounds move faster/higher because they have less affinity for the polar stationary phase[span_36](end_span).
    5. [span_37](start_span)
  5. Polar compounds move slower/lower due to strong interaction with the stationary phase[span_37](end_span).

32. Types of errors and their minimization. [3+2=5]

[span_38](start_span)

Types of Errors: 1. Determinate (Systematic) Errors: Constant errors like instrumental faults or reagent impurities[span_38](end_span). 2. [span_39](start_span)Indeterminate (Random) Errors: Unpredictable fluctuations[span_39](end_span).

[span_40](start_span)

Minimization: Use calibrated instruments, run "blank" determinations, use standard reference materials, and perform replicate measurements to average out random errors[span_40](end_span).

33. Basic principle of complexometric titration and chelation. [3+2=5]

[span_41](start_span)

Principle: It is based on the formation of a stable, soluble complex between a metal ion and a ligand (complexing agent like EDTA)[span_41](end_span). [span_42](start_span)The endpoint is detected using a metal-ion indicator[span_42](end_span).

[span_43](start_span)

Chelation: It is a type of bonding where a single ligand (chelating agent) forms multiple bonds with a central metal ion, creating a ring-like structure that is highly stable[span_43](end_span).

37. Properties of a preservative and use of salt. [2+3=5]

[span_47](start_span)

39. Procedure for determining the dissolved oxygen (DO) in water. [5]

[span_49](start_span)

DO is commonly determined using the Winkler Method[span_49](end_span):

    [span_50](start_span)
  1. Fix the oxygen in the water sample using Manganese sulfate and Alkali-iodide-azide reagent[span_50](end_span).
    2. [span_51](start_span)
  2. Acidify the sample to release iodine equivalent to the amount of oxygen present[span_51](end_span).
    3. [span_52](start_span)
  3. Titrate the released iodine against a standard Sodium thiosulfate solution using starch as an indicator[span_52](end_span).
    4. [span_53](start_span)
  4. The volume of thiosulfate used is used to calculate the DO concentration[span_53](end_span).

40. Determination of Calcium and Magnesium by complexometric titration. [5]

    [span_54](start_span)
  1. Total Hardness (Ca + Mg): Titrate the sample with EDTA at pH 10 using Eriochrome Black T indicator[span_54](end_span).
  2. Calcium alone: Adjust pH to 12-13 (this precipitates Magnesium). [span_55](start_span)Titrate with EDTA using Murexide indicator[span_55](end_span).
    3. [span_56](start_span)
  3. Magnesium: Calculated by subtracting the Calcium value from the Total Hardness value[span_56](end_span).
