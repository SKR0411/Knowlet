What are tight junctions?[span_9](end_span)

[span_10](start_span)
Tight junctions are specialized intercellular connection sites where the membranes of two adjacent cells are fused together to form a barrier that prevents the leakage of fluids and solutes between cells.[span_10](end_span)

Question 2 (a) 10 Marks

[span_11](start_span)

Explain the different types of membrane transport mechanism in cells, highlighting their significance.[span_11](end_span)

Membrane transport is essential for maintaining cellular homeostasis. [span_12](start_span)The primary mechanisms include:[span_12](end_span)

  • Passive Transport: Movement along the concentration gradient without energy. [span_13](start_span)Includes simple diffusion, osmosis, and facilitated diffusion (using carrier/channel proteins).[span_13](end_span)
  • Active Transport: Movement against the concentration gradient using ATP. [span_14](start_span)Includes primary active transport (e.g., Sodium-Potassium pump) and secondary active transport.[span_14](end_span)
    • [span_15](start_span)
  • Vesicular Transport: Transport of large particles via endocytosis (taking in) and exocytosis (releasing out).[span_15](end_span)
[span_16](start_span)

Significance: These mechanisms ensure the cell receives nutrients, removes waste, and maintains electrochemical gradients necessary for nerve impulses and muscle contraction.[span_16](end_span)

Question 2 (b) 10 Marks

Describe the composition of plasma membrane. [span_17](start_span)Add a note on structure and function of desmosomes.[span_17](end_span)

[span_18](start_span)

The plasma membrane follows the Fluid Mosaic Model:[span_18](end_span)

    [span_19](start_span)
  • Lipids: Primarily phospholipids arranged in a bilayer, along with cholesterol for fluidity.[span_19](end_span)
    • [span_20](start_span)
  • Proteins: Integral proteins (spanning the bilayer) and peripheral proteins (attached to surfaces).[span_20](end_span)
    • [span_21](start_span)
  • Carbohydrates: Attached to lipids or proteins as glycolipids/glycoproteins for cell recognition.[span_21](end_span)
[span_22](start_span)

Desmosomes: These are "spot-weld" junctions that mechanically attach cells to each other, providing structural integrity to tissues like skin and heart muscle.[span_22](end_span)

Question 4 (a) 10 Marks

Describe the structure of mitochondria with suitable diagram. [span_28](start_span)Add a note on its functions.[span_28](end_span)

Structure: Double-membrane organelle. The outer membrane is smooth, while the inner membrane is folded into cristae. [span_29](start_span)The interior space is the matrix containing mitochondrial DNA and ribosomes.[span_29](end_span)

Functions: Known as the "powerhouse of the cell," it is the site of cellular respiration and ATP production. [span_30](start_span)It also plays a role in calcium storage and apoptosis.[span_30](end_span)

Question 4 (b) 10 Marks

Describe the structure and function of peroxisomes. [span_31](start_span)Add a note on function of lysosomes.[span_31](end_span)

Peroxisomes: Small, membrane-bound organelles containing oxidative enzymes like catalase. [span_32](start_span)They break down long-chain fatty acids and detoxify harmful substances (like hydrogen peroxide) into water and oxygen.[span_32](end_span)

[span_33](start_span)

Lysosomes: These are the "suicidal bags" of the cell, containing hydrolytic enzymes used for intracellular digestion, recycling of old organelles (autophagy), and breaking down foreign substances.[span_33](end_span)
